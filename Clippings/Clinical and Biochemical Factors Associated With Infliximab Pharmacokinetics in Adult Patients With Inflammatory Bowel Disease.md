## Abstract

Many patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) do not adequately respond to infliximab, potentially due to individual differences in pharmacokinetics (PK). This study aimed to identify clinical and biochemical variables associated with infliximab PK. Adult IBD patients treated with infliximab and with registered infliximab trough levels between March 1, 2021 and November 22, 2023, were included. Univariable and multivariable linear mixed‐effects models were used to evaluate associations between clinical and biochemical covariates and infliximab trough levels. A systematic literature review was conducted to contextualize findings. The study included 259 patients (CD: n = 202; UC: n = 47; IBD‐unclassified: n = 10) with 560 infliximab trough levels available for analysis. Higher hemoglobin was associated with lower infliximab levels (IBD: β = 0.668, *p* = 0.036; UC: β = 1.639, *p* = 0.018), as were albumin (IBD: β = 0.214, *p* = 0.002; CD: β = 0.345, *p* < 0.001) and AST (CD: β = 0.040, *p* = 0.019). Inverse associations were also found for ALT (UC: β = −0.100, *p* = 0.016), ALP (CD: β = −0.029, *p* = 0.021; UC: β = −0.018, *p* = 0.037) and GGT (IBD: β = −0.015, *p* = 0.028; UC: β = −0.034, *p* < 0.001). Endoscopic inflammation was positively associated with infliximab levels in CD (β = 3.402, *p* = 0.035). Systemic steroid use was associated with lower infliximab levels in UC (β = −4.263, *p* = 0.013). In conclusion, this study identified several clinical and biochemical variables, including albumin, hemoglobin and GGT across all patients, albumin, AST and ALP in CD, and hemoglobin, ALT, ALP and GGT in UC, that were associated with infliximab trough levels. Given the correlative nature of this study, these results should be interpreted cautiously. These findings merit validation and could inform model‐informed precision dosing strategies in IBD clinical care.

**Keywords:** Inflammatory bowel disease, infliximab, pharmacokinetics, therapeutic drug monitoring

## Introduction

Inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD), encompassing Crohn's disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC), are characterized by chronic immune‐mediated inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract. Approximately 6.8 million people live with IBD worldwide, with incidences rising sharply.[^1] A key target of IBD management is to achieve and maintain clinical, biochemical and endoscopic remission, which are all associated with improved quality of life and socioeconomic participation of patients.[^2] IBD pathophysiology involves dysregulated immune responses against intestinal microorganisms in genetically susceptible individuals.[^3] In addition, lifestyle‐ and dietary factors contribute to disease pathogenesis.[^4] However, the exact mechanisms leading up to disease onset remain unclear. Alongside this pathophysiological complexity, IBD is also clinically a complex, unpredictable, and heterogeneous disease, which is difficult to treat effectively. This is illustrated by the fact that approximately 30% of patients fail to respond to initial biologic treatment and 50% of patients experience loss‐of‐response during follow‐up, highlighting the need for more effective treatment strategies.[^5]

Over the past decades, the therapeutic armamentarium for IBD has significantly expanded, with a myriad of novel drugs that have become available. Infliximab constitutes the mainstay of treatment in IBD and is the most widely used biologic.[^6] In patients with CD, initial response rates range from approximately 25%‐48% and clinical maintenance rates range from approximately 39%‐59% at weeks 20‐30. In patients with UC, initial response rates vary from 28%‐39% with clinical maintenance rates around 26%‐37% at week 30.[^7] In both CD and UC, the annual loss of response is approximately 10%.[^8], [^9]

Infliximab is known as a chimeric mouse‐human monoclonal IgG antibody targeting tumor necrosis factor‐alpha (TNF‐α). Due to its large molecular structure, infliximab is not resistant to the gastrointestinal environment and is therefore administered either intravenously or subcutaneously. Infliximab is characterized by a high peak‐to‐trough profile, driven by relatively large dosages and extended intervals between administrations.[^10] Its high molecular weight and hydrophilicity prevent renal clearance, with catabolism primarily occurring via the reticuloendothelial system (RES), resulting in a half‐life of approximately two weeks.[^10] This process is accelerated during inflammatory states, as marked by elevated C‐reactive protein (CRP) and indirectly by low albumin levels.[^10]

The efficacy of dashboard‐driven dosing of infliximab in IBD has been increasingly studied over the past couple of years. Strik et al. developed a Bayesian dashboard system to determine optimal dosing regimens for patients with CD or UC. All patients were in clinical remission (CD: Harvey Bradshaw index (HBI) < 4, UC: Partial Mayo (PM) score < 2 for UC) and received infliximab for at least 14 weeks. After 1 year, 88% of patients who received personalized dosing were in remission versus 64% of patients in the control group who received standard dosing.[^11] This example highlights the role and importance of personalized therapy based on pharmacokinetics (PK) in achieving remission and desired treatment outcomes. Another novel approach to dosing infliximab is therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM), which primarily aims at optimizing dosing strategies by measuring biologic concentrations in the blood and the potential presence of antibodies.[^12] However, therapeutic strategies to identify optimal drug concentration and dose interval for each individual patient are lacking.[^12] This issue can be addressed by model‐informed precision dosing (MIPD). MIPD uses mathematical predictions of dosing, accounting for interindividual PK variability, ensuring a personalized treatment.[^12] Using these techniques to tailor dosages based on clinical, biochemical and endoscopic variables may improve the efficacy, safety, and tolerability of infliximab treatment. However, associations between potentially relevant clinical and biochemical covariates and infliximab PK remain incompletely defined.

In this study, we therefore aimed to identify clinical and biochemical variables influencing infliximab PK in adult patients with IBD. To achieve this, extensive clinical and biochemical data from a large cohort of infliximab‐treated patients with IBD with serial measurements of infliximab through levels were retrospectively collected, and an exploratory association analysis to identify relevant covariates was performed.

## Methods

### Study Cohort and Design

This retrospective observational cohort study included patients with IBD aged 18 years or above, who were treated with infliximab at the University Medical Center Groningen (UMCG) in the Netherlands between March 1, 2021 and November 22, 2023. Most patients originated from the northern part of the Netherlands and were identified based on available infliximab trough levels recorded in a hospital‐based pharmacy registry. Drug levels were excluded based on patients <18 years of age, no IBD diagnosis or uncertainties regarding IBD diagnosis, no trough levels, prior infliximab use, use of multiple biologics at the same time, and uncertainties regarding dose intervals and the use of infliximab. All patients provided written informed consent for the use of their data and biomaterials as part of their participation in the Parelsnoer IBD biobank (Institutional Review Board (IRB) no. 2008/338). This study was reviewed by the IRB of the UMCG and classified as non‐Medical Research Involving Human Subjects Act research (IRB no. 2023/545).

### Data Collection and Study Definitions

The primary outcome of this study consisted of infliximab trough levels measured in patients with IBD and their potential association with clinical and biochemical covariates. Clinical and biochemical parameters were collected. Clinical variables included dosing intervals, dose of infliximab, age, sex, ethnicity, diagnosis (CD, UC or IBD‐unclassified (IBD‐U)), body mass index, extra intestinal manifestations (EIM), primary sclerosing cholangitis, smoking behavior, Montreal classification, concurrent medication use (including steroids \[systemic, local and as premedication\], thiopurines, aminosalicylates \[systemic and local\], methotrexate, calcineurin inhibitors, antibiotics and proton pump inhibitors), surgical history (colectomy and ileocecal resection) and disease activity. Clinical disease activity was assessed using the HBI for CD and the Simple Clinical Colitis Activity Index for UC. Biochemical parameters that were recorded included infliximab trough concentrations, faecal calprotectin (fCal), CRP, hemoglobin, white blood cell count (WBC), platelets, albumin, ferritin, transferrin saturation, bilirubin, alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), gamma‐glutamyl transferase (GGT), alkaline phosphatase (ALP), creatinine, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), presence of anti‐ infliximab antibodies (ATI) and their titers, all of which were measured simultaneously or at maximum within 7 days of infliximab trough level measurements. In a subset of patients (n = 63, 24.0%) 84 endoscopies were conducted. The presence of endoscopic inflammation when endoscopy was performed with a maximum time window of 3 months from the date of the infliximab trough level measurement was recorded. When biochemical covariates were reported as below the lower limit of quantification, the value equal to the reported quantification limit was used. Missing data were assessed for each covariate (Table [S1](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001)). Since the amount of missing data was as low as 5% for the majority of covariates, no imputation was performed. Covariates with higher levels of missingness were interpreted with caution in subsequent analyses.

### Measurements of Infliximab Trough Levels and ATI

Blood samples to assess infliximab trough levels were taken prior to infliximab infusions. Samples were analyzed at Sanquin Diagnostics Laboratory in the Netherlands, following a previously described protocol.[^13], [^14] In short, infliximab trough levels were measured using an enzyme‐linked immunosorbent assay, commonly referred to as ELISA.[^15] The samples were analyzed at room temperature at 4°C and could be analyzed up to 14 days after collection.[^14] Detectable concentrations ranged from 0.03 to 250 µg/mL.[^15] An antigen‐binding assay to assess ATI levels was conducted in cases where infliximab levels were <1 µg/mL or when the clinical efficacy of infliximab was uncertain.[^16] Results were subdivided into four different groups: <12 AE/mL was not detectable, 12‐30 AE/mL was detectable, but not quantifiable, 30‐875 AE/mL was detectable and quantifiable, and >875 AE/mL was detectable, but above the upper limit of quantification.[^17]

### Statistical Analysis

Demographic characteristics of the study population were presented as means with standard deviations (SD), medians with interquartile range (IQR), or as proportions (n) with corresponding percentages (%), depending on variable types and distributions. Normal probability (Q–Q) plots and histograms were used to assess normality of distributions.

Linear mixed‐effects regression models were constructed to determine associations between infliximab trough levels at the maintenance phase and the variables of interest, which allowed to account for repeated measurements. Trough levels measured during the induction phase were excluded from the linear mixed‐effects models, because they are substantially higher than trough levels measured during the maintenance phase and could therefore introduce bias. Variables of interest were first univariably evaluated as individual fixed effects, either as factor (enabling to compare estimated marginal means \[EMM\] between groups) or as covariate (enabling to analyze linear trends). A random effect was applied to individual subjects to account for repeated measurements. Parameter estimates were presented as beta‐coefficients with standard errors (SE) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs). For categorical variables, EMMs were presented with their corresponding 95% CIs. Analyses were performed for the entire cohort, including CD, UC, and IBD‐U. Furthermore, stratified analysis was performed for CD and UC and for biochemical disease activity (based on fCal levels > 200) in CD and UC separately.[^18] Prior to multivariable analysis, biochemical covariates were tested for multicollinearity using variance inflation factors (VIFs) and multicollinearity matrices, Tables [S2‐S7](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001). VIFs > 5 were considered as having high multicollinearity and were therefore excluded from multivariable modeling. Multivariable analysis was performed based on significantly associated parameters from univariable analysis (adopting a *P* ‐value threshold ≤ 0.1), which were subsequently analyzed by backward selection. A best‐fitting model to study potential interactions between these parameters was identified based on the −2 log‐likelihood and Akaike Information Criterion. Statistical analyses were performed using SPSS Statistics software package (v.29.0.1.0) (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA). Two‐sided *P* ‐values ≤ 0.05 were considered statistically significant. Data visualizations were made using the matplotlib (v.3.4.1) and seaborn (v.0.11.1) packages in Python (v.3.11, Python Software Foundation), while schematic visualizations were created in BioRender.

### Systematic Literature Review

A systematic literature review was performed focusing on population PK models (popPK models) for infliximab in patients diagnosed with IBD and other immune‐mediated inflammatory diseases, including rheumatoid arthritis and ankylosing spondylitis. The systematic literature search was performed using PubMed/MEDLINE and took place between September 2023 and July 2024. Both retro‐ and prospective studies were included. Only articles written in English were included irrespective of the date of publication. The search terms included “population pharmacokinetic model,” “popPK,” and “Infliximab”. First, the title and abstract of each article were screened for relevance, focusing on infliximab as the drug of interest and the presence of new or updated popPK models. Second, a full text assessment was performed in order to determine whether the article met the inclusion criteria. The study should have developed a new or updated popPK model of infliximab in the adult population. If the popPK model was developed from a study population consisting of pediatrics and adults, the difference between pediatrics and adults should be investigated as possible covariate. PopPK models that were developed from solely a pediatric study population were excluded. The references of the included articles were screened for adult patients with IBD and other immune‐mediated inflammatory diseases. A summary table including analyzed covariates, patient characteristics and their relationships with the PK of infliximab was established to create a comprehensive overview of known infliximab PK covariates.

## Results

### Study Cohort and Descriptive Data

In total, 560 infliximab trough levels were included for analysis (maintenance trough levels: n = 529, 94.5%; induction trough levels: n = 31, 5.5%) (Figure [1](#jcph70111-fig-0001)). Baseline characteristics of the study sample are shown in Table [1](#jcph70111-tbl-0001). A total of 259 patients were included. Most of them were diagnosed with CD (n = 202, 81.1%), while the other 47 got diagnosed with UC (18.8%). Another 10 patients were diagnosed with IBD‐U, who accounted for 28 (5.0%) infliximab trough levels. These patients were excluded from CD‐ and UC‐specific analyses, due to their small group size and indifferent diagnosis. The median age of patients with CD was 38.3 years \[IQR: 24.0; 50.0\] and 116 (57.4%) were female. Among patients with UC, 22 (46.8%) were female and had a median age of 32.4 \[IQR: 23.4; 53.3\] years. Patients were treated with a median infliximab dose of 5.0 mg/kg in both CD and UC (CD: \[IQR: 5.0; 6.3\], UC: \[IQR: 5.0; 7.0\]) with median dosing intervals of 8.0 weeks (CD: \[IQR: 6.0; 8.0\], UC: \[IQR: 6.0; 8.0\], respectively).

### Associations Between Infliximab Levels and Clinical Covariates

Following univariable analysis of the total IBD cohort, the use of CNIs was significantly associated with higher infliximab trough levels (unstandardized β = 8.438; *P* = 0.002). However, it should be noted that calcineurin inhibitors were only used concurrently in 5 (0.9%) cases, warranting cautious interpretation of this finding. No other types of medications were identified that were significantly associated with infliximab trough levels. Likewise, other patient characteristics, including sex, age, ethnicity, EIM, smoking, Montreal classification, and disease activity, were also not associated with infliximab trough levels.

Following univariable analysis only in patients with CD, no clinical covariates were identified in significant association with infliximab trough levels.

Among patients with UC, only the use of systemic steroids was positively correlated with infliximab trough levels (β = 1.688, *P* = 0.013).

### Associations Between Infliximab Levels and Biochemical Covariates

Subsequently, the relationships between biochemical variables and infliximab trough levels were studied. Among the several blood parameters analyzed, we found that albumin (β = 0.214, *P* = 0.002) (Figure [2a](#jcph70111-fig-0002)) and hemoglobin (β = 0.668, *P* = 0.036) were positively correlated with infliximab trough levels in the entire cohort. Moreover, GGT levels were inversely associated with higher infliximab trough levels (β = −0.015, *P* = 0.028).

### Associations Between Infliximab Levels and Endoscopic Covariates

Potential associations between endoscopic inflammation and infliximab trough levels were also examined. Here, we found that the presence of endoscopic inflammation in CD was positively associated with infliximab trough levels (β = 3.402, *P* = 0.035). However, it should be noted that endoscopic data was only available for 48 (23.8%) patients, with a total of 60 endoscopies performed.

### Biochemical Disease Activity Impacts Associations With Infliximab Trough Levels

Based on fCal levels, stratified analysis for biochemical disease activity (fCal > 200) was performed separately in a subset of patients with CD and UC (Tables [S11‐S15](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001)). Patients with active CD showed a positive correlation between AST (β = 0.044, *P* = 0.002) (Figure [4a](#jcph70111-fig-0004)) and endoscopic inflammation (β = 5.529, *P* = 0.013) with infliximab trough levels. Patients with a longer history of disease had lower infliximab trough levels (β = −0.050, *P* = 0.032). Furthermore, ALP (β = −0.057, *P* = 0.044) (Figure [4b](#jcph70111-fig-0004)) and PPIs (β = −2.976, *P* = 0.050) were negatively associated with infliximab trough levels in patients with quiescent CD. Among patients with active UC, CRP (β = −0.053, *P* = 0.009), ALT (β = −0.135, *P* = 0.027) (Figure [4c](#jcph70111-fig-0004)) and GGT (β = −0.033, *P* = 0.007) (Figure [4d](#jcph70111-fig-0004)) were all higher in patients with lower infliximab trough levels. Hemoglobin (β = 3.040, *P* = 0.031) (Figure [4b](#jcph70111-fig-0004)) and systemic steroids (β = −5.997, *P* = 0.031) were inversely related to infliximab trough levels. However, steroids as premedication resulted in higher trough levels (β = 14.379, *P* = 0.007). No significant associations with infliximab trough levels in patients with quiescent UC were found.

### Multivariable Analysis in the IBD and CD Cohorts

As a next step, we performed multivariable analysis to study associations between factors from univariable analysis (*P ≤* 0.1) and infliximab trough levels in combination with each other. On multivariable analysis using data from all patients, infliximab dose interval (β = −1.666, *P* ≤ 0.001), ATI (β = −5.142, *P* ≤ 0.001) and albumin (β = 0.249, *P* ≤ 0.001) remained significantly associated with infliximab trough levels (Table [2](#jcph70111-tbl-0002)). Since no evidence of multicollinearity was identified (Tables [S2‐S7](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001)), the drop‐out of other biochemical covariates is likely due to the fact that albumin is a stronger independent correlate of infliximab trough levels. Among patients with CD, infliximab dose interval (β = −0.789, *P* ≤ 0.041), age of disease onset (A2: β = −2.599, *P* = 0.011; A3: β = −2.463, *P* = 0.011), fCal (β = −0.001, *P* ≤ 0.001) and endoscopic inflammation (β = 5.333, *P* ≤ 0.001) remained significantly associated (Table [3](#jcph70111-tbl-0003)). No significant results in multivariable analysis of patients with UC were identified, likely due to the limited sample size. Finally, among patients with quiescent CD the following combination was identified: proton pump inhibitors (β = −2.999, *P* = 0.039), ALP (β = −0.057, *P* = 0.038), and ATI (β = −7.222, *P* = 0.009) (Supplementary Table [S13](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001)).

### Findings of Systematic Literature Study

Last, a systematic literature study focusing on popPK models for infliximab was performed to better contextualize the obtained findings (Table [4](#jcph70111-tbl-0004) and Supplementary Tables [S18‐S20](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001)). In total, 26 studies were included. Based on our literature study, several previously unreported covariates in IBD, CD, and UC were found that may be potentially relevant to infliximab PK. For the entire cohort, hemoglobin and GGT emerged as potential novel covariates. Among CD, AST and ALP were suggested as potential new covariates. Finally, hemoglobin, ALT, ALP and GGT have not previously been described as covariates for UC [^19], [^20] (see references S1 to S24 in Supporting Information). It should be noted that the retained covariates in the popPK models typically result from multivariate analyses, whereas in our study, we compare them with our univariate analyses.

## Discussion

In this study, positive associations between infliximab trough levels and hemoglobin, albumin and GGT in patients with IBD were identified. Among patients with CD, albumin, AST and endoscopic inflammation were predominantly found to be positively associated with infliximab trough levels, while ALP was inversely related. Among patients with UC, hemoglobin was positively associated with infliximab trough levels, while ALT, ALP, GGT, and systemic steroids were all inversely associated. Following our systemic literature study, hemoglobin and GGT have not been previously reported in popPK studies in relation to infliximab trough levels. Similarly, in patients with CD, AST and ALP have not been previously identified in popPK studies, while hemoglobin, ALT, ALP, and GGT appear to be novel identified covariates in patients with UC. Altogether, these factors may potentially be relevant to infliximab PK. In the multivariate model of all patients, infliximab interval and ATI were negatively associated with infliximab trough levels, while albumin was found to be positively associated with the trough levels. On multivariable analysis of patients with CD, we identified fCal, dose interval, and age of disease onset as being inversely associated with infliximab trough levels, with a concurrently positive association for endoscopic inflammation. Collectively, this study identifies distinct clinical and biochemical covariates longitudinally associated with infliximab trough levels, which may potentially serve as relevant factors considered in MIPD tools for infliximab in IBD.

### Clinical Covariates

A higher age of disease onset was found to be associated with lower infliximab trough levels in the multivariable model of patients with CD. One of the studies, which focused on the effect of age (6‐76 years) on infliximab PK in CD, demonstrated that age did not have an effect on infliximab clearance.[^21] Another study showed that patients > 30 years had lower infliximab serum levels and higher ATI compared to patients ≤ 30 years.[^22] Nevertheless, stratified analysis on associations of ATI with infliximab trough levels for disease onset in CD showed that patients with a disease onset ≤ 16 years (β = −4.593, *P =* 0.018) and between 17‐40 years (β = −6.419, *P* ≤ 0.001) were more likely to develop ATI compared to patients with a disease onset at >40 years (β = −0.929, *P* = 0.526). Additional research is needed regarding the effect of age on infliximab PK to gain more insights in treatment discontinuation in elderly individuals and to obtain more insights in personalized dosing among specific age groups.

Long term disease duration in patients with CD with active disease was associated with lower infliximab trough levels. It could be argued that longer disease duration is associated with increased immunogenicity, enhancing infliximab clearance. To our knowledge, little is known about the effect of disease duration on infliximab treatment outcomes. A study focusing on achieved response and remission rates of certolizumab pegol in patients with short‐term CD showed that disease duration was inversely related to the proportions of patients who responded to certolizumab.[^23] It may therefore be interesting to further study disease duration as a determinant of infliximab efficacy.

Among the medications evaluated, the use of proton pump inhibitors was associated with lower infliximab trough levels in patients with quiescent CD. Proton pump inhibitors have been shown to alter the gut microbiome diversity due to a shift in pH.[^24] This could exacerbate intestinal inflammation and increase the clearance of infliximab. One study examining the impact of proton pump inhibitors use on achieving remission in IBD with infliximab showed that patients using proton pump inhibitors were less likely to reach remission.[^25] These findings support a cautious use of proton pump inhibitors in IBD to prevent reduced efficacy of medications such as infliximab.

Systemic steroids were found to be inversely related to infliximab trough levels for UC and predominantly in active UC. Systemic inflammation is associated with increased infliximab clearance by several mechanisms. First of all, inflammation is associated with lower albumin levels. Notably, albumin has been shown to impact infliximab PK.[^26] Second, severe intestinal inflammation may cause fecal loss of albumin and infliximab by a ‘leaky gut’ and thereby accelerating infliximab clearance.[^26], [^27] Third, higher disease activity is characterized by an increased TNF‐α production resulting in accelerated infliximab clearance by target‐mediated drug disposition (TMDD).[^28] Fourth, a study on risk factors for ATI showed that patients with increased disease activity were more prone to develop ATI, which may also contribute to increased infliximab clearance.[^29] To counteract this, steroids can be effective by facilitating rapid control of inflammation and therefore inducing clinical remission, while adjusting for infliximab dose or interval. However, results from previous studies focusing on the effect of steroids on infliximab PK seem contradictory. One study found that the volume of distribution of infliximab was 33% larger when concomitant steroids were used at the induction phase and 23% larger when patients were diagnosed with pancolitis.[^20] Another study found that steroids did not have an effect on infliximab PK in UC.[^30] Moreover, a recent study on the effectiveness of prednisolone in patients with UC who were treated with infliximab concluded that steroids did not interfere with infliximab PK in the induction phase.[^20] On the contrary, in patients with a more severe disease burden, corticosteroids did appear to be effective, suggesting a role of steroids in optimizing infliximab PK in patients with UC and a higher disease burden.

Despite treatment‐related factors, body weight is widely recognized as a covariate interfering with infliximab PK.[^30] Nonetheless, here body weight was not found to be significantly associated with infliximab trough levels in our cohort. We speculate that this may be due to the relatively low variation in body weight among study subjects, as reflected by a median weight of 74.8 kg with an IQR ranging from 65.0 to 89.5 kg.

### Biochemical Covariates

Hemoglobin was identified to be positively associated with infliximab trough levels, particularly in patients with UC. Systemic inflammation is characterized by rectal blood loss and is known to accelerate infliximab clearance. Stratified analysis for UC with disease activity indeed showed a positive association between hemoglobin and infliximab trough levels, explaining the aforementioned correlation. A possible explanation for this correlation being observed only in UC is as follows. Patients with UC showed higher median calprotectin (355.0 \[57.0‐1880.0\]) than CD (87.0 \[35.5‐192.5\]). This suggests more mucosal inflammation and associated blood loss, which may explain the association between hemoglobin and infliximab trough levels in UC only. Although hemoglobin was not previously found in relation to infliximab PK following our systematic literature study, it may still be a potentially relevant covariate. For example, a study focusing on the effect of TNF‐α inhibitors on hemoglobin levels in IBD showed a significant long‐term improvement of hemoglobin levels.[^31]

Another factor that was found to be positively associated with infliximab trough levels, even in multivariable analysis, was albumin. Albumin is one of the most common patient factors interfering with infliximab PK, making albumin a predictive marker of infliximab PK.[^32] There are several potential explanations for this observation. First, the neonatal fragment crystallizable receptor (FcRn) binds to both immunoglobulin G (including infliximab) and albumin, preventing them from intracellular catabolism and thereby increasing the half‐life of both infliximab and albumin.[^33], [^34], [^35] Higher levels of albumin are associated with higher levels of FcRn receptors, protecting albumin from degradation in the blood.[^35] Moreover, FcRn receptors protect infliximab from breakdown in the blood, resulting in decreased infliximab clearance.[^35] Second, albumin and infliximab have comparable molecular weights (approximately 66.5 and 150 kDa, respectively) and primary distributions within the plasma.[^35] Albumin can therefore function as a surrogate for monoclonal antibodies such as infliximab, especially during uncontrolled inflammation, due to their behavioral similarities. As explained previously, inflammation is often associated with fecal loss of albumin, causing decreased albumin levels in the blood. Although albumin does not directly interfere with infliximab PK, its serum concentration may serve as a marker of inflammation and FcRn‐mediated preservation of infliximab. However, stratified analysis on disease activity in both CD and UC did not show any significant results for albumin with infliximab trough levels, presumably due to decreased statistical power.

Another interesting set of observations from this study relates to the liver enzymes, including AST, ALT, ALP, and GGT, which were identified as potential covariates. Notably, ALT and GGT were inversely associated with infliximab trough levels, specifically in patients with UC. Similarly, ALP was inversely correlated with infliximab trough levels in CD and UC. In contrast, a positive association between AST and infliximab trough levels in CD was identified. Hepatobiliary manifestations are common in IBD: 30% of the patients present with abnormal liver test results, though not all of them are symptomatic.[^36] The most plausible explanation for these abnormal liver function parameters in the context of IBD relates to disease activity.

It can be argued that increased production of acute‐phase proteins, such as CRP and fCal, during inflammation could stress the liver, resulting in mildly elevated liver enzymes, which was confirmed by the negative association between ALT and GGT with infliximab trough levels in active UC. Interestingly, ALP was found to inversely correlate with infliximab trough levels in quiescent CD and even in multivariable analysis. An explanation for this may be found in the following. ALP is not only known as a cholestatic enzyme, but also as a group of dephosphorylating enzymes.[^37] One of the isoforms of ALP is intestinal ALP (IAP), an enzyme known to dephosphorylate lipopolysaccharide (LPS).[^38], [^39] LPS plays a role in exacerbating IBD by binding to the Toll‐like receptor 4. IAPs therefore, have a crucial role in maintaining epithelial integrity and preventing inflammation.[^39] Our finding, ALP being inversely related with infliximab trough levels in quiescent CD, seems therefore counterintuitive. When no disease activity is present, immune activity is also reduced, leading to less production of TNF‐α resulting in decreased clearance of infliximab. Moreover, TNF‐ α is known to inhibit IAP production.[^40] Therefore, a positive association between ALP with infliximab trough levels would be expected. Nevertheless, our analysis found the opposite to be true. Conceivably, during a low inflammatory state, the need for IAP to detoxify LPS is reduced, which may be reflected by decreased levels of circulating ALP. However, the precise role of ALP in infliximab PK remains unclear in our study, making it an interesting covariate to further analyze.

Furthermore, ALT and GGT exhibit a negative association with infliximab trough levels in UC, but not in CD. As mentioned before, patients with UC showed higher median calprotectin levels compared to patients with CD. Stratified analysis showed significant associations of ALT and GGT with infliximab trough levels in active UC only, indicating the role of inflammation in this correlation.

Interestingly, a positive association between AST with infliximab trough levels was found in CD, particularly those with active disease. AST is, in contrast to the other liver enzymes, widely distributed throughout the body, including the heart, skeletal muscles, kidneys, brain and red blood cells.[^41] It may be hypothesized that the presence of systemic inflammation leads to an increase in AST levels and an alteration of the normal RES functioning. In situations where inflammation is present, RES can become overloaded or dysfunctional and therefore decelerate infliximab clearance.[^42] A possible explanation for observing this association in CD only, is that CD tends to have a more systemic inflammation pattern than UC, which may increase the likelihood of interfering with normal RES functioning.

Nevertheless, thiopurines are widely used in our study population (CD 51.5%, UC 55.3%) and are known to increase AST and ALT as well.[^43] Therefore, stratified analysis based on thiopurine use (Supplementary Tables [S16](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001) and [S17](#jcph70111-supinfo-0001)) were performed. Patients with CD who were not treated with thiopurines had a positive association of AST with infliximab trough levels, indicating that increased AST levels are not caused by liver damage related to thiopurine use. Finally, based on our systematic literature study, AST, ALT, ALP and GGT have not yet fully been studied in their relation with infliximab PK, which makes them interesting covariates to further investigate.

### Endoscopic Covariates

A potential association between endoscopically proven intestinal inflammation with infliximab trough levels was studied and a positive significant association was found between endoscopic inflammation with infliximab trough levels in CD, in CD with active disease and in multivariable analysis of CD. To the best of our knowledge, this finding is contradictory with previous studies. A study assessing a popPK/pharmacodynamic (PD) model of the interactions between infliximab dose, exposure, biomarker and response (fCal and endoscopic remission) in CD demonstrates that higher disease activity is associated with increased inflammation and accelerated infliximab clearance.[^19]

Not only does our finding appear to contradict previous studies, but it also contradicts our earlier results. Steroid use and higher hemoglobin and albumin levels were associated with lower infliximab trough levels, likely driven by inflammatory processes accelerating infliximab clearance. A plausible explanation for these inconsistencies is as follows. Most of the infliximab trough levels that were analyzed, were part of maintenance therapy (414, 96.3%), and most of the endoscopies were performed in a three‐month time window around the infliximab trough level. Therefore, in future studies, it would be interesting to perform an endoscopy on the same day that the trough levels are measured, to further assess a potential association between the presence of endoscopic inflammation and infliximab trough levels.

### Strengths and Limitations

One of the key strengths of this study is the large sample size, supporting the reliability of linear mixed effects modeling. Moreover, all measurements in this study were conducted in a consistent and standardized manner and data was collected from well‐documented electronic medical records. Furthermore, the covariates potentially relevant to infliximab PK, are easily measurable and noninvasive, making them accessible for clinicians to use in MIPD. Last, a literature review on covariates potentially relevant to infliximab PK was performed to better contextualize the obtained findings.

Some limitations of the study should also be taken into account. First, because of the limited amount of available trough levels at the induction phase, the PK of infliximab in this phase was not studied. Second, while we studied associations of infliximab trough levels with clinical and biochemical covariates, we cannot determine whether these findings reflect correlation or causation. Similarly, given the sparsity of our dataset (with on average 2‐3 trough concentrations per individual), our study design did not allow for more complex pop PK modeling, for which larger datasets with additional longitudinal measurements would be required. Third, whereas most of the studies included in our systematic literature review identified covariates trough multivariable analyses, we also considered factors from univariate analyses as potentially relevant. Fourth, stratified analysis in active and quiescent UC resulted in relatively small cohorts, warranting caution for interpretating these results. Fifth, disease activity was measured based on fCal > 200. However, disease activity can most reliably be measured through the use of endoscopies. Unfortunately, endoscopies were not routinely performed. This prevented us from performing stratified analysis on endoscopically measured disease activity. Sixth, this study retrospectively analyzed the association between many different covariates and infliximab trough levels over time. Due to this retrospective nature, no standardized TDM strategy was used. Therefore, this study population may not be representative of the IBD population at large. Repeating the study in non‐academic hospitals, as well as screening patients on infliximab trough levels regardless of disease activity, may lead to broader identification of covariates potentially relevant to infliximab PK in IBD. Last, not all required data could be retrieved from the electronic health records. Due to the retrospective study design, additional information could not be required.

## Conclusions

This study shows a potential role of hemoglobin, albumin and GGT as covariates potentially relevant to infliximab PK in adult patients with IBD. In patients with CD, albumin, AST, ALP and endoscopic inflammation were associated with infliximab trough levels, while hemoglobin, ALT, ALP and GGT were identified as potentially relevant in patients with UC. Albumin findings were confirmed by our literature review, while hemoglobin and GGT emerged as potential new covariates. In patients with CD, AST, and ALP were suggested as new covariates, and in patients with UC, hemoglobin, ALT, ALP, and GGT were main factors. However, it should be noted that these covariates stemmed from univariate analyses and it remains uncertain whether these findings reflect causation or correlation. In future studies, these covariates should be validated in multivariate models and be considered for integration in popPK/PD models and PK/PD dashboards in order to make patient‐specific prediction models for infliximab treatment.
