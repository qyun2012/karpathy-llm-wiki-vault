## Introduction

Inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD), primarily comprising ulcerative colitis (UC) and Crohn's disease (CD), are chronic inflammatory disorders affecting the gastrointestinal tract. With prevalence rates approaching 1 % in Western countries and rising rapidly in newly industrialized nations, IBD poses substantial challenges to both patient quality of life and healthcare systems. The pathogenesis of IBD involves complex interactions between genetic susceptibility, environmental factors, and dysregulated immune responses to gut microbiota. Central to this process is the disruption of host-microbiota homeostasis, leading to chronic mucosal inflammation. This inflammatory cascade is primarily mediated by TNF-α, which activates NF-κB and MAPK pathways, compromising intestinal barrier function and promoting tissue damage \[,, \].

The introduction of anti-TNF biologics, particularly infliximab, has revolutionized IBD management. However, treatment outcomes remain notably variable, with primary non-response rates ranging from 10 % to 40 % in clinical practice. Moreover, up to 46 % of patients experience secondary loss of response within the first year of treatment. This variability, coupled with potential adverse effects and substantial healthcare costs, underscores the critical need for predictive biomarkers to optimize treatment strategies. Recent advances in microbiome research have revealed the crucial role of gut microbiota in health and disease. Studies show that CD patients exhibit characteristic alterations in their gut microbiome, including reduced diversity and specific taxonomic changes. For instance, depletion of protective short-chain fatty acid (SCFA)-producing bacteria and enrichment of pro-inflammatory species are consistently associated with disease activity \[,, \].

Furthermore, gut microbes significantly influence disease pathogenesis through their impact on host metabolism. Metabolomics studies have identified distinct biochemical profiles that differentiate IBD patients from healthy controls, with significant alterations in microbial metabolic functions and microbiota-derived products \[,\]. The fecal metabolome has emerged as a direct readout of gut microbiota function, providing crucial insights into the consequences of dysbiosis \[\].While integrative analysis of microbiome and metabolomics data offers a comprehensive understanding of host-microbe interactions \[,\], several critical knowledge gaps persist. The extent to which anti-TNF therapy modulates gut microbial communities and their metabolic functions remains poorly understood. Moreover, conventional statistical approaches often fail to capture the complex variations in microbiome structure and their relationship to treatment outcomes.

To address these challenges, we initiated a prospective observational study involving 100 CD patients treated with infliximab. Our research employs a multi-omics approach, combining fecal bacteriomics and metabolomics analyses to provide a comprehensive view of the gut ecosystem in CD. We developed and compared various machine learning models to predict treatment outcomes, with innovative features including longitudinal sampling to track temporal changes, comprehensive integration of clinical, microbiome, and metabolomic data, as well as mechanistic validation through animal models. This study not only advances our understanding of microbe-metabolism interactions in anti-TNF therapy response but also establishes a framework for personalized therapeutic approaches in CD management.

## Materials and methods

### Study design and participants

All participants were verbally informed and provided consent prior to biological sample collection. The cohort comprised adult CD patients (18–60 years) initiating infliximab therapy at the gastroenterology department between April 2018 and June 2022, following standard induction protocol (5 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, 6, 14, 22, 30). Diagnostic confirmation adhered to 2015 WGO Inflammatory Bowel Disease Guidelines \[\], with enrollment requiring active disease status (Crohn's Disease Activity Index \[CDAI\] > 150). Exclusion critetia were: differential diagnoses (intestinal tuberculosis, lymphoma, radiation/ischemic colitis), infectious colitides (dysentery, schistosomiasis), comorbid malignancies or severe systemic diseases (hepatic/renal failure, metabolic disorders), and gestational/lactational status. Antibiotic/microbiological exposure within 4 weeks prior to fecal sampling constituted additional exclusion criteria. Following rigorous screening, 100 treatment-naïve CD patients met inclusion requirements. A matched control group (n = 49) of healthy cohabitants was established to mitigate lifestyle/dietary confounding variables, with all controls demonstrating absence of gastrointestinal pathologies through comprehensive clinical evaluation. Clinical efficacy of infliximab was assessed at week 14 using a comprehensive multi-parameter approach integrating clinical, pharmacokinetic, and immunogenicity criteria. Clinical response was defined as achieving a CDAI score <150 or a reduction of ≥70 points from baseline CDAI. Pharmacokinetic assessment included measurement of infliximab trough levels (TLI) and anti-drug antibodies (ATI) at week 14. Primary response (R) was defined as meeting clinical response criteria combined with adequate drug exposure (TLI ≥ 2.5 μg/mL) and absence of immunogenicity (ATI < 20 ng/mL). Primary non-response (NR) was subcategorized into: (1) Pharmacokinetic failure: failure to achieve clinical response (CDAI ≥ 150 with <70-point reduction) combined with inadequate drug exposure (TLI < 2.5 μg/mL or ATI ≥ 20 ng/mL); (2) Pharmacodynamic failure: failure to achieve clinical response despite adequate drug exposure (TLI ≥ 2.5 μg/mL and ATI < 20 ng/mL); and (3) Intervention-required failure: need for surgery, dose escalation, or additional medications during induction. ### Sample collection and preparation

Fresh stool samples (approximately 10 g) were collected from CD patients and healthy controls using sterile collection containers and plastic spoons. Samples were immediately divided into aliquots and stored in sterile cryovials, then frozen at −80 °C within 2 h of collection to preserve microbial integrity and metabolite stability for subsequent high-throughput 16S rRNA sequencing and untargeted metabolomics analysis. All samples were maintained at −80 °C until processing to ensure optimal preservation of both microbial DNA and metabolite profiles.

### High-throughput sequencing

Total genomic DNA was extracted from fecal samples using the cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) method and assessed via agarose gel electrophoresis. The V3-V4 region of 16S rRNA gene was PCR amplified using universal primers and subjected to paired-end sequencing on Illumina MiSeq/HiSeq 2500 platform. Raw sequences underwent quality control and were clustered into operational taxonomic units (OTUs) at 97 % similarity threshold using the Ribosomal Database Project (RDP) classifier. Microbial diversity was analyzed using alpha diversity indices (observed richness, Chao, Shannon, and Simpson) and beta diversity methods (principal coordinate analysis \[PCoA\] based on Bray-Curtis dissimilarity) using Quantitative Insights into Microbial Ecology (QIIME2) software. Statistical differences between groups were analyzed using Kruskal-Wallis H test for alpha diversity and Anoism for beta diversity. Differential abundance analysis was performed using Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) Effect Size (LEfSe) with corresponding LDA score threshold. Functional prediction was conducted using Phylogenetic Investigation of Communities by Reconstruction of Unobserved States 2 (PICRUSt2) with Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway analysis. Microbial co-occurrence networks were constructed using Spearman's rank correlation analysis with significance threshold P < 0.05 and correlation coefficient |r| > 0.50, and visualized using Gephi software. Network topology parameters including modularity, clustering coefficient, and average path length were calculated to characterize microbial community interactions.

### Fecal metabolomics

Fecal samples were homogenized in methanol/water (4:1, v/v) extraction buffer, sonicated, and centrifuged to obtain supernatants. Metabolomic analysis was performed using ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography (UHPLC) coupled with mass spectrometry on Agilent 6550 iFunnel Q-TOF and Triple TOF 6600 systems. Detection was performed using electrospray ionization (ESI) in both positive and negative ion modes. Raw data were processed using XCMS software for peak detection and alignment. Multivariate analyses including principal component analysis (PCA), partial least squares discriminant analysis (PLS-DA), and orthogonal PLS-DA (OPLS-DA) were performed to assess group differences. Differential metabolites were identified using variable importance in projection (VIP) scores >1.0 and P < 0.05. Metabolite identification was achieved by matching accurate masses and fragmentation patterns against HMDB and METLIN databases. Pathway annotation and enrichment analysis were conducted using the KEGG database.

### Integration of bacteriomics and metabolomics data

Integration of 16S rRNA sequencing and metabolomics data was performed through correlation analysis and mechanistic modeling. Spearman's rank correlation coefficients between bacterial genera and differential metabolites were calculated using the Hmisc package in R, with significance thresholds of P < 0.05. Results were visualized as heatmaps to identify significant microbe-metabolite associations. For mechanistic insights, data were integrated using Model-based Integration of Metabolite Observations and Species Abundances 2 (MIMOSA2). This computational framework predicts metabolic turnover by converting predicted enzymatic gene abundances into community-based metabolite potential (CMP) scores and evaluates species-specific contributions to metabolite production or consumption based on KEGG database enzyme activities. MIMOSA2 extends beyond simple correlation analysis by identifying potential biochemical mechanisms underlying species-metabolite interactions and quantifying their contributions to metabolite variance \[\].

### ML model building

The machine learning approach focused on developing predictive models for Crohn's disease treatment outcomes through a structured process. The dataset was randomly divided into training (50 %) and test (50 %) sets. To address class imbalance, the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) was applied exclusively within the training set during 5-fold cross-validation. Specifically, SMOTE was implemented within each fold to prevent data leakage, with 200 % oversampling of the minority class and 150 % undersampling of the majority class. The test set remained untouched and in its original imbalanced state to ensure an unbiased performance evaluation. Model development employed 5-fold cross-validation on the training set, evaluating 12 machine learning algorithms: Random Forest, Extreme Gradient Boosting Tree (XGBTree), Logistic Regression, K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), Partial Least Squares (PLS), Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM), Neural Network (NNet), Naive Bayes (NB), Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator Regression (Lasso), Categorical Boosting (CatBoost), and Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM). For CatBoost and LightGBM, SMOTE was applied to the entire training set before model fitting, as these algorithms have their own internal cross-validation mechanisms. Model performance was comprehensively evaluated using the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC) with 95 % confidence intervals, sensitivity, specificity, accuracy, positive predictive value (PPV), negative predictive value (NPV), and F1-score. Performance metrics were calculated on both the cross-validated training set predictions and the original imbalanced test set to assess real-world generalization. The optimal model was selected based on the highest AUC in the test set. Model interpretability was enhanced through Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP) analysis on both the training and test sets, generating feature importance rankings, bee swarm plots for feature effect distributions, and force plots for individual prediction explanations.

### Animal models and experimental design

Male BALB/c mice (3–4 weeks old) from Vital River Laboratory Animal Technology were maintained in specific pathogen-free conditions with standard light–dark cycles and autoclaved food and water. Following a 7-day acclimatization period, the experimental protocol was implemented.

The study investigated microbiota's role in infliximab therapy response using fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) in 2,4,6-trinitrobenzenesulfonic acid (TNBS)-induced colitis mice. Fecal samples were collected from selected NRCD and RCD patients, processed under sterile conditions, and stored at −80 °C. For FMT procedures, fecal samples were reconstituted in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), filtered, and standardized to 3 × 10 <sup>8</sup> cells mL <sup>-1</sup>, with 200 μL administered via oral gavage. Prior to FMT, mice underwent a two-week antibiotic treatment protocol consisting of vancomycin (500 mg L <sup>-1</sup>), neomycin sulfate (200 mg kg <sup>−1</sup>), metronidazole (200 mg kg <sup>−1</sup>), and ampicillin (200 mg kg <sup>−1</sup>). Following antibiotic treatment, FMT was conducted over two weeks, with control groups receiving PBS. During the second FMT week, TNBS-treated mice underwent skin sensitization with 1 % TNBS. Colitis was then induced by intracolonic administration of 2.5 % TNBS under ketamine/xylazine anesthesia, while control mice received 50 % ethanol. Infliximab treatment (5 mg kg <sup>−1</sup>, twice weekly) was initiated at one and three days post-TNBS administration according to group assignments.

### Transcriptome sequencing of colonic tissue (RNA-seq)

Total RNA was extracted from mouse colonic tissue using TRIzol reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) according to the manufacturer's protocol. RNA concentration, purity, and integrity were assessed using a NanoDrop ND-1000 spectrophotometer and Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100. RNA sequencing libraries were constructed and sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq™ 6000 platform by LC Bio-Technology Co., Ltd. (Hangzhou, China) to generate 150 bp paired-end reads. Differential gene expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 with significance thresholds of |log2(fold change)| > 1 and adjusted P < 0.05. Immune cell type deconvolution was conducted using ImmuCellAI to quantify 36 immune cell populations. Functional enrichment analysis was performed using KEGG pathway database. Bioinformatic analysis and data visualization were carried out using OmicStudio tools and R software.

### Statistical analysis

Descriptive statistics, including the mean (standard deviation, SD) or median (interquartile range, IQR) for continuous variables, and frequencies (percentages) for categorical variables, were calculated to assess baseline characteristics. Normality of continuous variables was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test. Group differences were tested using the chi-square test or Fisher's exact test for categorical variables and the Student's *t* -test or Mann-Whitney *U* test for continuous variables, depending on data distribution. All statistical analyses were performed using R software (version 4.3.0), with a two-sided P-value < 0.05 considered statistically significant.

## Results

### Characterization of the microbial ecosystem in CD and effects of infliximab treatment

The study included 100 CD patients and 49 healthy controls from Nanfang Hospital, analyzing changes in gut microbiome and metabolome in relation to infliximab treatment response (). High-throughput 16S rRNA gene sequencing generated 42,992 quality sequences from 249 samples, clustered into 2,190 OTUs at 97 % similarity. Alpha diversity indices (Ace and Chao) revealed significantly lower microbial richness and diversity in CD patients pre-treatment (CD\_Pre) compared to healthy controls (A and B). The Shannon index was notably reduced in the CD\_Pre group compared to the HC group (C). PCoA demonstrated distinct microbial community structures among HC, CD\_Pre, and post-treatment (CD\_Post) groups (D). Taxonomic analysis showed decreased *Firmicutes* and increased *Proteobacteria* at the phylum level in CD\_Pre patients. At the family level, CD\_Pre patients exhibited reduced *Lachnospiraceae* and *Ruminococcaceae*, with increased *Enterobacteriaceae* and *Streptococcaceae* (E). LEfSe analysis identified 29 differentially abundant genera between HC and CD\_Pre groups. Specifically, CD\_Pre patients showed significant enrichment of pro-inflammatory genera including *Enterococcus*, *Pyramidobacter*, *Prevotella\_9*, *Ruminococcus gnavus group*, *Haemophilus*, and *Prevotella\_7*, while beneficial genera such as *Faecalibacterium*, *Roseburia*, *unclassified Lachnospiraceae*, *Megamonas*, *Blautia*, *Dialister*, and *Phascolarctobacterium* were significantly depleted compared to healthy controls (F). Following infliximab treatment, LEfSe analysis revealed 22 genera with significant abundance differences between CD\_Pre and CD\_Post states. Post-treatment samples (CD\_Post) demonstrated enrichment of *Subdoligranulum*, *Lachnospira*, and several unclassified taxa, suggesting partial restoration of beneficial microbial communities. Conversely, CD\_Pre samples showed higher relative abundances of *Faecalibacterium*, *Bacteroides*, *Ruminococcus gnavus group*, *Fusobacterium*, *Lactobacillus*, and other taxa, indicating dynamic microbial community restructuring during anti-TNF therapy (G). Microbial co-occurrence network analysis revealed substantial differences in community structure and connectivity patterns across study groups. Healthy controls exhibited the most complex microbial network with the highest average degree, suggesting robust inter-microbial interactions. CD\_Pre patients showed reduced network complexity with lower average degree, but higher modularity and clustering coefficient, indicating more fragmented but tightly clustered microbial communities. Post-infliximab treatment, the microbial network demonstrated partial restoration with increased nodes and edges, higher average degree, but reduced modularity and clustering coefficient, suggesting improved microbial connectivity and community integration (H).

BugBase phenotype prediction revealed significant ecological disruptions in CD patients, with CD\_Pre samples showing markedly reduced proportions of anaerobic bacteria and Gram-positive bacteria compared to healthy controls, while displaying increased abundance of bacteria containing mobile genetic elements. No significant differences were observed for other phenotypic characteristics including biofilm formation, potential pathogenicity, Gram-negative bacteria, facultative anaerobes, stress tolerance, and aerobic bacteria (I and J). Functional pathway analysis using PICRUSt2 at KEGG Level 3 revealed significant alterations in predicted microbial metabolic capacity between CD patients and healthy controls. CD\_Pre patients exhibited significantly increased phosphotransferase system (PTS) activity and *Staphylococcus aureus* infection-related pathways, while demonstrating decreased predicted capacity for histidine metabolism and glutamatergic synapse functions (K). Following infliximab treatment, PICRUSt2 analysis revealed extensive functional restoration in the gut microbiome. The most significantly upregulated pathways in CD\_Post compared to CD\_Pre included glutamatergic synapse, proteoglycans in cancer, and Toll and Imd signaling pathway. Additionally, multiple biosynthetic pathways showed enhanced predicted activity, including indole alkaloid biosynthesis, isoflavonoid biosynthesis, tight junction, and gap junction pathways. Conversely, lipoarabinomannan (LAM) biosynthesis showed reduced activity post-treatment (L).

### Baseline gut microbiome correlated with therapeutic response to infliximab therapy in patients with CD

Analysis of baseline gut microbiome characteristics revealed distinct patterns associated with the therapeutic response to infliximab in CD patients. While alpha diversity metrics were comparable across non-responders (CD\_NR0), responders (CD\_R0), and HC (A and B), beta diversity analyses indicated clear differences in community structure between responders and non-responders (C and D). Taxonomic analysis revealed that CD\_NR0 patients exhibited a reduction in *Actinobacteriota* and an increase in *Proteobacteria*, *Fusobacteriota*, and *Synergistota* at the phylum level. At the family level, CD\_NR0 patients showed a depletion of beneficial bacteria (*Bifidobacteriaceae*, *Lachnospiraceae*, *Ruminococcaceae*) and an enrichment of potentially pathogenic families (*Enterobacteriaceae*, *Streptococcaceae*, and others). In contrast, CD\_NR14 patients displayed a decreased abundance of *Firmicutes* and *Actinobacteria*, alongside an increase in *Proteobacteria* and *Fusobacteriota* compared to CD\_R14 and HC groups. Family-level analysis revealed reduced *Bifidobacteriaceae* and *Ruminococcaceae* and increased *Enterobacteriaceae*, *Streptococcaceae*, and *Fusobacteriaceae* in CD\_NR14 (E). Co-occurrence network analysis revealed distinct microbial interaction patterns between treatment response groups that evolved dynamically throughout therapy. At baseline, CD\_R0 patients demonstrated more robust microbial networks compared to CD\_NR0 patients, characterized by higher network complexity, greater connectivity, and superior average degree. Notably, baseline responders exhibited predominantly positive correlations with minimal negative interactions, suggesting more harmonious microbial relationships. In contrast, non-responders showed higher proportions of negative correlations, indicating more antagonistic microbial interactions. Following 14 weeks of infliximab treatment, dramatic network restructuring occurred, particularly in non-responders. CD\_NR14 networks exhibited substantial expansion in complexity with markedly increased connectivity and network density, suggesting compensatory microbial proliferation in response to persistent inflammation. However, this expansion was accompanied by reduced modularity and shorter path lengths, indicating less organized community structure. Conversely, CD\_R14 patients maintained more stable and organized networks with moderate connectivity, higher modularity, and preserved positive interactions, reflecting sustained microbial homeostasis (F). LEfSe analysis identified significant genus-level differences between treatment response groups at baseline. CD\_NR0 patients showed enrichment of potentially pathogenic genera including *Escherichia\_Shigella*, *SH-PL14*, *Virgibacillus*, *Providencia*, and *Abiotropha*. In contrast, CD\_R0 patients demonstrated significantly higher abundances of beneficial genera, particularly *Bifidobacterium*, *Blautia*, *Treponema*, and multiple *Lachnospiraceae* family members including *Lachnospiraceae\_AC2044\_group*, *Lachnospiraceae\_UCG-001*, and *Rikenellaceae\_RC9\_gut\_group*. Additional genera enriched in responders included *Acidothermus*, *Christensenella*, *Gordonibacter*, *Oscillibacter*, and various unclassified taxa from beneficial bacterial families (G). Post-treatment LEfSe analysis revealed persistent genus-level differences between response groups after 14 weeks of infliximab therapy. CD\_NR14 patients continued to show enrichment of potentially harmful genera including *Alistipes*, *Megamonas*, *Desulfovibrio*, *Dysgonomonas*, and several unclassified taxa from less beneficial bacterial families. Conversely, CD\_R14 patients maintained higher abundances of beneficial genera, most notably *Bifidobacterium* and *Faecalibacterium*, along with *Veillonella*, *Limosilactobacillus*, *Ligilactobacillus*, *Erysipelotrichaceae\_UCG-003*, *Clostridium\_innocuum\_group*, and *Prevotella* (A).

BugBase phenotypic analysis revealed striking differences in bacterial community characteristics between treatment response groups at baseline. Detailed examination of specific bacterial phenotypes showed that CD\_NR0 patients had significantly reduced proportions of anaerobic bacteria, characterized by markedly lower *Bifidobacterium* abundance compared to both CD\_R0 patients and healthy controls. Similarly, Gram-positive bacterial communities were substantially depleted in non-responders, again primarily due to reduced *Bifidobacterium* representation, while responders and healthy controls maintained robust Gram-positive communities dominated by *Bifidobacterium*, *Roseburia*, and other beneficial genera. Conversely, Gram-negative bacterial communities were significantly expanded in CD\_NR0 patients, primarily driven by increased *Escherichia-Shigella* abundance, while responders and controls showed minimal Gram-negative representation (H). Comprehensive phenotypic profiling using Wilcoxon rank-sum tests confirmed significant ecological disruptions in non-responders compared to responders at baseline. CD\_NR0 patients demonstrated significantly reduced anaerobic bacteria and Gram-positive bacteria, while exhibiting significantly increased Gram-negative bacteria, facultatively anaerobic bacteria, and stress-tolerant bacteria. Additionally, non-responders showed elevated proportions of bacteria containing mobile genetic elements, suggesting increased antimicrobial resistance potential. No significant differences were observed for potentially pathogenic bacteria and aerobic bacteria between the two groups (I). Phenotypic analysis using BugBase also showed a reduction in anaerobic and Gram-positive bacteria, coupled with an increase in Gram-negative bacteria in CD\_NR14 (B and C). Functional pathway analysis using PICRUSt2 at KEGG Level 3 demonstrated significant metabolic differences between treatment response groups at baseline. CD\_NR0 patients exhibited significantly elevated activity in multiple metabolic pathways compared to CD\_R0 patients, including phosphotransferase system (PTS), fructose and mannose metabolism, pyruvate metabolism, amino sugar and nucleotide sugar metabolism, and lipopolysaccharide biosynthesis. Additional upregulated pathways in non-responders included glycerophospholipid metabolism, fatty acid metabolism, sulfur relay system, biotin metabolism, and base excision repair. Conversely, CD\_R0 patients showed higher predicted activity in proteasome, autophagy-yeast, and lipoarabinomannan (LAM) biosynthesis pathways (J). Remarkably, these functional differences persisted after 14 weeks of infliximab treatment. Post-treatment analysis revealed that CD\_NR14 patients continued to exhibit significantly elevated metabolic activity compared to CD\_R14 patients in similar pathways, including fructose and mannose metabolism, pyruvate metabolism, amino sugar and nucleotide sugar metabolism, lipopolysaccharide biosynthesis, and glycolysis/gluconeogenesis. Additional pathways showing persistent upregulation in non-responders included fatty acid metabolism, lysine degradation, carbon fixation in photosynthetic organisms, biotin metabolism, and fatty acid biosynthesis. Meanwhile, CD\_R14 patients maintained higher activity in proteasome and autophagy-yeast pathways (D). These compositional and functional alterations in the gut microbiome may influence the treatment response outcomes and suggest that intrinsic microbial metabolic characteristics persist despite anti-TNF therapy.

### Fecal metabolome variations in patients with CD and HC

Multivariate analyses using PLS-DA and OPLS-DA revealed significant metabolic differences between control and CD groups (A and B), with permutation tests confirming the robustness of these findings (C and D). Volcano plot analysis demonstrated predominantly down-regulated metabolites in CD patients (A). Classification of differential metabolites using the HMDB showed lipids and lipid-like molecules as the largest class (35.37 %), followed by organic acids and derivatives (18.31 %), and organoheterocyclic compounds (12.89 %, B). The most significantly altered metabolites included TG(16:0/16:0/18:0), Safynol, and Epsilon-(Hexanoyl)lysine (E). Next, we performed KEGG pathway enrichment analysis on the 1,116 differential metabolites. The results highlighted pathways associated with lipid metabolism, digestive system processes, cancer-related pathways, signal transduction, and cell growth and death (C). The significantly enriched pathways included Steroid hormone biosynthesis and Sphingolipid signaling pathway (P < 0.001, F). Additionally, KEGG topology analysis identified 10 relevant metabolic pathways, such as steroid hormone biosynthesis, primary bile acid biosynthesis, and sphingolipid metabolism, which exhibited significant correlations with CD (G). Integration of microbiome and metabolome data through Spearman's correlation analysis revealed strong associations between 29 bacterial genera and 380 differential KEGG-annotated metabolites (H). MIMOSA2 analysis identified key species-metabolite relationships, with *Bifidobacterium\_catenulatum* DSM\_16992 contributing to Chenodeoxycholic Acid production, and *Bacteroides\_dorei* DSM 17855 and *Streptococcus\_parasanguinis* ATCC 903 associated with Deoxyuridine and L-Cysteine production, respectively (D and E). Significant differences in these metabolites were observed between CD and HC groups (F), suggesting specific gut microbes may influence metabolic alterations in CD.

### Differentially abundant fecal metabolites and their associations with infliximab response

Multivariate analyses using PLS-DA and OPLS-DA showed significant separation between response groups (A and B), with permutation tests confirming the reliability of these patterns (C and D). Volcano plot analysis revealed predominantly down-regulated metabolites in the CD\_NR0 group (E). Classification of differential metabolites using HMDB showed lipids and lipid-like molecules as the largest class (46.15 %), followed by organic acids and derivatives (11.24 %), and organic oxygen compounds (10.65 %) (F). The most significantly altered metabolites included Acebutolol, Soyasaponin A1, and Metipranolol (G). Next, we conducted KEGG pathway enrichment analysis on the 179 named differential metabolites. The results indicated that the altered metabolites were primarily associated with lipid metabolism, digestive system, and Signal transduction (H). These 179 metabolites were enriched in 20 KEGG pathways (P < 0.05). Notably, GnRH signaling pathway: OS, Fc gamma R-mediated phagocytosis: OS, Phospholipase D signaling pathway: EIP emerged as the three most significantly enriched pathways (P < 0.001, I). To explore the metabolic pathways potentially responsible for the treatment non-response, a KEGG topology analysis was performed. Small P-values and large pathway impact values indicated highly influenced pathways. Based on these criteria, 4 meaningful pathways were identified, including Glycerophospholipid metabolism, Ubiquinone and other terpenoid-quinone biosynthesis, Isoquinoline alkaloid biosynthesis, and Primary bile acid biosynthesis, which were altered in the CD\_NR0 group (J). Integration of metabolomic and microbiome data through Spearman's correlation analysis revealed significant correlation pairs between 28 bacterial genera and 61 differential KEGG-annotated metabolites (A). MIMOSA2 analysis identified specific species-metabolite relationships, with *Bifidobacterium\_catenulatum* DSM\_16992 contributing to Taurochenodesoxycholic acid (TCDCA) production and *Coprococcus\_catus* GD\_7 influencing Withanolide B (Menatetrenone) generation (B–E). Both metabolites showed significant differences between response groups (F and G), suggesting specific microbe-metabolite interactions may influence treatment response through modulation of drug efficacy-related pathways. To evaluate whether metabolic differences persist after treatment, we compared fecal metabolomes between non-responders (CD\_NR14) and responders (CD\_R14) at week 14. Multivariate analyses using PLS-DA and OPLS-DA demonstrated sustained metabolic segregation between response groups even after 14 weeks of treatment (A and B). Differential metabolite analysis identified 325 significantly altered metabolites between CD\_NR14 and CD\_R14 groups. Volcano plot visualization revealed 250 down-regulated and 75 up-regulated metabolites in non-responders (C). The top 30 discriminatory metabolites, ranked by VIP scores, included key immunomodulatory and inflammatory mediators. Notably, anti-inflammatory metabolites such as pentanoic acid derivatives and caftaric acid derivatives remained significantly depleted in CD\_NR14 (D). KEGG pathway classification of differential metabolites revealed predominant involvement of metabolic and signaling pathways. At the functional level, lipid metabolism pathways contained the highest number of differential metabolites (E). Detailed pathway enrichment analysis identified the most significantly enriched pathways included secondary bile acid biosynthesis: M, choline metabolism in cancer: HD, and glycerophospholipid metabolism: M (F). KEGG topology analysis, integrating pathway enrichment significance with impact scores, highlighted five pathways with both high significance and substantial metabolic impact in sustained non-response: glycerophospholipid metabolism, sphingolipid metabolism, steroid hormone biosynthesis, citrate cycle and primary bile acid biosynthesis (G). These findings indicate that metabolic dysregulation persists in non-responders despite 14 weeks of infliximab therapy, suggesting that intrinsic metabolic alterations may contribute to sustained treatment resistance rather than being merely secondary to ongoing inflammation.

### Detrimental clinical effects following FMT in a mouse model

To elucidate the mechanistic role of gut microbiota in mediating infliximab treatment outcomes, we established a translational mouse model incorporating FMT from clinical responders and non-responders. Specifically, we collected fecal samples from carefully selected patients (3 responders and 3 non-responders) and transplanted them into TNBS-induced colitis mice pretreated with broad-spectrum antibiotics to eliminate native microbiota (A). Histopathological examination revealed striking differences among treatment groups. Mice receiving NR fecal material exhibited significantly enhanced mucosal epithelial damage and inflammatory infiltration compared to both the R-FMT group and the infliximab monotherapy control group (B). This exacerbated inflammatory phenotype was further validated through comprehensive clinical assessments. The NR-FMT group demonstrated markedly accelerated body weight loss, elevated disease activity index (DAI) scores, reduced colon length, and increased pathological scores compared to the R-FMT group, indicating that the dysregulated microbiota from non-responders actively promotes infliximab treatment resistance and therapeutic non-responsiveness (C–F). To characterize the immune landscape underlying these clinical differences, we performed RNA sequencing analysis of colonic tissues using ImmuCellAI, which enabled detailed profiling of 36 distinct immune cell populations. This analysis revealed a pronounced pro-inflammatory shift in the NR-FMT group, characterized by expanded populations of M1 macrophages and neutrophils, coupled with a significant reduction in regulatory T (Treg) cells (G). Transcriptomic pathway analysis further illuminated the molecular basis of this immune dysregulation, with cytokine-cytokine receptor interactions emerging as the most significantly enriched pathway. Additional enriched pathways encompassed cancer-related signaling, cell adhesion molecules, and critical immune modulators including chemokine signaling, complement cascades, and TNF signaling. Notably, stress response pathways including MAPK, PI3K-Akt, and NF-κB signaling were prominently activated in the NR group, suggesting widespread cellular stress and inflammatory activation (H). To explore potential metabolic mediators of these immune alterations, we conducted targeted bile acid metabolomics on fecal samples. This analysis identified 12 differentially abundant metabolites between R and NR groups, with TCDCA showing particularly reduced levels in the NR group, consistent with our previous clinical observations (I). Integration of metabolomic and immune cell data through co-occurrence network analysis revealed a significant association between TCDCA and Treg cell populations, highlighting bile acid metabolism as a potential mechanistic link between microbial metabolism and immune regulation (J). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that microbiota from non-responders can actively suppress infliximab therapeutic efficacy by promoting a pro-inflammatory immune environment and disrupting key metabolic pathways, providing crucial insights into the mechanisms of treatment resistance in inflammatory bowel disease.

### Development and validation of an integrated multi-omics model for predicting infliximab response

We developed a comprehensive machine learning framework to predict infliximab response by integrating baseline gut microbiome and metabolomic profiles. Following dataset stratification into training (50 %) and test (50 %) sets, random forest-based feature selection identified the most predictive biomarkers from both omics layers. Among microbiome features, ten bacterial taxa emerged as key predictors (A), with *Bifidobacterium* demonstrating the highest importance, followed by *Morganella* and *Enterococcus*. Additional significant predictors included *Clostridium\_sensu\_stricto\_13*, *Anaerostipes*, unclassified *Sphingomonadaceae*, *Lactobacillus*, *Phascolarctobacterium, Klebsiella*, and *Escherichia\_Shigella*. Similarly, metabolomic analysis identified ten discriminatory metabolites (B), dominated by the complex lipid DG(i-17:0/0:0/22:5(4Z,7Z,10Z,13Z,19Z)-O(16,17)), along with various other lipids, histamine, and bioactive compounds.

We systematically evaluated twelve machine learning algorithms using 5-fold cross-validation: Random Forest, XGBTree, Logistic Regression, KNN, PLS, GBM, NNet, NB, LDA, Lasso, CatBoost, and LightGBM. Model performance exhibited substantial variation across omics layers and algorithmic approaches. For microbiome-based prediction, training performance ranged considerably, with LightGBM achieving superior discrimination (AUC = 0.981), followed by CatBoost (0.946) and PLS (0.832). However, test set evaluation exposed critical differences in generalization capacity. LDA demonstrated exceptional robustness (test AUC = 0.805) with a smaller training-test performance gap, contrasting sharply with LightGBM (test AUC = 0.677) and CatBoost (0.720), which exhibited classic overfitting patterns (C and D). Confusion matrix analysis of the optimal model revealed moderate discriminatory ability with inherent prediction bias, correctly identifying 14/20 non-responders (sensitivity = 70.0 %) but only 10/30 responders (specificity = 33.3 %), indicating a tendency toward false-positive predictions (E, ). Metabolomics-based models demonstrated distinct performance characteristics (F and G). Despite excellent training metrics for CatBoost (0.956) and LightGBM (0.940), test set performance uniformly declined below AUC 0.65, suggesting limited generalizability of metabolomic signatures. The optimal metabolomics model achieved balanced but modest classification, correctly identifying 10/20 non-responders (sensitivity = 50.0 %) and 20/30 responders (specificity = 66.7 %) in the test set (H, ). Multi-omics integration yielded paradoxical results (I and J). Despite superior training performance with CatBoost (AUC = 0.971) and other ensemble methods, integrated models failed to exceed single-omics performance in independent validation. NB emerged as the best-performing integrated model (test AUC = 0.779), yet remained inferior to the microbiome-only LDA model (0.805). The integrated model's confusion matrix revealed extreme prediction bias, correctly classifying 15/20 non-responders (sensitivity = 75.0 %) but 20/30 responders (specificity = 66.7 %), with a pronounced tendency toward false-positive predictions (10 responders misclassified as non-responders), suggesting model instability when combining heterogeneous data types (K, ).

To elucidate the mechanistic basis of predictions, we applied SHAP analysis to the optimal microbiome-based model. Global feature importance rankings demonstrated remarkable consistency between training and test sets (A-B), with *Bifidobacterium* emerging as the dominant predictor (mean |SHAP| = 0.237 training, 0.240 test) compared to *Phascolarctobacterium* (0.105 training, 0.089 test) and *Escherichia\_Shigella* (0.060 training, 0.075 test). SHAP summary plots revealed critical feature-response relationships (C-D). *Bifidobacterium* exhibited strong protective effects: elevated abundance generated negative SHAP values reducing treatment failure probability, while depletion produced positive SHAP values increasing non-response risk. *Phascolarctobacterium* demonstrated bidirectional effects with higher abundance conferring protection and lower abundance contributing to treatment failure. *Escherichia\_Shigella* showed predominantly detrimental contributions across high abundance levels. Individual-level force plots illuminated patient-specific mechanisms across four representative samples (E-H). Sample 1 (E) demonstrated strong responder prediction (f(x) = 0.126) where *Phascolarctobacterium* depletion (abundance = -0.455) contributed to treatment failure risk (+0.0562), but was overwhelmed by dominant protective effects from *Anaerostipes* (SHAP = -0.273, abundance = 1.95) and *Escherichia\_Shigella* (SHAP = -0.0699, abundance = -0.447), collectively driving the prediction well below the population baseline. Sample 2 (F) demonstrated high-risk prediction (f(x) = 0.998) driven by dominant *Enterococcus* contributions (SHAP=+0.392, abundance = 6.16). Sample 3 (G) illustrated moderate risk prediction (f(x) = 0.533) where risk-contributing effects from depleted beneficial bacteria including *Anaerostipes* (+0.0341), *Bifidobacterium* (+0.0707), and *Phascolarctobacterium* (+0.0875) were partially offset by protective contributions from *Escherichia\_Shigella* (−0.0729), *Enterococcus* (−0.0243) and other features, resulting in a prediction slightly above the population baseline. Sample 4 (H) demonstrated strong responder prediction (f(x) = 0.0765) driven predominantly by high-abundance *Bifidobacterium* (SHAP = -0.354, abundance = 0.844), whose dominant protective effect overwhelmed minor risk contributions from *Phascolarctobacterium* depletion(+0.0492), resulting in a prediction well below the population baseline and indicating high treatment success probability. These analyses demonstrate that infliximab response prediction emerges from complex microbial community interactions rather than individual taxa abundance. While *Bifidobacterium* serves as the primary protective determinant, its influence is modulated by the broader microbial ecosystem, underscoring the necessity of systems-level approaches for precision therapeutic prediction in inflammatory bowel disease.

## Discussion

Achieving clinical remission remains the paramount objective in CD management. While anti-TNF agents such as infliximab have transformed therapeutic outcomes for moderate to severe CD, the substantial rates of primary non-response (up to 30 %) and secondary loss of response (approximately 40 % within the first year) highlight the critical need for predictive biomarkers \[,, \]. The gut microbiome, increasingly recognized as a pivotal determinant in CD pathogenesis, exhibits characteristic dysbiotic patterns marked by diminished biodiversity and profound alterations in bacterial community structure. Our integrated multi-omics approach elucidates a mechanistic cascade wherein baseline microbial signatures orchestrate metabolic perturbations, particularly within bile acid metabolism, that subsequently govern immune responses and ultimately dictate infliximab treatment efficacy. Notably, this microbiome-metabolome-immune axis not only serves as a predictor of treatment response but also demonstrates remarkable persistence in non-responders following therapy, indicating intrinsic biological divergences rather than merely transient inflammatory manifestations.

Our comprehensive microbiome profiling unveiled distinctive microbial signatures intimately linked to both CD pathogenesis and therapeutic responsiveness. We documented a pronounced expansion of pro-inflammatory taxa, including *Enterococcus, Pyramidobacter,* and *Prevotella\_9*, in CD patients relative to healthy controls. These pathogenic organisms perpetuate chronic inflammation through diverse pathways, most notably via NLRP3 inflammasome activation and dysregulated Th17-mediated immune cascades \[,\]. In stark contrast, beneficial microbial communities such as *Faecalibacterium, Roseburia,* and *Blautia*, recognized as key producers of anti-inflammatory metabolites including butyrate, exhibited significant depletion. The concurrent reduction in Shannon diversity indices and altered PCoA clustering patterns in CD patients reflect not merely taxonomic redistribution but profound functional ecosystem perturbations that compromise metabolic homeostasis.

Critically, baseline microbiome characteristics demonstrated remarkable predictive capacity for treatment outcomes through intricate mechanistic networks. Non-responders consistently displayed marked depletion of beneficial families *Bifidobacteriaceae* and *Ruminococcaceae*, coupled with significant enrichment of potentially pathogenic lineages including *Enterobacteriaceae, Streptococcaceae*, and *Fusobacteriaceae*. These taxonomic redistributions translate into profound functional ramifications: the depleted families represent primary sources of SCFA production and bile acid-modifying enzymatic activity, whereas the enriched families encompass pro-inflammatory species that compromise intestinal barrier integrity. Importantly, while alpha diversity metrics showed no significant differences between infliximab responders and non-responders at baseline, this finding provides crucial mechanistic insights that extend beyond simple taxonomic richness. The preservation of overall microbial diversity in both groups suggests that treatment response is not determined by gross ecosystem collapse, but rather by specific functional shifts within taxonomically similar communities. This observation aligns with emerging concepts in microbiome research demonstrating that functional capacity, rather than species richness alone, drives host-microbe interactions and therapeutic outcomes. The lack of diversity differences between response groups paradoxically highlights the sophisticated nature of microbiome-mediated treatment resistance. Non-responders maintained comparable species richness to responders but harbored fundamentally different functional profiles, as evidenced by our PICRUSt2 analysis revealing significant alterations in metabolic pathways including phosphotransferase systems, lipopolysaccharide biosynthesis, and glycerophospholipid metabolism. This preservation of diversity in functionally distinct communities may reflect a pathological 'pseudo-stability' wherein dysbiotic ecosystems maintain taxonomic richness through compensatory expansion of inflammatory-associated species that replace depleted beneficial taxa. This phenomenon, termed 'dysbiotic resilience', explains how microbial communities can appear quantitatively normal while being functionally compromised, highlighting that community function rather than composition drives treatment outcomes.

The notably more sophisticated microbial co-occurrence networks observed in treatment responders suggest that therapeutic success necessitates not merely the presence of specific beneficial taxa, but rather the establishment of complex ecological interactions that sustain metabolic equilibrium and immune homeostasis. Our findings specifically demonstrated that treatment responders harbored significantly elevated baseline abundances of the *Bifidobacteriales* order. *Bifidobacterium*, a widely distributed commensal genus, possesses well-characterized beneficial properties including pro-homeostatic and anti-inflammatory immunomodulatory functions. These observations are corroborated by Lewis et␣al., who identified infliximab responsiveness as being associated with decreased *Actinomyces* abundance \[\], and Kowalska-Duplaga et␣al., who documented post-infliximab reductions in *Actinomycetales* \[\].

The microbial dysbiosis observed in non-responders directly translates into profound metabolic perturbations that mechanistically drive treatment resistance. Metabolomic investigations have established distinct biochemical signatures that effectively discriminate IBD patients from healthy individuals, with microbially-derived metabolites, including SCFA, medium-chain fatty acids, tryptophan derivatives, bile acids, and sphingolipids, emerging as promising diagnostic and prognostic biomarkers \[\]. However, comparative analyses between CD and UC have been constrained by limited sample sizes and cohort heterogeneity. Our comprehensive metabolomics profiling, utilizing advanced PLS-DA and OPLS-DA methodologies, unveiled profound alterations in gut microbiota-derived metabolites characteristic of CD pathophysiology. Pivotal observations included significantly elevated concentrations of complex lipids, notably TG(16:0/16:0/18:0), lysophosphatidic acid (LysoPA), phosphatidylinositol (PI), and lysophosphatidylcholine (LysoPC), indicative of perturbed lipid homeostasis in CD. These lipid perturbations exhibit direct mechanistic connections to the observed microbial dysbiosis: the depletion of bacteria possessing lipid metabolic capabilities culminates in the pathological accumulation of unprocessed lipids that perpetuate inflammatory cascades. Subsequent KEGG pathway enrichment analysis illuminated the upregulation of critical metabolic networks, including primary bile acid biosynthesis, sphingolipid metabolism, and linoleic acid processing, pathways intimately associated with immune activation and sustained inflammation. Importantly, these metabolic reprogramming events demonstrated remarkable concordance with functional predictions derived from PICRUSt2 analysis, substantiating the intricate interdependence between gut microbiome architecture and host metabolic activity. Through sophisticated MIMOSA2 network analysis, we delineated specific bacteria-metabolite interactions with unprecedented mechanistic resolution. Most critically, *Bifidobacterium\_catenulatum DSM\_16992* demonstrated robust capacity for chenodeoxycholic acid biosynthesis, establishing a direct causal link between this protective microorganism and anti-inflammatory bile acid metabolism. Additionally, *Bacteroides\_dorei DSM\_17855* facilitated deoxyuridine synthesis, while *Streptococcus\_parasanguinis ATCC 903* actively consumed L-cysteine, highlighting the diverse metabolic contributions of individual species.

Metabolomic analysis revealed profound differences in gut microbiota-derived metabolites between infliximab responders and non-responders at baseline, as demonstrated through comprehensive PLS-DA and OPLS-DA analyses. Critical metabolic pathways underlying treatment sensitivity encompassed glycerophospholipid metabolism, ubiquinone biosynthesis, isoquinoline alkaloid biosynthesis, and primary bile acid biosynthesis. Most notably, non-responders exhibited marked depletion of TCDCA, a bile acid with established anti-inflammatory properties \[,\]. The glycerophospholipid metabolism pathway emerged as particularly significant, involving key metabolites including phosphatidylserine (PS), diacylglycerol (DG), and lysophosphatidylethanolamine (LysoPE). Beyond its recognized role as a membrane nutrient supplement for cognitive enhancement, PS demonstrates remarkable therapeutic potential by enhancing survival rates, modulating gut microbiome composition, preserving intestinal barrier integrity, and mitigating neuroinflammation in sepsis survivors \[\]. Similarly, LysoPE, predominantly derived from Gram-negative bacterial membranes, exhibits dual functionality through its antimicrobial properties and modulation of bacterial potassium transport systems \[\]. Through sophisticated MIMOSA2 network analysis, we identified *Bifidobacterium\_catenulatum DSM\_16992* as a pivotal contributor to TCDCA biosynthesis, while *Coprococcus catus GD\_7* was mechanistically linked to Withanolide B production. These findings align with previous evidence demonstrating that *Bifidobacterium\_longum* enhances infliximab therapeutic efficacy through bile acid metabolic modulation \[\], with TCDCA mediating anti-inflammatory responses via TGR5 receptor activation \[\]. The observed baseline depletion of *Bifidobacterium* species in non-responders parallels findings in vedolizumab response prediction \[\], suggesting conserved microbial determinants transcend specific biologic therapeutic classes.

Bile acids, primarily synthesized in hepatic tissue and subsequently modified by intestinal microbiota, orchestrate fundamental physiological processes encompassing lipid digestion, glucose homeostasis, and immunological equilibrium. Following hepatic synthesis, approximately 95 % of bile acids undergo efficient ileal reabsorption, while the residual fraction undergoes microbial biotransformation into secondary bile acids through the enzymatic activity of bacterial bile salt hydrolases (BSH) and hydroxysteroid dehydrogenases (HSDHs). These microbially-modified secondary bile acids function as potent immunomodulatory molecules, exerting their effects through specialized receptors including the G-protein-coupled bile acid receptor 1 (TGR5) and the nuclear farnesoid X receptor (FXR), thereby regulating intestinal barrier function, immune cell differentiation trajectories, and broader metabolic processes. Exemplifying this paradigm, chenodeoxycholic acid, a primary bile acid, mediates robust anti-inflammatory responses through FXR activation and concurrent suppression of pathogenic bacterial growth \[\]. The widespread distribution of BSH enzymatic activity among beneficial genera, particularly *Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium,* and *Clostridium*, further emphasizes the pivotal role of microbial metabolism in sculpting bile acid profiles and maintaining host physiological homeostasis \[\].

Our FMT experiments provide definitive causal validation of the microbiome-metabolome-immune regulatory cascade. Mice receiving non-responder fecal material demonstrated exacerbated colonic inflammation, reduced colon length, and elevated disease activity indices compared to responder-FMT recipients. The immune landscape analysis, revealing expanded M1 macrophage and neutrophil populations coupled with diminished regulatory T cell frequencies in non-responder FMT mice, directly corroborates our metabolomic observations. Specifically, reduced TCDCA concentrations correlated significantly with decreased Treg populations through bile acid metabolic pathway modulation, demonstrating that dysbiotic microbiomes actively orchestrate treatment resistance through metabolic reprogramming of immune responses rather than functioning merely as passive biomarkers. This mechanistic framework provides continuity with the established FXR/IL-10 anti-inflammatory axis in IBD pathophysiology \[\].

Our investigation contributes substantially to the expanding body of evidence demonstrating that infliximab therapy profoundly modulates gut microbiome-metabolite networks. We documented significant increases in microbial diversity and taxonomic richness throughout the treatment course, accompanied by a pronounced expansion of *Faecalibacterium*, a genus renowned for its beneficial probiotic attributes, including robust butyrate biosynthesis and pathogen antagonism \[\]. The selective restoration of microbial diversity in treatment responders, but not in non-responders, provides compelling evidence that baseline microbiome architecture fundamentally determines the capacity for ecological rehabilitation. This observation aligns with accumulating evidence that biologic therapies can orchestrate beneficial microbial community shifts, thereby enhancing immune regulatory networks and fortifying intestinal barrier integrity. Notably, infliximab treatment achieved partial restoration of the compromised pre-treatment metabolic capabilities characteristic of CD patients, suggesting that anti-TNF therapy may directly ameliorate microbial functional dysfunction. Particularly noteworthy, the post-treatment recovery of *Faecalibacterium* abundance correlated strongly with metabolic normalization, positioning this genus as both a prognostic biomarker and a functional mediator of therapeutic response \[\]. Furthermore, infliximab therapy demonstrated partial restoration of compromised microbial metabolic capacity, evidenced by upregulated biosynthetic pathways for thiamine, fatty acids, and cobalamin \[,\], accompanied by the re-establishment of beneficial taxa including *Faecalibacterium*, *Lactobacillus*, and *Roseburia*.

These therapeutic-induced changes likely arise from both direct and indirect mechanisms of anti-TNF intervention. Through the suppression of mucosal inflammation, infliximab facilitates the restoration of epithelial architectural integrity and immune tolerance, consequently reshaping microbiota composition through improved niche conditions. Interestingly, mechanistic distinctions exist among anti-TNF agents: etanercept, a TNF receptor-Fc fusion protein, uniquely antagonizes both TNF-α and lymphotoxin-α, thereby influencing IgA induction and microbiota composition through pathways distinct from those of monoclonal antibodies such as infliximab \[,\]. The clinical relevance of these immunological interactions is exemplified by the observation that increased IgA coating of Oscillospira correlates with delayed surgical intervention, highlighting the potential utility of IgA-microbiome interactions as prognostic biomarkers for treatment outcomes \[\].

The persistence of metabolic perturbations in non-responders following 14 weeks of infliximab therapy yields critical mechanistic insights into treatment failure paradigms. Our identification of 325 differentially abundant metabolites between persistent non-responders and sustained responders, characterized by continued depletion of anti-inflammatory mediators (pentanoic acid derivatives, caftaric acid derivatives), demonstrates that metabolic dysfunction represents an intrinsic phenotypic characteristic rather than a secondary consequence of ongoing inflammation. The sustained enrichment of secondary bile acid biosynthesis and glycerophospholipid metabolism pathways in non-responders indicates that their microbiomes maintain pro-inflammatory metabolic programming despite intensive anti-TNF intervention. This persistence suggests that the microbiome-metabolome-immune axis exists in stable alternative states: a 'responsive' ecotype characterized by *Bifidobacterium* dominance, TCDCA sufficiency, and balanced Treg/M1 dynamics, versus a 'resistant' ecotype marked by *Escherichia-Shigella* dominance, TCDCA deficiency, and immune dysregulation. The stability of these alternative states explains why baseline microbial features predict long-term therapeutic outcomes and why non-responders fail to achieve ecological rehabilitation even after prolonged anti-TNF therapy. These findings indicate that treatment resistance represents an intrinsic biological state rather than a reversible inflammatory consequence.

Despite encouraging initial response rates, the long-term efficacy of infliximab remains suboptimal, necessitating the identification of robust predictive biomarkers to optimize therapeutic strategies and minimize associated healthcare costs and complications. Our comprehensive analysis reveals that treatment responders demonstrate characteristic expansion of SCFA-producing bacterial communities, notably *Bifidobacterium, Lachnospiraceae*, and *Blautia*, while non-responders exhibit pronounced enrichment of potentially pathogenic *Escherichia-Shigella* complex. These distinctive microbial signatures, demonstrating remarkable consistency across independent studies, underscore the paramount importance of *Firmicutes* phylum constituents, particularly butyrate-producing taxa, in modulating therapeutic responsiveness. Corroboratively, *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* has emerged as a pivotal predictive biomarker for anti-TNF response, with its abundance demonstrating strong correlations with sustained remission and reduced disease recurrence rates \[,\]. In healthy individuals, the gut microbiota is predominantly characterized by the balanced co-dominance of *Bacteroidetes* and *Firmicutes*, with *Proteobacteria* representing a minor constituent. In CD pathophysiology, dysbiosis manifests as pronounced elevation of *Proteobacteria* (exemplified by *Escherichia-Shigella* complex) coupled with significant depletion of *Firmicutes*, a pattern particularly pronounced among infliximab non-responders \[\]. Conversely, treatment responders demonstrate progressive *Proteobacteria* decline over time, suggesting that anti-TNF therapy may facilitate partial restoration of microbial ecological balance. The pathogenic potential of expanded *Proteobacteria* stems from their capacity to evade host immune surveillance and compromise intestinal barrier integrity \[\]. *Fusobacterium nucleatum* represents a paradigmatic example of microbial pathogenicity extending beyond CD, as this organism has been implicated in colorectal carcinogenesis and therapeutic resistance in autoimmune disorders. Its arsenal of virulence factors, including FadA and Fap2 adhesins, drives tumorigenesis, suppresses host immune responses, and promotes chemotherapeutic resistance through TLR-4/MyD88 and NF-κB signaling cascades \[\]. Through the recruitment of immunosuppressive cellular populations and the establishment of pro-inflammatory microenvironmental conditions, *F.usobacterium nucleatum* exemplifies the critical need for targeted interventions designed to disrupt pathogenic microbial mechanisms.

The prognostic utility of microbial profiling extends well beyond CD to encompass diverse pathological conditions. In colorectal cancer, *Escherichia coli, Bacteroides fragilis*, and *Fusobacterium nucleatum* have been validated as robust prognostic biomarkers \[,, \]. Additionally, gut microbiota composition significantly modulates chemotherapy toxicity profiles, with bacterial β-glucuronidases influencing irinotecan-associated adverse effects, while an intact microbiome enhances oxaliplatin therapeutic efficacy \[,\]. In melanoma and rheumatoid arthritis, microbiome signatures demonstrate strong correlations with immunotherapy response rates, although emerging evidence suggests that oral microbiota may exert more pronounced influences than gut microbiota in rheumatological conditions \[,,, \]. These convergent findings emphasize the broad translational potential of microbiome-based biomarker strategies across diverse disease contexts.

The inherent complexity of microbial ecosystems, governed by sophisticated quorum sensing mechanisms and dynamic environmental adaptability, fundamentally challenges traditional approaches relying on single taxonomic markers or simplified ratios such as *Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes* for diagnostic applications. Machine learning methodologies offer transformative analytical frameworks for deciphering complex microbial community architectures and identifying disease-associated microbial signatures. Exemplifying this paradigm, convolutional neural network-based algorithms have achieved exceptional diagnostic precision in ulcerative colitis severity assessment through endoscopic feature analysis \[\], while integrated histomic and fecal microbiome data have successfully predicted treatment responses in pediatric IBD cohorts \[,\]. These applications underscore machine learning's superior capacity for risk stratification and outcome prediction across high-dimensional, multi-variable datasets.

The implementation of machine learning for therapeutic outcome prediction through microbiome profiling represents a rapidly advancing frontier in precision medicine. Our investigation demonstrates several methodological innovations, particularly through the sophisticated integration of multi-omics analytical approaches. The superior performance of our microbiome-based LDA model compared to metabolomics-only or integrated multi-omics approaches reflects the fundamental biological principle that the microbiome functions as the upstream orchestrator of the metabolic cascade. This performance advantage, with our microbiome model achieving AUC = 0.805 versus metabolome-only models reaching merely 0.634, validates the hierarchical nature of microbiome-metabolome interactions. A pivotal innovation in our analytical framework involves the implementation of SHAP analysis, providing unprecedented mechanistic insights into treatment response determinants. This approach revealed critical directional effects of specific bacterial taxa: *Bifidobacterium* demonstrated robust protective effects through negative SHAP values that reduced non-response probability, while *Phascolarctobacterium* and *Escherichia-Shigella* complex exhibited deleterious effects promoting treatment failure. These observations complement recent investigations by Onwuka et␣al., where SHAP analysis illuminated complex microbiome-host interaction networks in IBD pathophysiology \[\]. Our individual-level force plot analyses demonstrate that identical bacterial taxa can exert contrasting effects depending on the broader microbial community context, emphasizing that therapeutic responses emerge from sophisticated community-level interactions rather than isolated taxonomic abundance patterns.

Collectively, our findings establish a hierarchical biological cascade that determines anti-TNF responsiveness through three interconnected levels. At the microbial level, baseline *Bifidobacterium* abundance serves as the primary determinant, functioning as the upstream orchestrator of the entire cascade. At the metabolic level, *Bifidobacterium* -mediated TCDCA production creates the critical biochemical link between microbial composition and immune function. At the immune level, TCDCA availability governs Treg/M1 macrophage homeostasis through TGR5 and FXR receptor signaling, ultimately determining tissue responsiveness to anti-TNF intervention. This mechanistic framework explains why microbiome-based models outperform metabolomics-only approaches (AUC 0.805 vs 0.634) and why baseline microbial features predict sustained treatment outcomes. The cascade's hierarchical nature suggests that therapeutic interventions targeting upstream microbial drivers may be more effective than downstream metabolic or immune interventions alone.

While our investigation establishes a robust predictive framework for infliximab response, several opportunities and methodological challenges warrant future investigation. The single-center study design necessitates validation through large-scale multicenter cohorts to demonstrate reproducibility across diverse patient populations and clinical environments. Emerging technological innovations, including spatial transcriptomics-microbiome integration platforms and sophisticated gut-on-chip modeling systems, offer transformative potential for elucidating location-specific microbe-host interactions and characterizing dynamic metabolic flux patterns. However, methodological limitations persist, notably 16S rRNA sequencing's genus-level taxonomic resolution constraints and untargeted metabolomics' inherent technical variability. Future investigations should prioritize metagenomic shotgun sequencing approaches coupled with targeted metabolomics platforms to achieve species-level precision and absolute quantification of critical metabolites such as TCDCA. These technological advances, integrated with deep learning architectures and sophisticated network analysis algorithms, will further refine predictive model accuracy and mechanistic understanding, ultimately catalyzing the clinical implementation of personalized IBD therapeutic strategies.

## Conclusion and future perspective

Our study establishes that the gut microbiome-metabolome axis critically determines anti-TNF responsiveness in CD through bile acid-mediated control of Treg/M1 macrophage dynamics, with non-responders exhibiting a distinct ecotype marked by *Bifidobacterium* depletion, *Escherichia-Shigella* dominance, and TCDCA deficiency. The mechanistic cascade from microbial dysbiosis through metabolic dysfunction to immune dysregulation provides both predictive biomarkers and therapeutic targets. The LDA model utilizing baseline microbiome features outperforms conventional clinical predictors, enabling early identification of treatment resistance. Crucially, fecal microbiota transplantation validated the causal role of this dysbiotic network in driving colitis progression via TCDCA-Treg suppression and M1 polarization. Building on these findings, future efforts should prioritize multicenter validation of the predictive biomarkers, development of rapid diagnostic assays targeting TCDCA and *Bifidobacterium* species, and therapeutic trials combining infliximab with microbiome-modulating strategies such as engineered probiotics or responder-derived FMT. Advanced spatial multi-omics and causal AI frameworks will further unravel the temporal hierarchy of microbial-metabolite-immune interactions, accelerating the shift from empirical treatment to mechanism-guided precision therapy in IBD.
