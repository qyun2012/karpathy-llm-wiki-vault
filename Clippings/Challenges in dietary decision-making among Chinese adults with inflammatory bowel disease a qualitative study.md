## Abstract

### Abstract

### Objectives

This study aimed to explore the challenges encountered during dietary decision-making among Chinese adults with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and provide a theoretical basis for developing targeted strategies to improve dietary decision-making ability.

### Design

A descriptive qualitative interview study.

### Setting

This study was conducted in four hospitals in Nanjing, China.

### Participants

From June 2023 to July 2024, semi-structured interviews were held with 24 patients diagnosed with IBD across four hospitals in Nanjing through purposive sampling.

### Results

Five themes and twelve sub-themes were identified. The five themes were as follows: (1) challenges in problem identification (insufficient perception of disease threat and excessive fear of flare-ups); (2) challenges in information integration (absence of individualised professional guidance and decision-making burden due to overwhelming information); (3) challenges in decision implementation (conflicts between dietary preferences and diet management, socialising and diet management, and limited choices and diet management); (4) challenges in post-decision reflection (disappointment and regret over decision outcomes and self-regulatory fatigue); and (5) other factors influencing dietary decisions (dietary decision dilemmas under inappropriate social support, avoidance of decisions under emotional influence, and Chinese cultural influences on dietary decision-making).

### Conclusion

The results of this study showed that patients with IBD encountered various challenges that impeded healthy dietary decisions. Our findings provide a foundation for the future development of dietary decision-making support strategies. This study may provide a framework to guide the development of dietary decision-making aids tailored to the dietary challenges encountered at various decision-making stages, thereby promoting patients’ healthy eating behaviours.

**Keywords:** QUALITATIVE RESEARCH, Chronic Disease, Inflammatory bowel disease, Self-Management, Self Care, Decision Making

## STRENGTHS AND LIMITATIONS OF THIS STUDY.

- Analysing the challenges faced by patients with IBD in making and implementing healthy dietary decisions from a patient-led decision-making perspective may provide valuable guidance for designing targeted educational interventions and support strategies in clinical practice, promoting healthy eating among this population.
- Qualitative descriptive research captures participants’ first-hand experiences and perspectives on their patient-led dietary decision-making process through in-depth interviews, providing rich and detailed insights.
- Most participants were recruited from a single province in China, which may limit the transferability of the findings to broader contexts or more diverse populations.

## Introduction

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), encompassing Crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC), is a chronic, progressive and lifelong disease with relapsing and remitting symptoms. Patients with IBD experience a wide range of physical symptoms, including but not limited to abdominal pain, diarrhoea, haematochezia, malnutrition and extraintestinal manifestations. These symptoms impose a substantial psychological burden and lead to a decline in health-related quality of life (HRQOL).[^1] In China, the number of patients with IBD has increased by approximately 0.91 million over the past 30 years, indicating that the disease burden may continue to increase.[^2] Diet and enteral nutrition can effectively modulate intestinal inflammatory activity and the symptom burden, and decrease the need for surgery.[^3] In contrast, unhealthy dietary decisions, such as high consumption of ultra-processed foods and sugar-sweetened beverages, are linked to worse clinical outcomes in patients with IBD, including more frequent disease flare-ups.[<sup>4 5</sup>](#R4) Thus, promoting healthy eating among patients with IBD is crucial for their disease management and recovery.

Due to the limited evidence available, synthesising guidance on healthy eating for patients with IBD remains challenging. After a comprehensive literature review, healthy eating for patients with IBD can be preliminarily defined as follows: in the absence of specific dietary interventions that are recommended by IBD healthcare professionals (HCPs), patients with IBD should follow the diet recommended for the general population; if special therapeutic needs arise, individualised dietary interventions should be implemented under the guidance of IBD HCPs, taking disease type and phenotype into account.[<sup>6 7</sup>](#R6) Given the chronic nature of IBD, patients are responsible for making dietary decisions most of the time. Such dietary decision-making encompasses not only action-level choices, including decisions about food types, intake and eating environments, but also reflection-level decisions made on the basis of individual beliefs, knowledge and goals, such as adhering to a healthy diet for 1 week.[^4] These decisions serve to inform future dietary behaviours. Therefore, focusing on how patients with IBD make self-imposed dietary decisions and the challenges they face in the context of limited professional support is essential for enhancing disease management and recovery.

However, little is known about the current status and challenges of dietary decision-making among patients with IBD. Crooks *et al* [^5] reported that following diagnosis, patients with IBD often decide to actively modify their diets in an effort to control symptoms and restore health. Schneider *et al* [^6] identified factors such as value preferences, medication dependence and social norms as major influences on patients’ decisions to yield to food temptations. Yin *et al* [^7] found that dietary decision-making among patients with IBD generally fell into three patterns: actively adjusting diet, self-restriction and making no changes, and that limited knowledge of dietary guidelines was a key factor influencing healthy dietary decisions. These findings suggest that, in daily dietary decision-making, patients with IBD appear to face multiple challenges that hinder their ability to make decisions conducive to health. Nevertheless, few studies [<sup>10 12</sup>](#R10) have examined these challenges from the perspective of patients’ active decision-making. Understanding the challenges that may influence dietary decisions is critical for enhancing self-management and promoting patient-centred care.[^8] Therefore, this study adopted a qualitative descriptive design to provide a comprehensive and in-depth understanding of the challenges faced by patients with IBD in their dietary decision-making.

## Methods

### Study design

A descriptive qualitative study design was conducted. It allowed researchers to engage closely with data and obtain a straightforward description of events or phenomena that were not yet well understood.[^9] Reporting was performed in accordance with the Consolidated Criteria for Reporting Qualitative Studies (COREQ) guidelines [^10] (see [online supplemental file 1](#SP1)).

### Participants and settings

Participants were recruited via purposive sampling from June 2023 to July 2024 in four tertiary hospitals in Nanjing, China. Participants were recruited from two sources: patients with IBD attending hospitals, and individuals who had previously participated in a cross-sectional survey to assess the prevalence of avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder conducted by the research team.[^11] For hospital patients, eligibility was assessed by clinical staff, who informed potential participants about the study either through WeChat (a widely used social application in China) or during routine visits and provided them with an invitation letter. Contact details of interested patients were shared with the interviewer, who arranged interviews during the same or a subsequent visit while emphasising the voluntary nature of participation and the right to withdraw at any time. For participants who had previously taken part in the cross-sectional survey, researchers contacted them via WeChat and explained the main content and objectives of the study. On obtaining consent, interviews were scheduled either by telephone or in person.

The study selection criteria required participants to have a confirmed diagnosis of either CD, UC or IBD unclassified (IBDU) for more than 6 months, be at least 18 years old and be willing to participate voluntarily. To avoid interference from special diet-related therapies or eating disorders in exploring self-imposed dietary decision-making in patients with IBD, corresponding exclusion criteria were established. Participants were excluded if they were receiving treatment with exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN) or parenteral nutrition (PEN) without eating solid food. Participants were also excluded if they were pregnant or breastfeeding (due to special dietary needs and ethical considerations), or if they had a diagnosis of an eating disorder or chronic disease that could affect the participant’s ability to eat and drink normally.

### Interview guideline

The interview guide for this study was developed through a comprehensive review of relevant literature, prior research and extensive discussions with the research team. Pilot interviews were conducted with two participants and resulted in minor modifications to the interview guide. The final semi-structured interview guide begins with some narrative stimulus questions. As participants became more willing to engage in the conversation, we gradually asked questions about the dietary decision-making process (see [online supplemental material 2](#SP2)).

### Data collection

After informed consent was obtained, interview times and locations were scheduled. Sixteen participants (66.7%) participated in the interviews in a quiet and undisturbed consultation room, and eight (33.3%) via telephone or WeChat. Semi-structured interviews in Chinese were performed by the first author for data collection. Before the interview, patients completed a form on demographic and clinical characteristics, including age, sex, marital status, education and disease type. Disease status was assessed by experienced clinicians using the CDAI for participants with CD and the Mayo score for participants with UC. During the interviews, the interviewer listened attentively and respected their responses. The sample size was determined based on the principle of saturation. When researchers find that further interviews no longer yield new information or insights, the data can be considered saturated, and further data collection is stopped. In our study, no new themes emerged from participants’ experiences once the number of participants reached 22, indicating that the data set had reached a saturation point. Two additional interviews were then conducted and no new themes or codes emerged, resulting in a total of 24 interviews. The duration of the interviews ranged from 35 min to 2 hours (M=90 min).

### Data analysis

The interview recordings were transcribed verbatim within 24 hours and then uploaded to NVivo 12 for analysis, while pilot interview recordings were excluded from the formal analysis. Observations from the interview process were placed in brackets in the text to explain the surrounding environment and participants’ states. The data were analysed using inductive thematic analysis guided by Braun and Clarke’s [^12] cyclical six-step reflexive approach ([figure 1](#F1)). This method accurately reflects participants' attitudes, opinions and experiences while acknowledging the reflexive influence of researchers' interpretations. The two researchers (LYT and WMR) independently reviewed the full transcripts and repeated listening records from ten interviews and reread them to assign codes. After reviewing and discussing these codes with a neutral researcher (TWJ), the first author (LYT) coded the remaining fourteen transcripts, while WMR conducted cross-validation by coding five randomly selected transcripts. All codes were then reviewed and discussed by LYT and WMR, and initial themes were identified from the coded data. Then, themes and subthemes, including their definitions, were finalised through regular discussions between coders and reviewed by the research team to ensure rigour. The final themes, subthemes and supporting quotations were translated into English by the first author (LYT) and confirmed by all researchers.

### Ethical considerations

The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Nanjing Hospital of Chinese Medicine (KY2022029). Before the interview, the researchers obtained either written informed consent or audio recorded verbal consent from the participants, while also reiterating that they had the right to withdraw from the study at any point. Pseudonyms were used to maintain confidentiality, and any potentially identifying information was removed from the transcripts and audio-recordings.

### Rigour

All the interviews were carried out by the same researcher (the first author), who had previously received systematic training in qualitative research methodologies and undertook several qualitative studies. The first author, a female graduate student with long-term clinical internship and outpatient training in gastroenterology, had prior opportunities to meet all potential participants in the clinical setting. Therefore, most participants established contact with the first author and built a close rapport prior to the interview, which helped them feel more comfortable and willing to share their experiences openly. After each interview, the researchers reviewed field notes and wrote reflexive reports. Throughout the data analysis process, the research team held regular meetings to compare analysis results and identify themes.

### Patient and public involvement

Two patients were involved in the study design phase and contributed to refining the interview guide. During the data collection phase, we invited patients to participate as interviewees, allowing them to share their experiences and insights in a collaborative setting. Two participants were asked to review the findings and provide feedback.

### Theme 1: challenges in problem identification

Problem identification referred to the initial decision-making process by which individuals recognised and defined diet-related issues requiring management in relation to their disease, thereby facilitating the continuation of healthy dietary decision-making. This theme described contexts in which the problem identification process was inappropriate, namely insufficient perception of disease threat and excessive fear of food.

#### Insufficient perception of disease threat

Some participants reported a limited understanding of the disease threat. They described that they tended to regard gastrointestinal symptoms as transient rather than signs of a lifelong and recurrent illness, which led them to downplay disease management and overlook the potential role of dietary management in IBD care.

> *I’m only in my twenties, and the suggestion of taking medication for the rest of my life (pause) until I’m in a coffin, makes me resistant to take medicine…* (UC, male)

#### Excessive fear of flare-ups

After experiencing painful symptoms like abdominal pain, diarrhoea and bloody stools, some participants believed that flare-ups were caused by a varied or overly stimulating diet. Their dietary decisions were generally cautious, specifically avoiding the intake of potentially ‘harmful’ foods.

> *I was thinking it might have been because I ate too many different things and too much greasy food, which damaged my intestines…Therefore, I tried only drinking porridge and avoiding other foods*. (UC, male)

Even during periods of remission, some participants remained anxious about flares to resume a normal diet.

> *At first, I didn’t dare to eat; I was afraid of diarrhea. I stopped eating everything and only ate vegetables, noodles or porridge every day for a long time, and I lost a lot of weight*. (UC, female)

### Theme 2: challenges in information seeking

Information seeking referred to the process by which individuals collected information to provide a basis for weighing potential pros and cons. At this stage, participants’ decisions mainly concerned whether to seek information and which sources to trust. The main challenges identified were the absence of individualised professional guidance and the decision-making burden caused by overwhelming information.

#### Absence of individualised professional guidance

Most participants mentioned that they gave priority to seeking dietary and other lifestyle guidance from HCPs, but the responses they received were often vague.

> *When I saw the doctor back then, they just told me to avoid spicy and irritating foods, and that was it. They wouldn’t specifically tell you what you shouldn’t eat, like celery and chives*. (UC, male)

This lack of individualised guidance could lead participants to rely on information from the internet and fellow patients to fill this gap, increasing their susceptibility to misinformation.

> *One of my colleagues has UC and follows a very simple, restricted diet, and his condition is well-controlled. So, I thought I’d try this extreme method of diet control too*. (IBDU, male)

#### Decision-making burden due to overwhelming information

Most participants indicated that they obtained dietary information from various sources, including HCPs and external sources (the Internet, fellow patients, etc.). As the amount of information increased, some participants expressed feelings of being ‘bombarded’ by information.

> *I used to seek help from doctors, the internet, and consult fellow patients, but I found the information was too much. My family also tells me what I should and should not eat. Although they mean well, I feel as if I am being bombarded…* (UC, female)

When faced with conflicting and complex information, participants experienced cognitive burden and found it difficult to make dietary decisions, struggling to decide which information to trust and how to act. In some cases, they adopted avoidance-oriented decisions, such as stopping information seeking or avoiding certain foods.

> *I searched online…Some people said they could be eaten, while others said they couldn’t… When both of these opinions reached me, I didn’t know which one to follow. In the end, I simply didn’t bother—I just didn’t eat those foods*. (UC, male)

### Theme 3: challenges in decision implementation

Decision implementation referred to the process by which participants made a series of action-level decisions based on their own set goals. This theme referred to the difficult choices participants had to make when they encountered conflicting requirements during the implementation of their daily decisions.

#### Conflict between dietary preferences and diet management

Participants often faced the decision of whether to comply with medical dietary advice or to indulge in the trigger foods. Many found it difficult to resist the sensory appeal of familiar flavours, even when advised against them by HCPs.

> *Although my doctor advised me to avoid it, I find that my meals lack flavor if I go without it for too long. Therefore, I occasionally indulge in spicy food*. (UC, male)

Some participants admitted to making conscious trade-offs, prioritising immediate pleasure over long-term health.

> *I considered the consequences, but I just couldn’t control myself. When it (trigger food) was right in front of me, I thought, I’ll just enjoy it—even if it gives me diarrhea. That’s what everyone thought*. (UC, male)

#### Conflict between socialising and diet management

In the context of social dining, participants often faced dilemmas regarding whether to attend gatherings. They wished to maintain relationships and a sense of normalcy, yet worried about symptom flare-ups, unfamiliar food and exposure to trigger foods. As a result, they stated frequently adopting avoidance-oriented decisions, such as declining invitations to reduce their decision-making burden.

> *I often feel torn by attending friends’ gatherings or not. If I go, I usually watch my friends enjoy the dishes while I can only cautiously pick a few dishes, afraid to eat much. But if I decline the invitation, I worry that it will gradually distance between us*. (UC, male)

Additionally, patients mentioned that social environments created pressure on their dietary decisions, leaving them to navigate a dilemma between asserting their specific dietary needs at the risk of inconvenience or embarrassment, and compromising to avoid unwanted attention.

> *My boyfriend’s father prepared fresh cow’s milk for me (had lactose intolerance before diagnosed). Since it was offered out of kindness, I felt refusing it would be disrespectful, so I drank a little… Two days later, I suffered from diarrhea*. (UC, Female)

#### Conflict between limited choices and diet management

Participants also described decisions shaped by practical constraints, such as time, availability or family eating habits. When faced with limited options, they sometimes chose convenience over a healthy diet.

> *Just like the last time I ate fried food, I was working non-stop without time to think about lunch. My colleagues brought KFC for us, and after having a bit, I ended up with diarrhea*. (IBDU, Male)

### Theme 4: challenges in post-decision reflection

Post-decision reflection referred to a subsequent stage in the dietary decision-making process, where individuals reviewed past decisions and considered whether to maintain, adjust or abandon their dietary decisions. This theme represented participants’ challenges following the implementation of their decisions.

#### Disappointment and regret over decision outcomes

Some participants held unrealistic expectations about dietary restrictions, and when confronted with the gap between their ideals and reality, they experienced disappointment and frustration over the lack of desired outcomes. These negative emotions further influenced their experience of dietary management and may have led to unhealthy decisions.

> *I strictly restrained my diet for about a month to improve my bowel condition. When I saw no improvement, I completely gave up… So, for a while, I just went back to eating things like spicy and greasy foods. I figured, whether I controlled my diet or not, I would still have diarrhea anyway…* (UC, male)

#### Self-regulatory fatigue

Prolonged dietary control gradually depletes participants' self-control resources, leading them to rely on habits or instincts when making dietary decisions to conserve energy.

> *I used to eat five meals a day, and I was very regular with my diet, paying close attention. That was when I had just gotten sick, and I was worried about a relapse, so I was quite obedient. But now, after such a long time living with this disease, I’m just worn out*. (CD, female)

### Theme 5: other factors influencing dietary decisions

#### Dietary decision dilemmas under inappropriate social support

Most participants noted that a lack of social support left them feeling neglected and increased the difficulty of maintaining dietary decisions.

> *My wife finds me… annoying. When I ask her to cook specific dishes, she keeps harping on. For example, if I want to eat something today, she says, “You can buy it yourself.”* (UC, male)

Some younger participants mentioned that their family members often browsed inappropriate health information and pressured them to follow dietary advice found online, even though such information might not be scientifically sound.

> *My mom was really worried about me (suffering from such disease). She searched online for advice and told me what I could or couldn’t eat. She was like, “don’t eat beef, egg yolks or vegetables” and kept forcing me to cut back on those foods. But I thought, I’m already so thin. If I can’t eat anything, how am I supposed to get enough nutrition? So I often argued with her about it. (UC, male)*

#### Avoidance of decisions under emotional influence

Participants described that, under the influence of negative emotions, they lacked the energy to manage their diet or forgo immediate pleasures for long-term health benefits, leading them to either passively or actively avoid making healthy dietary decisions.

> *At that time, even though I tried to adjust my diet, my emotions were in a bad place. My adjustments felt more like giving up. I was already fed up with life, and nothing mattered*. (UC, female)

#### Chinese cultural influences on dietary decision-making

Many participants reported avoiding foods categorised as ‘fawu’, such as milk, ‘raw’ or ‘cold’ foods like seafood in traditional Chinese medicine (TCM), fearing these might trigger disease flare-ups.

> *I don’t eat cold food and ‘fawu’ like seafood, milk, and chives. Occasionally, I eat warm-natured fruits, such as durian, or I steam them*. (UC, Male)

Additionally, some participants described feeling torn during traditional festivals. They wanted to enjoy festive food but felt obligated to stick to dietary rules. They said following these rules made it harder to fully enjoy the celebrations and required extra effort to resist food and cultural traditions.

> *Sometimes it’s difficult to manage… like during the Spring Festival (everyone gathers for the New Year’s Eve dinner). I had to take my bowl and eat alone in another room. It made me a bit unhappy and lonely because everyone was enjoying a big meal, but I couldn’t join them…* (UC, female)

## Discussion

This study provides a thorough and insightful analysis of the challenges faced by patients with IBD in making healthy dietary decisions across different stages, including problem identification, information seeking, decision implementation and post-decision reflection. The primary challenges were centred around the themes of information needs and the conflict between balancing different needs and adhering to dietary recommendations.

Problem identification primarily referred to whether participants recognised the importance of disease management and healthy eating, which influenced whether they proactively evaluated their dietary patterns for potential issues, such as nutritional adequacy and disease management. Similar to the findings of Wang *et al* [^13] regarding stroke patients, some patients tried to align their understanding of the disease with their pre-existing knowledge and experiences, often resulting in misconceptions. This study revealed that some patients with IBD, particularly those newly diagnosed, regarded IBD as comparable to common enteritis, assuming that once symptoms improved, they could discontinue medication and resume unhealthy dietary habits. By contrast, some participants, motivated by fear of the disease, imposed overly restrictive dietary limitations. Fear, as a powerful emotional experience, heightened patients’ focus on potential negative outcomes, leading to risk-averse behaviour.[^14] These findings suggest that HCPs should support patients in establishing accurate disease understanding at the time of diagnosis and encourage them to follow general dietary guidelines intended for the general population to improve overall health and quality of life. In addition, HCPs should place greater emphasis on nutrition education, guiding patients with IBD to correctly identify potential nutritional concerns through the use of appropriate tools or regular visits so that they can manage their diet appropriately.

Information seeking represented a reflection level decision that offered a foundation for participants to weigh potential pros and cons, while challenges in this process could shape their dietary decisions. Patients often sought information from HCPs, the internet or fellow patients. This study identified the main challenges encountered by patients during this stage, notably insufficient dietary advice from professionals and an overwhelming amount of information. Most participants preferred dietary advice from HCPs, especially doctors. However, many reported experiences of receiving inadequate and inaccurate dietary advice from HCPs. Previous studies [^15] noted several reasons why HCPs rarely provided dietary recommendations: lack of compelling research evidence, brief consultation times and lack of interest in this area. However, given the pressing needs from patients, it is essential for administrators to prioritise dietary guidance for patients with IBD and strengthen the training of HCPs, continuously improving the quality of their dietary counselling. Moreover, the majority of participants had experiences of seeking dietary advice online and from fellow patients. A recent scoping review on health information-seeking behaviours among patients with IBD highlighted the benefits of actively seeking health information.[^16] However, participants in this study experienced negative emotions and cognitive paralysis due to overwhelming, confusing or contradictory information during dietary decision-making, sometimes even leading them to abandon dietary management altogether. Participants in this study reported experiencing paralysis or stagnation in dietary decision-making when confronted with confusing and contradictory information, sometimes even abandoning dietary management altogether. This may be related to the lack of specificity and professionalism in the general dietary information that patients accessed from external resources.[^17] Future research could focus on developing evidence-based platforms tailored to patients’ dietary concerns. Such platforms could enable rapid access to scientific dietary information during daily decision-making.

Challenges in decision implementation referred to the dilemmas participants faced when competing real-life demands compromised their efforts to follow their healthy eating goals. Consistent with previous studies,[<sup>23 24</sup>](#R23) most patients with IBD avoided trigger foods as a dietary goal, even during remission, often continuing restrictive strategies developed during relapse. Yet, when faced with preferred foods, they showed tendencies toward hedonic eating. Dong *et al* [^19] suggested that such tendencies may arise from neural responses in taste and reward-related brain regions due to prolonged dietary restrictions, leading to strong cravings. Such cravings may predispose patients with IBD to disordered eating behaviours, including binge eating, as these may impair self-control.[^20] In line with prior findings,[^21] participants also struggled to balance dietary restrictions with social demands, resulting in decision-making dilemmas and, at times, social isolation. Given the lack of high-quality evidence that avoiding trigger foods supports remission,[<sup>7 28</sup>](#R7) it may be helpful for HCPs to emphasise the benefits of following general dietary guidelines during remission and to highlight the potential drawbacks of unnecessary restriction, thereby supporting appropriate dietary perceptions and behaviour. Notably, practical constraints (eg, work schedules) also limited participants’ ability to implement healthy dietary decisions. Pender *et al* [^22] defined this phenomenon as immediate competing preferences, which may derail individuals’ decisions to engage in health-promoting behaviours. Shahabi *et al* [^23] found that enhancing social support and developing contingency plans for specific situations reduced the impact of immediate competing preferences on treatment adherence among patients with diabetes. Based on these insights, it may be valuable for HCPs to strengthen family and social support for patients with IBD and to develop context-specific alternative plans informed by our findings to facilitate the implementation of healthy dietary decisions.

Post-decision reflection referred to participants reviewing the experiences of their own dietary decisions and considered the subsequent process of self-regulation, which helped support adherence to healthy eating behaviours. Challenges at this stage were disappointment and regret over dietary decisions and self-regulatory fatigue. Some participants in this study had unrealistically high expectations regarding dietary control of their disease, leading to frustration and, in some cases, abandonment of dietary management. Quantitative research has similarly shown that patients with IBD generally have low self-efficacy in controlling disease symptoms,[^24] and dietary management failures can further diminish their confidence, resulting in hopelessness. Rines *et al* suggested that patients can find their way to knowledge and experience, acquire acceptance and maintain hope through trial and error. Trial and error is the common dietary management approach among patients with IBD.[^25] In practice, HCPs can encourage patients to listen to their bodies and to gradually build confidence and maintain hope through trial and error. Notably, given the widespread dietary restrictions among patients with IBD, it may be important to guide them in interpreting diet-related symptoms appropriately, rather than imposing broad restrictions on foods or food groups based on a single incident. The strength model of self-control suggests that self-control relies on limited resources that become depleted with use.[^26] This study found that prolonged dietary control and the associated negative emotions rapidly depleted patients’ self-control resources. As self-control weakened, patients were more likely to revert to unhealthy eating behaviours, affecting the quality of their choices. However, research on self-control depletion in the context of IBD management remains limited. Future studies could further investigate its impact on patients' healthy dietary behaviours.

The results of this study suggested that social support and emotional factors significantly influenced the dietary decision-making process of patients with IBD. Limited social support may restrict patients' access to healthy foods.[^27] In this study, participants also reported experiences where family members compelled them to follow dietary advice from uncertain sources. Interestingly, participants reported feeling stressed and uncomfortable when family and friends paid excessive attention to their special diets, which created a sense of distance and challenged their desire to regain normality.[^28] These findings suggest that nutritional interventions may benefit from considering the role of family members in shaping the environment and conditions that support healthy dietary decision-making. Previous studies highlighted that the special diet associated with IBD could lead to negative emotions such as frustration, confusion and distress, severely impacting the food-related quality of life and dietary management of patients with IBD.[^21] In this study, some participants turned to high-calorie, high-fat foods to alleviate negative emotions, even when these foods were on the prohibited list. This behaviour might align with emotional eating, where individuals under stress choose high-calorie, sugary and high-fat ‘comfort foods’ for solace.[^29] However, such foods were linked to the onset and progression of IBD.[^30] Therefore, future research should explore the impact of emotions, particularly negative ones, on food choices and dietary patterns in patients with IBD.

Chinese culture and beliefs significantly influenced the dietary decisions of patients with IBD. For thousands of years, food has been regarded as closely related to disease management and health maintenance, with the belief that what is eaten may cause disease, while controlling some foods can maintain health.[^31] Consistent with the research of previous studies in China,[<sup>11 23</sup>](#R11) our study indicated that participants valued the dietary concepts embedded in TCM and used them to guide their food choices and avoidances. Although these concepts are widely acknowledged among patients with IBD in China, their efficacy and impact on the disease require further clinical evidence. Furthermore, previous studies have identified challenges for patients with IBD when eating at social gatherings,[^18] and these challenges may be particularly complex in the Chinese cultural context. In China, most social interactions revolve around food, with people often encouraging others to try dishes and sometimes even serving food directly into others' bowls as a sign of care and hospitality.[^32] Coupled with the cultural emphasis on respect for elders,[^33] patients with IBD might worry about appearing disrespectful and choose to conceal their dietary restrictions. Additionally, some Chinese festivals use food to symbolise wishes for reunion, happiness and prosperity.[^32] Dietary restrictions during significant holidays may increase the psychological burden and sense of social isolation for patients with IBD. Therefore, it is crucial to develop and implement culturally sensitive dietary recommendations for IBD prevention and management, particularly for patients who need to strictly limit their diets during festive periods, as they require more attention and timely psychological support.

### Limitations

This study has several limitations. First, participants were recruited from four hospitals in southeastern China. Given that dietary culture varies across regions and may influence the symptom experience of patients with IBD, the findings should be further explored in patients from other areas. Second, the study focused on the challenges of patient-led dietary decision-making. Future research could recruit both HCPs and patients to explore challenges to promoting healthy dietary decisions and to identify effective dietary intervention models.

## Conclusions

This study explored the challenges faced by Chinese adults with IBD in their dietary decision-making. The main challenges include insufficient disease perception and professional guidance, overwhelming information and conflicts between different needs and dietary advice. Further research is needed to determine how to develop dietary decision support systems tailored to patients with IBD based on these findings to facilitate healthier dietary decision-making.
