## Highlights

- Differential genomic and microbiota signatures in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) across different geographies and ethnicities influence the occurrence and prevalence of IBD.
- Key factors that shape gut microbial landscapes include dietary preference, urbanization, lifestyle patterns, and regional medication practices.
- Microbiome research is geographically imbalanced, with most studies focusing on Western populations, leading to taxonomic bias and a high proportion of unclassified species in remote regions. There are inadequate comprehensive and systematic studies that span multiple regions and continents.
- Understanding IBD pathogenesis requires a consideration of the interplay of genetic, microbial, immune, and environmental factors, demanding an integrative research approach to achieve a comprehensive understanding of this intricate condition.
- Integrating geographical diversity into IBD diagnostics and therapeutic strategies is crucial for broader applicability and generalizability across populations. This approach not only enriches our understanding of this complex condition but also paves the way for more effective, personalized treatments.

## Current genetic landscape and variations in IBD

Genetic factors significantly influence IBD susceptibility. **Genome-wide association studies (GWAS)** have identified over 240 IBD risk loci \[17\] regulating immune response, intestinal barrier integrity, and microbiota interactions \[18\]. However, these loci vary across diverse regions \[13\] (Table 1). To date, most IBD genetic associations are from European ancestry. For instance, *NOD2*, a key risk gene for CD, exhibited 13% allele frequency in Caucasians but only 0.05% in East Asians \[19,20\].

## Epigenetic variations across geography and populations

Beyond genomic modifications, geographical disparities are also observed in epigenetic profiles. Epigenetic shifts regulate IBD risk and severity by altering gene expression without changing DNA sequences \[27\]. These comprise DNA methylation, histone modifications, and noncoding RNAs, which are environmentally influenced and contribute to regional variations \[28\] (Figure 2).

## Geographically driven variations in human gut microbiome

Gut microbiota is pivotal in regulating host **homeostasis**, with a balanced and diverse microbiome maintaining gut function, pathogen defense, and immune regulation \[36\]. Although predominant **phyla** in healthy individuals are overall consistent globally \[37\], the gut microbiota also exhibits dynamic changes and regional variations (Figure 1B). ## Cross-cultural studies and geographically diverse cohorts for IBD diagnostics and therapeutics

Detecting genetic variants and microbial changes allows for identifying high-risk individuals before symptom manifestation, facilitating early intervention and reducing the risk of delayed diagnosis \[11,43\]. However, variations in genetics, epigenetics, and gut microbiome profiles across diverse geographic populations pose significant challenges for the identification, development, and validation of universal diagnostic or prognostic biomarkers with reproducible performance globally (Table 2).
