## Abstract

Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) are a group of organic compounds produced by the fermentation of dietary fibre by the human gut microbiota. They play diverse roles in different physiological processes of the host with implications for human health and disease. This Review provides an overview of the complex microbial metabolism underlying SCFA formation, considering microbial interactions and modulating factors of the gut environment. We explore the multifaceted mechanistic interactions between SCFAs and the host, with a particular focus on the local actions of SCFAs in the gut and their complex interactions with the immune system. We also discuss how these actions influence intestinal and extraintestinal diseases and emerging therapeutic strategies using SCFAs.

## Introduction

Dietary fibre mainly originates from plant cell wall and storage carbohydrates, also commonly referred to as glycans. It is highly complex and consists of carbohydrates that differ in their monosaccharide constituents, chemical linkages, chain lengths and non-carbohydrate constituents. The main carbohydrate types include cellulose, β-glucan, (arabino-)xylan, xyloglucan, glucomannan, galactomannan, pectin, fructan and resistant starch [^1] [^2]. Fibre is indigestible in the human small intestine, either because the corresponding digestive enzymes are lacking or because their action is not sufficient to achieve complete breakdown during ileal passage, as is the case for resistant starch. Consequently, fibre reaches the colon, where it is a major substrate for the resident microbiota. The colon is a highly anaerobic environment, and few microorganisms are able to carry out complete substrate oxidation by aerobic respiration (Box [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Sec2)). Anaerobic respiration, in which other compounds replace oxygen as terminal electron transport chain acceptors, is used by some microorganisms (for example, sulfate-reducing bacteria). Most gut bacteria, however, perform fermentation, which generates large quantities of reduced fermentation products. Fermentative bacteria need to compromise between energy gain and the requirement for overall redox balance (Box [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Sec2)) and usually produce a range of different fermentation products simultaneously. These include the short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) formate, acetate, propionate and butyrate (carbon chain length C1–C4; the longer SCFAs valerate (C5) and caproate (C6) may also be produced by some gut bacteria), other organic acids (mainly lactate and succinate), alcohols and the gases carbon dioxide and hydrogen. In addition to dietary fibre, host-derived compounds (in particular mucin) [^3], amino acids and organic acids can also lead to SCFA production (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig1)).

Gut microorganisms differ in their substrate utilization capacities and product formation. A complex web of metabolic interactions between different microorganisms leads to the production of mainly acetate, propionate and butyrate in healthy individuals [^2]. Primary degraders occupy the top level of fibre breakdown and some of the liberated polysaccharides, oligosaccharides and monosaccharides become available to secondary fibre degraders. The level of cross-feeding depends on the nature of the extracellular enzyme and uptake systems present in the respective primary degrader [^2]. Other cross-feeding microorganisms make use of some of the fermentation products generated by fibre degraders (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Tab1)). Some microorganisms are generalists that show a high level of adaptability to the presence of many different substrates, whereas others are more specialized. Furthermore, which SCFAs are generated by a specific microorganism may differ based on the growth substrate available [^2].

SCFAs are taken up by the host and elicit numerous effects on host physiology, both locally in the intestine and systemically. They are implicated in many aspects of host health, including cancer development, immune function, metabolic, cardiovascular and neurological health. Much progress has been made in recent years in deciphering the molecular mechanisms underlying those effects. In this Review, we focus on the latest developments in SCFA production by the gut microbiota and how this is influenced by gut environmental conditions. We then explore the current knowledge on host interactions of SCFAs. This is a vast topic area [^4] [^5] and not all aspects can be comprehensively reviewed here. We provide an overview of the molecular mechanisms underlying the action of SCFAs and the role they play in maintaining gut homeostasis, before summarizing the implications for disease development and therapeutic application.

## Microbial short-chain fatty acid metabolism

Many different carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZymes) [^6] are required for fibre breakdown and assessing their genome carriage helps to deduce the roles of different gut microorganisms in fibre fermentation [^7]. SCFA production depends on which bacteria are directly promoted by the substrates entering the colon and on the prevailing environmental conditions, as well as microbial cross-feeding interactions.

### Fermentation pathways

Most gut bacteria can produce acetate, formate and lactate, whereas propionate and butyrate production is more restricted [^8] [^9] (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Tab1)). Propionate is mainly produced by Bacteroidota species from carbohydrates via the succinate pathway, and *Veillonella* species also utilize this pathway for growth on lactate (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig1)). The propanediol pathway from the deoxy sugars fucose and rhamnose is present in some phylogenetically diverse species, whereas the acrylate pathway (operating from lactate) is restricted to some Peptostreptococcaceae and not believed to play a major role in propionate formation in the gut [^9] [^10]. Butyrate production is present in many species within the Bacillota phylum for growth on carbohydrates [^8] [^9] (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Tab1)). It starts with the condensation of two acetyl-CoA molecules to acetoacetyl-CoA and its reduction to butyryl-CoA, which is converted via a CoA-transferase to butyrate with acetate as co-substrate in the majority of gut bacteria [^8]. Some microorganisms, mainly belonging to Bacillota, can ferment amino acids to butyrate [^9], including *Intestinimonas butyriciproducens*, which ferments the Maillard reaction product fructoselysine [^11]. Glutamate, lysine and succinate feed into the pathway intermediate crotonyl-CoA (Fig. [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig1)). A small number of butyrate producers can grow on lactate, having an important role in preventing the build-up of toxic lactate levels [^12] [^13]. The different SCFA formation pathways are principally well understood, but details of the underlying biochemistry and alternative enzymatic steps are still being discovered in intestinal bacteria [^14]. This should be considered when microbiome metagenomic pathway analyses are used to assess SCFA production capacities in humans.

### Influence of environmental conditions

The collective action of the microbiota shapes the intestinal environment, which can influence SCFA production. The intestinal pH drops during fermentation, providing a competitive advantage to many butyrate producers over propionate-producing Bacteroidota [^15] [^16], and the proportion of butyrate generated relative to other fermentation products increases with lower pH in some microorganisms [^8]. This likely contributes to a recent observation that the relative proportion of faecal butyrate increases with higher total SCFA concentrations [^17] [^18]. Intestinal hydrogen concentration can also influence SCFA production. Hydrogen is produced as an alternative electron sink to organic acids in some fermentative bacteria and consumed by hydrogenotrophs (methanogenic Archaea, sulfate reducers and acetogens). High intestinal hydrogen concentrations make further hydrogen production energetically unfavourable and shift the balance towards butyrate formation in some bacteria [^19]. The magnitude of these environmental effects is dependent on the individual microbiota composition, which shows a large variation between healthy humans [^20]. Consequently, specific microbial functional groups (for example, lactate utilizers) can differ in abundance, and functional variation also occurs between closely related microorganisms down to strain level [^13] [^21].

The amount and type of fibre entering the colon are an important determinant of SCFA formation. A diverse range of glycans likely promotes a diverse microbiota [^22], and the level of fermentability, influenced by physicochemical determinants such as solubility, can influence where in the colon fermentation takes place [^1] [^23]. Fermentation along the intestine cannot easily be assessed in vivo, but the usefulness of telemetric capsules was recently assessed during a fully controlled dietary trial with patients presenting irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) [^24]. There were no clear trends in pH between different diets, possibly reflecting confounding factors in vivo. Intestinal hydrogen concentration, however, showed a clear trend towards more distal colonic fermentation with a diet containing fibres with different levels of fermentability, which is desirable for delivering health-promoting SCFAs to the distal gut epithelium. Our understanding of microbial fibre metabolism is mainly based on assessments with individual purified glycans, and the breakdown of more complex fibre remains poorly understood [^1] [^2] [^23]. Whole plant-based diets contain insoluble plant cell wall fragments that may require additional microbial functions (for example, attachment) and provide a surface for biofilm formation. This may promote community interactions, such as synergistic fibre breakdown or enhanced cross-feeding [^2] [^23]. Differences in fibre composition between plants and food processing (for example, cooking and milling) also affect the physicochemical properties of fibres, and studies on their impact on microbiota metabolism are emerging [^25] [^26] [^27].

### Microbial community interactions

Factors influencing microbial competitiveness, such as enzyme or transporter specificities and efficiencies, require careful biochemical analysis, as exemplified for xylan degradation by the butyrate producer *Roseburia intestinalis* [^28]. In addition to direct competition for specific substrates and cross-feeding interactions, recent studies provide insights into additional community interactions that influence SCFA production. *Bacteroides* species are generalists that carry polysaccharide utilization loci for many different carbohydrates. CRISPR genome editing was used to unravel their complex expression control, which contributes to strain fitness in the context of the nutrient environment and interactions with different butyrate-producing microorganisms [^29]. SCFAs may exert inhibitory effects, as demonstrated for butyrate via the build-up of a high intracellular butyryl-CoA pool in *Bacteroides* strains. This was dependent both on the growth substrate and strain-specific genetic factors, in particular carriage of enzymes involved in acyl-CoA metabolism and nucleotide polymorphisms in their promoter and coding regions that affect gene regulation and protein function [^21]. Thus, similar intestinal butyrate concentrations in different individuals may result in different propionate concentrations, depending on which *Bacteroides* strains prevail. It would be desirable to predict SCFA production in response to different types of dietary fibre from the microbiota composition to develop personalized nutritional strategies that aim to maximize health. Mathematical modelling strategies are being developed, and machine learning may have an increasingly important role in this area in the future [^30] [^31] [^32].

## Molecular host interactions of short-chain fatty acids

Total SCFA concentrations in the colon are around 100 mM as determined in sudden death victims, with an acetate:propionate:butyrate ratio of approximately 3:1:1 (ref. [^33]). More than 90% of the SCFAs are absorbed from the intestine [^34] and they exert numerous local and systemic effects on the host. Here, we briefly outline the major mechanistic routes by which SCFAs modulate host physiology.

### Short-chain fatty acid transport and energy provision

The colonic epithelium absorbs SCFAs mainly via two transporters: solute carrier family 26 member 3 (SLC26A3) (by exchange with bicarbonate) and monocarboxylate transporter 1 (MCT1) (by proton symport). Basolateral export of SCFAs from colonocytes is less well understood, but likely involves chloride antiporter SLC4A1/2, proton symporter MCT4 and an anion channel [^35] (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig2)). All three major SCFAs are detectable in portal blood [^33] and a recent study determined their concentrations in the small veins emanating from the colon in a pig model (acetate 3.8 mM, propionate 1.1 mM and butyrate 0.29 mM) [^36]. The authors propose that these concentrations are in the appropriate range to condition the immune system by signalling to G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), which are present on immune cells passing through the portal venous system. Portal butyrate concentrations are lower than those of acetate and propionate when considering their respective concentrations in the lumen of the colon, as butyrate is mainly metabolized in colonocytes. Propionate, by contrast, is largely metabolized in the liver. Together, this results in low peripheral venous blood concentrations of both SCFAs compared with acetate [^33]. All three SCFAs, however, remain detectable in different tissues. For example, concentrations in the cerebrospinal fluid were reported to range from 0 to 171 μM for acetate, 0 to 5 μM for propionate and 0 to 2.8 μM for butyrate [^37].

SCFAs can be metabolized in host tissues by entering the tricarboxylic acid cycle after conversion to acetyl-CoA (acetate; butyrate via β-oxidation) or succinyl-CoA (propionate), providing energy. Alternatively, acetyl-CoA can be utilized for lipogenesis and succinyl-CoA for gluconeogenesis [^38]. Collectively, the SCFAs contribute an estimated 5–10% to the host energy requirements [^37] [^38]. Butyrate has an important trophic role in the colon, as it is the preferred energy source of colonocytes and has been estimated to provide ~70% of their total energy needs in mice [^37]. Its importance relative to other potential fuels has recently been questioned, as butyrate cannot provide building blocks for cell proliferation owing to the nature of its metabolism. Therefore, the role of other metabolites, such as monosaccharides, also needs to be considered [^34]. In addition to providing energy to the host, SCFAs have also been shown to increase energy expenditure, and several mechanistic routes, including neuronal and metabolic pathways in various tissues, are under investigation [^38].

### Interaction with host receptors

Various intestinal and extraintestinal host cells express GPCRs that respond to SCFAs and trigger host responses, which has recently been reviewed [^39] [^40]. Two free fatty acid receptors (FFARs) are activated by all three major SCFAs: FFAR2 (also known as GPR43; higher affinity for the shorter SCFAs acetate and propionate) and FFAR3 (also known as GPR41; higher affinity for the longer SCFAs propionate and butyrate). A butyrate-specific (GPR109A) and acetate-specific and propionate-specific (OLFR78) receptor has also been identified. In view of much of the research in this field being carried out in mouse models, it is important to note that SCFA potencies differ between human and mouse orthologues of FFAR2 and FFAR3 (ref. [^39]). Activation of GPCRs results in disassociation of the associated G protein α-subunit, which leads to a cellular response via signalling cascades, such as gene expression changes (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig2)). Different GPCRs associate with different G proteins, which influence downstream signalling. A complex interplay of further factors modulates receptor action, including desensitization and resensitization processes, post-translational modifications and regulatory proteins [^39] [^41].

GPCRs are involved in the regulation of many aspects of host physiology, including hormone secretion, glucose and lipid metabolism and immune function, some of which are discussed further in the sections subsequently. SCFA affinities of FFAR2 and FFAR3 are in the micromolar range, broadly in line with the systemic concentrations in the many tissues they are expressed in, including adipocytes, immune cells, neurons and pancreatic β-cells. Colonic SCFA concentrations are in the millimolar range, but apical expression has been confirmed for several GPCRs on colonocytes [^39]. FFAR2, however, has also been shown to be expressed at the basolateral membrane of enteroendocrine cells involved in satiety-enhancing gut hormone secretion [^40]. It appears that the complex interplay among overlapping functions, tissue-specific expression levels and different signalling cascades of diverse GPCRs enables the fine-tuning of body homeostasis [^39] [^40] [^42].

### Epigenetic regulation

Epigenetics refers to inheritable changes of gene function that do not involve changes in the DNA sequence [^43]. DNA methylation, mediated by DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs), involves the addition of a methyl group to the c-5 position of cytosine in the promoter CpG region, which results in the suppression of gene expression. Butyrate has been shown to downregulate DNMT1 by phosphorylation of mitogen-activated protein kinase 1 and extracellular signal-regulated kinase signalling [^44].

Histone post-translational modifications involve the covalent attachment of functional groups to the histone tails that affect transcription, the predominant epigenetic mechanism by which SCFAs influence several gut–organ axes to modulate gene expression in multiple cell types [^43] (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig2)). Acetylation induced by histone acetyltransferases relaxes condensed chromatin, making it more accessible to gene expression. Conversely, deacetylation mediated by histone deacetylases (HDACs) leads to compaction of the chromatin, making genes less accessible for transcription. SCFAs have been proven to act as class I and II HDAC inhibitors [^45], acting directly or through GPCRs. The resulting increased expression of target genes impacts cellular differentiation, apoptosis and tumour suppression [^46] [^47]. Butyrate is the most studied and most potent HDAC inhibitor, whereas acetate is the least potent SCFA [^43]. Acetate and butyrate also get metabolized to acetyl-CoA and increase histone acetylation directly [^48] [^49]. Other novel post-translational protein modifications, propionylation, butyrylation and lysine crotonylation, are alternate pathways for the action of SCFAs [^50] [^51]. It has also been suggested that butyrate acts as a ligand for several transcription factors independent of HDAC inhibition [^52], but this has recently been called into question for the aryl hydrocarbon receptor pathway [^53].

The third epigenetic mechanism is RNA interference, a process by which a class of non-coding RNA species called microRNAs inhibit the translation process of mRNA. There is increasing evidence that microRNAs have a vital role in the initiation, progression and spread of cancers [^54]. Butyrate reduced the expression of the miR-17-92 oncogenic cluster, which has a prominent role in proliferation, angiogenesis, differentiation and apoptosis in colorectal cancer (CRC) [^55]. The evidence for the epigenetic effect of butyrate through RNA interference has been discussed in a recent review [^56].

## Short-chain fatty acids and gut homeostasis

SCFAs have an important role in maintaining gut health through a multitude of different actions. They contribute to colonization resistance against pathogens via several mechanistic pathways, which have recently been reviewed [^57]. Pathogens, however, may overcome SCFA-mediated colonization resistance. For example, *Salmonella enterica* serovar Typhimurium could overcome propionate inhibition in the presence of inflammation-derived nitrate by enabling anaerobic respiration and propionate catabolism [^58]. SCFAs also influence gut physiology. In addition to providing energy to host tissues as discussed earlier, they are also implicated in aiding mineral absorption via several mechanisms of action, including a lowering of gut pH leading to enhanced mineral solubility, an increase in the intestinal surface area and regulatory effects [^59]. Here, we focus on the role of SCFAs in influencing gut motility, the physical gut barrier and the host immune system.

### Modulation of gut physiology by short-chain fatty acids

Gut homeostasis is maintained by achieving a delicate equilibrium between the resident microorganisms in the gut lumen with the host cells that are in close relationship with them. SCFAs have a distinctive role in this critical crosstalk that determines the overall gut health of the host.

#### Gut motility and transit time

One of the fundamental functions of the gut is the ability to mix the intestinal contents and propel them forward to facilitate digestion and absorption. Microbial and luminal metabolites are sensed by enterochromaffin and L cells in the mucosa to initiate downstream signals via enteric neurons to the muscular layers and modulate gut motility [^60]. The role of the gut microbiota has been highlighted by the reduced enteric neurons and impaired motility in germ-free mice [^61]. Butyrate, acetate and propionate have all been shown to activate enterochromaffin cells to produce 5-hyroxytryptamine (5-HT), which activates 5-HT4 receptors on enteric neurons, promoting gut motility [^62]. Conversely, L cells sense SCFAs and secrete glucagon-like peptide 1 and peptide YY, both of which inhibit gut motility [^62]. The association of SCFAs with gut motility in humans has been highlighted in a recent meta-analysis, which showed that faecal propionate and butyrate were lower in patients with constipation-predominant IBS, whereas faecal butyrate was higher in patients with diarrhoea-predominant IBS [^63].

#### Mucous layer

The intestinal mucous layer is the most proximal layer of the gut barrier and is critical for protection from luminal pathogens. Butyrate directly influences mucin 2 production, which is the most important secretory mucin component in the intestine, either via selective acetylation or methylation of histones of the *MUC2* gene or indirectly through the mediation of fibroblasts [^64] [^65]. In a mouse model, administration of butyrate enemas increased mucin 2 production, although the actual mucosal layer decreased in thickness [^66]. However, butyrate enemas in a human trial with patients with ulcerative colitis showed no change in *MUC2* gene expression in rectal biopsies [^67]. The direct effect of SCFAs on mucin production in vivo still needs further elucidation.

#### Epithelial cell barrier

The epithelial cell layer is by far the most important component of the gut barrier. The cells are held in proximity by junctional proteins, which effectively seal off the paracellular spaces. Several in vitro cellular models have confirmed the beneficial effects of SCFAs, especially butyrate, by positive regulation of claudin, zonulin and occludin expression, resulting in restoration of transepithelial electrical resistance. However, these effects are dose-dependent, with lower doses of butyrate conferring these beneficial effects, but higher doses having the opposite effect on Caco-2 cell monolayers [^68]. Butyrate was also found to mitigate epithelial barrier injury in a mouse model [^69]. However, this protective effect was not demonstrated in studies on epithelial cells isolated from healthy individuals or those with ulcerative colitis [^70] [^71].

### Immune regulation

SCFAs have a crucial role in immune system regulation, influencing both innate and adaptive immune responses. The players of the innate arm are in close proximity to the intestinal lumen, whereas the adaptive immune components are affected through smaller concentrations of SCFAs that are absorbed into the systemic circulation (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig3)).

#### Innate immunity

The innate immune system has the critical task of early recognition of pathogens and activation of mechanisms for their rapid clearance, irrespective of prior exposure. Macrophages and monocytes are the first line of defence whose functions are altered by SCFAs. The evidence is mostly based on observations of cytokine responses of different cell types following direct interactions with SCFAs. Butyrate, propionate and acetate have been shown to impact the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumour necrosis factor, IL-1β, IL-6 and nitric oxide and inhibit the activity of inducible nitric oxide synthase in macrophages to dampen inflammation [^72]. Butyrate also directly suppressed the kinase activity of mammalian target of rapamycin to enhance phagocytosis and increased antimicrobial peptide synthesis [^73]. SCFAs have been found to attenuate inflammation by remodelling macrophages from the pro-inflammatory M1 polarized macrophage phenotype to the anti-inflammatory M2 polarized macrophage phenotype in dextran sulfate sodium colitis and alcoholic hepatitis mouse models [^74] [^75]. Butyrate reduced the expression of monocyte attractant protein 1 and vascular cell adhesion molecule 1, both of which are critical in leukocyte migration to the intestine [^76] [^77]. Collective evidence suggests that SCFAs have a dampening impact on macrophages.

SCFAs modulate the phagocytic function of neutrophils and impair the production of reactive oxygen species [^78]. This also results in decreased production of inflammatory cytokines [^79]. All SCFAs induced apoptosis in neutrophils and accelerated death [^80]. By impairing the chemotaxis of neutrophils, SCFAs have an attenuating effect on intestinal inflammation [^81] [^82]. Overall, evidence suggests that SCFAs have an inhibitory effect on neutrophil function, highlighting their anti-inflammatory role.

As specialized antigen-presenting cells, dendritic cells are the bridge between innate and adaptive immunity. In vitro studies showed that pretreatment with butyrate resulted in a significant reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-12 and interferon-γ (IFNγ) and a significant increase in anti-inflammatory IL-10 secretions [^83] [^84]. Butyrate and propionate have been found to have a critical role in the differentiation, maturation and function of these cells towards a predominant tolerogenic phenotype [^85].

#### Adaptive immunity

Adaptive immune system CD4 <sup>+</sup> T cells can differentiate into various subtypes, such as T helper 1 (T <sub>H</sub> 1) cells and T <sub>H</sub> 17 cells directed at cell-mediated immunity, T <sub>H</sub> 2 cell subtypes promoting humoral immunity and regulatory T (T <sub>reg</sub>) cells, which suppress adverse immune reactions, maintain immune tolerance and contribute to gut homeostasis. Through HDAC inhibition, both butyrate and propionate increase the expression of transcription factor Foxp3 to induce the generation and activation of T <sub>reg</sub> cells, pointing to an anti-inflammatory effect [^86] [^87]. However, butyrate has also been shown to generate pro-inflammatory T <sub>H</sub> 17 and T <sub>H</sub> 1 effector cell subtypes [^88]. In a dextran sulfate sodium colitis mouse model, butyrate induced a regulatory phenotype at physiological concentrations, whereas higher doses stimulated the pro-inflammatory subtype with increased levels of IFNγ [^89]. Butyrate and, to a lesser extent, propionate have also been shown to induce both IFN-γ and granzyme B gene expression in CD8 <sup>+</sup> T cells [^90]. This suggests that SCFAs induce anti-inflammatory T <sub>reg</sub> cells in normal homeostatic conditions, but SCFA-mediated pro-inflammatory cytokines may predominate in the presence of inflammation. This is by a dual action of the SCFAs activating both the effector and T <sub>reg</sub> cell pathways [^88].

SCFAs have been shown to amplify antibody production by modulating gene expression, leading to a differentiation of B cells into specific plasma cells [^91]. Butyrate enhanced the intestinal IgA response to microbiota by affecting the activity of B cells in the Peyer’s patches via GPR43 (ref. [^92]). Butyrate and propionate were found to affect B cells through HDAC inhibition, leading to reduced autoantibody production in lupus-prone mice [^93]. Butyrate, propionate and acetate stimulated development into regulatory B cells by induction of IL-10 (refs. [^94] [^95]). The pivotal effects of SCFAs on antibody production, B cell development and differentiation and local IgA responses highlight their mediatory role in immune-related inflammatory diseases and autoimmune disorders.

## Short-chain fatty acids, health and disease

There is a large body of evidence that links the gut microbiota to health and disease, mediated by SCFAs in the gut and further afield [^96]. This section provides a broad overview of the impact of SCFAs on disease conditions and SCFA-targeted therapeutic approaches, mostly based on studies in human subjects.

### Intestinal diseases

The intestinal lumen, as the site of bacterial fermentation, has the highest concentration of SCFAs, so it is not a surprise that they play a critical role in several intestinal diseases. The ‘leaky gut’ in these disorders tends to allow transit of not only luminal pathogens but also toxic metabolites such as endotoxin from the lumen into the bloodstream. SCFAs have a putative anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory function to counteract the inflammatory cascade that is triggered by this disruption.

#### Inflammatory bowel disease

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) comprises two different phenotypes, ulcerative colitis and Crohn’s disease, characterized by an alteration in luminal bacteria, barrier dysfunction, immune dysregulation and chronic inflammation [^97]. An alteration in SCFA-producing bacteria has been documented in IBD, with *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and *R. intestinalis* being significantly reduced [^98] [^99]. A decreased bacterial capacity for butyrate production, as measured by the gene content for one of the bacterial enzymes responsible for butyrate synthesis, butyryl-CoA:acetate CoA-transferase, has been detected in IBD [^100]. Furthermore, a meta-analysis of 11 studies showed that patients with IBD had significantly reduced concentrations of total faecal SCFAs, acetate, propionate and valerate compared with healthy subjects [^101]. In addition to a reduction in butyrate production, there are potential alterations of mucosal butyrate oxidation in patients with IBD [^102]. Additionally, the presence of an inflammatory milieu, including cytokines such as tumour necrosis factor, renders the epithelium less responsive to butyrate [^103]. This is borne out by a reduced expression of butyrate receptors in the colonocytes of patients with Crohn’s disease [^104]. One can consider a ‘double hit hypothesis’, wherein not only SCFA production is reduced in IBD but also its metabolism and effectiveness are further affected in the background of intestinal inflammation. There is emerging evidence that faecal SCFAs could be used as a marker of disease activity in patients with Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis, with levels improving as the inflammation decreases [^105] [^106].

Several studies have utilized SCFAs in the treatment of IBD (Table [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Tab2)). Butyrate enemas improved symptoms, decreased inflammation and showed a reduction in nuclear factor-κB activation in macrophages of the lamina propria from patients with ulcerative colitis in some studies [^107] [^108] [^109]. However, a recent systematic review did not support the application of butyrate enemas in ulcerative colitis, with no reliable data in patients with Crohn’s disease [^110]. Oral tablets and microencapsulated preparations of butyrate have shown some benefit in patients with ulcerative colitis and Crohn’s disease [^111] [^112]. This suggests that oral butyrate may help to augment existing treatment options for IBD, but further studies are required.

#### Colorectal cancer

CRC is the third most diagnosed cancer and the second commonest cause of cancer-related mortality [^113]. An inverse relationship between dietary fibre intake and CRC has been documented [^114] and a large prospective, observational cohort study (EPIC study, more than 500,000 European subjects) found a 25% reduction of CRC in the highest quintile of fibre intake when compared with the lowest [^115]. However, the linkage to SCFAs in protection against CRC is more tenuous, as a clear relationship between either faecal SCFA levels and dietary fibre intake in healthy individuals, or between patients with CRC and healthy subjects, has not been established [^116] [^117] [^118]. This may reflect the fact that faecal SCFAs are a poor marker of intestinal SCFA production owing to their extensive uptake in the colon. Nevertheless, combined metabolomic signatures have shown SCFAs to have an inverse correlation with CRC incidence [^119].

SCFAs, especially butyrate, have been studied extensively in colorectal carcinogenesis in animal models with putative anti-tumorigenic actions that include induction of cancer cell apoptosis, arrest of the tumour cell cycle and inhibition of oncogenic micro-RNA expression [^55] [^120] [^121] [^122] [^123]. Butyrate also acts via changes in the cellular metabolism of cancer cells, which mainly utilize glucose instead of butyrate for their energy needs (Warburg effect). This leads to the accumulation of larger concentrations of butyrate in the nucleus of cancer cells compared with normal colonocytes, inhibiting HDACs, which leads to the inhibition of cellular proliferation and promotion of apoptosis. This is described as the ‘butyrate paradox’, based on the opposing role of butyrate in cell proliferation between normal and cancerous cells [^48] [^124]. Recently, expression patterns of butyrate metabolism-related genes have been studied in tissue samples of CRC, and 18 such genes, including 4 high-risk genes (*FN1, SERPINE1, THBS2* and *COMP*), were identified to develop a prognostic model that could predict survival rate and the response of these patients to chemotherapy and immunotherapy [^125].

#### Food allergy

Food allergy encompasses adverse immune responses to specific food antigens, which can result in a spectrum of illnesses and even death in severe cases [^126] [^127]. The role of the gut microbiota is evidenced by the fact that germ-free mice cannot achieve immune tolerance to food antigens [^128]. In murine allergic models, changes in gut microbial composition have enhanced food allergen sensitization to potential food allergens such as peanuts and ovalbumin [^128] [^129], which in turn have been prevented by dietary interventions to propagate SCFA production [^130]. Several cross-sectional human studies and systematic reviews have also suggested a SCFAs-mediated link between food allergy and the gut microbiota [^94] [^131] [^132].

### Extraintestinal diseases

In the presence of an intact gut barrier, SCFAs have a role in systemic health, with physiological absorption of SCFAs into the circulation. The underlying mechanistic pathways are relatively poorly understood.

#### Extraintestinal cancers

The beneficial effects of butyrate, in particular, have been demonstrated in several extraintestinal cancers in the cervix, urinary bladder, breast, liver and lung [^133]. SCFAs are believed to influence cancer progression not only by affecting the tumour microenvironment but also by modulating signalling pathways in immune and cancer cells [^134]. Butyrate has been proven to alleviate the side effects of conventional chemotherapeutic agents, enhance the bioavailability of cancer drugs and augment the efficacy of both chemotherapy and immunotherapy. Additionally, faecal butyrate has been found to be an independent predictor of the therapeutic response with immunotherapy in a wide variety of cancers. These beneficial effects have been summarized in a recent review [^135].

#### Cardiovascular and metabolic disease, energy metabolism and obesity

The relationship among the gut microbiota, dietary fibre and SCFAs in metabolic and cardiovascular health has been well reviewed [^136]. Systematic reviews have demonstrated an association of dietary fibre intake with a lower incidence of coronary artery disease and associated mortality [^114] as well as a reduction in both systolic and diastolic blood pressures, although the exact role of SCFAs was not ascertained [^137]. Murine studies have also confirmed the beneficial role of a high-fibre diet and SCFAs, showing a reduction in systolic and diastolic blood pressures, cardiac index and cardiac function [^138] [^139]. In humans, however, butyrate supplementation has been reported to both reduce and increase blood pressure [^140] [^141]. A small, randomized placebo-controlled trial with oral butyrate also showed a rise in both systolic and diastolic blood pressures [^142]. With respect to atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, SCFA-producing bacteria (including *R. intestinalis* and *F. prausnitzii*) were less abundant in patients compared with healthy controls in a large cohort study [^143]. Propionate has been postulated to have parasympathetic effects, and this action has proved beneficial as an autonomic rebalancing strategy after myocardial infarction in a mouse model [^144]. Butyrate administration has also been found to improve cardiac function and prevent ventricular arrythmia in a similar mouse model of myocardial infarction [^145].

Both oral and colonic delivery of SCFAs has been found to impact dietary intake, fatty acid oxidation and energy expenditure in human subjects and to modulate metabolic health [^146] [^147] (Table [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Tab2)). A meta-analysis of SCFAs as anti-inflammatory agents in obesity noted a significant decrease in markers of systemic inflammation [^148]. Oral butyrate has been tried with little success in metabolic syndrome. Butyrate conferred benefits on glucose metabolism in lean individuals but not in subjects with metabolic syndrome, possibly due to altered SCFA handling in subjects with insulin resistance [^149]. This was confirmed in a randomized placebo-controlled trial of oral butyrate in individuals with type 2 diabetes, which showed no improvement in metabolic parameters, blood pressure or oxidative stress indices [^150]. Another randomized trial with individuals presenting type 1 diabetes showed that oral butyrate did not affect innate or adaptive immunity in these patients either [^151]. However, butyrate showed a significantly higher rate of decrease in body mass index in children with obesity than those on placebo, which suggests that this may be an effective treatment of paediatric obesity [^152]. SCFAs have also been postulated to have satiety-enhancing properties, which could be the modality of action [^153].

#### Respiratory diseases

The ‘hygiene hypothesis’ suggests that exposure to microorganisms in early childhood prevents the development of allergic disorders in adulthood [^154], and SCFAs may be involved in the underlying mechanisms. The important role of SCFAs in allergic asthma has been shown in neonatal mouse models, in which mice fed SCFAs had less severe allergic airway disease with attenuated airway hyperresponsiveness and reduced infiltration of eosinophils [^155]. Treatment of mice with vancomycin that depleted butyrate-producing bacteria was found to exacerbate allergic airway disease, but subsequent oral administration of SCFAs reversed this effect [^156] [^157]. Despite these murine studies, their role in humans is less clear. The PASTURE study group analysed faecal SCFA samples from around 300 children in a birth cohort and found that children with the highest butyrate levels were less likely to have childhood asthma [^155] [^158]. A recent systematic review that included observational and interventional studies showed that faecal acetate, propionate and butyrate concentrations in the first few years of life had a protective effect against the development of childhood asthma [^132]. The role of the gut microbiota and SCFAs in other respiratory diseases currently does not have a robust evidence base and is reviewed elsewhere [^159].

#### Neurological diseases

The role of SCFAs in neurological diseases is believed to be facilitated via their passage through the blood–brain barrier and mediated via GPCRs, HDAC inhibition, neurohumoral and immune pathways [^160]. A meta-analysis found alterations in the gut microbiota in Alzheimer disease, suggesting an associative role of decreased SCFA production in its pathogenesis [^161]. SCFAs have been postulated to have a beneficial effect in Alzheimer disease via gene expression regulation of Alzheimer disease-related genes, maintenance of blood–brain barrier integrity, reduced neuroinflammation and interference with brain amyloid production, as demonstrated in several murine studies [^162] [^163]. The gut microbiota and SCFAs have also recently been associated with Parkinson disease [^164] [^165]. Butyrate and propionate supplementation has been shown to exert a protective effect against Parkinson disease development in mice [^166] [^167]. SCFAs may also have a role in multiple sclerosis, which is an autoimmune neuroinflammatory disease characterized by demyelination and axonal damage of the central nervous system. Gut dysbiosis and reduced faecal SCFAs have been documented in a cohort of patients with multiple sclerosis [^168]. Plasma and faecal propionate levels were low in patients with multiple sclerosis and supplementation of oral propionate appeared to be a potent immunomodulatory agent in addition to traditional drugs in naive patients with multiple sclerosis [^169]. In a mouse model of experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis, oral supplementation with SCFAs also improved outcomes, with both butyrate and acetate supplementation reducing demyelination and altering neural lipid content [^170] [^171] [^172].

### Therapeutic applications of short-chain fatty acids

The emerging understanding of the role of SCFAs in health and disease has paved the way for potential therapeutic interventions, and the different approaches are summarized in Fig. [4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Fig4). The twin challenges of drug delivery involve ensuring that SCFAs reach specific regions in the gut and have a surrogate measure to confirm their successful conveyance. Poor access to the proximal colon, where endogenous SCFAs are maximally produced [^173], and the portal vein, which is the main transport blood vessel, makes both assessments very challenging.

Fig. 4: SCFA modulation strategies.

SCFAs can be administered directly via rectal enemas or oral tablets (Table [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41579-025-01183-w#Tab2)). Rectal infusion of butyrate led to a rapid increase in portal and hepatic venous concentrations in a surgical study, confirming the efficacy of this approach [^174]. Another study showed that distal infusion of acetate was more efficacious than proximal delivery [^175]. However, oral delivery of tributyrin showed a large degree of interpatient variability in the absorption of the drug [^176]. Microencapsulation and enteric-coated versions of butyrate led to a much better delivery and may be the preferred option [^177] [^178]. Other delivery vehicles for SCFAs include acylated compounds. High-amylose maize starch acting as an SCFA carrier showed significant increases of the respective SCFAs in the distal colon in a mouse model [^179]. Both acetylated and butyrylated starch was used as vehicles with good effect to prevent type 1 diabetes in a mouse model [^180]. Butyrate release was also demonstrated in the human colon, and the consumption of butyrylated starch prevented the formation of *O* <sup>6</sup> -methyl-2-deoxyguanosine DNA adducts on a high red meat diet, thus likely reducing the risk of CRC development [^181] [^182]. The potential physiological and microbiological impacts of the reduction of pH owing to SCFA delivery to the colon have not been fully elucidated.

SCFA production can also be stimulated in the gut by consuming a fibre-rich diet and/or prebiotics [^183]. A recent meta-analysis, which looked at 42 randomized controlled human trials, reported that the SCFA profile was not only influenced by type, structure and dose of dietary fibre but also by individual characteristics that included gut microbiota composition [^118]. Rapid increases in serum levels of SCFAs have been noted with ingestion of highly fermentable fibres such as inulin and guar gum [^184] [^185]. The use of probiotics (live organisms) is another therapeutic avenue, and a recent review summarized their effect on SCFA generation and their impact on intestinal health [^186]. Faecal microbiota transplantation is a more shotgun approach to change the host microbial composition by transferring a donor’s faecal microbiota, with the benefits possibly mediated through the effects of enhanced SCFA production [^187] [^188].

Understanding the key SCFA synthesis genes has also opened new avenues for genetic engineering by providing new targets to create ‘designer’ symbiotic bacteria [^189]. A cocktail of beneficial SCFA-producing bacteria can also be utilized as a multispecies consortium to benefit the host [^190]. Taken together, these applications have laid the foundation for exploring emerging and potential future approaches that include advanced delivery systems for SCFAs, dietary strategies and targeted supplements, culminating in personalized medicine strategies that will tailor SCFA-based therapies to individual microbiome profiles.

## Conclusions and outlook

SCFAs are important mediators of health effects exerted by the gut microbiota. We have a good understanding of which microorganisms are responsible for the production of different SCFAs, owing in part to (meta)genome-based analyses; however, deciphering microbial community interactions and the influence of environmental conditions are much more challenging. Many traits that dictate the activity of individual microorganisms within their ecosystem, such as pH tolerance, gene regulation or transporter specificity, cannot easily be deducted from genome information and require further research, as do microbial interactions during complex whole plant-based insoluble dietary fibre fermentation and the importance of different nutrient niches in the gut [^191]. This is exemplified by a recent study, which demonstrated that faecal particle size was counterintuitively not dependent on chewing efficiency but on microbiome characteristics, with a larger particle size being associated with higher faecal SCFA concentrations [^192]. Synthetic consortia are increasingly being used as model systems to reveal the microbial metabolic interactions within the microbiota [^193].

There is overwhelming evidence that SCFAs influence many aspects of host health, and much progress has been made in recent years in deciphering the underlying molecular mechanisms of action, which include energy provision, interaction with host receptors and epigenetic gene regulation. However, much remains to be explored for a fuller understanding of the complex interplay of SCFAs with their multiple molecular pathways of action. For example, the overlapping actions of different GPCRs are not fully understood and require further study in model systems, including the use of knockout or humanized animals and synthetic receptor ligands [^39]. SCFAs have been implicated in many different aspects of host physiology, including gut motility, strengthening of the physical gut barrier, numerous immune interactions and the prevention of diseases including cancer, inflammatory, cardiovascular, metabolic and neurological diseases. However, the underlying mechanisms often remain obscure, and it is important not to confuse association with causation. Owing to the inaccessibility of the system and the complexity of host and microbiota, human studies need to be complemented with in vivo, ex vivo and in vitro model systems to further our understanding. We should also be aware that much of the work to date is in the context of Westernized diets with a much lower fibre content than more traditional diets, which affects gut microbiota and SCFA output [^194] [^195] [^196] [^197] [^198].
