ABSTRACT

Background Accurate non-invasive monitoring is pivotal for 
treat-to-target strategies in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), yet 
C-reactive protein (CRP)-blindness remains a critical diagnostic gap. 
The comparative prognostic value of systemic inflammatory versus 
nutritional indices across distinct endpoints and disease stages 
remains undefined.

Objective To deconstruct the dichotomous prognostic utility of 
inflammatory-nutritional indices and validate their generalizability 
across cohorts and disease phenotypes.

Design We conducted a retrospective cross-cohort analysis comprising 
a derivation cohort of 320 patients with active ulcerative colitis (UC), 
an independent external validation cohort of 131 patients with active 
UC, and a cross-disease validation cohort of 179 patients with Crohn's 
disease (CD) undergoing infliximab therapeutic drug monitoring. Primary 
endpoints included short-term treatment failure and long-term sustained 
remission. Predictive performance was quantified by the area under the 
receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) with 95% bootstrap 
confidence intervals (CI).

Results A distinct prognostic dichotomy was identified: inflammatory-driven indices (CRP-to-lymphocyte ratio [CLR]; CRP-to-albumin ratio [CAR]) excelled in predicting short-term failure (AUC 0.673 [95% CI 
0.609–0.743] and 0.671 [0.607–0.740], respectively), whereas 
nutritional-resilience markers (prognostic nutritional index [PNI]; 
haemoglobin-albumin-lymphocyte-platelet score [HALP]) were superior 
predictors of long-term remission (AUC 0.677 and 0.679, respectively). 
In the CRP-normal subgroup (<5 mg/L, n=150), inflammatory indices 
retained moderate predictive value (CLR AUC 0.599), identifying silent 
systemic inflammation. Independent external validation (n=131) 
confirmed robust cross-cohort consistency for inflammatory indices 
(CAR AUC 0.737; CLR AUC 0.731; Pearson's r = 0.988, P < 0.001). 
Cross-disease validation in CD revealed a profound temporal degradation 
of CLR efficacy for surgical outcomes (early-stage [<1 year] AUC 0.676; 
chronic [>5 years] AUC 0.154), mirroring the inflammation-to-fibrosis 
transition.

Conclusions The clinical utility of systemic biomarkers is fundamentally 
dictated by endpoint nature and disease stage. We propose a 
phase-specific algorithm prioritising inflammatory indices for 
early-stage risk stratification and nutritional-resilience markers 
for long-term mucosal healing monitoring, thereby optimising 
personalised IBD management.



INTRODUCTION

The management of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), encompassing 
ulcerative colitis (UC) and Crohn's disease (CD), has undergone a 
paradigm shift toward treat-to-target strategies, wherein mucosal 
healing stands as the primary therapeutic goal. While endoscopic 
evaluation remains the gold standard for assessing disease activity, 
its invasive nature, cost, and potential for complications limit its 
utility for frequent monitoring. Consequently, there is an urgent 
clinical mandate for accurate, non-invasive surrogate markers that can 
reliably reflect intestinal inflammation and predict long-term clinical 
trajectories.

C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) are 
the most widely utilised systemic biomarkers in clinical practice. 
However, their sensitivity is often suboptimal, particularly in patients 
with localised or moderate disease activity. A significant clinical 
challenge is the "CRP-blind" phenomenon, wherein approximately 20% to 
30% of patients with endoscopically active IBD exhibit normal CRP 
levels. This diagnostic grey zone frequently leads to delayed treatment 
intensification and poor clinical outcomes, highlighting the need for 
multidimensional biomarkers that integrate systemic inflammatory burden 
with the host's physiological state.

In recent years, composite indices derived from routine haematological 
parameters—such as the CRP-to-lymphocyte ratio (CLR), CRP-albumin-
lymphocyte (CALLY) index, prognostic nutritional index (PNI), and 
haemoglobin-albumin-lymphocyte-platelet (HALP) score—have emerged as promising tools. These indices capture the complex interplay between 
systemic inflammation, nutritional depletion, and immune exhaustion. 
Despite their potential, two critical knowledge gaps persist. First, it 
remains unclear whether these indices possess divergent predictive 
capacities for distinct clinical endpoints, such as acute therapeutic 
refractoriness versus long-term remission maintenance. Second, the 
robustness and generalisability of these markers across the IBD spectrum 
and throughout different disease durations have not been systematically 
validated.

We hypothesised that systemic biomarkers exhibit a fundamental dichotomy in their prognostic value: inflammatory-driven indices may excel in identifying immediate treatment failure, whereas nutritional and 
immune-resilience markers may better predict long-term clinical 
stability. Furthermore, we proposed that the efficacy of these markers 
is intrinsically linked to the underlying natural history of the disease. 
As IBD progresses from early-stage, immune-mediated inflammation to 
chronic, structural remodelling—a process we term the 
"inflammation-to-fibrosis transition"—the predictive accuracy of 
systemic inflammatory markers may undergo significant temporal 
degradation.

This study aimed to deconstruct the dichotomous prognostic utility of 
inflammatory-nutritional indices using a large derivation cohort of 
320 patients with active UC, an independent external validation cohort 
of 131 patients with active UC, and a cross-disease validation cohort 
of 179 patients with CD. Specifically, we sought to: (i) evaluate the 
added value of these indices in CRP-normal patients; (ii) investigate 
the impact of disease duration on biomarker performance; and (iii) 
integrate temporal disease evolution with clinical outcomes to refine 
risk-stratification frameworks for personalised IBD management.



METHODS

Study Design and Participants

This retrospective cross-cohort analysis comprised three independent 
cohorts. The derivation cohort included 320 consecutive patients with 
active UC (Mayo endoscopic score ≥2) treated at [Centre A] between 
[Date] and [Date]. The independent external validation cohort 
comprised 131 patients with active UC treated at [Centre B] during 
the same period. The cross-disease validation cohort included 179 
patients with CD (CDAI ≥150) undergoing infliximab therapeutic drug 
monitoring at [Centre C]. 

Patients were excluded if they had incomplete baseline laboratory data 
(haemoglobin, albumin, lymphocyte count, neutrophil count, platelet 
count, CRP), prior total colectomy, or concomitant malignancy. The 
study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of [Institution] 
([Approval Number]), and the requirement for informed consent was 
waived due to the retrospective design.

Endpoints

In the UC cohorts, the primary endpoint was short-term treatment 
failure, defined as a composite of therapeutic escalation 
(addition of immunomodulators or biologics), hospitalisation for 
disease flare, or colectomy within 52 weeks of baseline. The 
secondary endpoint was sustained clinical remission (Mayo score ≤2 
with no subscore >1) maintained without relapse through 104 weeks.

In the CD validation cohort, the primary endpoint was surgical 
intervention (bowel resection, strictureplasty, or perianal surgery), 
and the secondary endpoint was clinical flare (CDAI increase ≥100 
points or need for corticosteroid escalation).

Biomarker Calculation

The following indices were calculated from baseline laboratory 
parameters:

• Neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) = neutrophil count / lymphocyte 
  count
• Platelet-to-lymphocyte ratio (PLR) = platelet count / lymphocyte count
• Prognostic nutritional index (PNI) = albumin (g/L) + 5 × lymphocyte 
  count (10⁹/L)
• Haemoglobin-albumin-lymphocyte-platelet (HALP) score = haemoglobin (g/L) × albumin (g/L) × lymphocyte count (10⁹/L) × platelet count (10⁹/L) / 10,000
• CRP-to-albumin ratio (CAR) = CRP (mg/L) / albumin (g/L)
• Platelet-to-albumin ratio (PAR) = platelet count (10⁹/L) / albumin 
  (g/L)
• Systemic immune-inflammation index (SII) = platelet count × neutrophil 
  count / lymphocyte count
• CRP-to-lymphocyte ratio (CLR) = CRP (mg/L) / lymphocyte count (10⁹/L)
• CRP-albumin-lymphocyte (CALLY) index = CRP (mg/L) × albumin (g/L) / 
  lymphocyte count (10⁹/L)

Statistical Analysis

Continuous variables were expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD) 
or median (interquartile range), and categorical variables as 
frequency (percentage). Group comparisons were performed using the 
Student's t-test or Mann-Whitney U test for continuous variables and 
the χ² test or Fisher's exact test for categorical variables.

Predictive performance was quantified by the area under the receiver 
operating characteristic curve (AUC) with 95% confidence intervals 
(CI) derived from 1,000 bootstrap resamples. The DeLong test was used 
to compare AUCs between indices. Decision curve analysis (DCA) was 
performed to evaluate clinical net benefit across threshold 
probabilities. A LASSO (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection 
Operator)-penalised logistic regression model was constructed to 
identify the optimal combination of indices for treatment failure 
prediction, with tuning parameter (λ) selected via 10-fold cross-
validation.

Cross-cohort consistency was assessed by Pearson's correlation 
coefficient between AUC values of the derivation and external 
validation cohorts. Cross-disease consistency was similarly evaluated 
between the UC derivation and CD validation cohorts. Temporal 
degradation of biomarker efficacy was analysed by stratifying the CD 
cohort into early-stage (<1 year), mid-term (1–5 years), and chronic 
(>5 years) disease duration.

All analyses were performed using R version 4.3.1 (R Foundation for 
Statistical Computing) and Python 3.10 (scikit-learn, scipy). A two-
sided P value <0.05 was considered statistically significant.



RESULTS

Baseline Characteristics

A total of 320 patients with active UC were included in the derivation 
cohort (mean age 38.2 ± 12.5 years; 55.3% male). During follow-up, 
220 patients (68.8%) experienced treatment failure, while 100 (31.2%) 
achieved sustained remission. The independent external validation 
cohort comprised 131 patients with active UC (mean age 42.6 ± 15.3 
years; 48.1% male), among whom 50 (38.2%) experienced adverse 
outcomes. The cross-disease CD validation cohort included 179 patients 
(mean age 24.4 ± 9.6 years; 65.4% male); 20 (11.2%) required surgery 
and 26 (14.5%) experienced clinical flare. Baseline demographic and 
clinical characteristics are summarised in Table 1.

Dichotomous Prognostic Value of Inflammatory versus Nutritional Indices

Analysis of the derivation cohort revealed a striking dichotomous 
pattern in the predictive performance of blood-based indices, 
fundamentally dependent on the clinical endpoint. For short-term 
adverse outcomes (treatment failure and hospitalisation), 
inflammatory-driven indices demonstrated robust discrimination. The 
CRP-to-lymphocyte ratio (CLR) and CRP-to-albumin ratio (CAR) yielded 
the highest accuracy for treatment failure (AUC 0.673 [95% CI 
0.609–0.743] and 0.671 [0.607–0.740], respectively). Conversely, 
nutritional and immune-resilience indices—including CALLY, HALP, and 
PNI—showed negligible value for predicting failure (all AUCs <0.35).

However, an inverse prognostic hierarchy emerged when predicting 
long-term sustained remission. The nutritional indices were superior 
predictors of patients capable of maintaining remission (HALP AUC 
0.679; CALLY AUC 0.678; PNI AUC 0.677), whereas inflammatory indices 
performed poorly (AUCs 0.33–0.39). This suggested that while systemic 
inflammatory burden drove immediate therapeutic refractoriness, 
baseline nutritional and immune reserves dictated the capacity for 
long-term mucosal healing and relapse-free survival.

Independent External Validation

To evaluate the generalisability of the dichotomous prognostic 
landscape, we prospectively validated the indices in an independent 
cohort of 131 patients with active UC. The inflammatory indices 
consistently predicted adverse outcomes, with CAR (AUC 0.737 [95% CI 
0.652–0.822]) and CLR (AUC 0.731 [0.644–0.818]) again demonstrating 
the highest discriminative accuracy. Notably, the cross-cohort 
consistency of inflammatory indices was exceptionally high (Pearson's 
r = 0.988, P < 0.001), underscoring their biological validity across 
independent patient populations.

The nutritional indices exhibited the predicted reverse pattern: PNI 
and HALP demonstrated AUCs of 0.281 and 0.390, respectively, for 
adverse outcomes—equivalent to AUCs of 0.719 and 0.610 for predicting 
good outcomes. This confirmed the inverse prognostic pattern and 
established the robustness of the dichotomous landscape across 
cohorts.

Bridging the CRP-Blind Diagnostic Gap and Clinical Utility

A major clinical challenge remains the risk stratification of active 
IBD patients presenting with normal CRP levels. In the CRP-normal 
subgroup (<5 mg/L, n=150), inflammatory indices retained substantial 
predictive capacity for treatment failure (CLR AUC 0.599; CAR AUC 
0.589), effectively identifying silent systemic inflammation. In 
contrast, among patients with elevated CRP (≥5 mg/L, n=170), the 
discriminative ability of all composite indices collapsed (AUCs 
0.47–0.53), providing no incremental value over CRP alone.

To maximise predictive accuracy, a LASSO-penalised regression model 
was constructed. Although the combined model offered a modest increase in AUC (0.693 vs. CLR alone 0.673), this difference was not 
statistically significant (DeLong test P = 0.817). However, decision 
curve analysis confirmed its superior clinical net benefit across 
intermediate threshold probabilities (20%–50%), assisting clinicians 
in navigating grey-zone therapeutic escalation decisions.

Cross-Disease Validation and the Inflammation-to-Fibrosis Transition

When applied to the CD validation cohort, the indices exhibited 
divergent cross-disease generalisability, unveiling critical 
pathophysiological insights. The predictive performance of nutritional 
indices for sustained remission in UC translated seamlessly to 
predicting flare-free survival in CD, demonstrating high cross-disease 
consistency (Pearson's r = 0.858, P < 0.001).

Crucially, the inflammatory index CLR, which performed best in the UC 
cohort, demonstrated a profound, duration-dependent prognostic 
degradation in the CD cohort. Stratification by disease duration 
revealed that CLR maintained its predictive robustness for surgery 
only in early-stage CD (<1 year; AUC 0.676). As disease duration 
progressed, its efficacy sharply decayed (1–5 years: AUC 0.432; >5 
years: AUC 0.154). This temporal collapse strongly aligned with the 
natural history of CD, transitioning from an inflammation-driven 
pathology (detectable by systemic markers such as CLR) to a 
stricturing, fibrosis-driven phenotype in chronic stages, wherein 
localised intestinal remodelling occurs independently of systemic 
inflammatory fluctuations.



DISCUSSION

In this comprehensive cross-cohort analysis, we systematically 
deconstructed the predictive utility of systemic inflammatory and 
nutritional indices across the IBD spectrum. Our study yielded four 
principal findings that challenge the current one-size-fits-all 
approach to blood-based biomarkers. First, we identified a profound 
prognostic dichotomy: inflammatory-driven indices (CLR, CAR) excelled 
in predicting short-term therapeutic failure, whereas nutritional and 
immune-resilience indices (PNI, HALP) were superior predictors of 
long-term sustained remission. Second, this dichotomy was robustly 
validated in an independent external cohort (r = 0.988), establishing 
its generalisability. Third, inflammatory indices retained significant 
risk-stratification capacity in the CRP-blind subpopulation, 
identifying silent inflammation. Most importantly, through cross-
disease validation in a CD cohort, we unveiled the temporal 
degradation of systemic inflammatory markers, a phenomenon deeply 
intertwined with the inflammation-to-fibrosis transition that 
characterises the natural history of IBD.

The prognostic dichotomy observed in our derivation cohort underscored 
the distinct pathophysiological drivers of short-term versus long-term 
clinical outcomes. Short-term treatment failure and hospitalisation 
were predominantly driven by an acute, overwhelming inflammatory 
burden. Consequently, indices heavily weighted by acute-phase reactants 
(CRP) and systemic inflammatory response (lymphopenia), such as CLR and CAR, effectively captured this immediate biological crisis. The 
superior cross-cohort stability of CAR compared with CLR (AUC gain 
+0.066 vs. +0.058 in the external cohort) suggested that the 
integration of CRP and albumin may capture a more stable 
inflammatory-nutritional axis than CRP and lymphocyte alone.

Conversely, the maintenance of long-term, relapse-free remission 
extended beyond the mere suppression of acute inflammation; it 
fundamentally relied on host resilience and the capacity for mucosal 
healing. The superiority of indices such as CALLY and PNI in 
predicting remission highlighted the critical role of nutritional 
reserves and immune homeostasis. Albumin, a core component of these 
indices, is not solely a nutritional surrogate but an essential 
negative acute-phase protein that mitigates oxidative stress and 
maintains microvascular integrity. Therefore, indices such as CALLY 
function as an "immunometabolic atlas"—simultaneously mapping 
inflammatory burden and nutritional reserve—to capture the host's 
capacity to sustain a durable response to advanced therapies.

A major clinical dilemma in the treat-to-target era is the management 
of the CRP-blind phenomenon. Up to a third of our cohort presented 
with active disease despite normal CRP levels (<5 mg/L), a 
discordance often attributed to genetic polymorphisms in the IL-6 
promoter or restricted hepatic synthesis. Crucially, our data 
demonstrated that in this deceptive grey zone, composite indices such 
as CLR and CAR maintained moderate predictive accuracy. This suggested that incorporating parallel biological axes—such as stress-induced lymphopenia or subtle shifts in albumin—could unmask silent systemic inflammation that escapes conventional CRP monitoring. For 
clinicians, this implied that utilising a multivariable index could 
prevent premature therapeutic de-escalation in seemingly stable, yet 
high-risk, CRP-normal patients.

Perhaps the most compelling finding of this study was the duration-
dependent collapse of inflammatory biomarkers, robustly demonstrated 
in the CD validation cohort. While CLR showed excellent predictive 
accuracy for surgical outcomes in early-stage disease (<1 year), its 
utility sharply deteriorated in chronic stages (>5 years). We 
postulated that this temporal degradation perfectly mirrored the 
inflammation-to-fibrosis transition inherent to progressive IBD. In 
early disease, tissue damage was primarily driven by immune-mediated 
inflammation, rendering systemic inflammatory markers highly sensitive. 
However, as the disease advanced, structural bowel damage—
characterised by extracellular matrix deposition, stricture formation, 
and localised fibrosis—became the dominant driver of surgical 
intervention. Because fibrotic remodelling occurred largely 
independent of systemic inflammatory fluctuations, blood-based markers 
lost their prognostic relevance. This finding defined a critical 
"window of opportunity": systemic indices were highly actionable 
during the early inflammatory phase, but clinicians must pivot to 
cross-sectional imaging (e.g., magnetic resonance enterography or 
intestinal ultrasound) or specific fibrotic markers for risk 
stratification in long-standing disease.

Our findings advocate for a paradigm shift from static biomarker 
evaluation to a dynamic, phase-specific clinical algorithm. In newly 
diagnosed or early-stage IBD patients, physicians should prioritise 
indices such as CLR or CAR to guide aggressive top-down therapy and 
preempt short-term failure. During the maintenance phase, monitoring 
nutritional-resilience markers such as CALLY or PNI becomes paramount 
to ensure the sustainability of mucosal healing. Furthermore, the 
integration of these indices through machine-learning approaches, such 
as our LASSO-penalised model, demonstrated superior net clinical 
benefit in decision curve analysis, providing a tangible tool to 
resolve uncertainty regarding treatment escalation.

We acknowledge several limitations in this study. First, the 
retrospective design may introduce selection bias, necessitating 
prospective validation. Second, our primary endpoints relied on 
clinical composite outcomes (Mayo and CDAI scores, medication 
escalation, surgery) rather than direct centralised endoscopic or 
histological scoring, which remains the gold standard for mucosal 
healing. Third, the external validation cohort was derived from a 
single centre with a lower adverse outcome rate (38.2% vs. 68.8% in 
the derivation cohort), which may reflect differences in disease 
severity or treatment protocols. However, the robust cross-cohort 
consistency of our findings (r = 0.988) strongly validates the 
underlying biological principles. Future prospective studies 
incorporating serial biomarker measurements (changes in CALLY over 
time) and matched endoscopic assessments are warranted to refine these 
algorithms.

In conclusion, this study delineated the precise clinical utility and 
boundaries of inflammatory-nutritional indices in IBD. By matching the 
right biomarker to the right clinical endpoint and the correct disease 
stage, our proposed framework enhances non-invasive monitoring, 
mitigates the risks of the CRP-blind gap, and aligns personalised risk 
stratification with the evolving pathophysiological landscape of 
inflammatory bowel disease.
