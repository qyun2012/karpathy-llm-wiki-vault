## Background and Context

Immune suppression with biologics is the recommended treatment for Crohn’s disease (CD); however, patients experience flares of disease despite treatment.

## New Findings

Perturbations in dietary, ileal, microbial, and metabolic signatures persist during CD remission despite effective advanced immune-targeted treatments.

## Limitations

The limitations of this study were the descriptive nature of analyzing complex human interactions and the inability to fully replicate this complexity in more functional contexts.

## Clinical Research Relevance

This work emphasizes the need to consider the inclusion of adjunctive interventions in therapeutic strategies that target the diet-epithelia-microbial axis, alongside immune suppression. These interventions might involve dietary modifications and the use of metabolites to help shift CD signals toward a healthier state, promoting deeper and longer-lasting remission.

## Basic Research Relevance

This work suggests new targets and potential interventions to address the altered diet-epithelia-microbial axis, which needs to be further explored in specified preclinical models as potential adjunct future treatments.

Population-based data indicate that Crohn’s disease (CD) incidence and prevalence continue to increase. This increase is attributed to industrialization, urbanization, and Westernized dietary exposures. As the number of CD patients living with these chronic conditions increases, strategies for managing patients in longer-lasting remission states are needed to reduce disabilities, lower care costs, and enhance overall outcomes. Biologic therapy, aimed at immune suppression, is considered the standard of care, but even with these effective therapies, the disease is not cured, and patients experience flares. Lifestyle and dietary modifications have been suggested to reduce CD incidence and to improve the overall CD course. Characterizations of signals that persist despite treatment may direct adjunct strategies toward an enhanced, healthier CD state.

Extensive molecular omics research has characterized treatment-naïve and active CD populations,<sup>,</sup> but less effort has been directed toward profiling CD patients in remission. Confocal laser endomicroscopy during remission suggests epithelial dysfunction. We have previously shown that microbial alterations persist, independent of mucosal inflammation, in ileal biopsy specimens of patients with treatment-naïve L2 colonic involvement. We now aimed to characterize ongoing gut mucosal signals, immune responses, and dietary habits in patients with CD as part of the CORE-CD (Capsule & Omics for pRedicting Exacerbation of Crohn’s disease) study, who were recruited while already on stable therapy for at least 3 months, and most were receiving biologic treatments. Patients underwent clinical assessment, colonoscopy, video capsule endoscopy (VCE), biomarker evaluation, dietary intake examination, ileal transcriptomics, microbiomics, and metabolomics phenotyping. Patients in remission were compared with controls without CD, serving as our reference for the desired healthy signals. We contrast these findings with signals from active, predominantly treatment-naïve CD patients, representing the other reference extreme. Our goal is to uncover signatures that still deviate from the healthy state during CD remission despite immune suppression treatments, thereby directing improved adjunctive CD management.

## Methods

### Study Design

A detailed description of the Methods is in the. The ethics committees at Sheba Medical Center approved the study. Written informed consent was obtained. Patients with CD in the remission group from the CORE-CD cohort were in continuous corticosteroid-free clinical remission (Crohn Disease Activity Index \[CDAI\] <150) for at least 3 months before enrollment and on a stable medication dosing as in previous designed cohorts.<sup>,</sup> Non–inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) controls were subjects who were investigated for various gastrointestinal symptoms, including abdominal pain, or had screening colonoscopies, but had normal endoscopic and histologic findings. Part of the newly diagnosed active CD and control subjects (56 of 191, indicated in ) were previously discussed. Subjects with severe comorbidities such as liver, kidney, neurologic, metabolic, or cardio-respiratory disorders were excluded. VCE (PILLCAM Crohn's, Medtronic) was performed as part of the CORE study in the group in remission, and the highest Lewis score (LS) was captured. Endoscopic evaluations included the simple endoscopic score (SES)-CD and Rutgeerts score (RS) when applicable. Fecal samples were frozen immediately at −80°C. Ileal biopsy specimens were gathered during colonoscopy, stored in RNAlater, and frozen at −80°C.

The per-protocol follow-up of the CORE-CD patients is still ongoing till completion of 24 months of follow-up, but here we used the baseline samples and censored and stratified the patients based on the available outcome at 6 months. The primary outcome was the occurrence of a clinical flare, defined as an increase of >70 points on CDAI from baseline and a CDAI >150, or a need for hospitalization/surgery/rescue medications for worsening CD. Remission or flare was defined during routine, planned clinical visits that occurred roughly every 3 months. We used either this outcome alone or together with an increase of fecal calprotectin (FCP) >300 at 6 months compared with baseline.

### Dietary Questionnaires

A comprehensive food frequency questionnaire (FFQ) was uniformly applied,<sup>,</sup> computing the daily macro and micronutrients, and main food item servings. Dietary data were normalized to 1000 kcal caloric intake. Adherence to the Mediterranean diet was assessed using the Mediterranean Diet Adherence Screener (MEDAS) score, ranging from 0–14, with higher scores indicating better adherence. NOVA classification key was applied, whereby class 4 indicates ultraprocessed food and drink products. We report on the rate of class 4 items out of the total consumed items. Principal component analysis was performed to summarize FFQ variation using R prcomp function with data scaled and centered. The top 10 largest combined absolute scaled loadings were overlaid as arrows to visualize the contributions of original variables to the principal components. Heatmap was generated using the R heatmap.3 function. Data, representing *z* scores of FFQ components, were hierarchically clustered using Euclidean distance and ward.D method for both rows and columns. Multivariate Association with Linear Models (MaAsLin2, R package 1.8.0) was used to identify differentially abundant metabolites (false discovery rate \[FDR\] ≤0.25) between CD in remission, active CD, and controls, controlling for age and gender.

### 16S seq Microbiome

Fecal V4 16S (515F/806R) sequencing was conducted,,, using Illumina MiSeq, and processed in QIIME 2 v.2023.7,<sup>,</sup> with amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) detection using Deblur, and taxonomy was assigned using the Greengenes2 (2022.10). Each unique ASV was assigned a specific identification number, with corresponding sequences provided in and relevant supplementary datasets. CD dysbiosis index was calculated based on Gevers et al. Faith’s phylogenetic diversity was used as a measure of α diversity, after rarefication to 4000 reads. Per-sample health index was calculated as previously described, calculating the log of the ratio of health- to disease-associated bacteria, with higher values indicating a better health-associated microbiome. A similar approach was used to define an IBD index for each sample, using ASVs that were significantly increased or decreased in IBD samples in the same study.

### Fecal Metabolomics Profiling

Fecal metabolites were extracted with methanol and water (3:1) and profiling as previously described. Dionex Ultimate ultra–high-performance liquid chromatography system coupled to Orbitrap Q-Exactive Mass Spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used with electrospray ionization with polarity switching mode. Thermo Xcalibur 4.1 was used for data acquisition. Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) metabolites peak areas were determined using Mzmine2.53, analyzing an in-house library of 545 metabolites built by running commercially available standards, with a matching HMBD ID. Total Sum Scaling normalization was applied per sample after exclusion of samples with sum peak area <1E9 () and excluding 3 metabolites higher in blank. MaAsLin2 R package 1.8.0 was used to identify differentially abundant metabolites (FDR ≤0.25) between CD in remission and controls, controlling for age and gender. These differentially abundant metabolites were compared with those that showed similar differences between the CD active and control groups, previously obtained from the SOURCE (Study of Urban and Rural Crohn disease Evolution) cohort.

### Ileal Transcriptomics

Terminal ileum (TI) biopsy specimen RNA was isolated using the Qiagen AllPrep RNA/DNA Mini Kit, poly-A selected, and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. Median read depth was ∼ 37 million (M) (34–41 M interquartile range \[IQR\]). Reads were quantified by kallisto v.42.5 using Gencode v24, and summarized to the gene level using the R package tximport v.1.22.0. Protein-coding genes with transcripts per million values >1 in at least 20% of the samples were used in downstream analysis (). Differential gene expression, between CD in remission and controls, active CD and controls, and between active CD vs CD in remission, was performed using DESeq2 version 1.34.0. Genes with FDR ≤0.05 and fold change >1.5 were considered significantly different. ToppGene<sup>,</sup> platforms were used for functional annotation enrichment analyses on the up- and down-regulated genes significantly different between CD remission and controls, and CD remission and CD active. To further explore selected top enrichment terms, a single summarizing index (eigengene) was calculated per sample for each term. This eigengene is derived from the first principal component of the expression values of all genes associated with that term (as identified by ToppGene). These enriched terms’ specific eigengene values were then correlated with clinical and dietary features, using Pearson correlation coefficients and corresponding Student asymptotic *P* values. Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction was applied to the terms-diet correlations, with FDR ≤0.25 considered significant. MaAsLin2 R package 1.8.0 was used to identify ASVs significantly correlated to the enriched term’s eigengenes, controlling for age and gender, with FDR ≤0.25. This was calculated separately for each enriched term.

### Statistical Analysis

Statistics used for FFQ, and omics were performed in R, and details are under these specific sections. Overall, chi-square test was used for categorical variables, Spearman rank correlation or Pearson correlation were used for continuous variables, as indicated, and the Mann-Whitney *U* test for categorical variables. FDR correction was performed using Benjamini-Hochberg Procedure.

## Results

### Cohort Characteristics

A total of 191 subjects participated in the study. Seventy-seven patients with CD were in stable clinical remission for at least 3 months, with the majority (81%) receiving biologic therapy (). Non-IBD controls (n = 77) were used as a reference for normal signals. We also compared these groups to data from 37 active, mostly treatment-naïve CD patients at diagnosis, before any IBD-related therapies or dietary guidance (*A*, ). The advantage in this design is the ability to compare untreated active patients’ signals, who will not be confounded by therapy, to those on treatment in remission to obtain cleaner signals with advanced effective treatments. Age and body mass index showed no significant differences between groups. Smoking rates were highest in the active, mostly newly diagnosed CD group. Only a small fraction (<5%) of CD patients had colon-only L2 involvement. The median disease duration time for the remission group was 5 years (IQR, 3–10 years), and more patients in this group exhibited a complicated B2/B3 phenotype during their disease course. Nineteen subjects in the remission group had IBD-related surgeries, with median time postsurgery of 4 years (IQR, 2.5–5.5 years), and with similar exposure to biologics (79%) as the overall CD group in remission (). C-reactive protein (CRP) levels did not differ between the CD remission group and controls but were significantly lower than those in the active CD group. FCP levels were significantly lower in remission compared with active CD (; median, 153 \[IQR, 66–412\] vs 915 \[IQR, 300–1000\]). VCE was available for 61 subjects in the CD remission group, with a median highest LS of 225 (IQR, 225–300). Fifteen (25%) of these had LS >350, which has been previously linked with a higher potential for future flares.<sup>,</sup> *A* and specify which omics, FFQ, and clinical data are available for each participant, and the overlap between them.

### Compared With Non-Inflammatory Bowel Disease Controls, Less Healthy Dietary Habits Persist During Crohn’s Disease Remission

A computerized, validated FFQ was used,<sup>,</sup> with output including average daily macronutrients and micronutrients, food item servings, adherence to the Mediterranean diet (MEDAS score; range, 1–14), and exposure to food items classified as NOVA class 4 foods and beverages, indicating exposure to ultraprocessed foods. Principal component analysis clustering (*B*) shows significant separation between CD and controls on PC1, regardless of activity status. Unsupervised hierarchical clustering (*C*), based on similar nutritional data fields similarly shows most CD cases, whether active or in remission, clustering together and separate from most controls (36 controls, 22 CD in cluster 1 vs 21 controls and 61 CD in cluster 2; chi-square *P* = 1.5E-5), showing nutritional factors that are higher or lower in each group. MaAsLin2 identified differentially abundant dietary exposures between active CD, CD in remission, and controls, controlling for age and gender (FDR <0.25). The coefficients are shown (*D*), highlighting higher exposures to NOVA class 4 ultraprocessed food and added sugar in CD, with lower intake of fibers, folate, vitamin C, vitamin K, and vegetables in CD compared with controls. These CD-specific dietary patterns mostly persisted across both active disease and remission states.

### Fecal Microbial Signals and Metabolic Landscape Persist Overall During Remission

Previous research indicated that diet explains some of the variability in fecal microbial composition and metabolism. Therefore, we tested (1) whether fecal microbial signals from CD patients in active stages persist during remission using various microbial indices from other cohorts; and (2) how dietary exposures correlate with these microbial signals. Despite being in remission, we still observed a persistent pathogenic gut microbial community, including a higher IBD dysbiosis index, which was defined based on treatment-naïve active CD. We also detected a lower general health index, a higher specific IBD index previously identified through meta-analysis, and reduced Faith pd alpha diversity despite CD remission (*A*, ). Additionally, we used MaAsLin2 to identify differentially abundant microbial taxa between active CD, remission CD, and control groups, adjusting for age and gender (FDR <0.25). Notably, an enrichment of *Escherichia*, *Ruminococcus gnavus*, and *Enterocloster bolteae* was observed in both CD groups, consistent with findings from other cohorts (, ). Fecal metabolic alterations were also identified using MaAsLin2; 39 of 43 (91%) significantly different fecal metabolites (*B*) between remission CD and controls exhibited similar patterns in active CD patients vs controls, suggesting persistent fecal metabolic changes during remission.

Spearman correlations were applied to link dietary exposures with microbial compositions, using the published IBD dysbiotic index and Faith pd alpha diversity (*C*; FDR <0.25). Subanalyses stratifying and including only CD subjects in remission on biologics vs treatment naïve showed overall similar patterns regarding ileum mRNAseq transcriptomics genes linked with a specific functional enrichment term, microbial signals, and exposure to NOVA 4 ultraprocessed food (*A*). Notably, there was a trend for higher NOVA exposures, higher dysbiosis, and reduced microbial diversity in those that flared, with no differences in the T-cell adaptive genes and signals, but with significant higher epithelial antimicrobial and mucus genes and signals in those that flared, vs those remaining in remission ().

### Host Mucosal Interactions With Abnormal Dietary Signals and Microbial Composition

As a representative transcriptomics expression profile that summarizes the genes linked with the specified functional term (*E*), we used their corresponding eigengene summary indices (see Methods). Both active vs remission CD and in remission CD vs controls showed positive correlations with higher antibacterial (GO:0009617) signatures. Adaptive and innate immune signals were higher in active patients vs remission, and these pathways were also correlated with CD biomarkers used to capture active systemic and mucosal inflammation (CRP levels or FCP; *A*), although these immune signals were reduced in the CD group in remission compared with controls. Compared with controls, patients with CD in remission showed positive correlations with higher goblet cell–associated and mucin glycosylation eigengene index values (GO:0008194 and RECTOME\_MM15636). Conversely, active CD patients, relative to controls, showed negative correlations with these goblet/mucus-related signals. No significant correlations were observed between these term-specific eigengenes and demographic variables, reducing the risk of confounding biases.

Dietary interactions with these host transcriptomics term-specific summarized signals indicated significant negative correlations between exposure to NOVA class 4 ultraprocessed food and beverage (*A* and *B*) and gene signatures enriched for goblet cell signals and mucin glycosylation (GO:0008194 and RECTOME\_MM15636). In contrast, the MEDAS score, which reflects adherence to the Mediterranean diet, was positively associated with the goblet and mucin glycosylation signatures (*A* and *C*). Other notable dietary transcriptomics associations included negative correlations between higher intakes of several vitamins (vitamins A/retinol, B1/thiamin, B2/riboflavin, B6/pyridoxine) and lower levels of adaptive T cells and innate immune signals, whereas higher consumption of salty snacks and sweet bakery products was positively correlated with increased immune signatures. To assess whether dietary effects are mediated by gut microbes (ie, microbial dysbiosis index) or host inflammation (ie, FCP), we examined correlations between NOVA class 4 processed foods (*D*) or adherence to the Mediterranean diet (*E*) and FCP, microbial dysbiosis index, and mucin glycosylation. Notably, both NOVA and MEDAS scores correlated with FCP, the gut dysbiosis index, and genes associated with mucus glycosylation. However, no significant correlations were observed between FCP or the gut dysbiosis index and mucus gene transcriptomics, suggesting that gut microbiome and inflammation are less likely to mediate the effect of diet on mucus-related gene expression. Although this analysis establishes correlations, the directionality will need to be further examined through specific interventions.

Host transcriptomics associations with microbial signals were further assessed using multivariate MaAsLin2 models, controlling for age and gender, and testing for associations between bacterial ASVs and each transcriptomics term, summarized by a term-specific eigengenes index (E and *F*). This indicated mostly positive correlations between higher abundance of bacterial ASVs like *Veillonella*, *Streptococcus*, *Gemella*, *Haemophilus* taxa previously linked with CD pathogenesis, and host T-cell activation (GO:0042110) and innate granulocyte (RECTOME MM15330). Other notable associations were between *Klebsiella* taxa and antibacterial (GO:0009617) and cytokine activity (GO:0005125). Mucus/goblet-associated bacteria included a negative correlation with *Porcincola* taxa, which was linked in murine models with carboxymethyl cellulose, a possible proxy of exposure to NOVA class 4 processed food, and positively correlated with *Enterocloster* taxa that were shown by others to modulate epithelial function against *Salmonella*.

## Discussion

Immune suppression with advanced biologics or small-molecule therapies is the recommended treatment for CD. However, biologics do not cure CD, and a significant portion of patients experience flares of disease despite treatment, for reasons that are still largely elusive. Characterizing gut signals and dietary habits that persist during CD remission can help clarify residual CD-associated pathogenic pathways and triggers. In turn, this may potentially lead to novel adjunct treatment strategies that promote a more deeply normal gut state. Here, we captured paired dietary exposures, ileal transcriptomics, microbiome, and metabolomics, involving a total of 191 participants, including patients in remission. We aimed to identify perturbed signals during remission, primarily in patients treated with effective advanced biologic therapies. Treatment-naive, newly diagnosed CD cases were used as a reference for the active CD state, whereas non-IBD controls served as a reference for the desired healthy signals.

During CD remission, we observed a significant suppression of genes associated with adaptive T-cell responses and innate granulocyte activity in the ileum, which was notably deeper than the levels seen in control subjects, and likely reflects some impairment of the host mucosal immune response. In contrast, and potentially as a compensatory mechanism, or in response to the persistent altered gut microbial signals, patients exhibited persistently elevated expression of epithelial antimicrobial pathways and genes such as *DUOX2*, *CEACAM5/6*, *REG4*, and *DMBT1* during remission. The sustained elevation of *DUOX2* during remission, similar to levels seen in active disease and as reported by several groups,<sup>,</sup> may contribute to disease pathogenesis and chronicity because increased levels and activity of *DUOX2* have been linked to gut permeability in IBD and mouse models. Higher epithelial antimicrobial pathway genes in our dataset were further linked to the occurrence of a flare within 6 months (). Additionally, we noted a novel increase in goblet cells and mucin glycosylated signals in the ileum of patients with CD in remission, which have not been previously reported during CD remission. However, the induction of mucus genes has been documented in remission cases of ulcerative colitis in the colon. In CORE-CD, only a small fraction (<5%) of the CD patients had colon-only L2 involvement, and we opted to specifically characterize ileal mucosal biopsies. Notably, subanalyses based on different stratifications from endoscopy or VCE disease activity supported consistent patterns in host transcriptomics, as observed in the overall study group comparisons ().

Lifestyle and dietary modifications have been suggested to reduce CD incidence and likely can also improve the overall course. We demonstrated that, compared with non-IBD controls, unhealthy dietary habits persist among CD patients even during periods of remission. We found that the pathogenic gut microbial composition endured, as indicated by several microbial measurements, including the published IBD index that was generated using treatment-naïve active North American patients from the pediatric RISK cohort, our general health measures, and specific IBD indexes, along with reduced alpha diversity. We linked specific dietary exposure to changes in CD microbial composition, including higher exposure to NOVA class 4 ultraprocessed foods, added sugars, salty snacks, sweet baked goods, and starches, to a more dysbiotic IBD index. In this cohort, we found that patients with CD in remission had a higher microbial dysbiosis index compared with the control group. This index was established using data from active CD patients in a different geographical region within the RISK cohort, and may reflect disease and environmental factors rather than confounding variables or signals related to genetics or geography. In contrast, higher intakes of vitamin B6, vitamin E, vitamin K, and vegetables correlated with less dysbiotic microbial signals. We also observed enduring gut metabolic changes during remission.

Alterations in microbiome signals seen during remission and metabolites align with previous studies.<sup>,</sup> However, here we also include paired transcriptomics and dietary exposure data, which provide a more comprehensive understanding of gut variation, potentially suggesting that diet may be one potential upstream modifiable cue that mediates these observed alterations. The paired diet and host ileal transcriptomic design highlighted an intriguing observation linking higher consumption of processed foods with negative effects on genes enriched for goblet and mucus glycosylation functions in the human gut (*B*). Mucus glycosylation is a key post-translational modification of mucin proteins that contributes to gut barrier homeostasis. A previous elegant study showed that dietary emulsifiers reduce mouse mucus thickness, enhancing gut microbiota contact with epithelia, thereby promoting colitis. Here, our human data further support the negative effect of ultraprocessed foods on gene expression related to these impaired defense pathways. Diet and its related metabolites may modulate mucin glycosylation gene expression through direct and indirect signaling. Intestinal epithelial cells were shown to sense and respond to specific amino acids, to modulate the gene expression of mucin synthesis and glycosylation.<sup>,</sup> Dietary fibers were shown to regulate histone modification, which may regulate mucin-associated genes. Vitamins like vitamin A were shown to regulate goblet cell differentiation and function. Finally, the activity and expression of glycosyltransferase enzymes can be influenced directly by dietary factors, via specific sugar precursors availability needed for glycosylation. A previous randomized controlled-feeding study of dietary emulsifier carboxymethylcellulose in healthy volunteers reveals detrimental impacts on the gut microbiota and metabolome, supporting the possibility that abnormal diet factors may fuel these changes.

Strengths of our study include the in-depth characterization of the CORE-CD cohort, emphasizing abnormal features despite effective biologic therapy. Extensive prior efforts have focused on the characterization of transcriptomics in treatment-naïve or active CD,<sup>,</sup><sup>,</sup><sup>,</sup> leaving a gap in our understanding of signals at the affected mucosa during remission. Although dietary habits have been noted, we add direct comparison across the health-remission-active spectrum in a harmonized manner. This comparison may suggest that less healthy dietary signals likely influence both microbial populations and gut metabolites during remission. This occurs despite patients’ being educated and aware regarding the importance of dietary choices in managing CD. Furthermore, our paired diet-omics design connects host transcriptomic signals with dietary inputs, revealing associations that would not be evident when analyzing each dataset in isolation. Limitations include the descriptive nature of analyzing complex human interactions in vivo, which means we cannot fully replicate this complexity or test specific causal relationships in more functional contexts. Furthermore, it lacks intervention that may direct a more causal relationship, for example, between NOVA class 4 ultraprocessed food consumption and the ileal gut mucosal signals linked with mucus glycosylation. Although a larger cohort might have been beneficial for detecting weaker signals, even with our relatively medium-sized cohort, we achieved significant and insightful biological and clinical conclusions regarding CD patients in remission. We lacked data regarding prescribed and other over-the-counter medications and stool consistency, which may impact microbial signals. Additionally, because remission patients have been sick for a long while, we cannot discern whether captured signals are due to disease duration or medication, but that there are abnormal signals during remission with effective advanced therapy. Due to the design of our study, we cannot rule out that some differences may originate from genetics/lifestyle factors. Examining first-degree relatives of the patients with CD may help identify such factors but may limit the availability of biopsy specimens for transcriptomics, which are key when aiming to capture host response during remission. Future efforts to replicate our findings in geographically and ethnically diverse patient populations will be invaluable.

In conclusion, comparing CD signals across health-remission-active spectra shows that some gut abnormal patterns persist during remission, despite advanced effective therapy. Although advanced therapies effectively suppress the immune system, and notwithstanding the dietary consultation patients receive postdiagnosis, disturbances in antibacterial epithelial signals, along with unhealthy dietary patterns, altered microbiome, and perturbed metabolome, continue and are partly linked to the risk of flare 6 months later. This emphasizes the need to consider the inclusion of adjunctive therapeutic strategies that target the diet-epithelia-microbial axis, alongside immune suppression. These interventions might involve dietary modifications and the use of metabolites to help shift CD signals toward a healthier state, promoting deeper and longer-lasting remission.

## Basic Research Relevance

This work suggests new targets and potential interventions to address the altered diet-epithelia-microbial axis, which needs to be further explored in specified preclinical models as potential adjunct future treatments.

Population-based data indicate that Crohn’s disease (CD) incidence and prevalence continue to increase. This increase is attributed to industrialization, urbanization, and Westernized dietary exposures. As the number of CD patients living with these chronic conditions increases, strategies for managing patients in longer-lasting remission states are needed to reduce disabilities, lower care costs, and enhance overall outcomes. Biologic therapy, aimed at immune suppression, is considered the standard of care, but even with these effective therapies, the disease is not cured, and patients experience flares. Lifestyle and dietary modifications have been suggested to reduce CD incidence and to improve the overall CD course. Characterizations of signals that persist despite treatment may direct adjunct strategies toward an enhanced, healthier CD state.

Extensive molecular omics research has characterized treatment-naïve and active CD populations,<sup>,</sup> but less effort has been directed toward profiling CD patients in remission. Confocal laser endomicroscopy during remission suggests epithelial dysfunction. We have previously shown that microbial alterations persist, independent of mucosal inflammation, in ileal biopsy specimens of patients with treatment-naïve L2 colonic involvement. We now aimed to characterize ongoing gut mucosal signals, immune responses, and dietary habits in patients with CD as part of the CORE-CD (Capsule & Omics for pRedicting Exacerbation of Crohn’s disease) study, who were recruited while already on stable therapy for at least 3 months, and most were receiving biologic treatments. Patients underwent clinical assessment, colonoscopy, video capsule endoscopy (VCE), biomarker evaluation, dietary intake examination, ileal transcriptomics, microbiomics, and metabolomics phenotyping. Patients in remission were compared with controls without CD, serving as our reference for the desired healthy signals. We contrast these findings with signals from active, predominantly treatment-naïve CD patients, representing the other reference extreme. Our goal is to uncover signatures that still deviate from the healthy state during CD remission despite immune suppression treatments, thereby directing improved adjunctive CD management.

## Methods

### Study Design

A detailed description of the Methods is in the. The ethics committees at Sheba Medical Center approved the study. Written informed consent was obtained. Patients with CD in the remission group from the CORE-CD cohort were in continuous corticosteroid-free clinical remission (Crohn Disease Activity Index \[CDAI\] <150) for at least 3 months before enrollment and on a stable medication dosing as in previous designed cohorts.<sup>,</sup> Non–inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) controls were subjects who were investigated for various gastrointestinal symptoms, including abdominal pain, or had screening colonoscopies, but had normal endoscopic and histologic findings. Part of the newly diagnosed active CD and control subjects (56 of 191, indicated in ) were previously discussed. Subjects with severe comorbidities such as liver, kidney, neurologic, metabolic, or cardio-respiratory disorders were excluded. VCE (PILLCAM Crohn's, Medtronic) was performed as part of the CORE study in the group in remission, and the highest Lewis score (LS) was captured. Endoscopic evaluations included the simple endoscopic score (SES)-CD and Rutgeerts score (RS) when applicable. Fecal samples were frozen immediately at −80°C. Ileal biopsy specimens were gathered during colonoscopy, stored in RNAlater, and frozen at −80°C.

The per-protocol follow-up of the CORE-CD patients is still ongoing till completion of 24 months of follow-up, but here we used the baseline samples and censored and stratified the patients based on the available outcome at 6 months. The primary outcome was the occurrence of a clinical flare, defined as an increase of >70 points on CDAI from baseline and a CDAI >150, or a need for hospitalization/surgery/rescue medications for worsening CD. Remission or flare was defined during routine, planned clinical visits that occurred roughly every 3 months. We used either this outcome alone or together with an increase of fecal calprotectin (FCP) >300 at 6 months compared with baseline.

### Dietary Questionnaires

A comprehensive food frequency questionnaire (FFQ) was uniformly applied,<sup>,</sup> computing the daily macro and micronutrients, and main food item servings. Dietary data were normalized to 1000 kcal caloric intake. Adherence to the Mediterranean diet was assessed using the Mediterranean Diet Adherence Screener (MEDAS) score, ranging from 0–14, with higher scores indicating better adherence. NOVA classification key was applied, whereby class 4 indicates ultraprocessed food and drink products. We report on the rate of class 4 items out of the total consumed items. Principal component analysis was performed to summarize FFQ variation using R prcomp function with data scaled and centered. The top 10 largest combined absolute scaled loadings were overlaid as arrows to visualize the contributions of original variables to the principal components. Heatmap was generated using the R heatmap.3 function. Data, representing *z* scores of FFQ components, were hierarchically clustered using Euclidean distance and ward.D method for both rows and columns. Multivariate Association with Linear Models (MaAsLin2, R package 1.8.0) was used to identify differentially abundant metabolites (false discovery rate \[FDR\] ≤0.25) between CD in remission, active CD, and controls, controlling for age and gender.

### 16S seq Microbiome

Fecal V4 16S (515F/806R) sequencing was conducted,,, using Illumina MiSeq, and processed in QIIME 2 v.2023.7,<sup>,</sup> with amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) detection using Deblur, and taxonomy was assigned using the Greengenes2 (2022.10). Each unique ASV was assigned a specific identification number, with corresponding sequences provided in and relevant supplementary datasets. CD dysbiosis index was calculated based on Gevers et al. Faith’s phylogenetic diversity was used as a measure of α diversity, after rarefication to 4000 reads. Per-sample health index was calculated as previously described, calculating the log of the ratio of health- to disease-associated bacteria, with higher values indicating a better health-associated microbiome. A similar approach was used to define an IBD index for each sample, using ASVs that were significantly increased or decreased in IBD samples in the same study.

### Fecal Metabolomics Profiling

Fecal metabolites were extracted with methanol and water (3:1) and profiling as previously described. Dionex Ultimate ultra–high-performance liquid chromatography system coupled to Orbitrap Q-Exactive Mass Spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used with electrospray ionization with polarity switching mode. Thermo Xcalibur 4.1 was used for data acquisition. Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) metabolites peak areas were determined using Mzmine2.53, analyzing an in-house library of 545 metabolites built by running commercially available standards, with a matching HMBD ID. Total Sum Scaling normalization was applied per sample after exclusion of samples with sum peak area <1E9 () and excluding 3 metabolites higher in blank. MaAsLin2 R package 1.8.0 was used to identify differentially abundant metabolites (FDR ≤0.25) between CD in remission and controls, controlling for age and gender. These differentially abundant metabolites were compared with those that showed similar differences between the CD active and control groups, previously obtained from the SOURCE (Study of Urban and Rural Crohn disease Evolution) cohort.

### Ileal Transcriptomics

Terminal ileum (TI) biopsy specimen RNA was isolated using the Qiagen AllPrep RNA/DNA Mini Kit, poly-A selected, and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. Median read depth was ∼ 37 million (M) (34–41 M interquartile range \[IQR\]). Reads were quantified by kallisto v.42.5 using Gencode v24, and summarized to the gene level using the R package tximport v.1.22.0. Protein-coding genes with transcripts per million values >1 in at least 20% of the samples were used in downstream analysis (). Differential gene expression, between CD in remission and controls, active CD and controls, and between active CD vs CD in remission, was performed using DESeq2 version 1.34.0. Genes with FDR ≤0.05 and fold change >1.5 were considered significantly different. ToppGene<sup>,</sup> platforms were used for functional annotation enrichment analyses on the up- and down-regulated genes significantly different between CD remission and controls, and CD remission and CD active. To further explore selected top enrichment terms, a single summarizing index (eigengene) was calculated per sample for each term. This eigengene is derived from the first principal component of the expression values of all genes associated with that term (as identified by ToppGene). These enriched terms’ specific eigengene values were then correlated with clinical and dietary features, using Pearson correlation coefficients and corresponding Student asymptotic *P* values. Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction was applied to the terms-diet correlations, with FDR ≤0.25 considered significant. MaAsLin2 R package 1.8.0 was used to identify ASVs significantly correlated to the enriched term’s eigengenes, controlling for age and gender, with FDR ≤0.25. This was calculated separately for each enriched term.

### Statistical Analysis

Statistics used for FFQ, and omics were performed in R, and details are under these specific sections. Overall, chi-square test was used for categorical variables, Spearman rank correlation or Pearson correlation were used for continuous variables, as indicated, and the Mann-Whitney *U* test for categorical variables. FDR correction was performed using Benjamini-Hochberg Procedure.

## Results

### Cohort Characteristics

A total of 191 subjects participated in the study. Seventy-seven patients with CD were in stable clinical remission for at least 3 months, with the majority (81%) receiving biologic therapy (). Non-IBD controls (n = 77) were used as a reference for normal signals. We also compared these groups to data from 37 active, mostly treatment-naïve CD patients at diagnosis, before any IBD-related therapies or dietary guidance (*A*, ). The advantage in this design is the ability to compare untreated active patients’ signals, who will not be confounded by therapy, to those on treatment in remission to obtain cleaner signals with advanced effective treatments. Age and body mass index showed no significant differences between groups. Smoking rates were highest in the active, mostly newly diagnosed CD group. Only a small fraction (<5%) of CD patients had colon-only L2 involvement. The median disease duration time for the remission group was 5 years (IQR, 3–10 years), and more patients in this group exhibited a complicated B2/B3 phenotype during their disease course. Nineteen subjects in the remission group had IBD-related surgeries, with median time postsurgery of 4 years (IQR, 2.5–5.5 years), and with similar exposure to biologics (79%) as the overall CD group in remission (). VCE was available for 61 subjects in the CD remission group, with a median highest LS of 225 (IQR, 225–300). ### Fecal Microbial Signals and Metabolic Landscape Persist Overall During Remission

Previous research indicated that diet explains some of the variability in fecal microbial composition and metabolism. Therefore, we tested (1) whether fecal microbial signals from CD patients in active stages persist during remission using various microbial indices from other cohorts; and (2) how dietary exposures correlate with these microbial signals. Despite being in remission, we still observed a persistent pathogenic gut microbial community, including a higher IBD dysbiosis index, which was defined based on treatment-naïve active CD. We also detected a lower general health index, a higher specific IBD index previously identified through meta-analysis, and reduced Faith pd alpha diversity despite CD remission (*A*, ). Additionally, we used MaAsLin2 to identify differentially abundant microbial taxa between active CD, remission CD, and control groups, adjusting for age and gender (FDR <0.25). Notably, an enrichment of *Escherichia*, *Ruminococcus gnavus*, and *Enterocloster bolteae* was observed in both CD groups, consistent with findings from other cohorts (, ). Fecal metabolic alterations were also identified using MaAsLin2; 39 of 43 (91%) significantly different fecal metabolites (*B*) between remission CD and controls exhibited similar patterns in active CD patients vs controls, suggesting persistent fecal metabolic changes during remission.

Notably, there was a trend for higher NOVA exposures, higher dysbiosis, and reduced microbial diversity in those that flared, with no differences in the T-cell adaptive genes and signals, but with significant higher epithelial antimicrobial and mucus genes and signals in those that flared, vs those remaining in remission ().

### Host Mucosal Interactions With Abnormal Dietary Signals and Microbial Composition

As a representative transcriptomics expression profile that summarizes the genes linked with the specified functional term (*E*), we used their corresponding eigengene summary indices (see Methods). Both active vs remission CD and in remission CD vs controls showed positive correlations with higher antibacterial (GO:0009617) signatures. Adaptive and innate immune signals were higher in active patients vs remission, and these pathways were also correlated with CD biomarkers used to capture active systemic and mucosal inflammation (CRP levels or FCP; *A*), although these immune signals were reduced in the CD group in remission compared with controls. Compared with controls, patients with CD in remission showed positive correlations with higher goblet cell–associated and mucin glycosylation eigengene index values (GO:0008194 and RECTOME\_MM15636). Conversely, active CD patients, relative to controls, showed negative correlations with these goblet/mucus-related signals. No significant correlations were observed between these term-specific eigengenes and demographic variables, reducing the risk of confounding biases.

Dietary interactions with these host transcriptomics term-specific summarized signals indicated significant negative correlations between exposure to NOVA class 4 ultraprocessed food and beverage (*A* and *B*) and gene signatures enriched for goblet cell signals and mucin glycosylation (GO:0008194 and RECTOME\_MM15636). In contrast, the MEDAS score, which reflects adherence to the Mediterranean diet, was positively associated with the goblet and mucin glycosylation signatures (*A* and *C*). Other notable dietary transcriptomics associations included negative correlations between higher intakes of several vitamins (vitamins A/retinol, B1/thiamin, B2/riboflavin, B6/pyridoxine) and lower levels of adaptive T cells and innate immune signals, whereas higher consumption of salty snacks and sweet bakery products was positively correlated with increased immune signatures. To assess whether dietary effects are mediated by gut microbes (ie, microbial dysbiosis index) or host inflammation (ie, FCP), we examined correlations between NOVA class 4 processed foods (*D*) or adherence to the Mediterranean diet (*E*) and FCP, microbial dysbiosis index, and mucin glycosylation. Notably, both NOVA and MEDAS scores correlated with FCP, the gut dysbiosis index, and genes associated with mucus glycosylation. However, no significant correlations were observed between FCP or the gut dysbiosis index and mucus gene transcriptomics, suggesting that gut microbiome and inflammation are less likely to mediate the effect of diet on mucus-related gene expression. Although this analysis establishes correlations, the directionality will need to be further examined through specific interventions.

Host transcriptomics associations with microbial signals were further assessed using multivariate MaAsLin2 models, controlling for age and gender, and testing for associations between bacterial ASVs and each transcriptomics term, summarized by a term-specific eigengenes index (E and *F*). This indicated mostly positive correlations between higher abundance of bacterial ASVs like *Veillonella*, *Streptococcus*, *Gemella*, *Haemophilus* taxa previously linked with CD pathogenesis, and host T-cell activation (GO:0042110) and innate granulocyte (RECTOME MM15330). Other notable associations were between *Klebsiella* taxa and antibacterial (GO:0009617) and cytokine activity (GO:0005125). Mucus/goblet-associated bacteria included a negative correlation with *Porcincola* taxa, which was linked in murine models with carboxymethyl cellulose, a possible proxy of exposure to NOVA class 4 processed food, and positively correlated with *Enterocloster* taxa that were shown by others to modulate epithelial function against *Salmonella*.

## Discussion

Immune suppression with advanced biologics or small-molecule therapies is the recommended treatment for CD. However, biologics do not cure CD, and a significant portion of patients experience flares of disease despite treatment, for reasons that are still largely elusive. Characterizing gut signals and dietary habits that persist during CD remission can help clarify residual CD-associated pathogenic pathways and triggers. In turn, this may potentially lead to novel adjunct treatment strategies that promote a more deeply normal gut state. Here, we captured paired dietary exposures, ileal transcriptomics, microbiome, and metabolomics, involving a total of 191 participants, including patients in remission. We aimed to identify perturbed signals during remission, primarily in patients treated with effective advanced biologic therapies. Treatment-naive, newly diagnosed CD cases were used as a reference for the active CD state, whereas non-IBD controls served as a reference for the desired healthy signals.

During CD remission, we observed a significant suppression of genes associated with adaptive T-cell responses and innate granulocyte activity in the ileum, which was notably deeper than the levels seen in control subjects, and likely reflects some impairment of the host mucosal immune response. In contrast, and potentially as a compensatory mechanism, or in response to the persistent altered gut microbial signals, patients exhibited persistently elevated expression of epithelial antimicrobial pathways and genes such as *DUOX2*, *CEACAM5/6*, *REG4*, and *DMBT1* during remission. The sustained elevation of *DUOX2* during remission, similar to levels seen in active disease and as reported by several groups,<sup>,</sup> may contribute to disease pathogenesis and chronicity because increased levels and activity of *DUOX2* have been linked to gut permeability in IBD and mouse models. Higher epithelial antimicrobial pathway genes in our dataset were further linked to the occurrence of a flare within 6 months (). Additionally, we noted a novel increase in goblet cells and mucin glycosylated signals in the ileum of patients with CD in remission, which have not been previously reported during CD remission. However, the induction of mucus genes has been documented in remission cases of ulcerative colitis in the colon. In CORE-CD, only a small fraction (<5%) of the CD patients had colon-only L2 involvement, and we opted to specifically characterize ileal mucosal biopsies. Notably, subanalyses based on different stratifications from endoscopy or VCE disease activity supported consistent patterns in host transcriptomics, as observed in the overall study group comparisons ().

Lifestyle and dietary modifications have been suggested to reduce CD incidence and likely can also improve the overall course. We demonstrated that, compared with non-IBD controls, unhealthy dietary habits persist among CD patients even during periods of remission. We found that the pathogenic gut microbial composition endured, as indicated by several microbial measurements, including the published IBD index that was generated using treatment-naïve active North American patients from the pediatric RISK cohort, our general health measures, and specific IBD indexes, along with reduced alpha diversity. We linked specific dietary exposure to changes in CD microbial composition, including higher exposure to NOVA class 4 ultraprocessed foods, added sugars, salty snacks, sweet baked goods, and starches, to a more dysbiotic IBD index. In this cohort, we found that patients with CD in remission had a higher microbial dysbiosis index compared with the control group. This index was established using data from active CD patients in a different geographical region within the RISK cohort, and may reflect disease and environmental factors rather than confounding variables or signals related to genetics or geography. In contrast, higher intakes of vitamin B6, vitamin E, vitamin K, and vegetables correlated with less dysbiotic microbial signals. We also observed enduring gut metabolic changes during remission.

Alterations in microbiome signals seen during remission and metabolites align with previous studies.<sup>,</sup> However, here we also include paired transcriptomics and dietary exposure data, which provide a more comprehensive understanding of gut variation, potentially suggesting that diet may be one potential upstream modifiable cue that mediates these observed alterations. The paired diet and host ileal transcriptomic design highlighted an intriguing observation linking higher consumption of processed foods with negative effects on genes enriched for goblet and mucus glycosylation functions in the human gut (*B*). Mucus glycosylation is a key post-translational modification of mucin proteins that contributes to gut barrier homeostasis. A previous elegant study showed that dietary emulsifiers reduce mouse mucus thickness, enhancing gut microbiota contact with epithelia, thereby promoting colitis. Here, our human data further support the negative effect of ultraprocessed foods on gene expression related to these impaired defense pathways. Diet and its related metabolites may modulate mucin glycosylation gene expression through direct and indirect signaling. Intestinal epithelial cells were shown to sense and respond to specific amino acids, to modulate the gene expression of mucin synthesis and glycosylation.<sup>,</sup> Dietary fibers were shown to regulate histone modification, which may regulate mucin-associated genes. Vitamins like vitamin A were shown to regulate goblet cell differentiation and function. Finally, the activity and expression of glycosyltransferase enzymes can be influenced directly by dietary factors, via specific sugar precursors availability needed for glycosylation. A previous randomized controlled-feeding study of dietary emulsifier carboxymethylcellulose in healthy volunteers reveals detrimental impacts on the gut microbiota and metabolome, supporting the possibility that abnormal diet factors may fuel these changes.

Strengths of our study include the in-depth characterization of the CORE-CD cohort, emphasizing abnormal features despite effective biologic therapy. Extensive prior efforts have focused on the characterization of transcriptomics in treatment-naïve or active CD,<sup>,</sup><sup>,</sup><sup>,</sup> leaving a gap in our understanding of signals at the affected mucosa during remission. Although dietary habits have been noted, we add direct comparison across the health-remission-active spectrum in a harmonized manner. This comparison may suggest that less healthy dietary signals likely influence both microbial populations and gut metabolites during remission. This occurs despite patients’ being educated and aware regarding the importance of dietary choices in managing CD. Furthermore, our paired diet-omics design connects host transcriptomic signals with dietary inputs, revealing associations that would not be evident when analyzing each dataset in isolation. Limitations include the descriptive nature of analyzing complex human interactions in vivo, which means we cannot fully replicate this complexity or test specific causal relationships in more functional contexts. Furthermore, it lacks intervention that may direct a more causal relationship, for example, between NOVA class 4 ultraprocessed food consumption and the ileal gut mucosal signals linked with mucus glycosylation. Although a larger cohort might have been beneficial for detecting weaker signals, even with our relatively medium-sized cohort, we achieved significant and insightful biological and clinical conclusions regarding CD patients in remission. We lacked data regarding prescribed and other over-the-counter medications and stool consistency, which may impact microbial signals. Additionally, because remission patients have been sick for a long while, we cannot discern whether captured signals are due to disease duration or medication, but that there are abnormal signals during remission with effective advanced therapy. Due to the design of our study, we cannot rule out that some differences may originate from genetics/lifestyle factors. Examining first-degree relatives of the patients with CD may help identify such factors but may limit the availability of biopsy specimens for transcriptomics, which are key when aiming to capture host response during remission. Future efforts to replicate our findings in geographically and ethnically diverse patient populations will be invaluable.

In conclusion, comparing CD signals across health-remission-active spectra shows that some gut abnormal patterns persist during remission, despite advanced effective therapy. Although advanced therapies effectively suppress the immune system, and notwithstanding the dietary consultation patients receive postdiagnosis, disturbances in antibacterial epithelial signals, along with unhealthy dietary patterns, altered microbiome, and perturbed metabolome, continue and are partly linked to the risk of flare 6 months later. This emphasizes the need to consider the inclusion of adjunctive therapeutic strategies that target the diet-epithelia-microbial axis, alongside immune suppression. These interventions might involve dietary modifications and the use of metabolites to help shift CD signals toward a healthier state, promoting deeper and longer-lasting remission.
