## Summary

### Background

For children with Crohn's disease, there is a marked demand for long-term nutritional treatment strategies to avoid the side-effects related to drug treatment. We aimed to investigate whether paediatric patients with Crohn's disease responding to nutritional induction therapy can be maintained in remission on dietary therapy without the use of medication or surgery.

### Methods

In this open-label, endpoint-blinded, randomised controlled trial (CD-HOPE) done in 21 hospitals of the GETAID pédiatrique network in France, eligible patients were aged 6 years to younger than 18 years with Crohn's disease (newly diagnosed or relapsing after drug treatment) who had reached clinical remission (weighted Paediatric Crohn's Disease Activity Index \[wPCDAI\] ≤12·5) after induction therapy with 6–12 weeks of exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN; 100% of calories). Stratified by age (≤10 years or >10 years) and relapse status, patients were randomly assigned (1:1) using block randomisation to receive either cyclic EEN (C-EEN; 100% of daily caloric requirements) for 2 weeks every 8 weeks for at least six cycles or daily partial enteral nutrition (PEN; 25% of daily caloric requirements) for 52 weeks. Oral MODULEN IBD was used for both. Except for the 2-week intervals of EEN in the C-EEN group, food access was not restricted. The primary endpoint was relapse rate at 12 months. A relapse was defined as: (1) a wPCDAI score greater than 12·5 at two successive visits, or (2) a wPCDAI greater than 12·5 once and the need for Crohn's disease-related surgery, medication, or study exit, or (3) a wPCDAI score continuously greater than 12·5 since the last study visit, or (4) incomplete wPCDAI and moderate or severe Physician Global Assessment at any timepoint. Primary and safety analyses were performed on an intention-to-treat basis. This trial was registered on [ClinicalTrials.gov](https://clinicaltrials.gov/) ([NCT02201693](https://clinicaltrials.gov/show/NCT02201693)) and is complete.

### Findings

Between Dec 12, 2014, and Sept 4, 2018 (last patient visit Oct 2, 2019), 112 patients were screened and 100 patients (70 boys and 30 girls) were randomly assigned to C-EEN (n=49) or PEN (n=51). Median age of participants was 12 years (IQR 11–13) in the C-EEN group and 13 years (11–14) in the PEN group. At 12 months, 24 (49%) of 49 patients had relapsed on C-EEN compared with 39 (76%) of 51 patients on PEN (adjusted odds ratio 0·29 \[95% CI 0·13–0·70\], p=0·0051). 19 adverse events occurred in 17 patients: four serious adverse events in four patients on PEN, not related to treatment, and 15 non-serious adverse events in 13 patients (nine in seven patients on C-EEN and six in six patients on PEN). The serious adverse events were all related to Crohn's disease relapse requiring hospitalisation for rescue therapy. Six adverse events were possibly related to study treatment: one patient in the C-EEN group reported transient vomiting and diarrhoea at the start of the first EEN cycle, one patient in the C-EEN group developed anorexia and, in the PEN group, four patients reported either weight loss, nausea, Crohn's disease relapse, or skin infection. No malignancy or death was reported in this study.

### Interpretation

C-EEN was superior to PEN in maintaining clinical remission over 1 year in paediatric patients with Crohn's disease responding to EEN induction therapy. These findings suggest a new way to use nutritional therapy for maintaining drug-free long-term remission in patients with Crohn's disease who are responding to EEN induction therapy.

Research in context

**Evidence before this study**

Nutritional therapy in the form of exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN) is recommended by the European Society for Paediatric Gastroenterology, Hepatology and Nutrition and the European Crohn's and Colitis Organisation as first-line induction therapy in children with active, mild-to-moderate, luminal Crohn's disease. Many studies and subsequent meta-analyses have confirmed its rapid and profound anti-inflammatory effect, with clinical remission rates of up to 60–80% on sole nutritional induction therapy and mucosal healing rates of 50% or more. However, after a first course of nutritional therapy, many patients receive medical therapy to maintain remission over time. We performed a literature search of MEDLINE and Web of Science from database inception to January, 2014, with an update to March 31, 2025, for clinical trials investigating the maintenance of remission in patients with Crohn's disease with nutritional therapy. Search terms included “inflammatory bowel diseases”, “Crohn's disease”, “pediatric”, “nutrition”, “maintenance therapy”, and “longterm remission”. No randomised controlled trials (RCTs) have addressed the question of whether nutritional therapy alone without added immunomodulators is efficacious in maintaining remission for up to 12 months in children and adolescents with Crohn's disease. Some data indicate that the addition of nutritional therapy to ongoing medical therapy or after surgical resection could increase remission rates at 6 or 12 months in adult patients with inflammatory bowel disease. One paediatric clinical trial showed that a 2-week course of EEN followed by the Crohn's disease exclusion diet and partial enteral nutrition (PEN) was able to maintain 18 (60%) of 30 patients in clinical remission for up to 24 weeks, with 75% of patients starting immunomodulators during the trial. In addition, no clinical or biological biomarkers exist that predict long-term response to nutritional maintenance therapy.

**Added value of this study**

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first RCT showing that standalone nutritional maintenance therapy (without the addition of immunomodulators) in the form of cyclic EEN (C-EEN) was able to maintain clinical remission in 25 (51%) of 49 paediatric patients with Crohn's disease over 12 months (*vs* 12 \[24%\] of 51 on PEN). As it is difficult to follow and adhere to prolonged nutritional therapy in the form of extreme food restriction (eg, to one liquid source of food), or in the form of a restrictive diet, the cyclic schedule of EEN used in this RCT (6 weeks of unrestricted food and 2 weeks of EEN) offers a feasible alternative. The microbial analyses suggested a specific pattern (high levels of *Enterobacter*) that predicts poor response to nutritional maintenance therapy.

**Implications of all the available evidence**

The findings of this RCT indicate that a select group of paediatric patients with Crohn's disease could be maintained in remission over 12 months without using immunosuppressors or biologics. Based on these findings, maintenance nutritional therapy in the form of C-EEN should be considered a treatment strategy for children and adolescents with mild-to-moderate Crohn's disease. These findings could also be extended to adult patients with Crohn's disease. The finding of a microbial pattern predictive of response to nutritional maintenance therapy requires confirmation in independent cohorts.

## Methods

### Study design and participants

CD-HOPE was an investigator-initiated, prospective, randomised controlled trial using a prospective randomised open blinded endpoint (PROBE) evaluation investigating the long-term efficacy of nutritional interventions to maintain remission in paediatric patients with Crohn's disease.

Patients were recruited from 21 hospitals in France of the French Groupe d'Étude Thérapeutique des Affections Inflammatoires du Tube Digestif (GETAID) pédiatrique IBD network ([appendix p 25](#supplementary-material)). Eligible patients were aged between 6 years and younger than 18 years; had a confirmed diagnosis of active Crohn's disease (newly diagnosed or longstanding disease exposed to various drugs, including mesalamine, steroids, immunomodulators, or anti-tumour necrosis factor \[TNF\] biologics, being treated with EEN for an acute relapse); and had reached clinical remission (weighted Paediatric Crohn's Disease Activity Index \[wPCDAI\] ≤12·5) after induction therapy with EEN (MODULEN IBD, Nestlé Vevey, Switzerland) covering at least 100% of caloric requirements (and no additional food intake) for at least 6 weeks (maximum 12 weeks).

All Crohn's disease-related drug therapy had to be stopped at least 4 weeks before inclusion (except for infliximab requiring an 8-week washout period and 5-aminosalicylates \[5-ASA\] and derivates, which had to be stopped at the screening visit at the latest). Antibiotic therapy had to be stopped at least 2 weeks before inclusion. Patients with symptomatic B2 or B3 disease behaviour (intestinal or colonic stenosis \[including ileocaecal valve\], intrabdominal abscess, or fistulising disease); with isolated and severe perianal disease; requiring surgical therapy at inclusion; or currently participating or having participated in another interventional trial in the previous 4 weeks were not eligible.

Both parents of each patient (or their legal representatives) read and signed the consent form. An information leaflet was read, along with the consent, to the child participant, who signed it if they could understand the content and provide a signature. This was done in accordance with current ethical regulations in France. The study was approved by the national ethics committee (CPP IDF 1 no 2013-nov-13408) and Commission nationale de l'informatique et des libertés (no DR-2014–352), and is registered with [ClinicalTrials.gov](https://clinicaltrials.gov/) ([NCT02201693](https://clinicaltrials.gov/show/NCT02201693)).

### Randomisation and masking

Eligible patients were randomly assigned (1:1) to receive either cyclic EEN (C-EEN) with repeated cycles of 2 weeks of EEN every 8 weeks or PEN (supplementary enteral nutritional support representing 25% of daily calories). Randomisation was blinded and centralised by a computer, and patients were stratified according to age (≤10 years or >10 years) and whether they had new-onset (no previous drug exposure) or relapsing (previous drug exposure) disease. Block randomisation was performed according to the above strata, with random blocks within these strata selected at random from a size of 2, 4, and 6. As patients and their treating physicians could not be masked, only the research physicians who assessed the primary endpoint were masked to randomisation group.

### Procedures

EEN induction therapy (oral or via nasogastric tube) was with MODULEN IBD covering at least 100% of caloric requirements (and no additional food intake) for 6–12 weeks. After randomisation, participants were weaned off EEN induction therapy over a maximum of 2 weeks, reducing MODULEN IBD by approximately 500 mL/day to stop (0 mL for patients in the C-EEN group and 25% of caloric needs for patients in the PEN group) over 4–5 days, with concomitant unrestricted food access.

The C-EEN group then started a 6-week period of unrestricted food intake, after which they switched to 2 weeks of MODULEN IBD EEN (either oral or via nasogastric tube) covering at least 100% of daily caloric requirements (usually 2–2·5 L/day; higher amounts were permitted, but no additional food was allowed; [figure 1](#fig1)). After these 2 weeks of EEN, MODULEN IBD was decreased by 500 mL/day and stopped over a maximum of 4–5 days and patients switched back to unrestricted food access for 6 weeks. These 8-week cycles of 6 weeks of unrestricted food followed by 2 weeks of EEN were repeated throughout the entire study for 52 weeks (in total at least six complete cycles). The interval of 2 weeks of EEN every 8 weeks could be adapted to 2 weeks of EEN plus 5 to 7 weeks of normal food (cycles of 7–9 weeks) to adjust for patients' requirements and personal planning (holidays, school exams, etc).

In the PEN group, participants received a daily oral MODULEN IBD supplement covering 25% of their daily calorie needs (usually around 500 mL/day) and had unrestricted food access throughout the entire trial (52 weeks).

No specific dietary advice by a dietitian was offered during CD-HOPE in either treatment group. Adherence to treatment was monitored by a food diary (most often completed by the parents). Data collection was done daily during the 2-week periods while on EEN in the C-EEN group, and the corresponding 2-week windows in patients on PEN, repeated every 8 weeks. Parents of the patients receiving C-EEN reported precise quantities of MODULEN IBD taken per meal, number of hard candies (not swallowed) eaten per day, and absence of other foods (yes or no), while parents of patients receiving PEN reported daily MODULEN IBD volumes and the details of their nutrition over the same period (qualitative data). Automatic SMS and email alerts were sent to the patients' parents monthly reminding them of the dates of EEN start and end. Patients who did not take the required quantities of MODULEN IBD for three or more consecutive reporting periods across both groups were considered to be non-adherent.

No Crohn's disease-related drugs were allowed during this trial. A patient requiring Crohn's disease-related surgery or drug medication was considered as relapsing. Patients who relapsed during this trial (as determined by the treating physician) had the possibility to receive immediate surgical or drug therapy according to their needs. For categorical variables, relapsing patients were kept in the intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis, whereas for continuous variables, data from relapsing patients were not taken into account.

There was a screening visit (visit 0), a baseline and randomisation visit (visit 1), and visits at months (M) 3, 6, 9, and 12 (visits two to five; [figure 1](#fig1)). The screening visit was performed after at least 4 weeks of EEN induction therapy. At baseline (visit 1; after completing 6–12 weeks of induction therapy), we collected data on demographics and clinical characteristics. Biological sex at birth (male or female) was collected by the physicians who enrolled the patients. No gender data were collected. At every visit, we collected data on (w)PCDAI, Physician Global Assessment (PGA; all disease activity scores assessed by a masked study physician); biological parameters (blood \[haemoglobin, white blood cell count, platelets, erythrocyte sedimentation rate, albumin, C-reactive protein\] and stool \[for faecal calprotectin\]); clinical examination (including weight and height); and quality of life (IMPACT-III). In addition, an additional faecal sample was collected at every visit from patients who volunteered to participate in a microbiome annex study.

Stool samples were collected, stored for a maximum of 24 h at 4°C at home, aliquoted (approximately 150 mg), and stored at –80°C until analysis. DNA was extracted from aliquots using the Qiamp PowerFecal kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) according to the supplier's recommendations. The DNA was resuspended in 75 μL of Tris-EDTA (edetic acid) buffer and stored at –20°C. DNA quality and concentrations were assessed using spectrophotometry (NanoDrop, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Madison, WI, USA). Microbial load was assessed in each sample by quantitative PCR using the universal primers All Bacteria (F\_Bact1369: CGGTGAATACGTTCCCGG and R\_Prok1492: TACGGCTACCTTGTTACGACTT). Microbiome composition and diversity was analysed using 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) gene sequencing with Illumina MiSeq (Eurofins, Nantes, France), following amplification of the V3–V4 region with the primers V3–341F (CCTACGGGNGGCWGCAG) and V4–805R (GACTACHVGGGTATCTAATCC). The residual adapter and primer sequences were removed, and the reads were checked for quality (minimum score ≥20) and length (minimum 200 base pairs) using cutadapt (version 5).

The primary endpoint was relapse rate at 12 months. A relapse was defined as: (1) a wPCDAI score greater than 12·5

An exploratory outcome was microbiological analysis of changes in the stool microbiome. In addition, C-reactive protein (CRP) and albumin were post-hoc outcomes. We do not report data on the explanatory variables for relapse at 12 months in the two treatment groups separately.

### Statistical analysis

Assuming a 12-month relapse rate of 60% in the PEN group and 30% in the C-EEN group (difference of 30 percentage points), at a type I error rate of 5% and an 80% power, the required sample size was calculated at 43 patients per treatment group. Considering a potential loss to follow-up of approximately 15%, the number of participants per treatment group was corrected to 50 patients. As C-EEN alone had never been investigated as maintenance therapy in children and adolescents with Crohn's disease, the estimated 30% relapse rate for C-EEN was based on preliminary data (Ruemmele FM, unpublished) of C-EEN used in an open-label manner for children and adolescents in clinical remission on EEN induction at Necker Enfants Malades Hospital Paris (seven \[70%\] of ten patients did respond with over 1 year of clinical remission) and on the extrapolation of response rates of EEN as an induction therapy, which are usually in the range of 60–80%.

All other secondary endpoints were compared using a logistic regression model with adjustment for stratification variables.

Median height Z scores remained stable between M0 and M12 for both groups and no significant catch-up growth (Z score >0) was observed in either group, with median Z scores at M12 of –0·15 (–0·80 to 0·40) for C-EEN and –0·10 (–1·10 to 0·60) for PEN (p=0·80; [appendix p 4](#supplementary-material)).

An additional 11 patients who relapsed and required medical rescue therapy had an endoscopic evaluation at M12, with six (55%) patients (one on C-EEN and five on PEN) having mucosal healing on anti-TNF medication.

The median delta score (change from M0) at M12 was 4·5 (IQR –3·0 to 15·0) for C-EEN and 6·0 (–5·0 to 10·0) for PEN (p=1·00).

In the sixth reporting period, 22 (88%) of 25 patients in the C-EEN group and seven (58%) of 12 patients in the PEN group completed the food diaries ([appendix p 7](#supplementary-material)). In patients completing the food diaries, none failed to take the required MODULEN IBD volume for three or more consecutive periods. Tolerance of MODULEN IBD (ie, the ability to consume the entire prescribed volume without physical or psychological difficulty) was 100% in both groups.

15 non-serious adverse events occurred in 13 patients: nine adverse events in seven patients on C-EEN and six adverse events in six patients on PEN ([appendix p 8](#supplementary-material)). Six adverse events were possibly related to the study treatment: one patient on C-EEN reported transient vomiting and diarrhoea at the start of the first MODULEN EEN cycle, one patient in the C-EEN group developed anorexia and, in the PEN group, four patients reported either weight loss, nausea, Crohn's disease relapse, or skin infection. When comparing microbial changes (Bray–Curtis distance at M3, M6, M9, and M12) between the two treatment groups, microbiome beta-diversity was numerically reduced based on general composition for PEN compared with C-EEN therapy, whereas the reduction at the amplicon sequence variant level was statistically significant at M6, M9, and M12 ([appendix p 10](#supplementary-material)). When analysing the intention-to-treat population (after strata adjustment) for factors potentially predictive for 12-month relapse, there was no signal for sex, age at inclusion, ASCA status, or the presence of granuloma ([appendix p 12](#supplementary-material)). In multivariable analysis, receiving C-EEN was predictive of a reduced risk of 12-month relapse (aOR 0·26 \[95% CI 0·10–0·68\], p=0·0058), as was a wPCDAI score of 0 (0·30 \[0·10–0·87\], p=0·027) and normal CRP (<6 mg/L; after at least 6 weeks of induction therapy with EEN; 0·27 \[0·08–0·87\], p=0·025). When analysing the microbial profiles at inclusion (all samples were pooled as at M0 there would be no treatment effect of C-EEN *vs* PEN), neither the main enterotypes, nor beta-diversity, were significantly correlated with risk for 12-month relapse ([appendix pp 18–19](#supplementary-material)). Nevertheless, the relative abundance of several genera was significantly different at inclusion between patients who relapsed versus those who maintained remission until M12. Proportions of three Gammaproteobacteria (*Klebsiella, Enterobacter*, and *Citrobacter*) and two Bacillota (*Flavonifractor* and *Faecalimonas*) were significantly higher at baseline in patients who relapsed, and higher proportions of two Lachnospiraceae (*Anthropogastromicrobium* and *Anaerostipes*), two Peptoniphilaceae (*Murdochiella* and *Peptoniphilus*), and one Oscillospiraceae (*Huintestinicola*), all of them belonging to the Bacillota phylum, were found in those who maintained remission (Wilcoxon rank-sum p<0·05 for all**;** [appendix pp 20–21](#supplementary-material)). Most of the genera associated with relapse at inclusion were significantly related to fewer days to relapse—however, the predictive value of genera associated with remission was lower.

High proportions of baseline *Enterobacter* were significantly associated with a shorter median relapse-free survival in both treatment groups (C-EEN p=0·0048; PEN p=0·0007; [appendix pp 22–24](#supplementary-material)). 22 (88%) of 25 patients from the *Enterobacter* -high group at M0 relapsed over the course of the trial compared with nine (35%) of 26 patients in the *Enterobacter* -low group ([appendix pp 22–23](#supplementary-material)). Only three (12%) of 25 patients from the *Enterobacter* -high group remained in remission at M12, all being treated with C-EEN. However, being in the *Enterobacter* -low group at inclusion was not significantly predictive of a reduced risk of relapse (χ <sup>2</sup> p=0·12 in the *Enterobacter* -low group *vs* p<0·0001 in the *Enterobacter* -high group).

## Discussion

The CD-HOPE trial showed that children and adolescents with Crohn's disease responding to EEN induction therapy can be maintained in remission using nutritional therapy alone, with fewer participants relapsing by 12 months on C-EEN (49%) than on PEN (76%). Being on C-EEN versus PEN, having a baseline wPCDAI score of 0, or a baseline CRP of less than 6 mg/L were associated with a reduced risk of relapse, and a high faecal *Enterobacter* load at baseline was identified as risk factor for early relapse.

Nutritional therapy has a well established place as an induction therapy in Crohn's disease.

Our study has several limitations. The experimental design, by nature, was not blinded, which can potentially bias the results. We tried to overcome this bias by using blinded endpoint evaluations, but most evaluators were informed of the treatment's nature by the families or patients when interviewing them during the study visits. Inclusion was based on the wPCDAI (allowing comparison to previously published studies), but this score does not include objective inflammatory parameters, such as CRP or calprotectin. Another weakness of the study is the incompleteness of data, such as stool samples and responses to questionnaires, and the fact that we only had partial data on the pre-inclusion status of the patient, not capturing, for example, disease severity before starting EEN induction therapy. Faecal samples for calprotectin measurements were missing in approximately 30% of patients at baseline, which could introduce a bias. However, this value is within the range of another randomised controlled trial (in which almost 50% of samples were missing for microbial analysis).

Mucosal and transmural healing data are also compromised by missing examinations (14% for endoscopies and 49% for MRE). In addition, the MRE evaluation reported only normal or abnormal status, which did not differentiate between a reduction of inflammation or absence of inflammation—no validated MRE score existed at the time of designing CD-HOPE.

Recruitment into this trial was a clear challenge, reflected by the 4-year inclusion period. Both treatment-naive patients with newly diagnosed Crohn's disease and drug-exposed, relapsing patients with Crohn's disease were eligible. As almost 90% of included patients were treatment-naive and as nutritional therapy is the standard first-line therapy for treating new-onset paediatric Crohn's disease (except for patients with severe or perianal disease), the findings of CD-HOPE still remain relevant. However, this trial does not answer the question as to whether patients who relapse on anti-TNF biologics respond to this type of nutritional therapy—the number of patients with Crohn's disease exposed to anti-TNF biologics has markedly increased over the past 10 years (and only one patient in CD-HOPE was infliximab-exposed).

Another major drawback of this study (motivated by optimising care for this particularly vulnerable population of children and adolescents with IBD) is inherent to the experimental design: patients relapsing in either treatment group were allowed to start immediate medical rescue therapy (most often anti-TNF biologics) and stop the nutritional intervention. Therefore, some follow-up analyses had to exclude relapsing patients on medical therapy to avoid interfering with the results of those continuing nutritional therapy.

Finally, an additional drawback is that we did not monitor precise food behaviours in either treatment group. The diary recorded MODULEN IBD volumes and intake, but no recording of food behaviour was foreseen as required in the C-EEN group, and insufficient data were collected in the PEN group for analysis. When CD-HOPE was designed, the notion of proinflammatory or anti-inflammatory foods was not fully established. Today, we know that specific food components can affect the intestinal microbiome, mucosal barrier, and the body's immune responses. Therefore, we cannot exclude a potential bias of food behaviours in either group. We also acknowledge the absence of IBD dietitians.

In conclusion, CD-HOPE found that C-EEN alone could maintain drug-free remission over 12 months in around 50% of responders to EEN induction therapy, and has identified potential predictors for relapse that need to be validated in independent cohorts. Thus, CD-HOPE encourages further studies examining the use of nutritional approaches as maintenance therapy in selected patients with Crohn's disease.
