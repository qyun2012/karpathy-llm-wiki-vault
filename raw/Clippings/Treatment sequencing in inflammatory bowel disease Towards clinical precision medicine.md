## 1\. Introduction

[Inflammatory bowel diseases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/inflammatory-bowel-disease) (IBD), encompassing [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease) (CD) and [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ulcerative-colitis) (UC), have brought significant health challenges due to their relapsing nature and impact on [quality of life](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/quality-of-life) \[,\]. These chronic disabling conditions represent a burden for patients from the very first years following diagnosis \[,, \]. In this regard, IBD management has evolved in the last years, with a better understanding of disease impact on the patient \[\]. For several years, conventional therapies such as [thiopurines](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/mercaptopurine), [methotrexate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/methotrexate), aminosalicylates and corticosteroids were the cornerstone of IBD therapeutic management \[,\]. In the late 1990s, the introduction of anti-TNF-alpha agents revolutionized the treatment landscape for these conditions and paving the way for other advanced therapies \[\]. Since then, an expanding array of treatments has emerged, offering new hope for more personalized and effective therapeutic approaches \[\].

For more than ten years, anti-TNF alpha agents were the only advanced therapy available in the management of IBD patients and therefore led to the overtreatment of many patients due to the lack of other therapeutic options. In recent years, the expansion of novel drug options has led to defining relevant treatment targets to improve IBD outcomes. Management of IBD focused for a long time on symptomatic control with little effect on the progressive [disease course](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-course) \[\]. Indeed, studies showed that up to 50 % of patients in clinical remission presented objective biochemical or endoscopic activity of the disease \[,\]. Hence, therapeutic management of IBD shifted to incorporate biochemical and endoscopic disease control to prevent bowel damage and disability \[,\].

Recognizing the need to minimize [disease activity](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-activity) in the early stages of the disease has led to the adoption of a “treat-to-target” strategy in disease management, endorsed by the Selecting Therapeutic Targets in Inflammatory Bowel Disease initiative (STRIDE) and STRIDE-II guidelines \[,\]. This approach involves regular follow-up and systematic monitoring of treatment response within a specified timeframe, guided by a predefined treatment target. If the target is not achieved, treatment escalation is initiated, which may include [dose intensification](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/drug-dose-intensification) of the current therapy, the addition of a supplementary therapy, or switching to an alternative treatment option. Nevertheless, in clinical practice, this strategy needs to be part of a shared decision-making model between the clinician and the patient \[\].

The emergence of tighter therapeutic targets led to higher rates of treatment failure and with it the need for additional advanced therapies \[,\]. Currently, ten advanced therapies are authorized for the treatment of IBD in Europe, but their use is influenced by their reimbursement in each country. Furthermore, various considerations are influencing the therapeutic sequencing, such as patient comorbidities, preferred course of administration, disease location or severity, and prior advanced therapies exposure \[\]. Therapeutic sequencing based on patient-specific [risk stratification](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/risk-stratification) is an emerging concept that will help shape the future of personalized medicine. The aim of this narrative review is to reflect on which sequence allows us to reach treatment target for our patients and how this sequence can be optimized according to each patient starting from their first line of advanced therapy.

## 2\. Manuscript body

### 2.1. Can we learn from experience in rheumatology?

[Rheumatoid arthritis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rheumatoid-arthritis) (RA), [psoriatic arthritis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/psoriatic-arthritis) (PsA) and ankylosing [spondylitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/spondylitis) (AS) are the most common forms of inflammatory [rheumatic diseases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/rheumatic-disease) \[\]. Therapeutic management of patients with inflammatory [rheumatic diseases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rheumatic-disease) shares common challenges with the management of [IBD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/inflammatory-bowel-disease) patients. Up to a quarter of patients with RA do not respond to their first advanced therapy and up to 10 % of patients have persistently active or progressive disease despite two or more advanced therapies, underlining the need for an optimal treatment choice \[\]. However, no strong recommendations are available regarding the treatment sequence in patients with inflammatory rheumatic diseases to this day. AS guidelines state that in the lack of relevant head-to-head trials, and with efficacy seemingly similar across existing drugs, no treatment should be prioritized over another \[\]. Recent guidelines highlight the importance of shared decision-making in treatment selection while considering the risk-benefit profile, patient values and preferences, and treatment costs, raising challenges of creating an appropriate treatment plan for each individual. \[\]. Therapeutic sequencing in inflammatory rheumatic diseases requires more head-to-head trials and real-world data regarding therapeutic sequences. This includes identifying factors that influence treatment response, as highlighted in the research agenda of the latest guidelines in the field \[,,\].

### 2.2. Crohn's disease ()

The recent European Crohn's and [Colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/colitis) Organisation (ECCO) Guidelines, published in June 2024, recommend a tight disease control and treat-to-target approach as essential for managing patients with CD, highlighting improved outcomes when biological therapies are initiated earlier in the [disease course](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-course) \[,\]. However, the heterogeneity of patients and clinical situations, as well as the number of molecules available make it impossible for guidelines to cover all clinical situations precisely. Hence, without a proposal for a therapeutic algorithm for advanced therapies, the decision should be made individually, with consideration of efficacy, safety, disease and [patients characteristics](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/patient-characteristics), but also in regards of prior biological therapies used and healthcare system considerations. In addition, whether some therapeutic sequences lead to a loss of chance for the patients is still unknown.

#### 2.2.1. Bio-naïve patients

##### 2.2.1.1. Anti-TNF alpha

In routine clinical practice, anti-TNF alpha agents, [infliximab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/infliximab) or [adalimumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/adalimumab), are commonly used as a first-line therapy in bio-naïve patients, either to induce or to maintain remission. Infliximab should be used in combination therapy with [thiopurines](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/mercaptopurine) for at least 6–12 months when initiated \[\]. In luminal disease, no significant differences in terms of effectiveness or safety were found between [adalimumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/adalimumab) or [infliximab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/infliximab) \[\]. These data were supported by two [systematic review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/systematic-review) and network-meta-analyses, where infliximab and adalimumab showed better outcomes to induce clinical remission in patients with moderate-to-severe CD compared to other biological agents, and may therefore be preferred as first-line agents \[,\].

##### 2.2.1.2. Vedolizumab

[Vedolizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/vedolizumab), an anti-integrin, is recommended for the induction but also the maintenance of remission in CD \[\]. Nevertheless, its position in the therapeutic arsenal can still be debated, with data suggesting poorer outcomes after anti-TNF alpha failure \[\]. A simulation model using-world evidence demonstrated that vedolizumab used as first-line treatment achieved better scores in terms of Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROs) and surgery rates versus vedolizumab in second and third biologic position but remained slightly inferior to adalimumab in this indication. Moreover, using vedolizumab later in the therapeutic sequence led to shorter mean duration of response or remission achieved \[\]. A cohort including 13,222 patients with [IBD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/inflammatory-bowel-disease) showed that vedolizumab was more effective when used as the first-line compared to later lines of therapy (p < 0.001) \[\]. In this study, vedolizumab was prescribed by physicians as a first-line therapy to older patients, those with uncomplicated disease, and those without perianal or [upper gastrointestinal tract](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/upper-digestive-tract) involvement \[\]. [Vedolizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/vedolizumab) could therefore be discussed as a first-line therapy in patients with moderate-to-severe CD, with reassuring real-world data. In the EVOLVE study, no statistical differences were found between vedolizumab and anti-TNF alpha agents in terms of clinical effectiveness, but vedolizumab showed a better safety profile \[\]. Nevertheless, head-to-head trials are still needed to reinforce its place as a first-line treatment in moderate-to-severe CD.

##### 2.2.1.3. Ustekinumab

The SEAVUE study, a randomized, double-blind, parallel group trial, conducted on 386 patients with moderate-to-severe bio-naïve CD patients investigated the efficacy and safety of ustekinumab or adalimumab as a first-line therapy \[\]. The primary outcome was clinical remission defined by [CDAI](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease-activity-index) <150 at week 52. Both treatments were equally efficacious for achieving clinical remission and safe, with no between-group statistical differences \[\]. These results were supported by real-world retrospective studies, with higher ustekinumab use in older patients with comorbidities or history of [malignancy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cancer) \[,\]. However, recent studies showed conflicting results when compared to anti-TNF alpha agents. In a retrospective study conducted on 206 patients, clinical response rates at three months were 64 % in the ustekinumab group versus 86 % in the anti-TNF alpha group (infliximab and adalimumab) (p = 0.01) \[\]. In a cohort of 68 biological-naïve patients, adalimumab was superior to ustekinumab in achieving endoscopic remission at week 26 and 52, with no difference in clinical remission \[\].

##### 2.2.1.4. Other advanced therapies

More recent advanced therapies, such as [risankizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/risankizumab) and [guselkumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/guselkumab), anti-IL23 [monoclonal antibodies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/monoclonal-antibody), and [upadacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/upadacitinib), a small molecule inhibitor, showed efficacy in inducing and maintaining remission in patients with moderate-to-severe CD \[,, \]. There is hitherto no data regarding the use of these therapies in bio-naïve patients as a first-line therapy except for those retrieved from subgroup analyses of phase III trials. Network meta-analyses based on these data suggest that [risankizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/risankizumab) could be an interesting option as first-line therapy \[\]. However, additional studies are needed to confirm this encouraging impression. Owing to an unfavorable tradeoff between efficacy and safety in most of the clinical situations of bionaïve patients, [JAK inhibitors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/janus-kinase-inhibitor) do not seem a good candidate as first line therapy.

#### 2.2.2. After failure of anti-TNF alpha agents

Approximatively 10–20 % of patients will not respond to anti-TNF agents, defined as primary non-response, and another 30–40 % may lose response over time (secondary loss of response), or may be treatment intolerant \[\]. Patients with primary non-response to anti-TNF alpha agents are 24 % less likely to achieve remission with the second-line biologic agent when compared to patients with secondary loss of response or intolerance \[\].

##### 2.2.2.1. Use of another anti-TNF agent

The use of another anti-TNF alpha agents after failure of the first is common, with outcomes depending on the reason for withdrawal. A meta-analysis including 46 studies showed higher [remission rates](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spontaneous-remission) after withdrawal for intolerance (61 %), compared with secondary (45 %) or primary failure (30 %) \[\].

A [retrospective cohort study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/retrospective-cohort-study) was conducted on 118 patients with CD with primary failure to an anti-TNF alpha to evaluate the effectiveness of the introduction of a second-one in these patients \[\]. In this study, primary failure was considered if patients discontinued treatment for non-response (54 %) or partial response (46 %). After the introduction of a second anti-TNF agent, 51 % of patients achieved remission in the short term, and up to 64 % maintained remission at 24 months \[\].

The Spanish Eneida Registry was used to conduct a study in 1122 patients who experienced intolerance, primary failure, or secondary failure to a first anti-TNF alpha agent \[\]. Primary-failure was the reason for the introduction of a second-line treatment in 22 % of the patients. No difference in remission was found between patients who switch due to primary or secondary failure (39 % vs 42 %) \[\]. Therefore, switching patients to another anti-TNF agent can be discussed, even after primary failure, but with higher risk of loss of response throughout time \[,\].

##### 2.2.2.2. Ustekinumab and vedolizumab

In 2022, Parrot et al. conducted a meta-analysis comparing the effectiveness of ustekinumab or vedolizumab after failure of anti-TNF alpha agents in real-world cohorts. For induction of remission, both treatment were found equally effective, but as maintenance treatment, ustekinumab appeared to be more effective in terms of clinical remission, steroid-free clinical remission, biological remission but also treatment persistence \[\]. These results are supported by a retrospective multicenter study conducted on 536 patients with CD, with better clinical and objective outcomes in short and long term for ustekinumab compared to vedolizumab \[\]. Moreover, when compared to anti-TNF alpha agents, ustekinumab showed lower risk of drug discontinuation (p = 0.004) \[\], including when compared to vedolizumab \[\].

For patients with primary failure to anti-TNF alpha, data regarding vedolizumab effectiveness are limited, with response rates ranging from 41 % to 50 % \[\]. A meta-analysis showed no difference in treatment response to vedolizumab between patients with primary or secondary failure to anti-TNF alpha \[\]. These results are consistent with the post-hoc analysis of the GEMINI 2 and 3 trials, with similar efficacy results regardless of the type of prior anti-TNF alpha failure \[\].

On the contrary, response to ustekinumab appears to be influenced by primary failure to anti-TNF alpha. In the meta-analysis conducted by Singh et al., including two [RCTs](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/randomized-controlled-trial) for ustekinumab, patients with prior primary non-response to anti-TNF were 24 % less likely to achieve remission with second-line biologics, particularly when treated with ustekinumab \[\]. Nonetheless, even if ustekinumab response appears to be lower in patients with prior primary failure to anti-TNF alpha compared to loss of response, it remained efficacious in up to 30 % in the CERTIFI and UNITI 1 trials and should therefore still be considered in these patients \[\].

##### 2.2.2.3. Anti-IL23 agents

In biologic-exposed patients, risankizumab appears to be effective in inducing and maintaining remission, and could be therefore proposed as a second-line therapy \[,\]. The SEQUENCE study, an open-label, multicenter, randomized, efficacy assessment-blinded study, showed that risankizumab was superior to ustekinumab in achieving endoscopic remission, deep remission and mucosal healing at both week 24 and week 48 in patients with moderate-to-severe CD with prior failure to anti-TNF therapy \[,\]. In 69 multirefractory patients with CD, with an exposure to at least four advanced therapies, risankizumab was effective in inducing both clinical remission and endoscopic response at week 52, with no safety issues \[\]. These data are consistent with recent real-world studies regarding the effectiveness of risankizumab in medically resistant patients \[\]. Compared to other agents (ustekinumab or vedolizumab), risankizumab appears to have longer persistence rate as a 4th line treatment \[\].

Two other novel therapies, [guselkumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/guselkumab) and [mirikizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mirikizumab), could be effective in inducing and maintaining remission in CD patients with inadequate response or intolerance to conventional or biologic therapy. In the GALAXI-1 RCT conducted on 309 patients, guselkumab induced greater clinical and endoscopic improvement compared to placebo at week 12 \[\]. Similar results were found in maintenance, with high rates of clinical and endoscopic efficacy up to week 48 \[\]. After demonstrating its efficacy in [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ulcerative-colitis), a RCT on 191 patients showed that mirikizumab was effective in inducing endoscopic response after week 12, with a durable efficacy up to week 52, with a significant improvement in fatigue and [quality of life](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/quality-of-life) \[,\]. The VIVID-1 RCT, including 1150 patients, showed that mirikizumab was effective in inducing and maintaining clinical remission and endoscopic response compared to placebo, for both patients with and without failure to biological therapies \[\]. Nevertheless, real-world study are still needed, and both of these treatments are yet to be authorized in Europe for the treatment of CD.

##### 2.2.2.4. Small molecules

Post-hoc analysis showed that [upadacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/upadacitinib) could provide a rapid relief of clinical symptoms within the first week of treatment in patients with CD with higher clinical and endoscopic outcomes compared to placebo, regardless of the prior exposure to anti-TNF alpha agents \[,\]. Real-world data on the effectiveness and safety of upadacitinib are also promising, especially in patients with refractory CD. In a retrospective cohort study of 33 patients, 69.7 % of patients were in clinical response, and 27.2 % in remission at 12 weeks \[\]. Another retrospective study conducted on 135 patients showed clinical remission rates of 64 %, with 87.1 % of treatment persistence \[\]. Hence, upadacitinib appears to be an interesting therapeutic choice in patients with difficult-to-treat CD, even after multiple drug failure \[,\].

#### 2.2.3. Natural history of post-operative recurrence

Despite major progress in medical treatment for patients with CD, surgical intervention remains relatively frequent and 30–50 % of patients will undergo surgery in the first decade after diagnosis \[\]. Despite surgical treatment, up to 35 % of patients will require another surgical resection within 10 years of the first intervention \[\]. Rutgeerts et al. showed in 1990 that 73 % of patients had endoscopic recurrence of CD although only 20 % of patients had symptoms \[\]. Therefore, risk factors were identified in order to select high-risk patients and prevent post-operative recurrence by early introduction of prophylactic treatment \[\]. However, even if multiples risks factors were identified, no statistical correlation were found between post-operative recurrence and patients considered low or high risk \[,\]. The only robust statistical association found was for active smokers, with a 2.5-fold increased risk of surgical recurrence and a 2-fold risk of clinical recurrence compared to non-smokers \[\].

There is no consensus regarding the use of prophylactic treatment post-surgery and the decision should be made according to the evaluation of risks and after discussion with the patient. For example, ECCO guidelines suggests post-operative prophylactic treatment in patients with higher risks of recurrence, defined by active smoking and penetrating disease, whereas the AGA suggests early pharmacological prophylaxis to all patients \[,\].

##### 2.2.3.1. Prevention of post-operative recurrence

In terms of advanced therapy, the efficacy of infliximab in preventing post-operative recurrence was evaluated in 297 patients in the PREVENT trial, with significantly reduced endoscopic reccurence compare to placebo at week 76 (30.6 % vs 60 %, p < 0.001), but with no statistical difference in clinical recurrence \[\]. Several meta-analyses evaluating the efficacy of anti-TNF alpha agents in preventing clinical and endoscopic post-operative recurrence showed greater outcomes, both clinical and endoscopic, in patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents compared to therapies such as [thiopurines](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mercaptopurine), making anti-TNF alpha a line of choice for the prevention of post-operative recurrence \[,\].

[Ustekinumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ustekinumab) appears to be more effective in preventing post-operative recurrence, in comparison to thiopurines \[\]. In a retrospective study conducted on 297 patients, no difference in the risk of post-operative recurrence were found between patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents, vedolizumab or ustekinumab \[\]. These results are supported by the results of the ENEIDA registry, with rates of post-operative recurrence of 32 % and 30 % for ustekinumab and vedolizumab, respectively, at 12 months \[\]. The REPREVIO RCT enrolled 84 patients who underwent ileocolonic resection and had one or more risk factors for recurrence to receive either vedolizumab or placebo \[\]. At week 26, patients had a propability of 77.8 % to present a lower Rutgeerts score if treated with vedolizumab compared to placebo, and severe endoscopic recurrence was observed in 23.3 % of patients on vedolizumab versus 62.2 % of patient on placebo (p = 0.0004) \[\].

##### 2.2.3.2. Treatment of post-operative recurrence

To treat post-operative recurrence, anti-TNF alpha agents appears to be the treatment of choice, in terms of endoscopic or clinical remission \[,\]. A multicenter retrospective study conducted on 179 patients showed that, in comparison to the use of adalimumab, combination therapy with thiopurines was associated with endoscopic improvement (reduction in the baseline Rutgeerts score) and endoscopic remission (Rutgeerts score < i2), as well as the use of infliximab in patient with postoperative recurrence \[\].

Data regarding ustekinumab and vedolizumab remains limited. [Ustekinumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ustekinumab) appears to be a promising treatment for post-operative recurrence of CD: in a retrospective study conducted in 44 patients, endoscopic success (defined as reduction of at least one point of Rutgeerts score compared to baseline) was obtained in 50 % of patients, with 27.3 % with a Rutgeerts score of i0 or i1 \[\]. In a retrospective study conducted in 58 patients, vedolizumab showed endoscopic success in 47.6 % of patients, with 12.1 % of new resection needed \[\].

#### 2.2.4. Treatment according to disease phenotype and location

##### 2.2.4.1. Luminal Crohn's disease

Luminal CD has a predilection for affecting the ileocolonic region, but up to 30 % of patients will present with isolated colonic (30 %) or ileal (30 %) involvement \[\]. Several studies have shown that CD location can change over time. Patients with ileal CD (isolated or associated with colonic location) are at higher risk to develop intestinal complications (HR of 9.2 for isolated ileal involvement, and HR 5.7 for ileocolonic involvement) \[\]. These data were supported by the post-hoc analysis of the REACT trial, in which patient with ileocolonic and isolated ileal diseased had a two-fold increased risk for disease-related complications or surgery over a 24 month period compared to patients with [colonic disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/colon-disease) \[\]. Therefore, it is legitimate to question the therapeutic implication of disease location. A systematic review highlighted that isolated [ileal disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ileum-disease) location could be a negative predictor of response to anti-TNF alpha agents, as seen in several [cohort studies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cohort-analysis) \[\]. Patients with isolated [ileal disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ileum-disease) were also significantly less likely to achieve response or remission with an advanced therapy in a meta-analysis conducted on patients on [certolizumab pegol](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/certolizumab-pegol), but also patients from the GEMINI, CERTIFI and UNITI trials, compared to [colonic disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colon-disease) (29 % versus 38 % respectively, RR = 0.7, IC 95 % 0.56–0.87) \[,\]. Nonetheless, data remains inconsistent and there is no recommendation suggesting a different treatment regimen for patient with ileal or [colonic CD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colon-crohn-disease) \[\].

##### 2.2.4.2. Perianal fistulizing disease

Perianal fistulizing CD represents an aggressive phenotype of the disease, and can affect up to 30 % of patients during their disease course \[\]. Infliximab is the only advanced therapy that has shown showed superiority over placebo in achieving complete [fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/fistula) closure \[\]. Infliximab was also found to be efficacious as maintenance treatment in the ACCENT II trial, with 36 % of patients with complete [absence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/epileptic-absence) of fistulas at week 54, compared to 19 % in the placebo group \[\]. For adalimumab, most efficacy data is derived from sub-analysis of [RCTs](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/randomized-controlled-trial). For example, subgroup analysis of CHARM study showed superiority of adalimumab compared to placebo in [fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/fistula) closure after one year of treatment (33 % versus 13 %) \[,\].

No RCT has investigated ustekinumab for fistulizing CD. In a retrospective cohort conducted on 27 patients with [perianal fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/anus-fistula), 48.1 % of patient achieved fistula response at 6 months and 55.6 % at 12 months on ustekinumab. Those results are consistent with the data of a meta-analysis conducted on 25 studies, with rates of clinical response at 6 moths and 12 months of 44 % and 53.9 % respectively \[\].

Supported by the retrospective multicenter BioLAP Study Group study on 207 patients, clinical success (defined by the absence of additional medical or surgical treatment for perianal CD) at 6 months was reached in 38.5 % of patients, with a probability of recurrence-free survival in patients with inactive perianal disease of 86.2 % at week 26 and 75.1 % at week 52 \[\].

Data regarding vedolizumab are scarcer. The ENTERPRISE RCT investigated the use of two vedolizumab regimens in patients with moderate-to-severe CD with 1–3 active perianal fistulae \[\].At week 30, 53.6 % of patients achieved ≥50 % decrease in draining fistulae and 42.9 % of patients achieved 100 % fistulae closure \[\]. The sub analysis of the GEMINI 2 trial in patients with fistulizing disease showed higher rates of fistula closure at week 14 compared to placebo, with faster time to fistula closure \[\]. However, a retrospective study conducted in 151 patients with perianal lesions found low rates of clinical success at 6 months (22.5 %) with discontinuation rates as high as 68 % over a median time of 33 weeks. A third of the patients with inactive perianal CD had perianal recurrence, with a median time of 22 weeks \[\].

More recently, the HEAL study was conducted in 155 patients with complex [perianal fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/anal-fistula), with 136 on ustekinumab and 35 on vedolizumab (16 received both): both treatments appeared effective in achieving remission around half of the patients, with a median follow up of 27 months for ustekinumab and 19 months for vedolizumab \[\].

In the post-hoc sub analysis of ADVANCE, MOTIVATE and FORTIFY studies, fistula closure was observed with risankizumab in 28.6 % of patients in induction, and 57.1 % in maintenance, but further studies are needed \[\]. Upadacitinib probably appears as the most promising drug in the management of fistulizing CD. The [post hoc analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/post-hoc-analysis) of 3 phase 3 trials (U-EXCEL, U-EXCEED and U-ENDURE) conducted in 143 patients with fistulas, greater proportions of patients receiving upadacitinib versus placebo achieved resolution of drainage of perianal fistulas, but also closure of perianal fistula external openings, at the end of induction and maintenance \[\].

##### 2.2.4.3. Non-perianal fistulizing disease

Data regarding non-perianal fistulizing CD are scarce, with no RCT conducted specifically for this indication. Most data available were obtained from observational studies, with a small number of patients, and mainly on anti-TNF alpha agents \[,\]. For internal fistulizing CD, a retrospective cohort conducted in 156 patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents, 44 % of whom had enteroenteric fistulas, fistula healing rates were up to 44 % at 5 years, but with 43 % of patients requiring major [abdominal surgery](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/digestive-system-surgery) during the 3.5-year follow up and 20 % of patients who developed an intestinal abscess \[\]. A prospective study conducted on 117 bio-naïve patients with resolved intra-abdominal abscess treated with adalimumab showed high rates of adalimumab success at week 24 (74 %), with a 72.9 % [survival rate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/survival-rate) without abscess recurrence or surgery at week 104 \[\]. For patients with enterovesical fistula, an observational cohort conducted on 97 patients, with 33 patients on anti-TNF alpha, showed that 45 % of those patients achieved remission without surgery over a follow-up period of 35 months \[\]. These results are supported by a systematic review, with 14 patients with entero-vesical fistulas treated with anti-TNF alpha, who showed 57 % of complete response rates \[\]. In the same systematic review, 78 patients with [rectovaginal fistulas](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rectovaginal-fistula) treated with anti-TNF alpha had an overall response rate of 63 % \[\]. In a large national-wide retrospective cohort conducted on 204 patients with [rectovaginal fistulas](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/rectovaginal-fistula) on anti-TNF alpha, 37 % had complete fistula closure and 22 % had a partial response at 1-year follow up \[\]. For [enterocutaneous fistulas](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/enterocutaneous-fistula), anti-TNF alpha appears to be also effective, with rates of complete closure of 33 %, but with a relapse found in half of the patients during the follow-up period \[\].

Due to the lack of data regarding efficacy of other advanced therapies in patients with non-perineal fistulizing disease, anti-TNF alpha agents should be preferred in these patients for medical management \[\].

##### 2.2.4.4. Stricturing disease

Efficacy data regarding advanced therapies in structuring CD remains limited. Anti-TNF alpha agents are the only biological therapy with proven efficacy in this indication \[\]. In 2018, the multicenter prospective CREOLE study, conducted in 97 patients with symptomatic small bowel stricture, showed a successful response in 64 % of patients at week 24, defined as adalimumab continuation without corticosteroid, need for another biologic, and need for surgery or [endoscopic dilatation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/endoscopic-dilatation). In this study, 50.7 % of patients were surgery-free four years after treatment initiation \[\]. Seven [predictive factors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/predictive-factor) of response to anti-TNF alpha agents were then identified: concurrent use of thiopurines, [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease) obstructive score (CDOS) DOS >4, time since the onset of obstructive symptoms <5 weeks, [stenosis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/stenosis) length <12 cm, pre-stenotic maximal diameter between 18 and 29 mm, late contrast enhancement in T1 sequence and absence of associated fistula. Patients with a score ≤2, = 3 or ≥4 were associated with a success probability at 24 weeks of 6 %, 61 % and 89 % respectively \[\]. Therefore, identifying patients with low success probability of response to anti-TNF alpha agents could allow an early referral for surgical management, for example. Regarding ustekinumab, the multicenter retrospective USTEKNOSIS study compared 34 patients with symptomatic stricture treated with ustekinumab and 36 patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents (after prior exposure to anti-TNF). Clinical remission rates at 6 months were 73.9 % in the ustekinumab group and 42.7 % in the anti-TNF one, with no statistical difference. However, the risk of treatment discontinuation for failure or bowel damage progression was significantly greater in patients receiving another anti-TNF agent, suggesting that ustekinumab could be more effective than another line of anti-TNF after prior exposure to anti-TNF in patients with symptomatic stricture \[\]. In another multicenter prospective study conducted in 28 patients with symptomatic stricturing disease, ustekinumab showed success rates of 71.4 % at week 52 (defined by ustekinumab continuation without corticosteroids, need for another biologic, or need for surgery or endoscopic dilatation) \[\].

For vedolizumab, a multicenter study conducted on 44 patients with symptomatic stricture suggest that vedolizumab could be effective but in selected patients. Indeed, predictive factors of worse outcomes were a higher CRP, obstructive symptoms at baseline, and the need for corticosteroid co-induction \[\]. For both ustekinumab and vedolizumab, further studies are needed in order to guide their place in the therapeutic arsenal for stricturing CD.

#### 2.2.5. Treatment according to disease severity

To this date, there is no consensus definition for assessing disease severity in patients with CD \[\]. The evaluation of disease severity in IBD necessitate a global evaluation of the patient: evaluation of the impact of the disease regarding clinical symptoms, quality of life and disability; evaluation of the inflammatory burden with the extent, disease location and severity of bowel involvement and finally, disease course and degree of [intestinal damage](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/intestine-injury) \[\]. Patients with severe CD are usually considered as patients having a high risk for adverse disease-related complications, such as surgery, hospitalization, and disability, assessed using a combination of degree of structural damage and inflammatory burden, and impact on quality of life \[\]. Criteria for severe disease also includes factors such as large or deep mucosal lesions on [endoscopy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/endoscopy) or imaging, presence of fistulas and/or [perianal abscesses](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/perianal-abscess), presence of strictures, prior [intestinal resections](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/bowel-resection), presence of a stoma, extensive disease (define as an ileal involvement >40 cm or pancolitis), [anemia](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/anemia), elevated C-reactive protein, and low albumin \[\]. Regarding symptoms, an individual evaluation needs to be performed but generally accepted criteria are a high number of [loose stools](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/loose-feces) per day, daily abdominal pain (which are the two criteria to evaluate Patients Reported Outcomes 2 – PRO2), the presence of anorectal symptoms (anorectal pain, bowel urgency, incontinence, tenesmus), the need for [systemic corticosteroid](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/corticosteroid) use, lack of symptomatic improvement despite prior exposure to biologics and/or [immunosuppressive agent](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunosuppressive-drug) or a significant impact of the disease in [activities of daily living](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/activities-of-daily-living) \[,\]. To define disease severity, several scores are available, such as the Crohn's Disease Activity Index (CDAI), which defines a disease as severe if > 450, or the Harvey Bradshaw Index (HBI), with severe disease if > 12 \[,\]. Nevertheless, these scores rely predominantly on symptoms, with no involvement of other factors for disease severity, such as disability, disease course or structural damage.

Given the lack of validated tools to assess disease severity, a literature review proposed preliminary criteria that should be useful to classify disease severity in inflammatory bowel disease \[\]. Those criteria are separated across three domains: impact of the disease on the patient, with clinical symptoms, quality of life, fatigue and disability; inflammatory burden, with C-reactive protein, mucosal lesions, upper gastrointestinal involvement, and disease extent; disease course, with structural damage, history of intestinal resection, perianal disease, number of flares and extra-intestinal manifestations \[\]. Therefore, patients with criteria for severe disease should be identified as they might warrant a more aggressive therapeutic approach. Time to clinical response of remission should be taken into account when selecting an advanced therapy in these patients, with encouraging data in recent therapies such as [JAK inhibitors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/janus-kinase-inhibitor) or anti IL-23 \[,,\].

### 2.3. Ulcerative colitis ()

#### 2.3.1. Bio-naïve patients

In routine clinical practice, anti-TNF alpha agents (adalimumab, infliximab, and golimumab), vedolizumab, ustekinumab and anti-JAK (tofacitinib, upadacitinib, filgotinib) are approved for induction and maintenance of remission in moderate-to-severe [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ulcerative-colitis) \[\].

In the VARSITY RCT, 769 patients with moderate-to-severe UC were randomly assigned to receive vedolizumab or adalimumab. Of these, 25 % of patients had prior exposure to anti-TNF alpha agents. In the bio-naïve group, vedolizumab showed higher rates of clinical remission and endoscopic improvement at week 52, compared to adalimumab \[\]. Higher rates of histological remission at week 14 and 52, but also in terms of mucosal healing were found in bio-naïve patients in the vedolizumab group \[\]. These results were corroborated in a prospective observational study conducted on 512 patients with UC. No statistical difference was found in clinical remission rate during induction therapy between patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents or vedolizumab. However, clinical remission rates after two years were significantly higher for patients on vedolizumab compared to anti-TNF (43.2 % versus 25.8 %, p < 0.011), with rates of discontinuation of 29 % and 54 % respectively \[\]. A meta-analysis published in 2018 showed that infliximab and vedolizumab had higher rates of clinical remission and mucosal healing in bio-naïve patients, but with less serious [adverse effects](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/adverse-event) for vedolizumab \[\]. Among anti-TNF alpha agents, infliximab appears to be more effective than adalimumab or [golimumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/golimumab) to induce clinical response, remission and mucosal healing according to several meta-analyses \[,\].

Ustekinumab was proven efficacious in inducing and maintaining remission in patients with moderate-to-severe UC \[\]. An indirect meta-analysis did not find a statistical difference between ustekinumab and anti-TNF alpha agents or [tofacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/tofacitinib) for clinical and endoscopic outcomes in bio-naïve patients, suggesting its possible use as a first-line therapy \[\].

Regarding [tofacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/tofacitinib), even if its use as a first line therapy to induce and maintain remission in patients with moderate-to-severe UC is recommended, data remains scarce in bio-naïve patients \[,\]. Indeed, a post-hoc analysis from four [clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/clinical-trial) on 659 bio-naïve patients with UC showed that infliximab was superior to tofacitinib to induce clinical remission, endoscopic improvement or endoscopic remission at week 8, but baseline characteristics between groups mismatches despites a [propensity score matching](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/propensity-score-matching) \[\]. When compared to vedolizumab, tofacitinib showed lower persistence rates and [tolerability](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/tolerability) in 158 patients with UC \[\].

#### 2.3.2. After failure of first-line therapy

As a second-line treatment, ustekinumab and tofacitinib showed better outcomes for induction of remission and endoscopic improvement in patient with moderate-to-severe UC compared to adalimumab or vedolizumab in several meta-analysis \[,\].

A retrospective cohort study conducted in 2141 patients with UC and prior exposure to anti-TNF alpha agents showed that patients treated with ustekinumab had lower risk of colectomy within 2-years compared to patients on tofacitinib, and lower risk of IV steroids or colectomy within 2-years compared to vedolizumab \[\]. But those results are conflicted with two other retrospective studies, which showed no statistical differences in terms of rates of clinical remission and medication persistence between ustekinumab and tofacitinib, but also comparable efficacy between ustekinumab, vedolizumab and tofacitinib as second-line therapies \[,\]. Another multicenter retrospective study conducted on 121 patients with UC showed no statistical difference between ustekinumab or vedolizumab as a second line therapy for steroid-free clinical remission \[\]. However, data from recent real-world retrospective studies are emerging regarding patient with UC and prior failure to anti-TNF agents. In a retrospective study conducted in 293 patients, no difference in short-term clinical remission was observed between patients treated with either vedolizumab or ustekinumab \[\]. Nonetheless, ustekinumab could be more effective than vedolizumab in inducing endoscopic and histological remission at week 16 \[\]. Also in comparison to vedolizumab, tofacitinib appears to be more effective, particularly in patients with severe disease and primary failure to biologics, with higher rates of endoscopic improvement and histological healing at week 16, but no statistical difference in terms of corticosteroid-free clinical remission \[\].

When comparing vedolizumab to anti-TNF alpha agents as a second-line therapy, a study led on 13222 patients, second-line treatment with vedolizumab showed better outcomes up to the 3-year follow-up compared to either infliximab or adalimumab \[\].

Mirikizumab, an anti-IL23, has been recently approved by the FDA and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) as a second-line treatment in patients with moderate-to-severe ulcerative colitis. The LUCENT-1 and LUCENT-2 [clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/clinical-trial) showed that mirikizumab was superior to placebo in inducing and maintaining clinical remission, regardless of previous exposure to biologics, with rapid improvement of symptoms, as early as three weeks \[\]. The LUCENT trials are the first to specifically address the efficacy of the treatment on symptoms such as bowel urgency, using the Urgency [Numeric Rating Scale](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/numeric-rating-scale), with better outcomes at one year in patients on mirikizumab compared to placebo, making mirikizumab an interesting treatment option in patients experiencing such symptoms \[\].

For second-line treatment, if data on patients with previous exposure to advanced therapy are mainly available for patients with failure to anti-TNF alpha, data regarding treatment after failure of other treatments as a first-line therapy, such as vedolizumab, are emerging \[\]. Indeed, anti-TNF alpha agents are no longer the only advanced therapy recommended as a first-line treatment. Therefore, treatment sequencing is evolving and should be modulated by taking into account which advanced therapy was used as a first-line.

Further studies are required to determine the selection strategy for patients with UC as a second-line treatment, especially for ustekinumab and tofacitinib. In clinical practice, vedolizumab and infliximab, which showed better outcomes compared to adalimumab, should be preferred as a second-line treatment \[\].

#### 2.3.3. Ulcerative proctitis

At the time of diagnosis, 25–55 % of patients with UC will present of form of the disease limited to the rectum, called ulcerative [proctitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/proctitis), which often leads to highly debilitating symptoms \[\]. Up to 43 % of patients experience symptoms such as rectal urgency, which is considered to be one of the symptoms most associated with compromised quality of life \[\]. Ulcerative [proctitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/proctitis) can extend proximally in 20–80 % of patients after 5–20 years, and is usually associated with a severe clinical course and an increased risk of morbidity and complications \[,\]. Undertreatment and uncontrolled disease are risk factors for the development of an extensive disease justifying the need for effective and timely treatment \[\].

Refractory ulcerative proctitis is defined as persistent active disease despite rectal and oral therapy with aminosalicylates and corticosteroids, and often requires the use of advanced therapies \[,\]. Data from [randomized clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/randomized-clinical-trial) regarding the use of biologics in ulcerative proctitis are lacking, as patients with disease limited to the rectum are often excluded from these studies \[\].

In a retrospective cohort conducted on 104 patients with refractory ulcerative proctitis on anti-TNF alpha agents, 77 % of patients had a clinical response and 52 % of patients achieved clinical remission with a median duration follow-up of 24 months, and 60 % of patients obtained endoscopic remission within two years \[\]. In another retrospective study including 118 patients, 50 % of patients treated with anti-TNF alpha were in remission after a median follow-up of 21 months \[\].

A multicenter retrospective cohort conducted on 167 patients with refractory ulcerative colitis found that short-term steroid free clinical remission was significantly associated with vedolizumab treatment, with a higher drug persistence \[\]. In retrospective studies, remission rates in patients with refractory ulcerative proctitis on vedolizumab are 65–70 % \[\]. There is currently in sufficient data regarding the use of ustekinumab in ulcerative proctitis \[\].

Data regarding the use of JAK inhibitors appears promising. In a multicenter retrospective cohort including 35 patients with refractory ulcerative proctitis treated with tofacitinib, 42.9 % of patients were in steroid-free clinical remission and 60 % in clinical response after induction (week 8–14) \[\]. Clinical remission and response rates after remission at 1 year were respectively 39.4 % and 45.5 %, with more than half of the patients still on tofacitinib \[\].

#### 2.3.4. Treatment according to disease severity

As for the evaluation of disease severity in patients with CD (see section II.e), there is no consensus regarding the definition of the severity of patients with ulcerative colitis (apart from acute severe ulcerative colitis) \[\]. The Mayo Score is the main score used to define disease severity, with a threshold of >11 \[\]. Predictors for aggressive or severe ulcerative colitis identified were identified as: a high relapse rate (defined as the need for 2 or more courses of steroids and/or hospitalization for flares after diagnosis despite optimal treatment with [mesalamine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/aminosalicylic-acid) and an immunomodulator), need for surgery, development of [colon cancer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colon-cancer), and the presence of extra-intestinal manifestations \[\].

As for CD, other factors need to be accounted in the evaluation of disease severity, such as disability, disease course, and structural damage in order to guide therapeutic decisions. Data from the disease severity index can also be applied in patients with ulcerative colitis and should be used to identify patients who might require a more aggressive approach: impact of the disease on the patient, with clinical symptoms, quality of life, fatigue and disability; inflammatory burden, with C-reactive protein, mucosal lesions, and disease extent; disease course, with structural damage, history of intestinal resection, number of flares and extra-intestinal manifestations \[\].

### 2.4. Extra-intestinal manifestations

The co-existing presence of extra-intestinal manifestations or immune-mediated inflammatory conditions is another factor to consider when selecting the best treatment for our patients with concurrent skin, joint, hepatic, and/or eye inflammation.

#### 2.4.1. Primary sclerosing cholangitis

[Primary sclerosing cholangitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/primary-sclerosing-cholangitis) (PSC) occurs in 2 %–8 % of those with IBD and has higher risks of developing hepatobiliary or [colorectal cancers](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colorectal-cancer) \[,\]. There are no advanced therapies showing efficacy in both IBD and PSC, neither for anti-TNF alpha agents, ustekinumab, vedolizumab, nor tofacitinib \[,, \].

#### 2.4.2. Rheumatological manifestations

Rheumatological manifestations can occur in up to 40 % of patients, mainly manifested by non-axial and axial [spondylarthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spondyloarthropathy) \[,\].

For axial [spondylarthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/spondyloarthropathy) (SpA), the preferred choice of treatment remains anti-TNF alpha agents, either adalimumab or infliximab, with a proven efficacy in open-label cohort studies \[,, \]. Ustekinumab and vedolizumab should not be used for this indication, with data of a large retrospective cohort study showing worsening of existing [arthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/arthropathy) in 35.9 % of patients on vedolizumab, and 22.5 % of patients on ustekinumab, with no improvement of SpA \[\]. JAK inhibitors are recommend for the treatment of active [spondylitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spondylitis), and their use could be discussed in patients with SpA associated with IBD \[,\].

As for SpA, anti-TNF alpha agents are the recommended therapy for peripheral [arthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/arthropathy), with an improvement up to 73 % in [arthralgia](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/arthralgia) and arthritis \[,\]. Regarding ustekinumab, a systematic review of patients receiving ustekinumab for [arthralgia](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/arthralgia) or arthritis, including nine studies and 254 patients with IBD, demonstrated its effectiveness in psoriatic arthropathy and arthralgia, but those results were not supported in the post hoc analysis of the UNITI studies \[,\]. Therefore, ustekinumab could be considered in patient with peripheral arthropathy. Vedolizumab and tofacitinib should not be used for this indication \[,\].

#### 2.4.3. Dermatological manifestations

Dermatological manifestations are found in approximately 15–20 % of patients with IBD. [Psoriasis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/psoriasis) and IBD are significantly associated, and 7–10 % of IBD patients will develop psoriasis \[,\]. For patients with concomitant [plaque psoriasis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/psoriasis-vulgaris) or psoriatic arthritis, advanced therapies with efficacy proven in both conditions include, anti-TNF alpha agents, ustekinumab, risankizumab, and anti-JAK drugs \[,,,, \]. An RCT conducted on 166 patients with moderate-to-severe [plaque psoriasis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/psoriasis-vulgaris) showed that risankizumab was superior in terms of clinical response at week 12 compared to ustekinumab and could therefore be used prior to ustekinumab in patients with concomitant IBD \[\].

[Erythema nodosum](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/erythema-nodosum) is one of the most frequently encountered [skin disorders](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/dermatosis) in IBD, that runs a parallel course with IBD activity \[,\]. Therefore, advanced therapies with anti-TNF alpha agents, vedolizumab and ustekinumab can be used, probably due to their effect on treating IBD \[\]. Other therapies might also be used, but data are lacking.

[Pyoderma gangrenosum](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/pyoderma-gangrenosum) is the second most common cutaneous manifestation associated with IBD \[\]. Here, anti-TNF alpha agents should be preferred, with complete response rates of 58 % for infliximab and 67 % for adalimumab \[\]. Ustekinumab and tofacitinib have also shown efficacy and could second-line options \[,\].

[Sweet syndrome](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/acute-febrile-neutrophilic-dermatosis), a neutrophilic dermatosis, is rarely diagnosed in patients with IBD, but is the third most common underlying condition \[\]. Systemic corticosteroids can be used as a first-line treatment and anti-TNF alpha agents can be used in steroid-refractory or steroid-intolerant cases, especially infliximab \[\].

[Hidradenitis suppurativa](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/suppurative-hidradenitis) is a condition associated with IBD, and more commonly observed in CD than in UC \[\]. Topical treatment or [systemic treatment](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/systemic-treatment) (antibiotics and dapsone) can be used in mild-to-moderate disease, but for severe disease, adalimumab should be used, with early [dose intensification](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/drug-dose-intensification) often required \[\]. For refractory disease, infliximab and ustekinumab could be considered \[\].

#### 2.4.4. Ocular manifestations

The most frequent ocular manifestation in IBD patients is [anterior uveitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/iridocyclitis), and management with [topical steroids](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/topical-corticosteroid) is typically straightforward, but in cases of intermediate, posterior or [panuveitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/uveitis), advanced therapies may be needed. Anti-TNF alpha agents have showed efficacy for this indication, with either adalimumab or infliximab with the most evidence \[,\].

[Scleritis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/scleritis) is associated with IBD in less than 1 % of patients and first-line therapy are oral selective COX-2 inhibitors or corticosteroids. In cases of severe, sight-threatening cases refractory to immunomodulatory therapy or corticosteroids, infliximab could be proposed \[\].

### 2.5. Patients characteristics

#### 2.5.1. Elderly patients

The [epidemiology](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cohort-effect) of IBD is in constant evolution, with more elderly patients requiring care and, with it, new challenges due to comorbities, polymedication, increased risk of serious infection, or malignancy \[\]. Among patients with IBD, around 25–30 % are aged >60 years, and 10–15 % of new IBD diagnosis occurs in older patients (>60 years) \[\]. [Frailty](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/frailty) was demonstrated to be an independent predictor of mortality in IBD patients though not all elderly patients are frail, and the use of advanced therapies in these patients requires a careful assessment of the efficacy and safety profile according to their individual characteristics \[\].

##### 2.5.1.1. Anti-TNF alpha

In elderly patients, the use of anti-TNF alpha agents is associated with higher risk of adverse events such as infections and malignancy \[\]. A meta-analysis showed that elderly patients treated with biologics presented more infections than younger patients (OR 2.28) under the same therapy or elderly patients not treated with biologics (OR 3.60). Older anti-TNF alpha users had an elevated risk of infection (OR 3.48) and malignancy (OR 3.47) than younger users \[\]. Combination therapy with anti-TNF alpha agents and thiopurines posed the greatest risk for serious infection in patients aged ≥65 years, compared to [monotherapy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/monotherapy) \[\]. Moreover, higher neoplastic risks of lymphoma and [melanoma](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/nodular-melanoma) has been demonstrated with anti-TNF alpha agents \[,\].

##### 2.5.1.2. Vedolizumab

Due to its gut-selective mechanism, vedolizumab appears to be an attractive choice of therapy in elderly patients. A post-hoc analysis of the GEMINI trials showed similar safety in all subgroups, even in patients >55 years of age \[\]. These results were supported by retrospective cohorts, such as an American retrospective cohort conducted on 1152 IBD patients >65 years started on anti-TNF alpha and 480 on vedolizumab. Patients treated with vedolizumab had lower risk of infection-related hospitalization compared to patients on anti-TNF alpha \[\]. No higher risk of malignancy was found in patients treated with vedolizumab compared to placebo or conventional therapy \[,\].

##### 2.5.1.3. Ustekinumab

Most data on ustekinumab in elderly patients were extrapolated from its induction and maintenance trials in patients with CD and UC, with similar rates of adverse events between the ustekinumab and the placebo group, and between all age subgroups \[\]. These results are consistent with data in patients with psoriasis, with two retrospective studies that did not show increased risk of adverse events in elderly patients \[,\]. A retrospective study conducted on 182 patients found no difference in terms of safety in elderly patients with CD and UC treated with either vedolizumab or ustekinumab, suggesting that both treatment could be used safely in older patients with IBD \[\].

##### 2.5.1.4. Risankizumab

Data on safety in elderly patients for risankizumab are scarce, but a recent post-hoc analysis of the phase 3 trials showed that treatment was well tolerated across all age subgroups, with no specific safety issues in patients ≥65–80 years old \[\]. However, data from elderly patients with psoriasis treated with anti-IL23 are reassuring with similar rates of adverse events compared to younger patients \[\].

### 2.6. JAK inhibitors

Regarding JAK inhibitors, data are mixed and their use in elderly patients (usually defined >65 years) needs to be discussed and treatment decisions in this age group need to be made with caution. In the post-hoc analysis of elderly patients from the OCTAVE trial, patients in the elderly cohort had a higher percentage of discontinuation due to severe adverse effects, and a higher risk of [herpes zoster](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/disseminated-herpes-zoster) events \[\]. Data regarding safety of tofacitinib in patients with rheumatoid arthritis >50 years, with at least one cardiovascular risk factor, risks of major cardio-vascular events and malignancy were higher in patients on tofacitinib in comparison to patients on anti-TNF alpha agents \[\].

#### 2.6.1. Pregnancy and breastfeeding

Robust data confirms the safety of anti-TNF alpha agents but also vedolizumab and ustekinumab during pregnancy, with a prevalence of adverse pregnancy outcomes consistent with the prevalence of the general population \[,\]. If discontinuation of therapy during the third semester has been proposed, it is now recommended, due to reassuring data, to pursue treatment during the whole pregnancy, especially in patients with difficult-to-control disease, active disease or with recent flares before conception \[\]. Anti-TNF alpha, vedolizumab and ustekinumab can be continued during breast-feeding.

Lack of data regarding risankizumab do not allow its use in pregnant or breastfeeding women.

Tofacitinib showed [teratogenic effects](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/teratogenesis) in animals models, but with higher dosage than the ones used in humans. Nevertheless, lack of reassuring data contraindicates the use of anti-JAK inhibitors use in pregnant or breastfeeding women.

#### 2.6.2. History of malignancy

Currently, data regarding the risk of [cancer recurrence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cancer-recurrence) associated with biologics are limited, but tends to be in favor of the absence of an increased risk \[,\].

Recommendations suggest to wait 2–5 years after the completion [of cancer treatment](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cancer-treatment) to resume or initiate an anti-TNF alpha agent, with no higher risk of recurrence with anti-TNF alpha compared to control group receiving non-biologic disease-modifying treatments \[,,\]. There is no evidence of an increased risk of incident cancer with subsequent exposure to ustekinumab or vedolizumab \[,\].

Anti-JAK inhibitors should not be used in patients with history of neoplasia. A randomized open-label trial showed higher rates of malignancy, such as [nonmelanoma skin cancer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/non-melanoma-skin-cancer), in patients treated with tofacitinib in comparison with anti-TNF alpha agents \[\]. These results were consistent with a meta-analysis conducted on 62 RCT and 16 long-term extension studies, with higher risk of malignancy in patients treated with anti-JAK inhibitors (IRR 1.50; 95 % CI 1.16 to 1.94) \[\].

### 2.7. Future perspectives and conclusions

Therapeutic management of inflammatory bowel disease is aiming towards a highly individualized model, where a patient's clinical profile is taken into consideration in order to decide which therapeutic sequence is the best suited for each individual (). This approach, in an era of expanding therapeutic options, has shown promises in achieving sustained remission, reducing disease complications, and improving quality of life for patients with IBD.

Despite the growing number of available advanced therapies, clinical remission rates one year after their introduction remains under 50 %, even in bio-naïve patients \[,,\]. This therapeutic ceiling has led to the development of new strategies, including advanced combination treatment. This is defined by the combination of at least two biologic agents or a biologic agent and a small molecule with different mechanisms of action \[\]. Simultaneously targeting several pathogenic pathways could provide an additive or synergistic benefit to achieve disease control. Recently, the VEGA study investigated the combination of an anti-IL 23 agent (guselkumab) and an anti-TNF agent (golimumab) in 214 patients bio-naïve patients with moderate-to-severe UC, with results suggesting that combination therapy might me more effective compared to either treatment alone \[\]. However, combining advanced therapies remains experimental, with no data regarding the appropriate combinations for various IBD sub-populations \[\]. Several ongoing trials should help define the place of advanced combination therapy in the therapeutic management of IBD \[\].

[Therapeutic drug monitoring](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/therapeutic-drug-monitoring) has proven to be a useful tool in the management of patients with IBD in clinical practice to optimize the use of biologics, especially anti-TNF alpha agents, but there are still questions unanswered regarding its utility for other advanced therapies \[,, \]. Nowadays, with the growing number of advanced therapies available, therapeutic management of IBD favors a change of mechanism of action in case of inadequate response of treatment, rather than a within-class switch, a therapeutic strategy used in [rheumatology](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rheumatology) \[,, \].

In this era of [clinical medicine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/clinical-medicine), the “treat-to-target” strategy needs to be adapted via an open physician-patient discussion, considering treatment preferences, potential [side effects](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/side-effect) and lifestyle factors to enhance patient adherence and long-term success. Further studies are needed to validate sequencing algorithms. The appropriate positioning of different [targeted therapies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/targeted-therapy) in the course of the disease is one of the main challenges of optimizing the therapeutic management of IBD. A better understanding on a molecular level could refine personalized IBD care. For example, IL-12 was found to initiate the primarily innate immune inflammatory reaction. Hence, therapies such as ustekinumab could be more effective in newly diagnosed IBD. IL-23 is mostly implicated in the chronic phase of the inflammatory response, suggesting that anti-IL 23 therapies could show better efficacy in later stages of the disease \[\]. In addition to guiding the introduction of advanced therapies, molecular studies could also help to determine patients with higher probability of response to certain therapies. The blockade of anti-IL-23p19 has shown efficacy, for example, in patients resistant to anti-TNF, suggesting its use in this selected population. The inhibition of IL-23 could also restore sensitivity to the action of anti-TNF alpha non-responders, which could be a lead for a potential combination of advanced therapies, to improve efficacy and duration of response \[\].

Clinical data provide guidance for the adequate treatment, even if the number of available therapies and, therefore, of sequencing treatment plans available does not allows an investigation for each sequence. Randomized controlled trials, such as the SEQUENCE trial, comparing risankizumab to ustekinumab in patients with CD and previous failure to anti-TNF alpha, or the ongoing STARTER trial but also comparative effectiveness research, such as the ongoing prospective study I-CARE, are needed to refine the therapeutic approach in IBD patients \[,,\]. Indeed, the VARSITY trial changed the landscape for therapeutic research in the IBD field by being the first head-to-head trial between biologics \[\]. These studies are essential for refining treatment sequencing and ensuring optimal patient outcomes. Head-to-head trials play a critical role for health authorities and payers regarding, notably, the reimbursement of novel therapies in certain indications, and provide a more “ethical” approach, especially in patients with moderate to severe disease, with placebo-controlled studies often leading to a loss of opportunity for patients in the placebo group. However, even if the research landscape in IBD saw flourish several head-to-head trials, there are still numerous gaps in the current evidence [base](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/base) that could be addressed in the next years. Many questions remains unanswered such as the place of the new molecules of interest in the therapeutic arsenal, that are always considered as second or third line therapy at first or the need for trials to reinforce the place of certain molecules with a better safety profile as first line therapy, especially in selected population (elderly patients and patients with frailty, history of malignancy …) but also which strategy to implement for patients with a history of cardiovascular events that can't be started on anti-JAK or S1P. Despite the difficulties to investigate each sequence, future research will need head-to-head trials to refine the therapeutic proposal for IBD patients, such as the DUET trials (NCT05242471; NCT05242484) or the ongoing study with three targeted therapies (NCT06548542).

For decades, standard of care for IBD treatment was based on a “step-up” strategy, with aminosalicylates, steroids, and immunomodulators used as first line therapies. In case of treatment failure, such as [disease progression](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/disease-exacerbation) or complication, treatment could be change in favor of biological therapies or small molecules \[\]. Nevertheless, early introduction of advanced therapies compared to conventional management is more effective to induce remission and to reduce the use of corticosteroids \[\]. In [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/crohns-disease), early introduction of biologics is associated with lower rates of surgery, higher rates of endoscopic remission, and less patients developing structuring disease behavior \[,\]. This strategy, also called “top-down” approach, is however raising concerns, such as overtreatment of patients with milder disease, higher costs of treatment, but also higher risks of adverse events \[,\]. The REACT study, a cluster randomized controlled trial conducted in 2015 on 1982 patients with Crohn's disease, showed that early combined [immunosuppression](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunosuppressive-treatment) had lower risk of major [adverse outcomes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/adverse-outcome) (defined by surgery, hospital admission or serious disease-related complication) compared to conventional management (\[HR\]: 0·73, 95 % CI 0·62 to 0·86, p = 0·0003), with no difference in symptom control \[\]. More recently, the stratified randomized PROFILE trial, showed that a top-down strategy (combination of infliximab and [immunomodulator](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunomodulating-agent) initiated at the time of diagnosis) was superior to an accelerated step-up strategy with higher rates of sustained steroid-free and surgery-free remission, and higher endoscopic remission rates at week 48. To be noted, in the PROFILE trial, patients were naïve of biologics, with symptomatic disease and elevated CRP or faecal calprotectine, with endoscopic signs of disease activity \[\]. However, one of the limitation of the PROFILE study is the inability to develop a new objective marker to guide therapeutic strategy, which did not show clinical utility, suggesting that the top-down strategy couldn't be possibly extrapolated to every patient. Real-world studies are still needed to determine if a top-down strategy is a reasonable approach to propose to our patients, with raising concerns regarding the economic burden of this approach, as well as the risk for adverse events, but should be discussed with patients with newly diagnosed Crohn's disease.

IBD management is evolving towards a more individualized approach, integrating patient characteristics and disease profiles to optimize outcomes. The development of advanced therapies and the growing focus on sequencing strategies hold the potential to improve remission rates, reduce complications, and enhance quality of life for patients. However, further clinical trials and real-world evidence are required to refine sequencing algorithms and fully implement personalized therapeutic strategies.

## 3\. Practice points

- •
	Despite the growing number of available advanced therapies, clinical [remission rates](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spontaneous-remission) one year after their introduction remains under 50 %, even in bio-naïve patients.
- •
	Therapeutic sequencing based on individual characteristics, such as disease severity, prior therapies and comorbidities, ensures a more effective and safer treatment strategy.
- •
	Better long-term management and adherence are promoted when patient preferences and risk-benefit evaluations are incorporated into the therapeutic decision-making process.

## 4\. Research agenda

- •
	Future research is needed to define and validate sequencing algorithms in order to optimize the use of advanced therapies.
- •
	Further studies are needed to investigate the efficacy and safety of combining biologics or small molecules to improve remission rates in refractory or severe patients with IBD.
- •
	The role of [therapeutic drug monitoring](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/therapeutic-drug-monitoring) needs to be explored for novel advanced therapies.
- •
	The development of predictive biomarkers and [molecular profiling](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/molecular-profiling) technique could be a research focus to identify patients most likely to benefit from specific therapeutic classes.

[^1]: ## 1\. Introduction

[Inflammatory bowel diseases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/inflammatory-bowel-disease) (IBD), encompassing [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease) (CD) and [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ulcerative-colitis) (UC), have brought significant health challenges due to their relapsing nature and impact on [quality of life](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/quality-of-life) \[,\]. These chronic disabling conditions represent a burden for patients from the very first years following diagnosis \[,, \]. In this regard, IBD management has evolved in the last years, with a better understanding of disease impact on the patient \[\]. For several years, conventional therapies such as [thiopurines](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/mercaptopurine), [methotrexate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/methotrexate), aminosalicylates and corticosteroids were the cornerstone of IBD therapeutic management \[,\]. In the late 1990s, the introduction of anti-TNF-alpha agents revolutionized the treatment landscape for these conditions and paving the way for other advanced therapies \[\]. Since then, an expanding array of treatments has emerged, offering new hope for more personalized and effective therapeutic approaches \[\].

For more than ten years, anti-TNF alpha agents were the only advanced therapy available in the management of IBD patients and therefore led to the overtreatment of many patients due to the lack of other therapeutic options. In recent years, the expansion of novel drug options has led to defining relevant treatment targets to improve IBD outcomes. Management of IBD focused for a long time on symptomatic control with little effect on the progressive [disease course](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-course) \[\]. Indeed, studies showed that up to 50 % of patients in clinical remission presented objective biochemical or endoscopic activity of the disease \[,\]. Hence, therapeutic management of IBD shifted to incorporate biochemical and endoscopic disease control to prevent bowel damage and disability \[,\].

Recognizing the need to minimize [disease activity](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-activity) in the early stages of the disease has led to the adoption of a “treat-to-target” strategy in disease management, endorsed by the Selecting Therapeutic Targets in Inflammatory Bowel Disease initiative (STRIDE) and STRIDE-II guidelines \[,\]. This approach involves regular follow-up and systematic monitoring of treatment response within a specified timeframe, guided by a predefined treatment target. If the target is not achieved, treatment escalation is initiated, which may include [dose intensification](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/drug-dose-intensification) of the current therapy, the addition of a supplementary therapy, or switching to an alternative treatment option. Nevertheless, in clinical practice, this strategy needs to be part of a shared decision-making model between the clinician and the patient \[\].

The emergence of tighter therapeutic targets led to higher rates of treatment failure and with it the need for additional advanced therapies \[,\]. Currently, ten advanced therapies are authorized for the treatment of IBD in Europe, but their use is influenced by their reimbursement in each country. Furthermore, various considerations are influencing the therapeutic sequencing, such as patient comorbidities, preferred course of administration, disease location or severity, and prior advanced therapies exposure \[\]. Therapeutic sequencing based on patient-specific [risk stratification](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/risk-stratification) is an emerging concept that will help shape the future of personalized medicine. The aim of this narrative review is to reflect on which sequence allows us to reach treatment target for our patients and how this sequence can be optimized according to each patient starting from their first line of advanced therapy.

### 2.1. Can we learn from experience in rheumatology?

[Rheumatoid arthritis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rheumatoid-arthritis) (RA), [psoriatic arthritis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/psoriatic-arthritis) (PsA) and ankylosing [spondylitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/spondylitis) (AS) are the most common forms of inflammatory [rheumatic diseases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/rheumatic-disease) \[\]. Therapeutic management of patients with inflammatory [rheumatic diseases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rheumatic-disease) shares common challenges with the management of [IBD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/inflammatory-bowel-disease) patients. Up to a quarter of patients with RA do not respond to their first advanced therapy and up to 10 % of patients have persistently active or progressive disease despite two or more advanced therapies, underlining the need for an optimal treatment choice \[\]. However, no strong recommendations are available regarding the treatment sequence in patients with inflammatory rheumatic diseases to this day. AS guidelines state that in the lack of relevant head-to-head trials, and with efficacy seemingly similar across existing drugs, no treatment should be prioritized over another \[\]. Recent guidelines highlight the importance of shared decision-making in treatment selection while considering the risk-benefit profile, patient values and preferences, and treatment costs, raising challenges of creating an appropriate treatment plan for each individual. \[\]. Therapeutic sequencing in inflammatory rheumatic diseases requires more head-to-head trials and real-world data regarding therapeutic sequences. This includes identifying factors that influence treatment response, as highlighted in the research agenda of the latest guidelines in the field \[,,\].

### 2.2. Crohn's disease ()

The recent European Crohn's and [Colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/colitis) Organisation (ECCO) Guidelines, published in June 2024, recommend a tight disease control and treat-to-target approach as essential for managing patients with CD, highlighting improved outcomes when biological therapies are initiated earlier in the [disease course](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-course) \[,\]. However, the heterogeneity of patients and clinical situations, as well as the number of molecules available make it impossible for guidelines to cover all clinical situations precisely. Hence, without a proposal for a therapeutic algorithm for advanced therapies, the decision should be made individually, with consideration of efficacy, safety, disease and [patients characteristics](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/patient-characteristics), but also in regards of prior biological therapies used and healthcare system considerations. In addition, whether some therapeutic sequences lead to a loss of chance for the patients is still unknown.

#### 2.2.1. Bio-naïve patients

##### 2.2.1.1. Anti-TNF alpha

In routine clinical practice, anti-TNF alpha agents, [infliximab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/infliximab) or [adalimumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/adalimumab), are commonly used as a first-line therapy in bio-naïve patients, either to induce or to maintain remission. Infliximab should be used in combination therapy with [thiopurines](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/mercaptopurine) for at least 6–12 months when initiated \[\]. In luminal disease, no significant differences in terms of effectiveness or safety were found between [adalimumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/adalimumab) or [infliximab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/infliximab) \[\]. These data were supported by two [systematic review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/systematic-review) and network-meta-analyses, where infliximab and adalimumab showed better outcomes to induce clinical remission in patients with moderate-to-severe CD compared to other biological agents, and may therefore be preferred as first-line agents \[,\].

##### 2.2.1.2. Vedolizumab

[Vedolizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/vedolizumab), an anti-integrin, is recommended for the induction but also the maintenance of remission in CD \[\]. Nevertheless, its position in the therapeutic arsenal can still be debated, with data suggesting poorer outcomes after anti-TNF alpha failure \[\]. A simulation model using-world evidence demonstrated that vedolizumab used as first-line treatment achieved better scores in terms of Patient-Reported Outcomes (PROs) and surgery rates versus vedolizumab in second and third biologic position but remained slightly inferior to adalimumab in this indication. Moreover, using vedolizumab later in the therapeutic sequence led to shorter mean duration of response or remission achieved \[\]. A cohort including 13,222 patients with [IBD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/inflammatory-bowel-disease) showed that vedolizumab was more effective when used as the first-line compared to later lines of therapy (p < 0.001) \[\]. In this study, vedolizumab was prescribed by physicians as a first-line therapy to older patients, those with uncomplicated disease, and those without perianal or [upper gastrointestinal tract](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/upper-digestive-tract) involvement \[\]. [Vedolizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/vedolizumab) could therefore be discussed as a first-line therapy in patients with moderate-to-severe CD, with reassuring real-world data. In the EVOLVE study, no statistical differences were found between vedolizumab and anti-TNF alpha agents in terms of clinical effectiveness, but vedolizumab showed a better safety profile \[\]. Nevertheless, head-to-head trials are still needed to reinforce its place as a first-line treatment in moderate-to-severe CD.

##### 2.2.1.3. Ustekinumab

The SEAVUE study, a randomized, double-blind, parallel group trial, conducted on 386 patients with moderate-to-severe bio-naïve CD patients investigated the efficacy and safety of ustekinumab or adalimumab as a first-line therapy \[\]. The primary outcome was clinical remission defined by [CDAI](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease-activity-index) <150 at week 52. Both treatments were equally efficacious for achieving clinical remission and safe, with no between-group statistical differences \[\]. These results were supported by real-world retrospective studies, with higher ustekinumab use in older patients with comorbidities or history of [malignancy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cancer) \[,\]. However, recent studies showed conflicting results when compared to anti-TNF alpha agents. In a retrospective study conducted on 206 patients, clinical response rates at three months were 64 % in the ustekinumab group versus 86 % in the anti-TNF alpha group (infliximab and adalimumab) (p = 0.01) \[\]. In a cohort of 68 biological-naïve patients, adalimumab was superior to ustekinumab in achieving endoscopic remission at week 26 and 52, with no difference in clinical remission \[\].

##### 2.2.1.4. Other advanced therapies

More recent advanced therapies, such as [risankizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/risankizumab) and [guselkumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/guselkumab), anti-IL23 [monoclonal antibodies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/monoclonal-antibody), and [upadacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/upadacitinib), a small molecule inhibitor, showed efficacy in inducing and maintaining remission in patients with moderate-to-severe CD \[,, \]. There is hitherto no data regarding the use of these therapies in bio-naïve patients as a first-line therapy except for those retrieved from subgroup analyses of phase III trials. Network meta-analyses based on these data suggest that [risankizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/risankizumab) could be an interesting option as first-line therapy \[\]. However, additional studies are needed to confirm this encouraging impression. Owing to an unfavorable tradeoff between efficacy and safety in most of the clinical situations of bionaïve patients, [JAK inhibitors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/janus-kinase-inhibitor) do not seem a good candidate as first line therapy.

#### 2.2.2. After failure of anti-TNF alpha agents

Approximatively 10–20 % of patients will not respond to anti-TNF agents, defined as primary non-response, and another 30–40 % may lose response over time (secondary loss of response), or may be treatment intolerant \[\]. Patients with primary non-response to anti-TNF alpha agents are 24 % less likely to achieve remission with the second-line biologic agent when compared to patients with secondary loss of response or intolerance \[\].

##### 2.2.2.1. Use of another anti-TNF agent

The use of another anti-TNF alpha agents after failure of the first is common, with outcomes depending on the reason for withdrawal. A meta-analysis including 46 studies showed higher [remission rates](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spontaneous-remission) after withdrawal for intolerance (61 %), compared with secondary (45 %) or primary failure (30 %) \[\].

A [retrospective cohort study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/retrospective-cohort-study) was conducted on 118 patients with CD with primary failure to an anti-TNF alpha to evaluate the effectiveness of the introduction of a second-one in these patients \[\]. In this study, primary failure was considered if patients discontinued treatment for non-response (54 %) or partial response (46 %). After the introduction of a second anti-TNF agent, 51 % of patients achieved remission in the short term, and up to 64 % maintained remission at 24 months \[\].

The Spanish Eneida Registry was used to conduct a study in 1122 patients who experienced intolerance, primary failure, or secondary failure to a first anti-TNF alpha agent \[\]. Primary-failure was the reason for the introduction of a second-line treatment in 22 % of the patients. No difference in remission was found between patients who switch due to primary or secondary failure (39 % vs 42 %) \[\]. Therefore, switching patients to another anti-TNF agent can be discussed, even after primary failure, but with higher risk of loss of response throughout time \[,\].

##### 2.2.2.2. Ustekinumab and vedolizumab

In 2022, Parrot et al. conducted a meta-analysis comparing the effectiveness of ustekinumab or vedolizumab after failure of anti-TNF alpha agents in real-world cohorts. For induction of remission, both treatment were found equally effective, but as maintenance treatment, ustekinumab appeared to be more effective in terms of clinical remission, steroid-free clinical remission, biological remission but also treatment persistence \[\]. These results are supported by a retrospective multicenter study conducted on 536 patients with CD, with better clinical and objective outcomes in short and long term for ustekinumab compared to vedolizumab \[\]. Moreover, when compared to anti-TNF alpha agents, ustekinumab showed lower risk of drug discontinuation (p = 0.004) \[\], including when compared to vedolizumab \[\].

For patients with primary failure to anti-TNF alpha, data regarding vedolizumab effectiveness are limited, with response rates ranging from 41 % to 50 % \[\]. A meta-analysis showed no difference in treatment response to vedolizumab between patients with primary or secondary failure to anti-TNF alpha \[\]. These results are consistent with the post-hoc analysis of the GEMINI 2 and 3 trials, with similar efficacy results regardless of the type of prior anti-TNF alpha failure \[\].

On the contrary, response to ustekinumab appears to be influenced by primary failure to anti-TNF alpha. In the meta-analysis conducted by Singh et al., including two [RCTs](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/randomized-controlled-trial) for ustekinumab, patients with prior primary non-response to anti-TNF were 24 % less likely to achieve remission with second-line biologics, particularly when treated with ustekinumab \[\]. Nonetheless, even if ustekinumab response appears to be lower in patients with prior primary failure to anti-TNF alpha compared to loss of response, it remained efficacious in up to 30 % in the CERTIFI and UNITI 1 trials and should therefore still be considered in these patients \[\].

##### 2.2.2.3. Anti-IL23 agents

In biologic-exposed patients, risankizumab appears to be effective in inducing and maintaining remission, and could be therefore proposed as a second-line therapy \[,\]. The SEQUENCE study, an open-label, multicenter, randomized, efficacy assessment-blinded study, showed that risankizumab was superior to ustekinumab in achieving endoscopic remission, deep remission and mucosal healing at both week 24 and week 48 in patients with moderate-to-severe CD with prior failure to anti-TNF therapy \[,\]. In 69 multirefractory patients with CD, with an exposure to at least four advanced therapies, risankizumab was effective in inducing both clinical remission and endoscopic response at week 52, with no safety issues \[\]. These data are consistent with recent real-world studies regarding the effectiveness of risankizumab in medically resistant patients \[\]. Compared to other agents (ustekinumab or vedolizumab), risankizumab appears to have longer persistence rate as a 4th line treatment \[\].

Two other novel therapies, [guselkumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/guselkumab) and [mirikizumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mirikizumab), could be effective in inducing and maintaining remission in CD patients with inadequate response or intolerance to conventional or biologic therapy. In the GALAXI-1 RCT conducted on 309 patients, guselkumab induced greater clinical and endoscopic improvement compared to placebo at week 12 \[\]. Similar results were found in maintenance, with high rates of clinical and endoscopic efficacy up to week 48 \[\]. After demonstrating its efficacy in [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ulcerative-colitis), a RCT on 191 patients showed that mirikizumab was effective in inducing endoscopic response after week 12, with a durable efficacy up to week 52, with a significant improvement in fatigue and [quality of life](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/quality-of-life) \[,\]. The VIVID-1 RCT, including 1150 patients, showed that mirikizumab was effective in inducing and maintaining clinical remission and endoscopic response compared to placebo, for both patients with and without failure to biological therapies \[\]. Nevertheless, real-world study are still needed, and both of these treatments are yet to be authorized in Europe for the treatment of CD.

##### 2.2.2.4. Small molecules

Post-hoc analysis showed that [upadacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/upadacitinib) could provide a rapid relief of clinical symptoms within the first week of treatment in patients with CD with higher clinical and endoscopic outcomes compared to placebo, regardless of the prior exposure to anti-TNF alpha agents \[,\]. Real-world data on the effectiveness and safety of upadacitinib are also promising, especially in patients with refractory CD. In a retrospective cohort study of 33 patients, 69.7 % of patients were in clinical response, and 27.2 % in remission at 12 weeks \[\]. Another retrospective study conducted on 135 patients showed clinical remission rates of 64 %, with 87.1 % of treatment persistence \[\]. Hence, upadacitinib appears to be an interesting therapeutic choice in patients with difficult-to-treat CD, even after multiple drug failure \[,\].

#### 2.2.3. Natural history of post-operative recurrence

Despite major progress in medical treatment for patients with CD, surgical intervention remains relatively frequent and 30–50 % of patients will undergo surgery in the first decade after diagnosis \[\]. Despite surgical treatment, up to 35 % of patients will require another surgical resection within 10 years of the first intervention \[\]. Rutgeerts et al. showed in 1990 that 73 % of patients had endoscopic recurrence of CD although only 20 % of patients had symptoms \[\]. Therefore, risk factors were identified in order to select high-risk patients and prevent post-operative recurrence by early introduction of prophylactic treatment \[\]. However, even if multiples risks factors were identified, no statistical correlation were found between post-operative recurrence and patients considered low or high risk \[,\]. The only robust statistical association found was for active smokers, with a 2.5-fold increased risk of surgical recurrence and a 2-fold risk of clinical recurrence compared to non-smokers \[\].

There is no consensus regarding the use of prophylactic treatment post-surgery and the decision should be made according to the evaluation of risks and after discussion with the patient. For example, ECCO guidelines suggests post-operative prophylactic treatment in patients with higher risks of recurrence, defined by active smoking and penetrating disease, whereas the AGA suggests early pharmacological prophylaxis to all patients \[,\].

##### 2.2.3.1. Prevention of post-operative recurrence

In terms of advanced therapy, the efficacy of infliximab in preventing post-operative recurrence was evaluated in 297 patients in the PREVENT trial, with significantly reduced endoscopic reccurence compare to placebo at week 76 (30.6 % vs 60 %, p < 0.001), but with no statistical difference in clinical recurrence \[\]. Several meta-analyses evaluating the efficacy of anti-TNF alpha agents in preventing clinical and endoscopic post-operative recurrence showed greater outcomes, both clinical and endoscopic, in patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents compared to therapies such as [thiopurines](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/mercaptopurine), making anti-TNF alpha a line of choice for the prevention of post-operative recurrence \[,\].

[Ustekinumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ustekinumab) appears to be more effective in preventing post-operative recurrence, in comparison to thiopurines \[\]. In a retrospective study conducted on 297 patients, no difference in the risk of post-operative recurrence were found between patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents, vedolizumab or ustekinumab \[\]. These results are supported by the results of the ENEIDA registry, with rates of post-operative recurrence of 32 % and 30 % for ustekinumab and vedolizumab, respectively, at 12 months \[\]. The REPREVIO RCT enrolled 84 patients who underwent ileocolonic resection and had one or more risk factors for recurrence to receive either vedolizumab or placebo \[\]. At week 26, patients had a propability of 77.8 % to present a lower Rutgeerts score if treated with vedolizumab compared to placebo, and severe endoscopic recurrence was observed in 23.3 % of patients on vedolizumab versus 62.2 % of patient on placebo (p = 0.0004) \[\].

##### 2.2.3.2. Treatment of post-operative recurrence

To treat post-operative recurrence, anti-TNF alpha agents appears to be the treatment of choice, in terms of endoscopic or clinical remission \[,\]. A multicenter retrospective study conducted on 179 patients showed that, in comparison to the use of adalimumab, combination therapy with thiopurines was associated with endoscopic improvement (reduction in the baseline Rutgeerts score) and endoscopic remission (Rutgeerts score < i2), as well as the use of infliximab in patient with postoperative recurrence \[\].

Data regarding ustekinumab and vedolizumab remains limited. [Ustekinumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ustekinumab) appears to be a promising treatment for post-operative recurrence of CD: in a retrospective study conducted in 44 patients, endoscopic success (defined as reduction of at least one point of Rutgeerts score compared to baseline) was obtained in 50 % of patients, with 27.3 % with a Rutgeerts score of i0 or i1 \[\]. In a retrospective study conducted in 58 patients, vedolizumab showed endoscopic success in 47.6 % of patients, with 12.1 % of new resection needed \[\].

#### 2.2.4. Treatment according to disease phenotype and location

##### 2.2.4.1. Luminal Crohn's disease

Luminal CD has a predilection for affecting the ileocolonic region, but up to 30 % of patients will present with isolated colonic (30 %) or ileal (30 %) involvement \[\]. Several studies have shown that CD location can change over time. Patients with ileal CD (isolated or associated with colonic location) are at higher risk to develop intestinal complications (HR of 9.2 for isolated ileal involvement, and HR 5.7 for ileocolonic involvement) \[\]. These data were supported by the post-hoc analysis of the REACT trial, in which patient with ileocolonic and isolated ileal diseased had a two-fold increased risk for disease-related complications or surgery over a 24 month period compared to patients with [colonic disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/colon-disease) \[\]. Therefore, it is legitimate to question the therapeutic implication of disease location. A systematic review highlighted that isolated [ileal disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ileum-disease) location could be a negative predictor of response to anti-TNF alpha agents, as seen in several [cohort studies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cohort-analysis) \[\]. Patients with isolated [ileal disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ileum-disease) were also significantly less likely to achieve response or remission with an advanced therapy in a meta-analysis conducted on patients on [certolizumab pegol](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/certolizumab-pegol), but also patients from the GEMINI, CERTIFI and UNITI trials, compared to [colonic disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colon-disease) (29 % versus 38 % respectively, RR = 0.7, IC 95 % 0.56–0.87) \[,\]. Nonetheless, data remains inconsistent and there is no recommendation suggesting a different treatment regimen for patient with ileal or [colonic CD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colon-crohn-disease) \[\].

##### 2.2.4.2. Perianal fistulizing disease

Perianal fistulizing CD represents an aggressive phenotype of the disease, and can affect up to 30 % of patients during their disease course \[\]. Infliximab is the only advanced therapy that has shown showed superiority over placebo in achieving complete [fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/fistula) closure \[\]. Infliximab was also found to be efficacious as maintenance treatment in the ACCENT II trial, with 36 % of patients with complete [absence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/epileptic-absence) of fistulas at week 54, compared to 19 % in the placebo group \[\]. For adalimumab, most efficacy data is derived from sub-analysis of [RCTs](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/randomized-controlled-trial). For example, subgroup analysis of CHARM study showed superiority of adalimumab compared to placebo in [fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/fistula) closure after one year of treatment (33 % versus 13 %) \[,\].

No RCT has investigated ustekinumab for fistulizing CD. In a retrospective cohort conducted on 27 patients with [perianal fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/anus-fistula), 48.1 % of patient achieved fistula response at 6 months and 55.6 % at 12 months on ustekinumab. Those results are consistent with the data of a meta-analysis conducted on 25 studies, with rates of clinical response at 6 moths and 12 months of 44 % and 53.9 % respectively \[\].

Supported by the retrospective multicenter BioLAP Study Group study on 207 patients, clinical success (defined by the absence of additional medical or surgical treatment for perianal CD) at 6 months was reached in 38.5 % of patients, with a probability of recurrence-free survival in patients with inactive perianal disease of 86.2 % at week 26 and 75.1 % at week 52 \[\].

Data regarding vedolizumab are scarcer. The ENTERPRISE RCT investigated the use of two vedolizumab regimens in patients with moderate-to-severe CD with 1–3 active perianal fistulae \[\].At week 30, 53.6 % of patients achieved ≥50 % decrease in draining fistulae and 42.9 % of patients achieved 100 % fistulae closure \[\]. The sub analysis of the GEMINI 2 trial in patients with fistulizing disease showed higher rates of fistula closure at week 14 compared to placebo, with faster time to fistula closure \[\]. However, a retrospective study conducted in 151 patients with perianal lesions found low rates of clinical success at 6 months (22.5 %) with discontinuation rates as high as 68 % over a median time of 33 weeks. A third of the patients with inactive perianal CD had perianal recurrence, with a median time of 22 weeks \[\].

More recently, the HEAL study was conducted in 155 patients with complex [perianal fistula](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/anal-fistula), with 136 on ustekinumab and 35 on vedolizumab (16 received both): both treatments appeared effective in achieving remission around half of the patients, with a median follow up of 27 months for ustekinumab and 19 months for vedolizumab \[\].

In the post-hoc sub analysis of ADVANCE, MOTIVATE and FORTIFY studies, fistula closure was observed with risankizumab in 28.6 % of patients in induction, and 57.1 % in maintenance, but further studies are needed \[\]. Upadacitinib probably appears as the most promising drug in the management of fistulizing CD. The [post hoc analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/post-hoc-analysis) of 3 phase 3 trials (U-EXCEL, U-EXCEED and U-ENDURE) conducted in 143 patients with fistulas, greater proportions of patients receiving upadacitinib versus placebo achieved resolution of drainage of perianal fistulas, but also closure of perianal fistula external openings, at the end of induction and maintenance \[\].

##### 2.2.4.3. Non-perianal fistulizing disease

Data regarding non-perianal fistulizing CD are scarce, with no RCT conducted specifically for this indication. Most data available were obtained from observational studies, with a small number of patients, and mainly on anti-TNF alpha agents \[,\]. For internal fistulizing CD, a retrospective cohort conducted in 156 patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents, 44 % of whom had enteroenteric fistulas, fistula healing rates were up to 44 % at 5 years, but with 43 % of patients requiring major [abdominal surgery](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/digestive-system-surgery) during the 3.5-year follow up and 20 % of patients who developed an intestinal abscess \[\]. A prospective study conducted on 117 bio-naïve patients with resolved intra-abdominal abscess treated with adalimumab showed high rates of adalimumab success at week 24 (74 %), with a 72.9 % [survival rate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/survival-rate) without abscess recurrence or surgery at week 104 \[\]. For patients with enterovesical fistula, an observational cohort conducted on 97 patients, with 33 patients on anti-TNF alpha, showed that 45 % of those patients achieved remission without surgery over a follow-up period of 35 months \[\]. These results are supported by a systematic review, with 14 patients with entero-vesical fistulas treated with anti-TNF alpha, who showed 57 % of complete response rates \[\]. In the same systematic review, 78 patients with [rectovaginal fistulas](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rectovaginal-fistula) treated with anti-TNF alpha had an overall response rate of 63 % \[\]. In a large national-wide retrospective cohort conducted on 204 patients with [rectovaginal fistulas](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/rectovaginal-fistula) on anti-TNF alpha, 37 % had complete fistula closure and 22 % had a partial response at 1-year follow up \[\]. For [enterocutaneous fistulas](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/enterocutaneous-fistula), anti-TNF alpha appears to be also effective, with rates of complete closure of 33 %, but with a relapse found in half of the patients during the follow-up period \[\].

Due to the lack of data regarding efficacy of other advanced therapies in patients with non-perineal fistulizing disease, anti-TNF alpha agents should be preferred in these patients for medical management \[\].

##### 2.2.4.4. Stricturing disease

Efficacy data regarding advanced therapies in structuring CD remains limited. Anti-TNF alpha agents are the only biological therapy with proven efficacy in this indication \[\]. In 2018, the multicenter prospective CREOLE study, conducted in 97 patients with symptomatic small bowel stricture, showed a successful response in 64 % of patients at week 24, defined as adalimumab continuation without corticosteroid, need for another biologic, and need for surgery or [endoscopic dilatation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/endoscopic-dilatation). In this study, 50.7 % of patients were surgery-free four years after treatment initiation \[\]. Seven [predictive factors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/predictive-factor) of response to anti-TNF alpha agents were then identified: concurrent use of thiopurines, [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease) obstructive score (CDOS) DOS >4, time since the onset of obstructive symptoms <5 weeks, [stenosis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/stenosis) length <12 cm, pre-stenotic maximal diameter between 18 and 29 mm, late contrast enhancement in T1 sequence and absence of associated fistula. Patients with a score ≤2, = 3 or ≥4 were associated with a success probability at 24 weeks of 6 %, 61 % and 89 % respectively \[\]. Therefore, identifying patients with low success probability of response to anti-TNF alpha agents could allow an early referral for surgical management, for example. Regarding ustekinumab, the multicenter retrospective USTEKNOSIS study compared 34 patients with symptomatic stricture treated with ustekinumab and 36 patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents (after prior exposure to anti-TNF). Clinical remission rates at 6 months were 73.9 % in the ustekinumab group and 42.7 % in the anti-TNF one, with no statistical difference. However, the risk of treatment discontinuation for failure or bowel damage progression was significantly greater in patients receiving another anti-TNF agent, suggesting that ustekinumab could be more effective than another line of anti-TNF after prior exposure to anti-TNF in patients with symptomatic stricture \[\]. In another multicenter prospective study conducted in 28 patients with symptomatic stricturing disease, ustekinumab showed success rates of 71.4 % at week 52 (defined by ustekinumab continuation without corticosteroids, need for another biologic, or need for surgery or endoscopic dilatation) \[\].

For vedolizumab, a multicenter study conducted on 44 patients with symptomatic stricture suggest that vedolizumab could be effective but in selected patients. Indeed, predictive factors of worse outcomes were a higher CRP, obstructive symptoms at baseline, and the need for corticosteroid co-induction \[\]. For both ustekinumab and vedolizumab, further studies are needed in order to guide their place in the therapeutic arsenal for stricturing CD.

#### 2.2.5. Treatment according to disease severity

To this date, there is no consensus definition for assessing disease severity in patients with CD \[\]. The evaluation of disease severity in IBD necessitate a global evaluation of the patient: evaluation of the impact of the disease regarding clinical symptoms, quality of life and disability; evaluation of the inflammatory burden with the extent, disease location and severity of bowel involvement and finally, disease course and degree of [intestinal damage](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/intestine-injury) \[\]. Patients with severe CD are usually considered as patients having a high risk for adverse disease-related complications, such as surgery, hospitalization, and disability, assessed using a combination of degree of structural damage and inflammatory burden, and impact on quality of life \[\]. Criteria for severe disease also includes factors such as large or deep mucosal lesions on [endoscopy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/endoscopy) or imaging, presence of fistulas and/or [perianal abscesses](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/perianal-abscess), presence of strictures, prior [intestinal resections](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/bowel-resection), presence of a stoma, extensive disease (define as an ileal involvement >40 cm or pancolitis), [anemia](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/anemia), elevated C-reactive protein, and low albumin \[\]. Regarding symptoms, an individual evaluation needs to be performed but generally accepted criteria are a high number of [loose stools](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/loose-feces) per day, daily abdominal pain (which are the two criteria to evaluate Patients Reported Outcomes 2 – PRO2), the presence of anorectal symptoms (anorectal pain, bowel urgency, incontinence, tenesmus), the need for [systemic corticosteroid](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/corticosteroid) use, lack of symptomatic improvement despite prior exposure to biologics and/or [immunosuppressive agent](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunosuppressive-drug) or a significant impact of the disease in [activities of daily living](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/activities-of-daily-living) \[,\]. To define disease severity, several scores are available, such as the Crohn's Disease Activity Index (CDAI), which defines a disease as severe if > 450, or the Harvey Bradshaw Index (HBI), with severe disease if > 12 \[,\]. Nevertheless, these scores rely predominantly on symptoms, with no involvement of other factors for disease severity, such as disability, disease course or structural damage.

Given the lack of validated tools to assess disease severity, a literature review proposed preliminary criteria that should be useful to classify disease severity in inflammatory bowel disease \[\]. Those criteria are separated across three domains: impact of the disease on the patient, with clinical symptoms, quality of life, fatigue and disability; inflammatory burden, with C-reactive protein, mucosal lesions, upper gastrointestinal involvement, and disease extent; disease course, with structural damage, history of intestinal resection, perianal disease, number of flares and extra-intestinal manifestations \[\]. Therefore, patients with criteria for severe disease should be identified as they might warrant a more aggressive therapeutic approach. Time to clinical response of remission should be taken into account when selecting an advanced therapy in these patients, with encouraging data in recent therapies such as [JAK inhibitors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/janus-kinase-inhibitor) or anti IL-23 \[,,\].

### 2.3. Ulcerative colitis ()

#### 2.3.1. Bio-naïve patients

In routine clinical practice, anti-TNF alpha agents (adalimumab, infliximab, and golimumab), vedolizumab, ustekinumab and anti-JAK (tofacitinib, upadacitinib, filgotinib) are approved for induction and maintenance of remission in moderate-to-severe [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ulcerative-colitis) \[\].

In the VARSITY RCT, 769 patients with moderate-to-severe UC were randomly assigned to receive vedolizumab or adalimumab. Of these, 25 % of patients had prior exposure to anti-TNF alpha agents. In the bio-naïve group, vedolizumab showed higher rates of clinical remission and endoscopic improvement at week 52, compared to adalimumab \[\]. Higher rates of histological remission at week 14 and 52, but also in terms of mucosal healing were found in bio-naïve patients in the vedolizumab group \[\]. These results were corroborated in a prospective observational study conducted on 512 patients with UC. No statistical difference was found in clinical remission rate during induction therapy between patients treated with anti-TNF alpha agents or vedolizumab. However, clinical remission rates after two years were significantly higher for patients on vedolizumab compared to anti-TNF (43.2 % versus 25.8 %, p < 0.011), with rates of discontinuation of 29 % and 54 % respectively \[\]. A meta-analysis published in 2018 showed that infliximab and vedolizumab had higher rates of clinical remission and mucosal healing in bio-naïve patients, but with less serious [adverse effects](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/adverse-event) for vedolizumab \[\]. Among anti-TNF alpha agents, infliximab appears to be more effective than adalimumab or [golimumab](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/golimumab) to induce clinical response, remission and mucosal healing according to several meta-analyses \[,\].

Ustekinumab was proven efficacious in inducing and maintaining remission in patients with moderate-to-severe UC \[\]. An indirect meta-analysis did not find a statistical difference between ustekinumab and anti-TNF alpha agents or [tofacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/tofacitinib) for clinical and endoscopic outcomes in bio-naïve patients, suggesting its possible use as a first-line therapy \[\].

Regarding [tofacitinib](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/tofacitinib), even if its use as a first line therapy to induce and maintain remission in patients with moderate-to-severe UC is recommended, data remains scarce in bio-naïve patients \[,\]. Indeed, a post-hoc analysis from four [clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/clinical-trial) on 659 bio-naïve patients with UC showed that infliximab was superior to tofacitinib to induce clinical remission, endoscopic improvement or endoscopic remission at week 8, but baseline characteristics between groups mismatches despites a [propensity score matching](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/propensity-score-matching) \[\]. When compared to vedolizumab, tofacitinib showed lower persistence rates and [tolerability](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/tolerability) in 158 patients with UC \[\].

#### 2.3.2. After failure of first-line therapy

As a second-line treatment, ustekinumab and tofacitinib showed better outcomes for induction of remission and endoscopic improvement in patient with moderate-to-severe UC compared to adalimumab or vedolizumab in several meta-analysis \[,\].

A retrospective cohort study conducted in 2141 patients with UC and prior exposure to anti-TNF alpha agents showed that patients treated with ustekinumab had lower risk of colectomy within 2-years compared to patients on tofacitinib, and lower risk of IV steroids or colectomy within 2-years compared to vedolizumab \[\]. But those results are conflicted with two other retrospective studies, which showed no statistical differences in terms of rates of clinical remission and medication persistence between ustekinumab and tofacitinib, but also comparable efficacy between ustekinumab, vedolizumab and tofacitinib as second-line therapies \[,\]. Another multicenter retrospective study conducted on 121 patients with UC showed no statistical difference between ustekinumab or vedolizumab as a second line therapy for steroid-free clinical remission \[\]. However, data from recent real-world retrospective studies are emerging regarding patient with UC and prior failure to anti-TNF agents. In a retrospective study conducted in 293 patients, no difference in short-term clinical remission was observed between patients treated with either vedolizumab or ustekinumab \[\]. Nonetheless, ustekinumab could be more effective than vedolizumab in inducing endoscopic and histological remission at week 16 \[\]. Also in comparison to vedolizumab, tofacitinib appears to be more effective, particularly in patients with severe disease and primary failure to biologics, with higher rates of endoscopic improvement and histological healing at week 16, but no statistical difference in terms of corticosteroid-free clinical remission \[\].

When comparing vedolizumab to anti-TNF alpha agents as a second-line therapy, a study led on 13222 patients, second-line treatment with vedolizumab showed better outcomes up to the 3-year follow-up compared to either infliximab or adalimumab \[\].

Mirikizumab, an anti-IL23, has been recently approved by the FDA and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) as a second-line treatment in patients with moderate-to-severe ulcerative colitis. The LUCENT-1 and LUCENT-2 [clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/clinical-trial) showed that mirikizumab was superior to placebo in inducing and maintaining clinical remission, regardless of previous exposure to biologics, with rapid improvement of symptoms, as early as three weeks \[\]. The LUCENT trials are the first to specifically address the efficacy of the treatment on symptoms such as bowel urgency, using the Urgency [Numeric Rating Scale](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/numeric-rating-scale), with better outcomes at one year in patients on mirikizumab compared to placebo, making mirikizumab an interesting treatment option in patients experiencing such symptoms \[\].

For second-line treatment, if data on patients with previous exposure to advanced therapy are mainly available for patients with failure to anti-TNF alpha, data regarding treatment after failure of other treatments as a first-line therapy, such as vedolizumab, are emerging \[\]. Indeed, anti-TNF alpha agents are no longer the only advanced therapy recommended as a first-line treatment. Therefore, treatment sequencing is evolving and should be modulated by taking into account which advanced therapy was used as a first-line.

Further studies are required to determine the selection strategy for patients with UC as a second-line treatment, especially for ustekinumab and tofacitinib. In clinical practice, vedolizumab and infliximab, which showed better outcomes compared to adalimumab, should be preferred as a second-line treatment \[\].

#### 2.3.3. Ulcerative proctitis

At the time of diagnosis, 25–55 % of patients with UC will present of form of the disease limited to the rectum, called ulcerative [proctitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/proctitis), which often leads to highly debilitating symptoms \[\]. Up to 43 % of patients experience symptoms such as rectal urgency, which is considered to be one of the symptoms most associated with compromised quality of life \[\]. Ulcerative [proctitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/proctitis) can extend proximally in 20–80 % of patients after 5–20 years, and is usually associated with a severe clinical course and an increased risk of morbidity and complications \[,\]. Undertreatment and uncontrolled disease are risk factors for the development of an extensive disease justifying the need for effective and timely treatment \[\].

Refractory ulcerative proctitis is defined as persistent active disease despite rectal and oral therapy with aminosalicylates and corticosteroids, and often requires the use of advanced therapies \[,\]. Data from [randomized clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/randomized-clinical-trial) regarding the use of biologics in ulcerative proctitis are lacking, as patients with disease limited to the rectum are often excluded from these studies \[\].

In a retrospective cohort conducted on 104 patients with refractory ulcerative proctitis on anti-TNF alpha agents, 77 % of patients had a clinical response and 52 % of patients achieved clinical remission with a median duration follow-up of 24 months, and 60 % of patients obtained endoscopic remission within two years \[\]. In another retrospective study including 118 patients, 50 % of patients treated with anti-TNF alpha were in remission after a median follow-up of 21 months \[\].

A multicenter retrospective cohort conducted on 167 patients with refractory ulcerative colitis found that short-term steroid free clinical remission was significantly associated with vedolizumab treatment, with a higher drug persistence \[\]. In retrospective studies, remission rates in patients with refractory ulcerative proctitis on vedolizumab are 65–70 % \[\]. There is currently in sufficient data regarding the use of ustekinumab in ulcerative proctitis \[\].

Data regarding the use of JAK inhibitors appears promising. In a multicenter retrospective cohort including 35 patients with refractory ulcerative proctitis treated with tofacitinib, 42.9 % of patients were in steroid-free clinical remission and 60 % in clinical response after induction (week 8–14) \[\]. Clinical remission and response rates after remission at 1 year were respectively 39.4 % and 45.5 %, with more than half of the patients still on tofacitinib \[\].

#### 2.3.4. Treatment according to disease severity

As for the evaluation of disease severity in patients with CD (see section II.e), there is no consensus regarding the definition of the severity of patients with ulcerative colitis (apart from acute severe ulcerative colitis) \[\]. The Mayo Score is the main score used to define disease severity, with a threshold of >11 \[\]. Predictors for aggressive or severe ulcerative colitis identified were identified as: a high relapse rate (defined as the need for 2 or more courses of steroids and/or hospitalization for flares after diagnosis despite optimal treatment with [mesalamine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/aminosalicylic-acid) and an immunomodulator), need for surgery, development of [colon cancer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colon-cancer), and the presence of extra-intestinal manifestations \[\].

As for CD, other factors need to be accounted in the evaluation of disease severity, such as disability, disease course, and structural damage in order to guide therapeutic decisions. Data from the disease severity index can also be applied in patients with ulcerative colitis and should be used to identify patients who might require a more aggressive approach: impact of the disease on the patient, with clinical symptoms, quality of life, fatigue and disability; inflammatory burden, with C-reactive protein, mucosal lesions, and disease extent; disease course, with structural damage, history of intestinal resection, number of flares and extra-intestinal manifestations \[\].

### 2.4. Extra-intestinal manifestations

The co-existing presence of extra-intestinal manifestations or immune-mediated inflammatory conditions is another factor to consider when selecting the best treatment for our patients with concurrent skin, joint, hepatic, and/or eye inflammation.

#### 2.4.1. Primary sclerosing cholangitis

[Primary sclerosing cholangitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/primary-sclerosing-cholangitis) (PSC) occurs in 2 %–8 % of those with IBD and has higher risks of developing hepatobiliary or [colorectal cancers](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colorectal-cancer) \[,\]. There are no advanced therapies showing efficacy in both IBD and PSC, neither for anti-TNF alpha agents, ustekinumab, vedolizumab, nor tofacitinib \[,, \].

#### 2.4.2. Rheumatological manifestations

Rheumatological manifestations can occur in up to 40 % of patients, mainly manifested by non-axial and axial [spondylarthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spondyloarthropathy) \[,\].

For axial [spondylarthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/spondyloarthropathy) (SpA), the preferred choice of treatment remains anti-TNF alpha agents, either adalimumab or infliximab, with a proven efficacy in open-label cohort studies \[,, \]. Ustekinumab and vedolizumab should not be used for this indication, with data of a large retrospective cohort study showing worsening of existing [arthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/arthropathy) in 35.9 % of patients on vedolizumab, and 22.5 % of patients on ustekinumab, with no improvement of SpA \[\]. JAK inhibitors are recommend for the treatment of active [spondylitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spondylitis), and their use could be discussed in patients with SpA associated with IBD \[,\].

As for SpA, anti-TNF alpha agents are the recommended therapy for peripheral [arthropathy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/arthropathy), with an improvement up to 73 % in [arthralgia](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/arthralgia) and arthritis \[,\]. Regarding ustekinumab, a systematic review of patients receiving ustekinumab for [arthralgia](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/arthralgia) or arthritis, including nine studies and 254 patients with IBD, demonstrated its effectiveness in psoriatic arthropathy and arthralgia, but those results were not supported in the post hoc analysis of the UNITI studies \[,\]. Therefore, ustekinumab could be considered in patient with peripheral arthropathy. Vedolizumab and tofacitinib should not be used for this indication \[,\].

#### 2.4.3. Dermatological manifestations

Dermatological manifestations are found in approximately 15–20 % of patients with IBD. [Psoriasis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/psoriasis) and IBD are significantly associated, and 7–10 % of IBD patients will develop psoriasis \[,\]. For patients with concomitant [plaque psoriasis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/psoriasis-vulgaris) or psoriatic arthritis, advanced therapies with efficacy proven in both conditions include, anti-TNF alpha agents, ustekinumab, risankizumab, and anti-JAK drugs \[,,,, \]. An RCT conducted on 166 patients with moderate-to-severe [plaque psoriasis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/psoriasis-vulgaris) showed that risankizumab was superior in terms of clinical response at week 12 compared to ustekinumab and could therefore be used prior to ustekinumab in patients with concomitant IBD \[\].

[Erythema nodosum](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/erythema-nodosum) is one of the most frequently encountered [skin disorders](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/dermatosis) in IBD, that runs a parallel course with IBD activity \[,\]. Therefore, advanced therapies with anti-TNF alpha agents, vedolizumab and ustekinumab can be used, probably due to their effect on treating IBD \[\]. Other therapies might also be used, but data are lacking.

[Pyoderma gangrenosum](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/pyoderma-gangrenosum) is the second most common cutaneous manifestation associated with IBD \[\]. Here, anti-TNF alpha agents should be preferred, with complete response rates of 58 % for infliximab and 67 % for adalimumab \[\]. Ustekinumab and tofacitinib have also shown efficacy and could second-line options \[,\].

[Sweet syndrome](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/acute-febrile-neutrophilic-dermatosis), a neutrophilic dermatosis, is rarely diagnosed in patients with IBD, but is the third most common underlying condition \[\]. Systemic corticosteroids can be used as a first-line treatment and anti-TNF alpha agents can be used in steroid-refractory or steroid-intolerant cases, especially infliximab \[\].

[Hidradenitis suppurativa](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/suppurative-hidradenitis) is a condition associated with IBD, and more commonly observed in CD than in UC \[\]. Topical treatment or [systemic treatment](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/systemic-treatment) (antibiotics and dapsone) can be used in mild-to-moderate disease, but for severe disease, adalimumab should be used, with early [dose intensification](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/drug-dose-intensification) often required \[\]. For refractory disease, infliximab and ustekinumab could be considered \[\].

#### 2.4.4. Ocular manifestations

The most frequent ocular manifestation in IBD patients is [anterior uveitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/iridocyclitis), and management with [topical steroids](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/topical-corticosteroid) is typically straightforward, but in cases of intermediate, posterior or [panuveitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/uveitis), advanced therapies may be needed. Anti-TNF alpha agents have showed efficacy for this indication, with either adalimumab or infliximab with the most evidence \[,\].

[Scleritis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/scleritis) is associated with IBD in less than 1 % of patients and first-line therapy are oral selective COX-2 inhibitors or corticosteroids. In cases of severe, sight-threatening cases refractory to immunomodulatory therapy or corticosteroids, infliximab could be proposed \[\].

### 2.5. Patients characteristics

#### 2.5.1. Elderly patients

The [epidemiology](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cohort-effect) of IBD is in constant evolution, with more elderly patients requiring care and, with it, new challenges due to comorbities, polymedication, increased risk of serious infection, or malignancy \[\]. Among patients with IBD, around 25–30 % are aged >60 years, and 10–15 % of new IBD diagnosis occurs in older patients (>60 years) \[\]. [Frailty](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/frailty) was demonstrated to be an independent predictor of mortality in IBD patients though not all elderly patients are frail, and the use of advanced therapies in these patients requires a careful assessment of the efficacy and safety profile according to their individual characteristics \[\].

##### 2.5.1.1. Anti-TNF alpha

In elderly patients, the use of anti-TNF alpha agents is associated with higher risk of adverse events such as infections and malignancy \[\]. A meta-analysis showed that elderly patients treated with biologics presented more infections than younger patients (OR 2.28) under the same therapy or elderly patients not treated with biologics (OR 3.60). Older anti-TNF alpha users had an elevated risk of infection (OR 3.48) and malignancy (OR 3.47) than younger users \[\]. Combination therapy with anti-TNF alpha agents and thiopurines posed the greatest risk for serious infection in patients aged ≥65 years, compared to [monotherapy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/monotherapy) \[\]. Moreover, higher neoplastic risks of lymphoma and [melanoma](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/nodular-melanoma) has been demonstrated with anti-TNF alpha agents \[,\].

##### 2.5.1.2. Vedolizumab

Due to its gut-selective mechanism, vedolizumab appears to be an attractive choice of therapy in elderly patients. A post-hoc analysis of the GEMINI trials showed similar safety in all subgroups, even in patients >55 years of age \[\]. These results were supported by retrospective cohorts, such as an American retrospective cohort conducted on 1152 IBD patients >65 years started on anti-TNF alpha and 480 on vedolizumab. Patients treated with vedolizumab had lower risk of infection-related hospitalization compared to patients on anti-TNF alpha \[\]. No higher risk of malignancy was found in patients treated with vedolizumab compared to placebo or conventional therapy \[,\].

##### 2.5.1.3. Ustekinumab

Most data on ustekinumab in elderly patients were extrapolated from its induction and maintenance trials in patients with CD and UC, with similar rates of adverse events between the ustekinumab and the placebo group, and between all age subgroups \[\]. These results are consistent with data in patients with psoriasis, with two retrospective studies that did not show increased risk of adverse events in elderly patients \[,\]. A retrospective study conducted on 182 patients found no difference in terms of safety in elderly patients with CD and UC treated with either vedolizumab or ustekinumab, suggesting that both treatment could be used safely in older patients with IBD \[\].

##### 2.5.1.4. Risankizumab

Data on safety in elderly patients for risankizumab are scarce, but a recent post-hoc analysis of the phase 3 trials showed that treatment was well tolerated across all age subgroups, with no specific safety issues in patients ≥65–80 years old \[\]. However, data from elderly patients with psoriasis treated with anti-IL23 are reassuring with similar rates of adverse events compared to younger patients \[\].

### 2.6. JAK inhibitors

Regarding JAK inhibitors, data are mixed and their use in elderly patients (usually defined >65 years) needs to be discussed and treatment decisions in this age group need to be made with caution. In the post-hoc analysis of elderly patients from the OCTAVE trial, patients in the elderly cohort had a higher percentage of discontinuation due to severe adverse effects, and a higher risk of [herpes zoster](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/disseminated-herpes-zoster) events \[\]. Data regarding safety of tofacitinib in patients with rheumatoid arthritis >50 years, with at least one cardiovascular risk factor, risks of major cardio-vascular events and malignancy were higher in patients on tofacitinib in comparison to patients on anti-TNF alpha agents \[\].

#### 2.6.1. Pregnancy and breastfeeding

Robust data confirms the safety of anti-TNF alpha agents but also vedolizumab and ustekinumab during pregnancy, with a prevalence of adverse pregnancy outcomes consistent with the prevalence of the general population \[,\]. If discontinuation of therapy during the third semester has been proposed, it is now recommended, due to reassuring data, to pursue treatment during the whole pregnancy, especially in patients with difficult-to-control disease, active disease or with recent flares before conception \[\]. Anti-TNF alpha, vedolizumab and ustekinumab can be continued during breast-feeding.

Lack of data regarding risankizumab do not allow its use in pregnant or breastfeeding women.

Tofacitinib showed [teratogenic effects](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/teratogenesis) in animals models, but with higher dosage than the ones used in humans. Nevertheless, lack of reassuring data contraindicates the use of anti-JAK inhibitors use in pregnant or breastfeeding women.

#### 2.6.2. History of malignancy

Currently, data regarding the risk of [cancer recurrence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cancer-recurrence) associated with biologics are limited, but tends to be in favor of the absence of an increased risk \[,\].

Recommendations suggest to wait 2–5 years after the completion [of cancer treatment](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cancer-treatment) to resume or initiate an anti-TNF alpha agent, with no higher risk of recurrence with anti-TNF alpha compared to control group receiving non-biologic disease-modifying treatments \[,,\]. There is no evidence of an increased risk of incident cancer with subsequent exposure to ustekinumab or vedolizumab \[,\].

Anti-JAK inhibitors should not be used in patients with history of neoplasia. A randomized open-label trial showed higher rates of malignancy, such as [nonmelanoma skin cancer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/non-melanoma-skin-cancer), in patients treated with tofacitinib in comparison with anti-TNF alpha agents \[\]. These results were consistent with a meta-analysis conducted on 62 RCT and 16 long-term extension studies, with higher risk of malignancy in patients treated with anti-JAK inhibitors (IRR 1.50; 95 % CI 1.16 to 1.94) \[\].

### 2.7. Future perspectives and conclusions

Therapeutic management of inflammatory bowel disease is aiming towards a highly individualized model, where a patient's clinical profile is taken into consideration in order to decide which therapeutic sequence is the best suited for each individual (). This approach, in an era of expanding therapeutic options, has shown promises in achieving sustained remission, reducing disease complications, and improving quality of life for patients with IBD.

Despite the growing number of available advanced therapies, clinical remission rates one year after their introduction remains under 50 %, even in bio-naïve patients \[,,\]. This therapeutic ceiling has led to the development of new strategies, including advanced combination treatment. This is defined by the combination of at least two biologic agents or a biologic agent and a small molecule with different mechanisms of action \[\]. Simultaneously targeting several pathogenic pathways could provide an additive or synergistic benefit to achieve disease control. Recently, the VEGA study investigated the combination of an anti-IL 23 agent (guselkumab) and an anti-TNF agent (golimumab) in 214 patients bio-naïve patients with moderate-to-severe UC, with results suggesting that combination therapy might me more effective compared to either treatment alone \[\]. However, combining advanced therapies remains experimental, with no data regarding the appropriate combinations for various IBD sub-populations \[\]. Several ongoing trials should help define the place of advanced combination therapy in the therapeutic management of IBD \[\].

[Therapeutic drug monitoring](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/therapeutic-drug-monitoring) has proven to be a useful tool in the management of patients with IBD in clinical practice to optimize the use of biologics, especially anti-TNF alpha agents, but there are still questions unanswered regarding its utility for other advanced therapies \[,, \]. Nowadays, with the growing number of advanced therapies available, therapeutic management of IBD favors a change of mechanism of action in case of inadequate response of treatment, rather than a within-class switch, a therapeutic strategy used in [rheumatology](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/rheumatology) \[,, \].

In this era of [clinical medicine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/clinical-medicine), the “treat-to-target” strategy needs to be adapted via an open physician-patient discussion, considering treatment preferences, potential [side effects](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/side-effect) and lifestyle factors to enhance patient adherence and long-term success. Further studies are needed to validate sequencing algorithms. The appropriate positioning of different [targeted therapies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/targeted-therapy) in the course of the disease is one of the main challenges of optimizing the therapeutic management of IBD. A better understanding on a molecular level could refine personalized IBD care. For example, IL-12 was found to initiate the primarily innate immune inflammatory reaction. Hence, therapies such as ustekinumab could be more effective in newly diagnosed IBD. IL-23 is mostly implicated in the chronic phase of the inflammatory response, suggesting that anti-IL 23 therapies could show better efficacy in later stages of the disease \[\]. In addition to guiding the introduction of advanced therapies, molecular studies could also help to determine patients with higher probability of response to certain therapies. The blockade of anti-IL-23p19 has shown efficacy, for example, in patients resistant to anti-TNF, suggesting its use in this selected population. The inhibition of IL-23 could also restore sensitivity to the action of anti-TNF alpha non-responders, which could be a lead for a potential combination of advanced therapies, to improve efficacy and duration of response \[\].

Clinical data provide guidance for the adequate treatment, even if the number of available therapies and, therefore, of sequencing treatment plans available does not allows an investigation for each sequence. Randomized controlled trials, such as the SEQUENCE trial, comparing risankizumab to ustekinumab in patients with CD and previous failure to anti-TNF alpha, or the ongoing STARTER trial but also comparative effectiveness research, such as the ongoing prospective study I-CARE, are needed to refine the therapeutic approach in IBD patients \[,,\]. Indeed, the VARSITY trial changed the landscape for therapeutic research in the IBD field by being the first head-to-head trial between biologics \[\]. These studies are essential for refining treatment sequencing and ensuring optimal patient outcomes. Head-to-head trials play a critical role for health authorities and payers regarding, notably, the reimbursement of novel therapies in certain indications, and provide a more “ethical” approach, especially in patients with moderate to severe disease, with placebo-controlled studies often leading to a loss of opportunity for patients in the placebo group. However, even if the research landscape in IBD saw flourish several head-to-head trials, there are still numerous gaps in the current evidence [base](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/base) that could be addressed in the next years. Many questions remains unanswered such as the place of the new molecules of interest in the therapeutic arsenal, that are always considered as second or third line therapy at first or the need for trials to reinforce the place of certain molecules with a better safety profile as first line therapy, especially in selected population (elderly patients and patients with frailty, history of malignancy …) but also which strategy to implement for patients with a history of cardiovascular events that can't be started on anti-JAK or S1P. Despite the difficulties to investigate each sequence, future research will need head-to-head trials to refine the therapeutic proposal for IBD patients, such as the DUET trials (NCT05242471; NCT05242484) or the ongoing study with three targeted therapies (NCT06548542).

For decades, standard of care for IBD treatment was based on a “step-up” strategy, with aminosalicylates, steroids, and immunomodulators used as first line therapies. In case of treatment failure, such as [disease progression](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/disease-exacerbation) or complication, treatment could be change in favor of biological therapies or small molecules \[\]. Nevertheless, early introduction of advanced therapies compared to conventional management is more effective to induce remission and to reduce the use of corticosteroids \[\]. In [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/crohns-disease), early introduction of biologics is associated with lower rates of surgery, higher rates of endoscopic remission, and less patients developing structuring disease behavior \[,\]. This strategy, also called “top-down” approach, is however raising concerns, such as overtreatment of patients with milder disease, higher costs of treatment, but also higher risks of adverse events \[,\]. The REACT study, a cluster randomized controlled trial conducted in 2015 on 1982 patients with Crohn's disease, showed that early combined [immunosuppression](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunosuppressive-treatment) had lower risk of major [adverse outcomes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/adverse-outcome) (defined by surgery, hospital admission or serious disease-related complication) compared to conventional management (\[HR\]: 0·73, 95 % CI 0·62 to 0·86, p = 0·0003), with no difference in symptom control \[\]. More recently, the stratified randomized PROFILE trial, showed that a top-down strategy (combination of infliximab and [immunomodulator](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunomodulating-agent) initiated at the time of diagnosis) was superior to an accelerated step-up strategy with higher rates of sustained steroid-free and surgery-free remission, and higher endoscopic remission rates at week 48. To be noted, in the PROFILE trial, patients were naïve of biologics, with symptomatic disease and elevated CRP or faecal calprotectine, with endoscopic signs of disease activity \[\]. However, one of the limitation of the PROFILE study is the inability to develop a new objective marker to guide therapeutic strategy, which did not show clinical utility, suggesting that the top-down strategy couldn't be possibly extrapolated to every patient. Real-world studies are still needed to determine if a top-down strategy is a reasonable approach to propose to our patients, with raising concerns regarding the economic burden of this approach, as well as the risk for adverse events, but should be discussed with patients with newly diagnosed Crohn's disease.

IBD management is evolving towards a more individualized approach, integrating patient characteristics and disease profiles to optimize outcomes. The development of advanced therapies and the growing focus on sequencing strategies hold the potential to improve remission rates, reduce complications, and enhance quality of life for patients. However, further clinical trials and real-world evidence are required to refine sequencing algorithms and fully implement personalized therapeutic strategies.

## 3\. Practice points

- •
	Despite the growing number of available advanced therapies, clinical [remission rates](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/spontaneous-remission) one year after their introduction remains under 50 %, even in bio-naïve patients.
- •
	Therapeutic sequencing based on individual characteristics, such as disease severity, prior therapies and comorbidities, ensures a more effective and safer treatment strategy.
- •
	Better long-term management and adherence are promoted when patient preferences and risk-benefit evaluations are incorporated into the therapeutic decision-making process.

## 4\. Research agenda

- •
	Future research is needed to define and validate sequencing algorithms in order to optimize the use of advanced therapies.
- •
	Further studies are needed to investigate the efficacy and safety of combining biologics or small molecules to improve remission rates in refractory or severe patients with IBD.
- •
	The role of [therapeutic drug monitoring](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/therapeutic-drug-monitoring) needs to be explored for novel advanced therapies.
- •
	The development of predictive biomarkers and [molecular profiling](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/molecular-profiling) technique could be a research focus to identify patients most likely to benefit from specific therapeutic classes.
