## Abstract

Background

Real-world data on medications used for conditions other than inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) are sparse. We examined how the onset of IBD affects the prescription pattern of selected non-IBD medication and the risk of becoming an incident user.

Methods

This nationwide cohort study utilized data from Danish health registers. We included incident patients with young adult–onset IBD (18-39 years of age), adult-onset IBD (40-59 years of age), and elderly-onset IBD (60+ years of age), from 1998 to 2018 and followed all for 3 years. We examined redeemed prescriptions before and after the onset of IBD and estimated the risk of becoming a user of non-IBD medications using logistic regression models.

Results

We identified 36165 patients, 16 771 (46%) with young adult onset, 10615 (29%) with adult onset, and 8779 (24%) with elderly onset. The onset of IBD increased the use of antidepressants, antipsychotics, sedatives/hypnotics, opioids, nonopioid analgesics, antidiabetics, and proton pump inhibitors, even in patients with no other underlying comorbid diseases. The adjusted odds ratio for using antidepressants 1 year after the onset of IBD in elderly was 1.50 (95% confidence interval \[CI\], 1.14-1.82), in opioids 1.69 (95% CI, 1.45-1.95), in nonopioid analgesics 2.10 (95% CI, 1.77-2.48), in cardiovascular medication 2.20 (95% CI, 1.86-2.61), and in proton pump inhibitors 1.51 (95% CI, 1.31-1.74) compared with adults.

Conclusions

In all 3 age groups, the proportions of patients with redeemed prescriptions for several groups of non-IBD medication were significantly increased after the IBD diagnosis compared with before. The risk of becoming an incident user for several groups of non-IBD medication was increased in elderly patients.

## Lay Summary

In patients with young adult onset, adult onset, and elderly onset of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), the proportions of prescriptions for non-IBD medication was significantly increased after the IBD onset compared with before. The risk of new use of non-IBD medication was increased in elderly-onset IBD patients.

What is already known?

Patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) have a higher comorbidity burden than persons without IBD. The use of medication used for other diseases/conditions than IBD is seldom described in an IBD population.

What is new here?

The proportion of redeemed prescriptions for selected non-IBD medication increased after the diagnosis of IBD compared with before. Elderly-onset patients have an increased risk of incident use of non-IBD medication compared with adults.

How can this help patient care?

We draw attention to a generally increased use of selected non-IBD medication when IBD is diagnosed across age groups. It is essential that clinicians carefully evaluate whether the use of this selected non-IBD medication is appropriate and simultaneously consider the possibility of emerging comorbidities associated with IBD.

## Introduction

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) includes Crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC). Both are autoimmune conditions characterized by chronic inflammation of the digestive tract. The incidence of IBD is increasing.<sup>1</sup> In Denmark, the IBD population comprised 75% patients with adult-onset IBD and 17% patients with elderly-onset IBD (65+ years of age) in 1995 to 2016.<sup>1</sup>

Comorbidities increase with age, and patients with IBD have a higher comorbidity burden than persons without IBD.<sup>2,3</sup> In studies of patients with IBD, most patients with comorbidity or elderly patients are excluded from randomized trials (eg, on the efficacy of pharmacological treatments of IBD); therefore, there is often a lack of data on drug efficacy in elderly patients.<sup>3,4</sup> Observational studies on the pharmacological treatment of IBD in elderly patients suggest that they receive less IBD-specific treatment with biologics and immunomodulators than their adult counterparts, but they receive more systemic corticosteroids.<sup>5-9</sup> However, the use of medication used for other diseases/conditions than IBD (here defined as non-IBD medication) is seldom described in an IBD population.

To the best of our knowledge, no studies have described patterns of non-IBD medication according to the onset of IBD and within age groups of young adult onset (defined as 18-39 years of age), adult onset (defined as 40-59 years of age), and elderly onset (defined as 60+ years of age). The definition of an adult-onset age group below and one above 40 years of age is adapted from the Montreal classification, and the elderly onset at 60+ years is a widely accepted definition.<sup>10,11</sup>

We aimed to describe whether a diagnosis of IBD affects the prescription pattern for selected groups of non-IBD medications (antidepressants, central nervous system \[CNS\]–stimulating agents, antipsychotics, anxiolytics, sedatives/hypnotics, antiepileptics, opioids, nonopioid analgesics, cardiovascular agents, antidiabetic medications, and proton pump inhibitors) in young adult–onset IBD, adult-onset IBD, and elderly-onset IBD. Furthermore, we aimed to compare patients with elderly-onset IBD with adult-onset IBD to estimate their risk of becoming incident users of 5 frequently prescribed non-IBD medications.

## Methods

### Setting and Study Population

In the present register-based nationwide cohort study, we used data from the Danish National Health Registries. In Denmark, all citizens have free access to the healthcare sector, approximately 5.8 million people, and for all activities in the healthcare sector, reporting to the registers is mandatory. This enabled us to use an unselected population‐based study design.<sup>12,13</sup> The study population included patients with 2 or more diagnostic codes of IBD (CD: DK50, UC: DK51) from January 1, 1998, to January 1, 2019, from the National Patient Register.<sup>12</sup> The Danish International Classification of Diseases codes for IBD are considered valid and with high accuracy when using 2 codes for IBD.<sup>12,14</sup> We linked the study population to their redeemed prescriptions from January 1, 1995, to December 31, 2021, registered in the Danish National Prescription Registry.<sup>15</sup> To ensure equal follow-up for all patients, we had at least 3 years of prescription data for all patients before and after the onset of IBD.

### Data Sources

The health registers were linked at patient level through the unique personal identification number from the Civil Registration System.<sup>16,17</sup> In addition, we included information on the date of birth, sex, migration, and mortality from the Civil Registration System.<sup>17</sup> We included information on the diagnoses of IBD and other diseases (International Classification of Diseases–Tenth Revision), and performed procedures (including surgeries) from the National Patient Registry.<sup>12</sup> We included data from the Danish National Prescription Register, which was initiated in 1995, and included data from all Danish community pharmacies.<sup>15</sup> The extracted data included key information on redeemed prescriptions, dates, and the Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical Classification System (ATC).<sup>15</sup>

### Study Cohorts

We established 3 groups according to the patient’s age at the onset of IBD; young adult onset (18-39 years of age), adult onset (40-59 years of age), and elderly onset (60+ years of age). We excluded patients who disappeared or migrated within 3 years before and after their onset of IBD.

### Non-IBD Medication and Users

We examined the redeemed prescriptions for antidepressants (ATC: N06A), CNS-stimulating agents (ATC: N06B), antipsychotics (ATC: N05A), anxiolytics (ATC: N05B), sedative/hypnotics (ATC: N05C), antiepileptics (ATC: N03), opioids (ATC: N02A), nonopioid analgesics (ATC: N02B), cardiovascular medications (ATC: C), antidiabetic medications (ATC: A10), and proton pump inhibitors (ATC: A02BC). To compare incident users (hereafter named users) of the 5 most used non-IBD medications, we defined users as patients with at least 2 redeemed prescriptions of the singular non-IBD medication of interest after the onset of IBD (yes/no) within 1 year or 3 years, and we applied a preonset IBD washout period for the medication of interest of 3 or 5 years. Thus, the patients were not allowed to have any redeemed prescriptions for the medication of interest in the preonset IBD washout period. We defined 3 groups of users: (1) with an applied 3-year washout period, users within 1 year after the onset of IBD; (2) with an applied 5-year washout period, users within 1 year after the onset of IBD; and (3) with an applied 3-year washout period, users within 3 years after the onset of IBD.

### Covariates

We included information on the age at diagnosis, sex, the year of IBD diagnosis, and the type of disease (CD or UC as defined by the patient’s last discharge registration). The Charlson Comorbidity Index (CCI) was calculated for all patients with 10 years of medical history before the onset of IBD and includes 19 major disease categories each contributing to individual weights to generate the index.<sup>18</sup> We defined 3 levels for the CCI: no comorbidity (CCI: 0), moderate comorbidity (CCI: 1-2), and severe comorbidity (CCI: 3+). Furthermore, we retrieved information on gastrointestinal surgery 3 years after the onset of IBD.

### Statistical Methods

We presented descriptive statistics using frequencies and percentages for the cohorts. We described the use of non-IBD medication 1 and 3 years before and after the onset of IBD by reporting the frequencies and percentages of any redeemed prescriptions of non-IBD medications (yes/no) within the 3 age groups. We visualized the data using bar graphs and included a 2-sample test of equality of proportions reporting *P* values (<.05). We estimated the odds ratio (OR) of being a user of the 5 frequently used non-IBD medications in patients with elderly-onset IBD (exposed) compared with patients with adult-onset IBD (reference). In logistic regression models, we estimated the crude OR and adjusted OR, adjusting for priori confounders sex, year of diagnosis, type of IBD, and CCI. In the analysis of users, using a 3-year washout period, we included patients diagnosed with IBD in 1998 to 2018 to ensure information on drug use 3 years prior to IBD (1995-1997). In the analysis of users, using a 5-year washout period, we included patients diagnosed with IBD in 2000 to 2018 to ensure information on drug use 5 years prior to IBD (1995-1999).

In a sensitivity analysis, we excluded patients with a CCI >0, thus leaving only patients with no severe comorbidity, and we repeated the analyses of the risk of becoming a user. In another sensitivity analysis, we stratified the analyses of users according to CD and UC.

### Ethics and Approval

This register-based, noninterventional study follows the STROBE (Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology) guideline.<sup>19</sup> The study was not interventional and did not influence the patient’s treatment. Thus, ethical approval was not needed according to Danish Law. The Region of Southern Denmark Data Protection Agency approved the handling of data (j.no. 21/21823).

## Results

### Study Population

We identified 36165 incident patients with IBD from 1998 to 2018. Of these, 16771 (46%) patients had young adult–onset IBD, 10615 (29%) had adult-onset IBD, and 8779 (24%) had elderly-onset IBD. The median age of young adult–onset IBD was 28 (interquartile range \[IQR\], 23-34) years, adult-onset IBD was 49 (IQR, 44-55) years, and elderly-onset IBD was 70 (IQR, 65-77) years. In patients with young adult–onset IBD, adult-onset IBD, and elderly-onset IBD, CD was diagnosed in 40%, 31%, and 28%, respectively. Additional characteristics are presented in Table 1.

### Redeemed Prescriptions of Non-IBD Medication Before and After the IBD Diagnosis

In patients with young adult–onset IBD, the proportion of redeemed prescriptions increased statistically significantly within 1 year after the diagnosis of IBD compared with 1 year before: for antidepressants (7.2% vs 6.4%), anxiolytics (2.4% vs 1.9%), sedative/hypnotics (5.1% vs 2.1%), opioids (12.7% vs 7.4%), nonopioid analgesics (9.3% vs 5.5%), and antidiabetic medication (1.0% vs 0.8%). No statistically significant changes were present for CNS-stimulating agents (0.8% vs 0.7%), antipsychotics (1.9% vs 1.8%), antiepileptics (0.1% vs 0.1%), and proton pump inhibitors (12.6% vs 12.7%). A statistically significant decrease was present for cardiovascular medications (11.8% vs 19.9%). ## Discussion

In this population-based cohort study, we addressed changes in the non-IBD medication pattern before and after the diagnosis of IBD. This was examined among patients with young adult–onset, adult-onset, and elderly-onset IBD, and we provided results for 36165 patients using more than 26 years of data. We found a statistically significantly increased proportion of redeemed prescriptions 1 and 3 years after the onset of IBD in all 3 age groups, except for cardiovascular medications. This increase was valid for antidepressants (not for adults within 1 year), sedatives/hypnotics, opioids, nonopioid analgesics, antidiabetics, and proton pump inhibitors (not for young adults within 1 year). The proportion of redeemed prescriptions for antipsychotics was significantly increased after 3 years, and anxiolytics were increased for young adults within 1 and 3 years. Particularly important was that the risk of becoming a user of 5 selected medications after the diagnosis of IBD was increased in patients with elderly-onset IBD, compared with their adult counterparts, within 1 and 3 years after the diagnosis. These results also applied to patients with no other underlying comorbid diseases.

No other studies have examined non-IBD medications before and after the onset of IBD in different age groups, but other articles have concentrated on selected issues related to non-IBD medications in IBD. For example, in an American study, the use of all classes of non-IBD medications in patients with IBD (18-70+ years) was higher compared with the general population in claims data from 2009 to 2010.<sup>20</sup> A Finnish study including 2381 patients with IBD (18-96 years) has reported a more frequent use of antidepressants, anxiolytics, cardiovascular medication, proton pump inhibitors, oral bisphosphates, antibiotics, analgesics, nonsteroidal drugs, and selective COX-2 inhibitors compared with the general population in 2006 to 2008.<sup>21</sup> In our study, we found that the onset of IBD seemed to change the prescription pattern for several non-IBD medication groups, and it is important to notice that this was valid across our 3 age groups. The increased use of non-IBD medication after the time of diagnosis could be the result of IBD itself, which might trigger the onset of other diseases or increase the focus on comorbidities. Alternatively, it may also be due to ascertainment bias because of frequent IBD-related contacts in which hospital physicians uncover other latent conditions.

We found an increase in redeemed prescriptions for antidepressants within 1 and 3 years after the diagnosis of IBD in all age groups (except adults within 1 year). Furthermore, our results point toward an increased risk of becoming a user of antidepressants after the onset of IBD in elderly patients compared with adult patients. In a recent meta-analysis, the pooled prevalence of depression was 32.1% among adult patients with IBD 18 years of age or older.<sup>22</sup> In a Finnish study population, the prevalence of use of antidepressants from 2006 to 2008 was 21% in adult patients with IBD.<sup>21</sup> In Denmark, 17.9% of elderly (65+ years of age) in the general population redeemed antidepressant prescriptions during 2015 to 2019.<sup>23</sup> Depression and IBD require focus because the combination has been shown to reduce the quality of life significantly and is associated with an increase in healthcare.<sup>24,25</sup> Our study documented that sedatives/hypnotics, antipsychotics, and anxiolytics were seldom used in the young population of IBD patients. Still, the use of these drug groups increased with the increasing age of onset of IBD. This may point toward the fact that a diagnosis of IBD has a significant negative impact on the mood. For young adult patients, we found that anxiolytics increased after the diagnosis of IBD. In adult and elderly patients, we did not find a statistically significantly increased use of anxiolytics. This may point toward particularly young adults being vulnerable after a diagnosis of IBD.

We found that 31% of the adult patients, and 43% of the elderly patients had redeemed opioid prescriptions within 3 years after the disease onset. Up to 21% of adult IBD outpatients used opioids in a systematic review based on Northern American study populations.<sup>26</sup> Another American cohort study of IBD patients reported a prevalence of 44% for prescribed opioids from 2009 to 2015 and 35% were opioid-naive.<sup>27</sup> In our study, 1 year after the onset of IBD 9.3% of young adults, 18.9% of adults, and 35.3% of elderly patients used nonopioid analgesics. Previously, Buckley et al <sup>20</sup> reported a proportion of 12.8% of the use of these drugs among prevalent patients of all ages from 2009 to 2010 in the United States. In our study, the use of nonopioid analgesics was considerable within 1 year after the diagnosis of IBD and increased further 3 years after the diagnosis to 17.9% in young adults, 31.2% in adults, and 50.2% in elderly patients. In another study, the usage of acetaminophen was 45.2% estimated by self-reported data among patients with UC in Spain.<sup>28</sup> However, figures for the prevalent use of opioids and nonopioid analgesics are difficult to compare across study populations because of variations in physicians’ prescribing patterns, periods, and definitions of users. The high proportion of the use of analgesics in elderly-onset IBD in this study period warrants further exploration, as it could be related to poor control of disease activity, complications to surgeries, comorbidities, altered prescription practice following the IBD diagnosis, or altered prescription practice during calendar time. This study did not have data on the underlying reason for these prescriptions.

Studies have pointed to an association between IBD and cardiovascular disease.<sup>2,3,29</sup> Rungoe et al <sup>30</sup> showed a small increased long-term risk of ischemic heart disease in patients with IBD. We found an increased risk of becoming a user of cardiovascular medications in patients with elderly-onset IBD compared with adults within 1 and 3 years after the onset of IBD, but this may be due to ascertainment bias. Thus, Rungoe et al found a high risk of being diagnosed with ischemic heart disease within the first year after diagnosis of IBD, potentially reflecting an ascertainment bias. However, in patients with young adult onset and adult onset, we observed a reduction in the use of cardiovascular medication after the onset of IBD, and this does not support our results being influenced by ascertainment bias but could indicate a de-prescribing pattern. The reduction among younger adult patients may be explained by the unadjusted nature of the estimate and the fact that we included the entire ATC group C (cardiovascular medication), which, for instance, includes medication used for treating high blood pressure that solely does not indicate severe heart disease. Speculatively, the close monitoring of newly diagnosed patients might lead to de-prescribing in younger patients. However, the reduction in the proportions warrants further exploration of causation beyond the scope of this study and explorations would benefit from information on indication, duration, and dosage. An increased risk of type 2 diabetes in patients with IBD has been reported,<sup>2,31</sup> and it might be reflected in our data on the redeemed prescription of antidiabetics in which an increase in all age groups was present. However, the time interval after the diagnosis of IBD in our study is relatively short, making this a more unlikely explanation for the observed increase in type 2 diabetes medication.

In recent years there has been an increased use of proton pump inhibitors, which have also been studied in relation to IBD.<sup>32,33</sup> In our study, the use of proton pump inhibitors increased statistically significantly in adult and elderly patients within 1 and 3 years after the IBD diagnosis. The risk of becoming a user of proton pump inhibitors was also increased in patients with elderly-onset IBD. The increased risk of becoming a user may reflect an increased focus on gastrointestinal symptoms in general after the diagnosis of IBD. In patients with young adult onset, however, we did not find an increased proportion of the use of proton pump inhibitors after the onset of IBD. One could speculate whether general practitioners or others prescribed proton pump inhibitors in elderly patients during the period around the time of diagnosis of IBD for treating gastrointestinal symptoms such as reflux or dyspepsia, which are more frequent in elderly patients.

The most important strength of the study is that we used nationwide data from the Danish health registers with 26 years of data, and the use of real-world data on non-IBD medication provides important information on some groups of patients (eg, elderly) who are often excluded from clinical trials. The study population is unselected with no loss to follow-up and high completeness of the included data.<sup>12</sup> To prevent misclassification of patients with IBD, we used 2 International Classification of Diseases–Tenth Revision codes to identify incident IBD in the National Patient Register.<sup>1</sup> A strength of utilizing the Danish Prescription Register for identifying non-IBD medication is that drug information was based on redeemed prescriptions rather than self-reported information.<sup>15</sup> This eliminates potential information bias. Another strength is that we used information based on 2 redeemed prescriptions to define a user of non-IBD medication in our comparative analysis to have certainty of actual drug use. Various definitions exist for classifying the use of medications based on prescription/claim data using different thresholds (e.g. the number of prescriptions and duration between prescriptions).<sup>20,26,27</sup> We used an approach to study medications before vs after a diagnosis of IBD to describe changes in medication patterns at the time of diagnosis and the patients thereby serve as their own control individuals. A control group from the general Danish population is not relevant in this context, as we a prior expected that patients with IBD would have higher use of medications than the general population. A recent study on the Danish IBD population has indeed shown higher use of almost every type of medication (all ATC groups) compared with control individuals from the general population before the diagnosis of IBD.<sup>34</sup> In our comparative analyses of the risk of being a user of antidepressants, opioids, nonopioid analgesics, cardiovascular medication, or proton pump inhibitors, we included a priori selected important confounders in the multivariable logistic regression models.

When using data from registries, limitations are inevitably due to the level of information. The main limitation of using prescription data is that information on the indication for use, duration, and dosage is not included.<sup>15</sup> Also, we did not include information on medications used to treat IBD or markers for the disease severity of IBD itself. Within the frame of this study it would not be possible to describe all types of treatments for IBD and at the same time give comprehensive information on non-IBD medications. Furthermore, we did not include information on the discontinuation of non-IBD medications, as this could require in-depth analyses for each drug class and is outside the limit of this study. We had a selected focus on certain types of non-IBD medication in which other groups of medication could be of interest for future studies (e.g. medication used for treating cancer). Finally, as in all observational studies, there is always a risk of unmeasured confounding when utilizing register data.

## Conclusions

The proportion of non-IBD medications increased with age. In all age groups, the proportion of patients with redeemed prescriptions for antidepressants (not for adults within 1 year), antipsychotics, sedatives/hypnotics, opioids, nonopioid analgesics, antidiabetics, and proton pump inhibitors (not for young adults within 1 year) increased statistically significantly 1 and 3 years after the diagnosis of IBD. Furthermore, the risk of becoming a user of antidepressants, opioids, nonopioid analgesics, cardiovascular medication, and proton pump inhibitors within 1 and 3 years after the diagnosis was increased in elderly patients. This also applied to patients with no other comorbid diseases. Our finding of increased use of non-IBD medication in all age groups after the diagnosis points toward a clinical awareness of comorbidities and careful evaluation of the non-IBD medications. Awareness of non-IBD medication is important for the patient’s quality of life because the use of non-IBD medication may induce complications to IBD treatments, adverse effects, and drug interactions. More studies are needed to examine the possible influence of non-IBD medications on IBD treatment, the timing of the different types of non-IBD medication, and problems related to polypharmacy.
