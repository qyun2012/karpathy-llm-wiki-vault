## Abstract

Background and aims

Limited data are available on long-term disease outcomes in elderly-onset (EO) inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD) from well-defined population-based cohorts. Our aim was to analyze incidence, disease course, surgery rates, and therapeutic strategies of EO IBD in a prospective population-based cohort.

Methods

Elderly-onset inflammatory bowel diseases were defined if diagnosis was established at ≥60 years of age. Patient inclusion lasted between 1977 and 2018. Study endpoints were compared between elderly- and adult-onset (AO) patients and different therapeutic eras. Data from medical records were prospectively collected and regularly reviewed, as part of the well-established *Veszprem IBD cohort*.

Results

Data from 946 Crohn’s disease (CD) and 1370 ulcerative colitis (UC) patients were analyzed. The proportion of EO CD and UC patients was 6.1% (58/946) and 13.4% (183/1370) in the total cohort. Biological therapy exposure was significantly lower in EO CD compared to AO \[pLogRank = 0.003\], but similar exposure was seen amongst UC patients \[pLogRank = 0.770\]. Early surgery rates (≤1 year from diagnosis) were significantly higher in EO CD patients compared to AO (27.6% vs 15.6%; *P*  <.001). However, there was no statistically significant difference in overall resective surgery rates between the EO and AO cohorts in CD \[pLogRank = 0.838\], nor colectomy rates differed in UC \[pLogRank = 0.435\]. Disease phenotype progression in CD and UC were both lower in EO disease \[pLogRank = 0.015; pLogRank = 0.022\].

Conclusion

Elderly-onset inflammatory bowel diseases represents an increasing proportion of IBD patients, with high exposure to biologicals in EO UC. Overall surgery rates were similar in EO and AO cohorts, however, early surgeries in CD were higher in EO patients.

## 1\. Introduction

Inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD) are idiopathic, progressive inflammatory disorders of the gastrointestinal tract, requiring complex medical treatment and often surgery, in order to avoid long-term bowel damage and disability.

According to a systematic review investigating the worldwide prevalence and incidence rates, the prevalence of IBD has reached over 0.3% of the population in the western world, which translates to an increasing global burden of IBD to health-care systems around the world.<sup>1</sup> In line with the growing incidence of IBD and aging population worldwide the number of elderly IBD patients is rapidly increasing. Elderly-onset (EO) IBD is a special subgroup of the disease that may differ in presentation, disease course, outcomes, and treatment efficacy in comparison to younger patients.

There is heterogeneity in the definition of elderly IBD. Most studies describe it as patients over 60 years of age, while in some cases the term is used above the age of 65 or 70. In addition, the majority of studies do not differentiate EO IBD from elderly patients who were diagnosed at a younger age. In 2016, the ECCO consensus defined the term of EO IBD as disease onset at an age of 60 years or older.<sup>2</sup>

In an earlier review paper, the incidence rates of UC ranged from 1.8 to 20/10 <sup>5</sup> in Europe and the United States, and those of CD are between 1.0 and 10/10 <sup>5</sup>.<sup>3</sup> Incidence rates for UC and CD peak in the third and fourth decade and show decline after, however, the rate of decline is slower in UC.<sup>4</sup> Approximately, 25%-35% of the IBD population is above 60 years of age, of which ~10%-15% is diagnosed over the age of 60 and ~20% transitioned into older age, having been diagnosed earlier in life.<sup>2,5,6</sup> In most cohorts, UC in the over 60s has a higher incidence than CD.

To date, there are very few prospective population-based cohorts assessing the characteristics of EO IBD. The largest available population-based cohort study of EO IBD is from northern France with over 11 000 incident IBD patients included, 9% of them (5% of CD and 11% of UC) being over 60 years of age at the time of diagnosis. The authors reported a stable yearly incidence rate over time with a mean of 2.6/10 <sup>5</sup> and 3.1/10 <sup>5</sup> persons-years in EO CD and UC.<sup>6</sup> According to the results of the Dutch IBD South Limburg (IBDSL), which is also one of the largest available population-based cohorts for EO IBD, there was a 2-fold increase in the incidence rate of EO IBD between 1991 and 2010.<sup>7</sup>

The *Veszprem IBD cohort* is a well-established prospective population-based inception cohort from Veszprem County, Hungary, originally initiated in 1977. Previous data from the same cohort concluded that the incidence of EO UC increased from 1.09/10 <sup>5</sup> to 10.8/10 <sup>5</sup> and EO CD increased from virtually absent to 3.04/10 <sup>5</sup> from 1977-1981 to 2002-2007. Stenosing and colon-only disease were characteristic for elderly CD patients, while the disease course in UC was milder.<sup>8</sup>

Since the importance of elderly age at disease onset in IBD is understudied in population-based settings, the present work aims to analyze the detailed incidence/prevalence, long-term disease course, surgery rates, medication exposures in a prospective population-based cohort of incident EO CD and UC patients, over multiple therapeutic eras, with more than 40 years of follow-up.

## 2\. Methods

### 2.1. Study population and design

The present study is based on a prospective population-based cohort from Veszprem County, Hungary. Veszprem County is an administrative region in western Hungary, and has a population of 353 068 residents (data from national census in 2011).<sup>9</sup> The *Veszprem IBD cohort* is a well-established inception cohort originally initiated in 1977. Detailed methodological and demographical description of this cohort is available from numerous previous publications, from both earlier and recent years.<sup>8,10–13</sup>

Patient inclusion of this Hungarian inception cohort lasted between January 1, 1977, and December 31, 2018. A total of 2316 incident cases with IBD were included. Elderly-onset inflammatory bowel diseases was defined if diagnosis was established at 60 years(y) of age or more, in accordance with the ECCO recommendations.<sup>2</sup> The primary outcome of the study was to assess medical therapy, long-term disease phenotype, and major surgery rates in EO CD and UC patients. Disease characteristics and study endpoints were compared between elderly- and adult-onset (AO) patients. Childhood-onset or pediatric IBD was defined if diagnosis was made at ≤16 years of age, in concordance with the Montreal classification. Pediatric IBD cases were excluded from our comparative analyses.

In a sub-analysis, we also compared disease phenotype progression and surgery rates between different therapeutic eras. Biological therapies have become commonly used and covered by the National Health Insurance Fund of Hungary since 2008. Therefore, we assessed the temporal trends by dividing the study population into 2 consecutive cohorts according to the year of diagnosis: cohort A, 1977-2008 (“pre-biological era”); and cohort B, 2009-2018 (“biological era”). Need for biological therapies (and comparison between the EO and AO populations) was evaluated in patients diagnosed after 2008 (biological cohort).

Detailed demographic data were collected. Disease phenotype was evaluated at diagnosis and during follow-up based on the Montreal classification.<sup>14</sup> Data on medical therapy were collected from patient visit medical records and prescription records and updated yearly. Cumulative exposure to medications was evaluated. Time to disease behavior change, time to first biological therapy initiation, and time to surgical outcomes were collected. Death and time to death were also registered and follow-up times were adjusted accordingly.

### 2.2. Data collection and reporting

All patients in the investigated area who were diagnosed with IBD in this period were included in the study. With each patient, diagnoses generated by in-hospital or outpatient visit records were reviewed thoroughly by an IBD expert gastroenterologist, using the Lennard-Jones <sup>15</sup> and the Copenhagen Diagnostic Criteria.<sup>16</sup> Time of diagnosis was defined when necessary diagnostic tests (endoscopic, histological, and/or radiological evidence) were carried out to support the diagnosis of IBD. In some cases, the final diagnosis was made years after the beginning of symptoms. Case validation and supervision was performed by the lead IBD specialist gastroenterologist in Csolnoky F. County Hospital upon data centralization. Patient follow-up ended on December 31, 2020.

Patients were followed prospectively from diagnosis to the end of the follow-up period or until the date of their emigration or death. Patient data were collected from four general hospitals in the province and accompanying gastroenterology outpatient units. Patient data were collected and reviewed every year using public health records and questionnaires filled out by treating physicians for missing/additional data. The provincial IBD register data were centralized in Veszprem. The majority of patients (over 90% of CD patients) were monitored at the Csolnoky F. County Hospital in Veszprem, with a specialized IBD gastroenterology outpatient unit that serves as a secondary referral center for IBD patients in the province. Since 2018, a cloud-based online interface—called National eHealth Infrastructure (EESZT)—has provided access to inpatient and outpatient medical records, extensive test results, and drug prescriptions, improving the appropriateness of the data collection in our cohort. Data on disease phenotype, medications, and surgical interventions were reported and updated yearly by the treating physician, using standardized data forms. Further detailed methodological description of data collection, case ascertainment, geographical, and socioeconomic background of the province is also available in our previous publications on this inception cohort.

This work was prepared in adherence to the STROBE guidelines.

### 2.3. Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis was performed using the SPSS software (v. 20.0; Chicago, IL). Total, age-, and gender-specific incidence and prevalence rates were calculated. The incidence gradient was analyzed using log-linear (multiplicative) Poisson regression. Data on the background population were obtained from the Hungarian Central Statistical Office. A detailed description of incidence and prevalence calculations is available in our previously published papers.<sup>12,17</sup> Continuous variables are presented as medians with interquartile range (IQR), whereas categorical variables are presented as numbers with percentages and standard deviation (SD). The *t* -test or Mann–Whitney *U* test was used to compare continuous variables, and the *χ* <sup>2</sup> test or Fisher’s exact test was used to compare categorical variables, as appropriate. Cumulative probabilities of medication use, disease phenotype change, and resective surgery were calculated using the Kaplan–Meier analysis and the values were compared between groups by using the log-rank test. Multivariate regression analysis was performed to identify predictors of resective surgery/colectomy. The following covariates were included in the statistical model: gender, disease behavior, disease location, smoking status, and perianal manifestation in CD, disease extent, continuous activity, and anemia in colitis. A *P* value of <.05 was regarded as statistically significant.

### 2.4. Ethical statement

The study was approved by the Csolnoky F. Province Hospital Institutional Committee of Science and Research Ethics (193/2004, 0712/2009, and 2/2021).

## 3\. Results

### 3.1. Incidence and prevalence in the elderly population

Nine hundred and forty-six CD (male/female: 496/450, mean age at diagnosis: 32.0 years, SD: 14.8 years) and 1370 UC (male/female: 702/668, age at diagnosis: 39.1 years, SD 16.7 years) patients were included in the total cohort. Fifty-eight (6.1%) of the CD patients and 183 (13.4%) of the UC patients were EO IBD patients. After the exclusion of pediatric-onset cases (CD/UC: 106/75), we evaluated data of 782 AO CD and 1112 AO UC patients, for the comparative analyses. Median follow-up time for the entire cohort was 15 years (IQR: 9-21). Median follow-up time for EO CD and UC patients were both 8 years (IQR: 5-14).

The incidence rates of EO CD and UC were evaluated in patients diagnosed after 2008, as a continuation to previously published epidemiological data from the *Veszprem* cohort.<sup>8</sup> In the elderly background population (≥60y), the mean incidence of EO CD was 3.67 (95% CI: 2.6-5.1)/10 <sup>5</sup> person-years \[males: 4.17 (95% CI: 2.4-6.7)/10 <sup>5</sup>; females: 3.33 (95% CI: 2.0-5.2)/10 <sup>5</sup>\]. The incidence rate for EO UC was 7.75 (95% CI: 6.1-9.6)/10 <sup>5</sup> person-years \[males: 8.84 (95% CI: 6.2-12.2)/10 <sup>5</sup>; females: 7.00 (95% CI: 5.0-9.5)/10 <sup>5</sup>\]. Age-specified incidence rates are presented in [Figure S1](https://oup.silverchair-cdn.com/oup/backfile/Content_public/Journal/ecco-jcc/19/1/10.1093_ecco-jcc_jjaf003/1/jjaf003_suppl_supplementary_figures_s1.jpeg?Expires=1779288079&Signature=U77Zf3VQSGZsZpr5~Pvr3KsV7AcmzztTc-Zelr6bW~R5Ti3c66nkhiYC7XOfBa6TZuvhNP34NeTxXT3ZIk8aJIym8MgcLjzC1gBqYvla6l3m536kbhAdy-~i-YOPnMhQ-qHJDr7gP4MNR5mehzUEeLz3dy3Y71y19Z-qx3cdFQ3Nsvp81WcO3PhUhAZtqP7nz2lhCDeVmxfdzBIlvTXVXF5OYr4So0azz4-C~nmcJBOlVplMA12ZvbtMgLjZNI6pP3t6QvGexbGGiUu7spqflV4xdE3uOVZZc8gtGuw5K1IQvKLsxTsY-P7~jYSccwrH0xHoiXQVjkIkHEpXbDtNpg__&Key-Pair-Id=APKAIE5G5CRDK6RD3PGA) (data previously published <sup>12,17</sup>).

The prevalence rate of EO CD was 28.6 (95% CI: 18.3-42.5)/10 <sup>5</sup> persons in 2011 and 48.9 (95% CI: 35.1-66.3)/10 <sup>5</sup> persons in 2015 in the elderly background population (≥60 years). Prevalence rates for EO UC were 88.2 (95% CI: 69.2-100.7)/10 <sup>5</sup> persons in 2011 and 106.1 (95% CI: 85.2-130.5)/10 <sup>5</sup> persons in 2015.

The proportion of EO CD among all patients in the investigated population showed a sharp increase over time: 0.7% in the era of 1977-1995, 5.2% in 1996-2008, and 9.5% in 2009-2018. Similar, but smaller increase was observed in the proportion of EO UC among all UC patients: 10.1%, 12.9%, and 17.2%, respectively.

### 3.3. Extraintestinal manifestations

The cumulative rate of extraintestinal manifestations during follow-up in EO CD/UC patients were as follows: hepatic manifestations: 3.4%/3.3% \[primary sclerosing cholangitis: 0%/0%; nonalcoholic fatty liver disease: 1.7%/2.6%\], arthritis: 8.6%/8.7%, ocular manifestations: 1.7%/2.2%, and cutaneous manifestations: 0%/4.9%.

### 3.6. Major resective surgery and colectomy rates

In a sensitivity analysis, we compared early surgery rates only in cohort B (2009-2018, “biological era”), where similar results were seen: the proportion of early resective surgeries was much higher in EO CD patients (29.4% vs 15.1%; *P*  <.001). ## 4\. Discussion

In the present study, we evaluated data over 4 decades from a well-established, large prospective population-based inception cohort. Elderly-onset inflammatory bowel diseases represent an increasing number of IBD patients, with an overall proportion of 6.1% for CD and 13.4% for UC among all patients in this cohort, respectively. Higher rates of colonic involvement and stenosing behavior were observed in EO CD, while left-sided colitis was more frequent in the EO group in UC. Exposure to biologicals in elderly UC patients was high compared to CD. The cumulative probability of resective surgery and colectomy were similar in the elderly and AO cohorts, however early surgery (<1 years) rate for EO CD was significantly higher.

The true epidemiology of EO CD and UC is difficult to determine because of differences in the study populations and case definitions. In our study, cases were identified by extensive evaluation of the original medical documents. The incidence and prevalence rates of EO UC are more than double compared to CD. We also observed a very steep increase in the prevalence of EO CD over the past decades (expressed by the increasing proportion of EO CD patients among all CD patients: 0.7% in the era of 1977-1995 vs 9.5% in 2009-2018). While the proportion of EO IBD among all incident IBD patients in our total cohort (1977-2018) is similar to Charpentier et al.’s results between 1988 and 2006,<sup>6</sup> while the Dutch IBDSL cohort of Jeuring et al. reported almost twice of those rates: 11.7% and 22.5% of EO CD and UC, among incident patients between 1991 and 2011.<sup>7</sup> These figures are rather comparable to our latest sub-cohort between 2009 and 2018, in which era we found the highest rates of EO CD and UC with 9.5% and 17.2%.

Charpentier et al. report a mean yearly incidence rate of 2.6/10 <sup>5</sup> and 3.1/10 <sup>5</sup> persons-years in EO CD and UC, from 1988 to 2006.<sup>6</sup> Comparing our study to this French cohort, we found somewhat higher incidence rates, especially in UC (7.75 vs 3.1/10 <sup>5</sup>); however, we do emphasize that these incidence rates were calculated in the latest sub-cohort (2009-2018). According to the results of the Dutch IBDSL cohort, there was a 2-fold increase in the incidence rate of EO IBD between 1991 and 2010. This cohort found higher incidence of EO CD and UC compared to our results with 6.23/10 <sup>5</sup> and 17.43/10 <sup>5</sup> person-years in 2010, respectively.<sup>7</sup> Of note, prevalence data are not presented in either of these 2 population-based studies.

Disease phenotype at diagnosis in the elderly population of our study resembled the results of the French cohort of Charpentier et al.,<sup>6</sup> with a higher rate of colonic (L2), localization in CD, and higher rate of left-sided colitis (E2) in UC. In contrast with their findings of less complicated (B2 or B3) disease behavior in the elderly population, we observed higher rate of stenosing (B2) behavior in EO CD. We observed a 31% rate of stenosing phenotype among EO CD, which is higher than any reported population-based data.<sup>6,7</sup> This unique finding cannot be explained by diagnostic delay, as this data were also collected and found to be similar between AO and EO patients in our cohort (see Table 1). The Dutch cohort found no significant differences in disease behavior between different age groups; however, ileo-colonic disease location was less frequent in EO CD.<sup>7</sup> Regarding colonic extent for UC in the Dutch IBDSL cohort, higher rates of left-sided colitis were also registered in the elderly.<sup>7</sup>

Medical therapies were meticulously evaluated in the present population-based cohort. In line with the findings of other EO IBD cohorts,<sup>6,7</sup> systemic corticosteroids and immunomodulators were used more frequently in EO CD than UC. In contrast, biological therapy use was significantly lower in EO CD compared to UC in our study. An opposite tendency is shown in the French population-based cohort by Charpentier et al.,<sup>6</sup> where biological therapy need in EO IBD is higher for CD than UC patients. When comparing the AO versus EO populations, we found no statistically significant difference in the probability of biological therapy use for UC, however much less biological therapy was applied in EO CD compared to the adult population. The 5-year probability of biological therapy in EO UC reached over 16%. This means that EO UC required “accelerated” treatment strategies, as reflected by the high biological use in this Hungarian cohort. In contrast, Jeuring et al. reported both 1.6 times less biological use in CD and 2.4 times less in UC in the elderly population.<sup>7</sup>

In the present paper, we report data on the probability of disease behavior progression in CD and disease extent progression in UC, both being significantly lower among elderly patients, compared to the AO population. Whereas, Jeuring et al. reported much higher results, being 19.8% and 29.9% in CD, 12.3% and 20.8% in UC after 5 and 10 years, respectively.<sup>7</sup> Charpentier et al. report a progression from uncomplicated to complicated disease in only 9% of CD patients.<sup>6</sup>

We observed significantly higher early (≤1 year from diagnosis) surgery rates for EO CD patients compared to AO, which may partially be explained by the high rates of stenosing behavior in this patient group. However, our results on long-term cumulative probabilities of resective surgery suggest no significant difference between AO and EO cohorts neither in CD nor in UC. The cumulative probability of major resective surgery in EO CD is similar to other population-based cohorts,<sup>6,7</sup> with the exception that early surgery rates were higher in our cohort. Charpentier et al. reported a cumulative probability of first intestinal resection of 18% at 1 year and 27% at 5 years. In contrast, in UC we found much lower colectomy rates of 3.4% after both 5 and 10 years compared to a 7-8% cumulative probability in the Dutch and French cohorts.<sup>6,7</sup> Charpentier et al.<sup>6</sup> examined risk factors for surgery, in line with our data, L1 and L3 localizations and B2 and B3 behavior at diagnosis of CD were associated with an increased risk of intestinal resection, extensive colitis at diagnosis of UC was associated with an increased risk of colectomy. In addition, they reported that systemic corticosteroid use meant a lower risk of surgery in CD and a higher risk in UC.

We performed sensitivity analyses, where AO and EO CD/UC patients were also stratified by the era of diagnosis (pre-biological cohort; 1977-2008 vs biological cohort; 2009-2018). As expected, there was no definitive decline in either disease progression or surgery rates in the biological cohort. In fact, there was a rise, especially in the colectomy rate in EO UC.

A very recent publication by Singh et al. from the Danish National Patient Register showed that the incidence of EO CD increased progressively from 1980 to 2018, while the incidence of EO UC stabilized after 2000, and then declined in the last decade. This work compared late-onset (60-69 years) versus very late-onset (over 70 years) CD and UC, and showed that cumulative incidence of IBD-related hospitalization and corticosteroid use was comparable in very late-onset versus late-onset patients. However, immunomodulator or TNF antagonist use, and major IBD-related surgery were significantly lower in patients with very late-onset IBD. A limitation of this study is that it is based on an administrative claims database, thus prospective follow-up of the natural history of the disease, or detailed information on disease phenotype is not available.<sup>18</sup>

The major strengths of this study are its prospective, population-based settings, long-term follow-up, and large number of incident IBD patients. This is one of the very few inception cohorts that evaluated the EO IBD as a separate entity, comparing to AO disease. We applied strict and consistent diagnostic criteria with reliable case ascertainment and centralized data capture with standardized data forms, which were regularly updated during follow-up. A limitation of this study is that the disease management and medical/surgical treatment strategy changed over time, thus exposures to advanced therapies cannot be directly compared to earlier cohorts. Hospitalization and mortality data were not included in our study.

In conclusion, the prevalence of EO IBD is growing over the past decades, alongside with the aging population. Our data support that disease presentation and disease course is different from that of an AO IBD. Stenosing and colonic disease is higher in EO CD, while left-sided colitis is dominant in EO UC. Differences in biological therapy use were also observed both between AO and EO disease, and also between CD and UC. Our results suggest high biological use in EO UC. Resective surgery needs were not lower in the elderly population, and a higher proportion of EO CD patients required early resective surgery. Thus, EO IBD was not associated with a milder disease course, which warrants extra alertness from IBD-treating physicians in this subgroup of patients. Differences in disease phenotypes, medication exposures, and disease outcomes are observed among available population-based cohorts, thus further research is required to study the natural history of EO IBD.
