## Background

We conducted a nationwide population-based study in Denmark to examine the incidence, natural history, treatment patterns and risk of complications in very late-onset inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) (age $\geq$ 70 years of age).

## Findings

While the cumulative incidence of IBD-related hospitalization and corticosteroid use was comparable in very late-onset vs late-onset patients, the burden of disease-modifying therapy, either immunomodulator or tumor necrosis factor antagonist use, and major IBD-related surgery was significantly lower in patients with very late-onset than in late-onset IBD.

## Implications for patient care

In this nationwide cohort study, patients diagnosed with very late-onset ($\geq$ 70 years of age) IBD had a higher relative burden of disease- and aging-related complications, with limited use of steroid-sparing strategies and surgery, compared with late-onset IBD.

With an aging population, the incidence and prevalence of inflammatory bowel diseases (IBDs) in older adults is rising globally. Within the next decade, more than 1 in 3 patients in an IBD clinic will be older than 60 years of age, representing a mix of patients with adult- and pediatric-onset IBD who are getting older, and a smaller subset of newly diagnosed patients with elderly onset IBD. Older patients with IBD represent a vulnerable population who are at high risk of disease- and treatment-related complications due to a variety of factors including delays in diagnosis, [multimorbidity](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/multiple-chronic-conditions), and frailty with associated [polypharmacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/polypharmacy) and limited evidence-based treatment guidance. Unfortunately, in the [absence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/epileptic-absence) of robust evidence-based guidance, there is considerable practice variability in managing older patients with IBD, with a preponderance of long-term corticosteroid use and limited use of steroid-sparing therapies. A systematic review of population-based studies observed that risk of IBD-related surgery and hospitalization is comparable in elderly onset and adult-onset IBD, but there is a lower likelihood of use of disease-modifying immunosuppressive therapy.

The cohort of disease- and treatment-experienced ageing patients with adult- or pediatric-onset IBD tolerate immunosuppressive therapies better with lower risk of treatment complications compared with patients with incident, elderly onset IBD, a particularly vulnerable subset of older patients may be those who develop IBD in their 70s and 80s (herein referred to as very late-onset IBD). With aging, there is acceleration of immunosenescence and inflammaging, both of which may contribute to a unique phenotype of IBD; additionally, because chronologic age correlates strongly with biologic aging, older patients’ ability to respond to a major external or internal stress may be limited, leading to marked impact of IBD on health. There are, however, very limited data on the natural history, disease and treatment course, and overall risk of complications in patients diagnosed with very late-onset IBD (ie, diagnosed in their 70s and 80s).

Hence, we conducted a nationwide population-based study in Denmark to examine the incidence, natural history, treatment patterns, and risk of complications in very late-onset IBD, compared with patients diagnosed between 60 and 69 years of age (referred to as late-onset IBD), over the last 4 decades.

## Materials and Methods

### Study Design and Period

We conducted a nationwide register-based cohort study in Denmark, between January 1, 1980, and December 31, 2018.

### Data Sources

The Danish Civil Registration System records demographic and vital data on all residents of Denmark and is continuously updated. We used the Civil Registration System to obtain information on all residents in Denmark between January 1, 1980, and December 31, 2018. The Danish National [Patient Registry](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/patient-registry) records all inpatient contacts from 1977 onward and all outpatient contacts from 1995 onward. Each outpatient contact is linked with ≥1 visit and with primary and secondary diagnosis codes using the International Classification of Diseases–Eighth Revision or –Tenth Revision. The National [Cancer Registry](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/cancer-registry) records data on all cancers diagnosed in Denmark between 1943 until December 31, 2017. The National Prescription Registry records all prescriptions to specific individuals between January 1, 1994, and December 31, 2022.

### Study Population

From these data sources, our source population was all individuals $\geq$ 60 years of age and living in Denmark in the period 1980–2018 who had never had any [IBD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/inflammatory-bowel-disease) contacts prior to their 60th birthday. From this population, patients with IBD were identified as those with at least 2 hospital registrations of IBD (in- or outpatient contacts but not emergency room contacts; International Classification of Diseases–Eighth Revision codes: Crohn’s disease \[CD\], 563.01, 563.02, 563.08, 563.09; [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ulcerative-colitis) \[UC\], 563.19, 569.04; International Classification of Diseases–Tenth Revision codes: CD, K50; UC, K51). For patients who had both CD and UC diagnoses recorded on the 2 registrations, IBD subtype was classified as CD. Using a pathology database as reference, an assessment of nearly 800 patients estimated the completeness of registration of IBD in the Danish National Patient Registry to be 94%, whereas the estimated validity, expressed as the proportion of confirmed diagnoses in the registry, was 97% for CD and 90% for UC.

We classified older patients with IBD as developing late-onset IBD if they were diagnosed between 60 and 69 years of age of age, and as very late-onset IBD if they were diagnosed between 70 and 79 years of age and 80 years of age or more at age of diagnosis.

For all analysis, we included age, sex, time period of diagnosis (1980–1989, 1990–1999, 2000–2009, 2010–2018), country of birth, and Charlson comorbidity index. lists the codes used to define each of these outcomes, and lists the codes used to identify comorbidities.

### Statistical Analysis

We followed all individuals in Denmark alive and aged $\geq$ 60 years of age after January 1, 1980, or from their 60th birthday and until they satisfied the criteria of 2 IBD registration or until death, emigration, or December 31, 2018. For the calculation of incidence, the date of the second of the 2 registrations was used as the time of diagnosis. Incidence of UC and CD per 100,000 person-years was estimated in 2-year intervals by Poisson regression models. We estimated cumulative incidence using nonparametric competing risk analysis treating death as competing risk. Cumulative incidence was split by IBD subtype, age at first IBD contact, and year at first IBD contact.

To examine cumulative incidence of outcomes, we followed patients diagnosed with IBD from start of first hospital contact (in- or outpatient contacts but not emergency room) with an IBD diagnosis until an outcome, death, emigration, or December 31, 2018, at most for 20 years since diagnosis; for the analysis of cancer risk, the end of follow-up was December 31, 2017. Because of the high age of the study population, death was considered a competing risk, and Gray’s test was used for comparisons of differences in cumulative incidences. All analyses was carried out in SAS 9.4 (SAS Institute), except the calculation of the average annual percentage change (AAPC), which was estimated using the Joinpoint Regression Program version 5.0.2.

The study was approved by the Danish Data Protection Agency. Ethics approval is not required for registry-based research in Denmark.

## Results

From the source population of 3.7 million residents, we identified 3459 patients newly diagnosed with CD and 10,774 patients newly diagnosed with [UC](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/ulcerative-colitis) in Denmark ≥60 years of age between 1980 to 2018 (). describes characteristics of patients by decade of first diagnosis. Because the diagnosis of [IBD](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/inflammatory-bowel-disease) relied on 2 diagnosis codes, and our focus was on older patients, we examined whether mortality may be contributing to a relatively lower incidence of IBD in patients being diagnosed in their 80s. We did observe that among patients with a first diagnosis code for IBD, 70.3% of patients in the 60–69 years group received a second code for IBD, compared with 62.3% of the 70–79 years group and 47.9% of the 80+ years group. We observed that approximately 4.0% in the 60–69 years group, 8.7% in the 70–79 years group, and 19.1% of the 80+ years group died within 180 days of the initial IBD diagnosis. This suggests that increased mortality may partly explain the relatively lower incidence of very late-onset IBD compared with late-onset IBD.

#### Incidence

The incidence of CD increased progressively from 1980 to 2018 both for patients diagnosed in their 60s (AAPC, 3.9%; 95% CI, 3.3%–5.0%), 70s (AAPC, 4.2%; 95% CI, 3.5%–5.4%), and 80s (AAPC, 3.9%; 95% CI, 2.3%–6.3%) with no statistically significant differences between the 3 groups ().

#### Hospitalization

The 5-year cumulative incidence of IBD-related hospitalization was comparable in very late-onset (70–79 years of age, 45%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 41%) and late-onset patients (60–69 years of age, 42%) (*P =*.30), even though the incidence of all-cause hospitalization was higher with increasing age of onset of CD (*P <*.01) (, ). Over the calendar time, the risk of IBD-related hospitalization decreased progressively both in patients with very late-onset and late-onset CD, with patients diagnosed in the most contemporary cohort (2010–2018) having a 3-fold lower risk of IBD-related hospitalization compared with patients diagnosed between 1980 and 1989 (*P <*.01) ().

#### Surgery

The 5-year cumulative incidence of major abdominal surgery progressively decreased from patients with late-onset (60–69 years of age, 29%) to very late-onset CD (70–79 years of age, 26%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 15%) (*P <*.01), with a particularly lower risk in patients diagnosed in their 80s. Risk of minor abdominal and perianal surgery was also lower in patients diagnosed in their 80s compared with 70s and 60s (*P <*.01) (, ). Over the calendar time, the risk of major abdominal surgery decreased progressively both in patients with very late-onset and late-onset CD, with patients diagnosed in the most contemporary cohort (2010–2018) having a 2-fold lower risk of major abdominal surgery compared with patients diagnosed between 1980 and 1989 (*P <*.01). No significant change was observed in the risk of minor surgeries over time ().

#### Corticosteroid Use

Similar to the risk of IBD-related hospitalization, the 5-year cumulative use of [systemic corticosteroids](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/corticosteroid) was comparable in late-onset (60–69 years of age, 52%) and very late-onset CD (70–79 years of age, 53%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 52%) (*P =*.61) (, ). The high burden of corticosteroid use persisted over calendar time, with no decline in risk among patients diagnosed between 1995 and 1999 compared with those diagnosed between 2010 and 2018 (*P =*.40) ().

#### IBD-Related Medication Use

The cumulative use of [immunosuppressive](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/immunosuppressive-agent) medications was significantly lower with increasing age of CD diagnosis. The 5-year cumulative use of [immunomodulators](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/immunomodulating-agent) and [TNF](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/tumor-necrosis-factor) antagonists progressively decreased from late-onset CD (immunomodulators: 60–69 years of age, 33%; TNF antagonists: 60–69 years of age, 13%) to very-late onset CD (immunomodulators: 70–79 years of age, 24%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 10%; TNF antagonists: 70–79 years of age, 7%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 2%) (*P <*.01 for both), with a particularly lower risk in patients diagnosed in their 80s (, ). Patients diagnosed with IBD in their 80s also had lower cumulative use of [mesalamine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/aminosalicylic-acid). Over the calendar time, the use of immunomodulators and TNF antagonists increased progressively both in patients with very late-onset and late-onset CD, with immunomodulator use rising in patients diagnosed in 2000s and then stabilizing, and TNF antagonist use continuing to rise in patients diagnosed in 2010s compared with patients diagnosed between 1995 and 1999 (*P <*.01) (). The use of mesalamine was also almost 3-fold lower in patients both with late-onset and very late-onset CD diagnosed in 2010–2018, compared with 1995–1999 (*P <*.01).

#### Other Complications

While patients with very late-onset CD had significantly higher risk of serious infections compared with patients diagnosed at an earlier age (5-year cumulative incidence: 60–69 years of age, 20%; 70–79 years of age, 28%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 32%; *P <*.01), no significant differences were observed in the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (*P =*.06), [venous thromboembolism](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/venous-thromboembolism) (*P =*.15), or cancer (*P =*.99) (, ). Over the calendar time, both patients with late-onset and very late-onset CD diagnosed in the most contemporary cohort (2010–2018) had significantly higher risk of serious infections (*P <*.01) and cancer (*P <*.01), without any differences in risk of major adverse cardiovascular event (*P =*.18) or venous thromboembolism (*P =*.30), compared with patients diagnosed between 1980 and 1989 ().

### Ulcerative Colitis

Of the 10,774 patients with UC, 4012 (37.2%) were diagnosed at 70–79 years of age and 1477 (13.7%) at ≥80 years of age, hence representing very late-onset UC, compared with 5285 patients (49.1%) diagnosed between 60 and 69 years of age. Very late-onset patients were significantly more likely to have diabetes, cerebrovascular disease, and chronic pulmonary disease within 5 years prior to IBD diagnosis.

#### Incidence

The incidence of late-onset UC increased initially from 1980 to 2000, stabilized over the next 5 years, and then declined in the last decade, with rates decreasing of both late-onset and very late-onset UC ().

#### Hospitalization

The 5-year cumulative incidence of IBD-related hospitalization was marginally higher in patients with very late-onset UC (70–79 years of age, 36%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 36%) than in late-onset UC (60–69 years of age, 32%) (*P <*.01), with larger differences in the incidence of all-cause hospitalization (*P <*.01) (, ). Over the calendar time, the risk of IBD-related hospitalization decreased progressively both in patients with very late-onset and late-onset UC, with patients diagnosed in the most contemporary cohort (2010–2018) having a 4-fold lower risk of IBD-related hospitalization compared with patients diagnosed between 1980 and 1989 (*P <*.01) ().

#### Surgery

The cumulative incidence of colectomy was significantly lower in patients diagnosed with UC in their 80s compared with older patients diagnosed at earlier ages (5-year cumulative incidence: 60–69 years of age, 10%; 70–79 years of age, 12%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 6%; *P <*.01) (, ). Over the calendar time, there was a marginal decrease in the cumulative risk of colectomy in patients with very late-onset and late-onset UC in the most contemporary cohort (2010–2018) compared with patients diagnosed between 1980 and 1989 (*P <*.01) ().

#### Corticosteroid Use

The 5-year cumulative use of systemic corticosteroids was slightly higher in patients with very late-onset UC (70–79 years of age, 54%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 51%) than in late-onset UC (60–69 years of age, 50%) (*P <*.01) (, ). This high burden of corticosteroid use persisted over time, with no decline in risk among patients with late-onset or very late-onset UC, diagnosed between 1995 and 1999 compared with those diagnosed between 2010–2018 (*P =*.13) ().

#### IBD-Related Medication Use

The cumulative use of immunosuppressive medications was significantly lower with increasing age at UC diagnosis. The cumulative use of immunomodulators and TNF antagonists progressively decreased from late-onset to very late-onset UC, with a particularly lower risk in patients diagnosed in their 80s (5-year cumulative use of immunomodulators: 60–69 years of age, 15%; 70–79 years of age, 13%; $\geq 80 \text{years} \text{of} \text{age}$, 6%; *P <*.01; 5-year cumulative use of TNF antagonists: 60–69 years of age, 5%; 70–79 years of age, 3%; $\geq 80 y e \text{ars} \text{of} \text{age}$, 1%; *P <*.01) (, ). While almost two-thirds of patients with late-onset UC used mesalamine within 1 year of diagnosis, rate of use was slightly lower in patients diagnosed in their 80s. Over the calendar time, the overall use of immunomodulators and TNF antagonists increased progressively both in patients with very late-onset and late-onset UC, whereas the use of mesalamine decreased in patients diagnosed after 2010 compared with patients diagnosed between 1995 and 1999 (*P <*.01) ().

#### Other Complications

Patients with very late-onset UC experienced slightly higher risk of serious infections (*P <*.01), major adverse cardiovascular events (*P <*.01), venous thromboembolism (*P =*.02), and cancer (*P <*.01), compared with patients with late-onset UC (, ). Over the calendar time, patients with late-onset and very late-onset UC diagnosed in the most contemporary cohort (2010–2018) had significantly higher risk of serious infections (*P <*.01), major adverse cardiovascular events (*P <*.01), venous thromboembolism (*P <*.01), and cancer (*P <*.01), compared with patients diagnosed between 1980 and 1989 ().

## Discussion

As the burden of IBD in older patients rises, there is a better need to understand the natural history and risk of disease and treatment complications in patients with elderly onset IBD, particularly those at extremes of age. In this Danish nationwide population-based study of more than 14,000 late-onset and very late-onset older patients with IBD, we made several key observations. First, the incidence of late-onset and very late-onset CD continues to rise, whereas the incidence of late-onset and very late-onset UC may be plateauing to decreasing. Second, very late-onset patients diagnosed with IBD in their 70s and 80s have a high burden of disease-related complications requiring hospitalization and corticosteroids similar to patients diagnosed in their 60s but have significantly lower likelihood of receiving more definitive therapy for their IBD, including [immunosuppressive](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/immunosuppressive-agent) medications or major surgery. While the likelihood of receiving immunosuppressive therapy is increasing in patients diagnosed in more recent decades, the burden of corticosteroid use continues to be high. Third, as expected, patients with very late-onset IBD are more susceptible to serious infections, but their overall risk of major adverse cardiovascular events, venous thromboembolism, and cancer may not be different compared with those diagnosed in their 60s. These findings can guide treatment approach and shared decision making in patients with elderly onset IBD, particularly those at extremes of age.

The incidence and prevalence of IBD is rising globally, particularly in newly industrialized countries. While adult- and pediatric-onset IBD constitute most incident cases of IBD, approximately 10%–20% patients with newly diagnosed IBD are elderly onset. In a systematic review of 68 population-based cohort studies, incidence of elderly onset CD and UC was 4.5 and 11.6 per 100,000 person-years, respectively. We observed similar rates in our cohort and, of clinical importance, found that changes in incidence patterns were similar for late-onset and very late-onset IBD. Several factors may contribute to this rising incidence of IBD, particularly CD, in older adults, including higher intake of ultraprocessed foods and lower intake of dietary fiber, obesity and low physical activity, smoking, [antibiotic](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/antibiotics) exposure, and higher intake of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs. It is unclear, however, if these factors are unique to elderly onset IBD because there are limited epidemiological studies on the incidence of IBD across the age spectrum.

While referral center studies variably suggest a milder course of elderly onset IBD compared with adult-onset IBD, subsequent population-based studies have refuted that notion, suggesting that elderly onset IBD is not necessarily milder.<sup>,</sup> In a systematic review of 9 population-based cohort studies comparing the natural history of elderly onset IBD vs adult-onset IBD, Rozich et al observed that the overall risk of [disease progression](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-exacerbation), surgery, and hospitalization was comparable in elderly onset IBD and adult-onset IBD. However, there have been limited data on the natural history of patients diagnosed with IBD at extremes of age and limited studies comparing patients with late- vs very late-onset IBD. We observed that patients diagnosed with IBD in their 70s and 80s continue to be at high risk of [adverse outcomes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/adverse-outcome) due to their IBD, with a high risk of IBD-related hospitalization and frequently needing corticosteroids, similar to those diagnosed in their 60s, and similar to rates historically observed in patients with adult-onset IBD.

Disease-modifying immunosuppressive therapies including immunomodulators and biologic agents such as TNF antagonists have significantly improved outcomes in patients with IBD reducing the need for surgery in the general population. Recent studies have suggested that early ileocolonic resection may also be a definitive treatment for CD, comparable to TNF antagonists, and may be considered in select group of patients. This may be particularly true for older patients in which risks of treatment-related complications with immunosuppressive therapy are higher than younger patients. The risk of [surgical complications](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/postoperative-complication) in healthy older patients may not be too different from younger patients. We observed that despite being at high risk of IBD-related hospitalization and need for corticosteroids (surrogate for disease flares), patients with very late-onset IBD were significantly less likely to receive definitive medical management with immunomodulators or TNF antagonists and were also less likely to receive surgical management for CD. Whether this lower cumulative risk of definitive medical and surgical management in patients at extremes of age may be due to undertreatment due to providers’ and patients’ hesitation in using immunosuppressive medications or surgery vs a milder disease phenotype is unclear. Given the high burden of IBD-related hospitalization and corticosteroid use, compared with risks observed in patients with adult-onset IBD or those diagnosed in their 60s, it is likely that providers avoid these interventions despite patients being at high risk of disease complications. In a Danish cohort study, patients with elderly onset IBD were significantly less likely to discontinue corticosteroids (or more likely to required repeated courses of corticosteroids), compared with patients with adult-onset IBD.

There are clear strengths of our study including (1) the nationwide population-based cohort setting, with the ability to identify all IBD patients and to accurately identify older patients with late-onset and very late-onset IBD; (2) access to a nationwide prescription registry covering all pharmacies in the country; and (3) disease and [cancer registries](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/cancer-registry) with complete and long-term follow-up for all patients on surgery, comorbidities, and cancer. We performed competing [risk analyses](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/risk-assessment) for all estimates of cumulative incidence, given higher mortality in patients with very late-onset IBD. There are also potential limitations to acknowledge. Because our approach to defining incident IBD relied on 2 diagnosis codes for IBD, there was a disproportionate number of adults in their 80s who died within 180 days of their first diagnosis code compared with diagnosed in their 60s, suggesting that mortality may have led to underdiagnosis of very late-onset IBD. We did not have access to patient records, and hence, do not have information on clinical [disease activity](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-activity), disease phenotype and behavior, and markers of inflammation. We are unable to draw causal inference on treatment patterns and IBD-related hospitalization and corticosteroid use. Similarly, we are unable to ascertain whether serious infections and cancer are directly related to IBD-related therapies.

In summary, in a nationwide population-based cohort study, we observed that the incidence of both late-onset and very late-onset CD, but not late-onset UC, is rising. Patients with very late-onset IBD (ie, those diagnosed in their 70s and 80s) have a similarly high risk of IBD-related hospitalization and need for corticosteroids as those diagnosed in their 60s, whereas these very late-onset IBD patients are significantly less likely to receive definitive medical and surgical management of IBD. This population-based nationwide cohort study shows that patients diagnosed with very late-onset ($\geq$ 70 years of age) IBD have a higher burden of disease- and aging-related complications, with limited use of steroid-sparing strategies and surgery, compared with those diagnosed in their 60s. These patients pose unique challenges to management which warrants careful attention.
