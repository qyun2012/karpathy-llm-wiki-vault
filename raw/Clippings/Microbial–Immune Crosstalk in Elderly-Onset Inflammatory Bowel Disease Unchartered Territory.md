## Abstract

Elderly-onset inflammatory bowel disease \[IBD\] patients exhibit a distinct natural history compared to younger IBD patients, with unique disease phenotypes, differential responses to therapy, and increased surgical morbidity and mortality. Despite the foreseeable high demand for personalized medicine and specialized IBD care in the elderly, current paradigms of IBD management fail to capture the required nuances of care for elderly-onset IBD patients. Our review postulates the roles of systemic and mucosal immunosenescence, inflammageing and a dysbiotic microbial ecosystem in the pathophysiology of elderly-onset IBD. Ultimately, a better understanding of elderly-onset IBD can lead to improved patient outcomes and the tailoring of future preventative and treatment strategies.

## 1\. Introduction

Inflammatory bowel disease \[IBD\], comprising ulcerative colitis \[UC\] and Crohn’s disease \[CD\], is an autoimmune condition of the gastrointestinal tract resulting from an aberrant immune response to an environmental trigger in a genetically predisposed individual.<sup>1</sup> While the majority of patients are diagnosed earlier in life, 10–15% are diagnosed at >60 years of age with UC being more common than CD.<sup>2</sup> Elderly-onset CD patients usually present with colonic disease while elderly-onset UC patients present with left-sided colitis <sup>2</sup> \[Table 1\]. In comparison, paediatric and adult-onset CD patients more commonly have ileocolonic disease whereas paediatric-onset UC patients more commonly have pancolitis and adult-onset UC patients have proctitis or left-sided disease <sup>2</sup> \[Table 1\]. Moreover, in paediatric-onset IBD genetics appear to play a greater role, whereas in elderly-onset IBD environmental factors seem to have a greater impact on disease pathogenesis.<sup>2,3</sup> While several genetic mutations have been discovered that establish a relationship between susceptibility genes and an earlier onset age for IBD, no genetic mutations have yet been identified that correlate with an older onset age in elderly IBD.<sup>4</sup> In contrast, there is an increased understanding that ageing is associated with pathophysiological alterations including cellular senescence, progenitor cell dysfunction, chronic inflammation, a decline in autophagic activity,<sup>5</sup> changes in the gut microbiota composition and functionality,<sup>6</sup> which may all contribute to an increased risk of IBD. Epidemiological studies suggest that the global incidence and prevalence of elderly-onset IBD will continue to rise,<sup>7</sup> creating a unique challenge for healthcare providers. Though elderly-onset IBD patients typically have less complicated disease behaviour \[Table 1\], they have a similar risk of surgery, but a lower cumulative exposure to immunomodulators and biologics compared to adult-onset IBD patients.<sup>8</sup> Moreover, elderly IBD patients who require surgery for medically refractory disease are at a greater risk for post-operative morbidity and mortality.<sup>9</sup>

Despite the fact that individuals over the age of 60 years account for up to 15% of all incident IBD cases, there are limited data available to support the management of elderly-onset IBD patients because this population is generally underrepresented in IBD clinical trials, and most of the current clinical data are based on observational data or indirect evidence.<sup>10</sup> Furthermore, age-specific concerns such as polypharmacy, comorbidities, malignancies and frailty are more relevant to elderly IBD patients, requiring the clinician to provide more nuanced care to this vulnerable population.<sup>11,12</sup> Increasing evidence highlights that IBD across different age groups may have a distinct pathogenesis,<sup>2</sup> which has implications for disease management. A recently published multicentre observational study demonstrated that anti-tumour necrosis factor \[anti-TNF\] therapy may be less efficacious in biologic-naïve elderly-onset IBD patients.<sup>13</sup> These findings mirror those from other retrospective studies revealing that elderly IBD patients exhibit a lower rate of short-term clinical response and an increased frequency of secondary loss of response to anti-TNF therapies.<sup>14,15</sup>

Considering the increasing incidence and prevalence of elderly-onset IBD as well as the associated economic implications, this niche population deserves more attention to facilitate both prevention and management strategies. In this review, we explore the ageing-driven factors contributing to IBD in the elderly patient population, emphasizing the role of immune remodelling \[termed ‘immunosenescence’\] and ageing of the gut microbiome. We propose that ageing and the gut–microbiome–immune axis interact reciprocally leading to IBD in the elderly.

## 2\. The Hallmarks of Immune Ageing in IBD

Biological ageing, as opposed to chronological ageing, reflects diminished reparative and regenerative capacity in tissues and organs, as well as remodelling of the immune system. The term ‘immunosenescence’ refers to age-associated functional impairments or aberrant immune responses.<sup>29</sup> Immunosenescence predisposes elderly people to viral and bacterial infections, autoimmunity, malignancies and mortality.<sup>30–32</sup> As IBD is fundamentally a disease of chronic inflammation, elevated risks of chronic basal inflammation associated with ageing \[i.e. ‘inflammageing’\] and immunosenescence together may contribute to the development and propagation of IBD in the elderly. There are several aspects of the ageing immune system that overlap with characteristics of immune responses in IBD. Herein, we explore the unique cellular and molecular basis for systemic and mucosal immunosenescence and postulate their role in the pathogenesis of elderly-onset IBD.

### 2.1. Systemic Immunosenescence and IBD

#### 2.1.1. Adaptive immunosenescence and IBD immunopathogenesis

The remodelling of adaptive immunity with age can be attributed to changes in the quality and quantity of T and B cell responses.<sup>33</sup> Hallmarks of T cell immunosenescence include an accumulation of antigen-experienced memory T cells at the expense of antigen-inexperienced naïve T cells due to age-associated thymic involution <sup>34</sup> \[Figure 1\]. This results in a diminished response against newly encountered antigens and further restricts repertoire diversity in the elderly. Furthermore, T cell immunosenescence is accompanied by an increase in the CD4 to CD8 T cell ratio,<sup>35</sup> an imbalance between type 1 helper \[Th1\] and type 2 helper \[Th2\] cells,<sup>36</sup> and an increase in the ratio of pro-inflammatory type 17 helper T \[Th17\] cells to anti-inflammatory regulatory T \[T <sub>reg</sub>\] cells <sup>37</sup> \[Figure 1\]. Similarly, the balance between T <sub>reg</sub> cells and effector T cells is also disrupted in patients with IBD,<sup>38</sup> where immune tolerance is suppressed and effector T cells such as Th1, Th2 and Th17 are activated, leading to IBD progression.<sup>39,40</sup> Moreover, an age-associated accumulation of senescent \[CD28 <sup>-ve</sup> CD57 <sup>+ve</sup>\] T cells has been widely reported, particularly in the terminally differentiated EMRA cells \[effector memory T cells that re-express CD45RA\].<sup>41</sup> These senescent T cells possess a pro-inflammatory pattern known as senescence-associated secretory phenotype \[SASP\] during ageing,<sup>42</sup> and these cells secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines (interleukin-g \[IL-6\] and IL-1β) <sup>41</sup> that play a key role in IBD pathogenesis.

Furthermore, chronic infection with cytomegalovirus \[CMV\] accelerates human T cell immunosenescence in elderly individuals.<sup>43</sup> Though the role of CMV infection in active IBD remains equivocal, studies have demonstrated that 20–40% of patients with severe and/or steroid-refractory IBD are seropositive for CMV infection.<sup>44</sup> Elderly IBD patients are also at a higher risk for CMV reactivation.<sup>45</sup> In the context of ageing, persistent infections with CMV may exacerbate immunosenescence by a dual immunosuppressive mechanism: occupying ‘immunological space’ that comprises up to 50% of the total memory T cell compartment in older individuals and causing blockade of antigen-presenting cells or cytokine secretion.<sup>46</sup> Considering the accumulation of large numbers of CMV-specific CD8 <sup>+</sup> effector T cell clones that have been demonstrated to be cytotoxic and capable of rapidly releasing proinflammatory cytokines (interferon γ \[IFN-γ\] and TNF-α) in response to stimulation,<sup>47</sup> it is a plausible hypothesis that CMV infection or reactivation may be associated with an increased risk of IBD in the elderly.

In addition, data from the human colonic mucosa transcriptome also support the hypothesis that viral infection-induced autoimmunity may represent a pathogenetic mechanism for IBD, particularly CD.<sup>48</sup> Herpes simplex virus \[HSV\] infection has been identified as an upregulated pathway in CD when compared with controls,<sup>48</sup> which involves major histocompatibility \[MHC\] molecules that are known for the presentation of viral and self-antigens to T cells. In contrast, increased numbers of Epstein–Barr virus \[EBV\]-infected cells have been detected in UC as compared to CD, with elevated expression of the EBV-induced gene 3 \[EBI3\], a molecule belonging to the IL-12 family.<sup>49</sup> Human metagenome studies analysing virus-like particle \[VLP\] preparations from faecal samples obtained from patients with IBD indicate a significant expansion of Caudovirales bacteriophages in UC and CD patients compared with controls.<sup>50</sup> Nonetheless, the precise role viruses and bacteriophages play in IBD pathogenesis remains to be determined.

Alongside viruses, several bacterial species have also been positively associated with IBD.<sup>51,52</sup> These include *Clostridioides difficile*, *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, *Campylobacter concisus*, *Mycobacterium avium* subspecies *paratuberculosis*, enterotoxigenic *Bacteroides fragilis*, *Fusobacterium varium*, *Escherichia coli*, enteric *Helicobacter* species and *Fusobacterium nucleatum*.<sup>51</sup> Overexposure of the immune system to excessive bacterial substances may lead to loss of immunological tolerance to normal commensals, eliciting intestinal inflammation and IBD development.<sup>53</sup> Finally, emerging evidence suggests that fungi may play a role in chronic intestinal inflammatory disorders. In this regard, a new study has shown that *Candida albicans* strains seem to dominate the colonic mucosa of patients with UC, but importantly, only some strains aggravated gut inflammation.<sup>54</sup>

In addition to T cells, an age-associated decline in B cell lymphopoiesis and remodelling has also been observed in the B cell pool \[Figure 1\]. This entails a decline in B cell repertoire diversity, a decrease in naïve B cells, an increase in antigen-experienced memory B cells and diminished humoral responses due to reduced protective antibody production, resulting in poor vaccination efficacy in older people.<sup>34,55</sup> Decreased antibody avidity may be attributed to down-regulated E47, a transcription factor that controls B cell function, and to a reduced activation-induced cytidine deaminase \[AID\], which is related to class switch recombination and Ig somatic hypermutation.<sup>56</sup> A further age-related change in antibody production occurs when immunoglobulin isotypes shift from IgD and IgM isotypes largely produced by naïve B cells, to increased levels of IgG or IgA isotypes produced by memory B cells.<sup>57</sup> Moreover, inflammatory markers occurring in the late/exhausted memory \[IgD <sup>−</sup> CD27 <sup>−</sup>\] B cells exhibit SASP, which involves the chronic transcriptional induction and increased secretion of pro-inflammatory mediators. This includes cell cycle regulators \[e.g. p16 <sup>INK,4</sup> which induces cell cycle arrest\], inflammatory micro-RNAs \[miR-155, miR-16, miR93\], pro-inflammatory cytokines \[TNF-α, IL-6 and IL-8\] and metabolic pathways \[AMPK\].<sup>55</sup> These factors constitute a source of chronic inflammation that may contribute to inflammageing and the development of elderly-onset IBD.

Lastly, a subset of atypical B cells, termed age-associated B cells \[ABCs\], secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines \[e.g. TNF-α\] as well as autoantibodies and accumulate with age.<sup>58</sup> Similarly, the accumulation of ABCs has also been observed in other autoimmune diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis in the elderly.<sup>59</sup> In addition, regulatory B cells \[B <sub>regs</sub>\], which can resolve pro-inflammatory events by secreting anti-inflammatory IL-10, IL-35 or TGF-β,<sup>60</sup> develop numerical and functional deficits with ageing,<sup>61</sup> increasing the risk of autoimmunity and chronic inflammation with age.<sup>62</sup> Notably in patients with IBD, such a numerical deficiency or decrease in B <sub>reg</sub> function can propagate disease progression and severity, exacerbating the intestinal inflammation that may contribute to IBD pathogenesis.<sup>63</sup>

#### 2.1.2. Innate immunosenescence and IBD immunopathogenesis

The peripheral innate immune system, particularly the neutrophils, natural killer \[NK\] cells, monocytes, macrophages and dendritic cells \[DCs\], are subject to profound changes with advancing age.<sup>64</sup> The dysfunction of DCs with increasing age is characterized by weakened antigen uptake and presentation as well as diminished phagocytosis of apoptotic cells,<sup>29</sup> which prolongs self-antigen exposure and can contribute towards autoimmune responses and inflammation.<sup>65</sup> In addition, DCs from older adults secrete higher basal levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines \[IL-6, TNFα\], accompanied by decreased levels of anti-inflammatory cytokines \[IL-10\],<sup>65,66</sup> contributing towards inflammageing. During ageing, neutrophils exhibit impaired phagocytosis, defects in chemotaxis and impaired formation of neutrophil extracellular traps \[NETs\],<sup>67–69</sup> resulting in compromised pathogen clearance and an inappropriate persistence of chronic inflammation. Macrophages also demonstrate a decrease in phagocytosis with ageing, which results in a delay in the resolution of inflammation.<sup>70,71</sup> Although an age-associated expansion of NK cells has been reported in aged individuals, these aged NK cells exhibit decreased per-cell cytotoxicity, contributing to an increased susceptibility to viral infections and possibly towards the accumulation of senescent cells.<sup>72,73</sup> Age-related redistribution of monocyte subsets has been observed with increased frequency of non-classical monocytes \[CD14 <sup>+ve</sup> CD16 <sup>++ve</sup>\] exhibiting SASP in a pro-inflammatory state and secreting high levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines basally <sup>74</sup> similar to DCs. Overall, the age-associated remodelling of the peripheral innate immune system could contribute towards a reduced clearance of senescent cells, a higher basal level of pro-inflammatory cytokines fuelling inflammageing, and an elevated risk of infections and autoimmune conditions, including IBD <sup>75</sup> \[Figure 1\].

In addition to the immune cells mentioned above, innate host defence mechanisms also depend on pattern recognition receptors \[PRRs\],<sup>76</sup> inflammasomes and autophagy.<sup>77</sup> The PRRs are a large family of proteins that are capable of directly recognizing pathogen-associated molecular patterns \[PAMPs\], damage-associated molecular patterns \[DAMPS\], as well as apoptotic host cells and damaged senescent cells associated with ageing.<sup>78</sup> Toll-like receptors \[TLRs\], the major PRRs expressed mostly by DCs, macrophages and monocytes, play an important role in the innate immune response to inflammatory stimuli.<sup>79</sup> However, excessive activation of these receptors may lead to chronic intestinal inflammation, and abnormal TLR signalling may trigger disease-related inflammation and an increased bacterial burden, hence being closely linked to the development of IBD.<sup>79</sup> Several members of the TLR family of receptors, including TLR5, TLR8 and TLR9, are positively correlated with the presence of UC and the severity of endoscopic and histological inflammation, with their mRNA levels being higher in active UC in comparison to quiescent UC, as well as strongly correlated with the transcription of inflammatory cytokines \[IL-6 and TNF\].<sup>80</sup> In particular, polymorphisms in the TLR5 gene, such as the *R392X*, *N592S* and *L616F* variants, are significantly associated with an increased risk for both UC <sup>81</sup> and CD.<sup>82</sup> In addition, expression level of TLR5 is elevated in human monocytes from the elderly, accompanied by activation of downstream NF-κB and MAPK signalling pathways that enhance the secretion of proinflammatory mediators,<sup>83</sup> which may contribute to the inflammatory milieu in elderly IBD.

Inflammasomes are multiprotein complexes assembled by PRRs that orchestrate proinflammatory responses and participate in innate responses by sensing microbial motifs, endogenous signals and environmental irritants.<sup>84,85</sup> NLRP3 \[NOD-, LRR- and pyrin domain-containing protein 3\], one of the better studied inflammasomes, activates when stress signals from PAMPs or DAMPs are present.<sup>86</sup> This results in a series of proinflammatory responses, including the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines such as IL-1β and IL-18, as well as the initiation of pyroptosis \[a proinflammatory type of programmed cell death\].<sup>86</sup> Furthermore, recent studies have highlighted the role of the NLRP3 inflammasome contributing to age-related inflammation or ‘inflammageing’ and the pathogenesis of a wide variety of age-related diseases,<sup>84</sup> as well as IBD.<sup>87</sup>

In particular, inflammasome activation has been shown to interact and reciprocally influence autophagy.<sup>88</sup> While activation of the inflammasome can trigger autophagy, autophagy in turn can inhibit excessive inflammasome activation via autophagic engulfment to maintain homeostasis.<sup>89</sup> A number of IBD susceptibility genes have been discovered to regulate crosstalk between autophagy and inflammasome activation, including autophagy-related 16-like 1 \[ATG16L1\] and immune-related GTPase M \[IRGM\].<sup>90</sup> With respect to ageing, it is noteworthy that autophagy could minimize ageing effects by removing endogenous DAMPs; however, autophagy activity is downregulated with ageing,<sup>5</sup> resulting in unrestricted inflammasome activation and consequent inflammation.<sup>91</sup>

#### 2.1.3. Inflammageing and IBD pathogenesis

‘Inflammageing’, a chronic low-grade systemic inflammation that increases with age, is another hallmark of immune ageing.<sup>66</sup> It is speculated that an increased asymptomatic pro-inflammatory state \[conceptualized as a proclivity of the innate immunity system to confront pathogens\] may have evolutionary advantages, leading to beneficial adaptations in the ageing process which maintain homeostasis.<sup>92</sup> The inflammation factor has been found to be a significant contributor to successful ageing in super semi-centenarians.<sup>93</sup> Nevertheless, as shown by the antagonistic pleiotropic theory of ageing,<sup>94</sup> these protective effects over time can lead to detrimental outcomes in the absence of adequate compensatory mechanisms. Inflammageing results from an imbalance between pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory networks.<sup>64</sup> It is characterized by a heightened pro-inflammatory environment associated with a subclinical accumulation of pro-inflammatory factors including IL-6, IL-1β, IL-18, IL-22 and TNFα without an effective counter-regulation by anti-inflammatory molecules such as IL-10, IL-4 and TGF-β1.<sup>64,92,95</sup> This contributes to the development of frailty and increased mortality in the elderly,<sup>95,96</sup> as well as chronic age-related diseases.<sup>97</sup>

Similar to inflammageing, an imbalanced inflammatory and anti-inflammatory equilibrium as well as remodelling of cytokine networks have also been demonstrated to contribute to the pathogenesis of IBD, which accordingly provides a rationale for the development of tailored cytokine-targeted therapies for IBD. These strategies aim to block pro-inflammatory cytokine signalling mediated by TNFα, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-12, IL-17 and IL-23, and/or to enhance anti-inflammatory pathways through IL-2, IL-10 or TGF-β1.<sup>79,98</sup> Interestingly, inflammageing occurs concurrently with immunosenescence.<sup>99,100</sup> Moreover, inflammageing is triggered by chronic repeated activation of the innate immune system, which exhausts the adaptive immune system, and in turn exacerbates immunosenescence.<sup>101</sup> Therefore, immunosenescence and inflammageing may function reciprocally to shape IBD in the elderly.

It is proposed that the chronic proinflammatory state of inflammageing is mediated by a number of molecular mechanisms including \[Figure 1\]: cellular senescence SASP factors,<sup>102</sup> mitochondrial dysfunction such as mitochondrial DNA \[mtDNA\] damage,<sup>103</sup> a decline in autophagy capacity that compromises cellular housekeeping and immune ‘rejuvenation’,<sup>104</sup> accumulation of DAMPs,<sup>105</sup> dysregulation of the ubiquitin–proteasome system \[UPS\],<sup>106</sup> activation of inflammasomes,<sup>107</sup> telomere shortening <sup>108</sup> and activation of the DNA damage response \[DDR\].<sup>109</sup> In addition to these factors, gut microbiota dysbiosis is of particular importance since the gut microbiota closely interacts with the diet, intestinal barrier, metabolism and systemic inflammation,<sup>100,110,111</sup> as well as being subject to profound remodelling with advancing age as discussed in section 3.

It is notable that studies in IBD demonstrate great promise for utilizing frailty and sarcopenia, which are interrelated concepts with inflammageing, as a risk stratification tool for IBD patients at the greatest risk of experiencing adverse outcomes.<sup>12,112</sup> Furthermore, it has been reported that 41.6% of patients with IBD have sarcopenia,<sup>113</sup> a condition characterized by progressive loss of muscle mass and function associated with ageing and considered to be a key component of frailty.<sup>114</sup> Although the pathogenesis of frailty and sarcopenia is still poorly understood, an emerging theory ties the two together because of inflammageing and immunosenescence.<sup>115,116</sup> In particular, the inflammatory cytokine components of inflammageing \[e.g. TNFα, IL-1β and IL-6\] contribute to muscle wasting, either directly by increasing protein degradation and catabolism, or indirectly by promoting anabolic resistance by inhibiting the expression and activity of growth hormone and insulin-like growth factor 1.<sup>117,118</sup>

There are still many unknowns about the intricacies of the ageing body and its clinical implications for IBD. It is generally believed that ageing induces systemic immune dysfunction, pro-inflammatory cytokine responses and an accumulation of senescent cells, leading to immunosenescence and inflammageing. If this combination persists to increase the imbalance between pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory responses without effective counter-regulation, it may provide favourable conditions that lead to IBD in the elderly \[Figure 1\].

### 2.2. Mucosal Immunosenescence and IBD

It is well established in IBD that the mucosal barrier is compromised and that both the mucosal barrier and the local immune system play a prominent role in the pathophysiology of disease.<sup>119–121</sup> Emerging data illustrate an overall decline in the integrity of the intestinal epithelial barrier of the elderly, with the first signs of ageing in the gut immune system occurring at mucosal surfaces earlier than in the systemic immune compartment.<sup>122</sup> Along with systemic immune disorders, age-associated changes in the local mucosal immune system may disrupt the integrity of the epithelial barrier, leading to a leaky gut and initiating the onset of IBD. An integrated series of changes occur at the mucosal interface of ageing guts, including physical changes (intestinal epithelial cells and tight junctions \[TJs\]),<sup>123–127</sup> immunological changes \[secretory IgA and mucosal immune cells\],<sup>122,128–133</sup> biochemical changes (mucus layer and antimicrobial peptides \[AMPs\]),<sup>134–137</sup> as well as microbial changes.<sup>122,138,139</sup> A summary of mucosal immune changes in the elderly that may represent a pathogenesis link to IBD is provided in Table 2.

#### 2.2.1. Physical changes in the intestinal barrier with ageing

The integrity of the gut epithelium is compromised with age in humans <sup>147,149</sup> as well as in various model organisms such as *Caenorhabditis elegans*,<sup>127</sup> *Drosophila*,<sup>127,160</sup> mice <sup>147</sup> and monkeys.<sup>161</sup> The ageing process may negatively impact the physical barrier of the gut epithelium by affecting two key components of intestinal barrier function: intestinal epithelial cells \[IECs\], and cell–cell junctional complexes known as TJs, which are implicated in the pathogenesis of IBD.<sup>40,120</sup>

The intestinal epithelium barrier comprises specialized cells with diverse functions that emerge from intestinal stem cells \[ISCs\] at the base of crypts.<sup>162</sup> However, ISCs are highly prone to stem cell exhaustion, an integrative hallmark of ageing,<sup>103</sup> as evidenced by a reduced tissue regenerative capacity after damage and a slower turnover of the epithelium with ageing.<sup>140,141</sup> The current emerging molecular mechanisms underlying ISC exhaustion may be related to increased DNA damage and an age-related dysfunction of the DDR,<sup>163</sup> increased apoptosis <sup>126</sup> and a decline in stemness maintaining Wnt signalling.<sup>140,142</sup> As the gut ages, the reduced mitotic rate <sup>140</sup> coupled with an increased level of apoptosis in active ISCs <sup>126</sup> lead to decreased cell proliferation within the crypt, and the ROCK pathway-dependent cellular motion of ISCs is also impaired in ageing crypts <sup>141</sup> \[Table 2\]. Of note, the regenerative potential of human and mouse intestinal epithelium declines with age not only due to defects in stem cells themselves, but also in their niche, which supports and regulates ISCs.<sup>142,164</sup> Transcriptomic data suggest that this functional decline may be caused by a decrease in canonical Wnt signalling, particularly *Wnt3*, mainly secreted by ISCs and their neighbouring Paneth cells and subepithelial mesenchymal cells in aged intestinal crypts.<sup>140,142</sup>

IECs have an essential role in maintaining homeostasis of the intestinal epithelial barrier by modulating responses to the gut microbiota.<sup>165</sup> Several types of specialized IECs including absorptive enterocytes, Paneth cells, goblet cells and microfold cells \[M cells\] are negatively affected by ageing, especially with respect to their function and composition <sup>124,166,167</sup> \[Table 2\]. Generally, proteomic analysis demonstrates that ageing affects epithelial immunity, metabolism and cell proliferation, and the composition of the epithelium changes region-dependently in aged mice,<sup>168</sup> which could account for the altered architecture and dysfunction of the ageing gut. To date, it is unclear what role each epithelial cell subtype plays in the breakdown of the epithelial barrier that underlies IBD. However, aberrant IEC responses to IBD-associated microbial sensing, TNF stimulation, and dysregulated inflammatory pathways in IECs (such as IFN-γ, NF-κB, RIPK, and endoplasmic reticulum \[ER\] stress) <sup>169–171</sup> may compromise intestinal barrier integrity, thereby promoting intestinal inflammation.

The TJ barrier defects, characterized by discontinuous TJ strands, have been implicated in the pathogenesis of IBD.<sup>40</sup> The underlying cellular mechanisms include altered expression of TJ proteins including occludin, claudins, zonula occludens \[ZOs\] and junctional adhesion molecule \[JAM\].<sup>165</sup> In aged humans, colonic tissue exhibits discontinued TJ strands accompanied by a decline in E-cadherin and occludin levels <sup>147</sup> as well as an increase in claudin-2,<sup>149</sup> resulting in a profound decrease in transepithelial electrical resistance \[TEER\], which indicates impaired integrity of the epithelial barrier.<sup>149</sup> The remodelling of the intestinal epithelial TJ proteins in aged non-human animals such as mice and baboons exhibits a similar pattern, with reduced levels of E-cadherin,<sup>147</sup> occludin,<sup>147,148</sup> ZO-1 <sup>148</sup> and JAM-A,<sup>148</sup> and increased levels of claudin-2.<sup>148</sup> In particular, occludin downregulation in patients with IBD,<sup>150,151</sup> and claudin-2 upregulation in both CD <sup>150</sup> and UC <sup>152</sup> patients is known to result in enhanced TJ permeability for cations, providing the molecular basis for a leaky gut \[Table 2\]. The altered intestinal TJ permeability may result from the accumulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-1β, IFN-γ and IL-6 secreted by IECs, which modulate TJ protein expression and function, or activate regulatory pathways linked to the TJ complex.<sup>172–174</sup> Interestingly, pro-inflammatory cytokine levels tend to increase with advancing age because of inflammageing.

#### 2.2.2. Immunological changes in the intestinal barrier with ageing

The gastrointestinal \[GI\] tract is generally acknowledged as the largest immunological organ with regards to lymphocyte numbers, as the gut-associated lymphoid tissue \[GALT\] accommodates nearly 70% of the total immune cells in the body.<sup>175</sup> Ageing is also associated with progressive alterations in mucosal immune responses driven by both impaired immune cells and gut mucosal regulatory immunity, referred to as mucosal immunosenescence,<sup>122</sup> which is functionally distinct from its systemic counterpart.

The major change seen in the aged mucosa is a failure to elicit an antigen \[Ag\]-specific secretory IgA \[SIgA\] antibody \[Ab\] response, which forms the first line of local defence against potentially invasive microorganisms and is central to the normal function of the GI tract as an immune barrier.<sup>176</sup> Although conflicting results have been reported on the magnitude of IgA-mediated responses in ageing,<sup>124</sup> Ag-specific mucosal SIgA Ab responses, which are particularly supported by the GALT inductive immune system, are markedly lower in aged animals,<sup>122,129</sup> suggesting that Ag-specific mucosal immune responses are attenuated. Further, adoptive transfer of adipose tissue-derived mesenchymal stem cells from young donors restored a youthful Ag-specific SIgA Ab response in aged mice.<sup>130</sup> These findings suggest a possible failure of induction of SIgA Ab responses for protection in ageing.

The mechanisms underlying the impaired Ag-specific SIgA Ab responses as well as lack of oral tolerance induction that occur in the ageing state have been proposed to involve decreased DCs and follicular DC functions in Peyer’s patches, in addition to impaired T cell responses.<sup>128</sup> Ageing deficits in mucosal DCs derived from the small intestine are characterized by a diminished capacity to prime T cell responses, failure to stimulate TGF-β secretion and differentiation of CD4 <sup>+</sup> LAP <sup>+</sup> T <sub>reg</sub> cells.<sup>131</sup> In addition, mucosal innate lymphoid cells \[ILCs\] are a heterogeneous group of innate immune cells that have been implicated in the pathogenesis of chronic intestinal inflammation in IBD,<sup>120,121</sup> by playing a critical role in maintaining the integrity of the mucosal barrier. In aged humans, intestinal group 3 \[ILC3\] that produce the Th17 cell-associated cytokines decrease with ageing, whereas classical NK cells exhibit a compensatory increase.<sup>132</sup> Parallel to this ageing-related change, ILC3 levels have been shown to decrease in colonic mucosa samples of patients with UC.<sup>154</sup> Furthermore, intestinal samples from CD patients have shown increased frequencies of ILC2,<sup>153</sup> which is also consistent with age-associated increases in ILC2.<sup>133</sup> A dysregulated mucosal immune response is the central driver of IBD, and aberrant functions of mucosal DCs and ILCs may contribute to elderly-onset IBD.

#### 2.2.3. Biochemical changes in the intestinal barrier with ageing

The intestinal mucus layer, which is formed by glycoprotein mucins secreted by goblet cells, coats IECs and functions as a biochemical defence barrier against microbial invasion.<sup>177</sup> In aged mice or accelerated ageing mice models, the colonic mucus layer becomes thinner or absent,<sup>155,156,178</sup> which may be due to a lack of mucus-secreting goblet cells in the colonic crypts.<sup>156</sup> Ageing-induced deterioration of the protective mucus layer is associated with increased bacterial penetrability, alterations in immunity and microbiota composition, as well as increased susceptibility to colonic inflammation.<sup>156</sup> In line with this, active UC is associated with thinner colonic mucus layers that are more permeable to bacteria,<sup>157</sup> along with a decrease in core mucus components such as MUC2 and FCGBP, a decrease in sentinel goblet cells and a reduced secretory response to microbes,<sup>179</sup> contributing to the development of UC.

The AMPs, produced by Paneth cells,<sup>40</sup> are another major component of the biochemical barrier as they localize to the mucus layer and act in conjunction with the mucus layer to prevent bacterial attachment and invasion.<sup>177,180,181</sup> In addition, intestinal AMPs can influence treatment outcomes, as anti-TNF therapy responders and non-responders in UC exhibit distinct patterns of mucosal AMP expression.<sup>136</sup> In aged mice, the transcript levels of Paneth cell-derived AMPs such as ileal α-defensin and lysozyme are decreased, while other AMP genes are increased, including regenerating islet-derived protein \[Reg\]-3β and -3γ, β-defensin 1, angiogenin-4 and resistin-like molecule beta \[Relmβ\].<sup>134</sup> The mechanism underlying the age-related upregulation of AMPs may be mediated by cytokines.<sup>135</sup> In contrast to the observations in aged mice, a study of elderly people suggests that levels of several serum AMPs are not affected by ageing, as elder individuals produce comparable levels of cathelicidin \[LL-37\] and β-defensin-2 \[hBD-2\] as healthy young adults.<sup>137</sup> However, it is noteworthy that the secretion of human α-defensin 5 is lower in the elderly than in middle-aged individuals.<sup>158</sup> This parallels the reduced expression of α-defensin 5 shown in patients with ileal CD, which compromises mucosal host defences and predisposes patients to CD.<sup>159</sup>

## 3\. Ageing Gut Microbiome and IBD

The gut is home to the largest microbial community of the body and is the most diverse and well-studied commensal ecosystem. However, a disrupted \[‘dysbiotic’\] gut microbiome has been extensively characterized in IBD, where it is closely associated with a reduction in the total number, and diversity, of microbial species.<sup>40,182–184</sup> Furthermore, dysbiosis is a hallmark of ageing. The microbiota profile of older adults is different from that of young adults for several reasons associated with senescence, including ageing-related changes in lifestyle and dietary intake, decreased locomotion, weakened immune strength, altered gut morphology and physiology, recurrent infections, hospitalizations, polypharmacy and frailty.<sup>138,185–188</sup> The causal relationship between changes in the microbiome and host ageing has yet to be determined. The substantial alteration of the microbiome in ageing may negatively impact gut physiology by leading to reduced gut motility, decreased mucus secretion and impaired intestinal barrier dysfunction.<sup>138</sup> This results in a cascade of inflammatory events that enhance the risk of developing ageing-associated pathologies including frailty, neurodegeneration, type-2 diabetes, cancer and cardiovascular disease.<sup>189</sup> It is likely that overlapping or shared microbiome alterations across these ageing-linked disorders are, in part, a consequence of general physiological decline, including inflammation and loss of mucosal barrier, which have been summarized by DeJong *et al*.<sup>190</sup> Importantly, Ghosh *et al.* have demonstrated that cross-disease microbiome alterations overlap with those changes associated with healthy ageing and unhealthy ageing.<sup>187,191</sup>

Although dysbiosis probably contributes to IBD pathogenesis,<sup>40,121</sup> the cause–effect relationship between microbial dysbiosis and IBD has not yet been fully elucidated.<sup>192,193</sup> In IBD, microbial dysbiosis manifests as an overall decrease in bacterial diversity, along with decreased protective bacteria (Bacteroidetes \[*Bacteroides fragilis*\], Firmicutes \[*Lactobacillus*, *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and *Clostridium* strains\] and Actinobacteria \[\[*Bifidobacterium*\]) <sup>120,184,194–196</sup> and increased pathogenic species (Proteobacteria \[Gammaproteobacteria, and *Escherichia coli*\] and Fusobacteria \[*Fusobacterium nucleatum*\]).<sup>120,194,197</sup> Furthermore, multi-omics approaches applied in cross-sectional and longitudinal studies have revealed that metagenomic, metatranscriptomic and stool metabolomic profiles are disrupted during IBD activity.<sup>183</sup> Dysbiosis in IBD involves an increase in facultative anaerobes at the expense of obligate anaerobes, disruptions of microbial transcription, and a decrease in beneficial metabolite pools, including short-chain fatty acids \[SCFAs\] such as butyrate and propionate, as well as secondary bile acids \[lithocholate and deoxycholate\].<sup>183,198</sup>

Similarly, ageing is associated with a decrease in the diversity of intestinal commensal microbes, manifested by a loss of beneficial commensal bacteria, and an increase in opportunistic and potentially pathogenic commensal microbes. Generally, the composition of the intestinal microbiota in older adults \[>65 years\] is extremely variable between individuals and differs from the core microbiota and diversity levels in younger adults.<sup>199</sup> In particular, the intestinal commensal bacteria \[bifidobacteria, lactobacilli, Bacteroides\] that are responsible for maintaining immune tolerance are reported to be reduced in the elderly, while Proteobacteria is elevated,<sup>6,185,188,200,201</sup> mirroring changes noted in IBD patients.<sup>120,184,194</sup> Furthermore, the ageing-associated changes in gut commensals are also accompanied by a decrease in microbiome-associated metabolites such as vitamin B12, vitamin B7, creatine and butyrate, as well as their respective microbial metabolic pathways, thus contributing to muscle atrophy and increasing frailty.<sup>202,203</sup>

To date, there is a lack of human research exploring the interaction between IBD–microbiome associations and ageing. However, a recent study has shown that ageing influences microbiome composition as well as microbiome–disease signatures in IBD.<sup>191</sup> In that study based on gut microbiomes derived from over 2500 individuals, age was identified as a strong covariate of disease signatures across multiple diseases, including IBD. In particular, IBD was associated with distinct bacterial species across three age groups (young \[20–39 years\], middle-aged \[40–59 years\], and elderly \[above 60 years\]) with a decrease in the prevalence of *Clostridium*, Lachnospiraceae, *Escherichia*, *Blautia producta* and streptococci in the elderly with IBD compared to the young.<sup>191</sup> Currently, the relationship between the IBD-associated gut microbiome and disease pathogenesis in elderly-onset IBD is poorly understood due to a lack of direct evidence and relatively small sample sizes in existing studies.

## 4\. Ageing Microbial–Immune Crosstalk in the Initiation of Elderly-Onset IBD

The advancements in genomics over the past few years have revealed how the host immune system contributes to the determination of the gut microbiota and, in turn, how the microbiota modulates the immune system.<sup>204</sup> The interplay between the immune system and the microbiome is balanced for the maintenance of homeostasis, but abnormalities in these tightly controlled regulatory circuits could be linked to ageing-related disorders. Although the exact pathogenesis of IBD remains unknown, IBD is believed to be associated with a breakdown in intestinal homeostasis pathways due to a dysfunctional communication between the epithelial barrier, intestinal flora and immune system.<sup>79,205</sup>

During ageing, immunosenescence and an ageing gut microbiome develop as both the immune system and the intestinal microbial ecosystem undergo profound remodelling. As such, this raises the question of whether immunosenescence and age-related changes in the microbiota are causally related. Recent studies have demonstrated the role of the microbiota in the regulation of immunosenescence using microbiome modulation. Faecal microbiota transplantation \[FMT\] has been demonstrated to be an effective tool for manipulating the ageing processes, by reversing ageing-associated dysbiosis and restoring secondary bile acids in progeroid mice, thereby counteracting the ageing process and extending lifespan.<sup>206</sup> In a multi-omics human study, reversal of immunosenescence features was observed in patients with severe or fulminant *Clostridioides difficile* infection undergoing successful FMT treatment,<sup>207</sup> suggesting that changes in the gut microbiome may influence immunosenescence pathways. Similarly, in middle-aged mice, syringaresinol \[SYR\], a polyphenolic lignan, reverses immunosenescence through enhancing beneficial bacteria \[*Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium*\] while reducing opportunistic pathogens \[*Akkermansia*\], suggesting that SYR contributes to the reversal of immunosenescence via modulation of gut integrity and microbiota diversity.<sup>208</sup> In addition, transferring gut microbiota from old to young mice promotes inflammation in the small intestine, upregulates inflammation-associated immune pathways \[PRRs, Th cell differentiation, B cell development\] and enhances leakage of inflammatory bacterial components into the circulation, indicating that an aged microbiota contributes to inflammageing.<sup>209</sup> These findings suggest that the restoration of a youthful microbiome promotes the rejuvenation of an aged host by counteracting immunosenescence and inflammageing, which may slow down ageing and its associated diseases.

The ‘leaky gut’, or increased intestinal permeability, is one of the key consequences of ageing-onset dysbiosis. The age-related deterioration of intestinal barrier function is proposed to lead to the leakage of gut microbes into the systemic circulation, thus increasing systemic inflammation and hyper-inflammatory responses, which ultimately increases the host’s susceptibility to various age-related diseases.<sup>111,210</sup> In turn, the translocation of microbes and their by-products, identified as microbe-associated molecular patterns \[MAMPs\] or PAMPs, may contribute to inflammageing.<sup>110,111</sup> Mice with ageing gut microbiomes produce more pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-6 and TNFα as well as a breakdown of the intestinal barrier.<sup>111</sup> Dysbiosis with advancing age is also linked to a reduction in commensal bacteria-derived metabolites, such as SCFAs and bile acids. In parallel, dysbiosis in IBD patients is associated with a decrease in the number of SCFAs and butyrate-producing bacteria, in particular members of the phylum Firmicutes.<sup>211,212</sup> Butyrate, a key SCFA, is a beneficial mediator of intestinal barrier integrity, as it promotes epithelial cell proliferation and increases the expression of TJ components such as occludin, ZO-1 and claudin-2.<sup>213</sup> Moreover, butyrate and secondary bile acids \[3β-hydroxydeoxycholic acid, isoDCA\] play a vital role in reducing intestinal inflammation through promoting the expansion of T <sub>regs</sub> <sup>214,215</sup> or regulating B <sub>reg</sub> functions.<sup>216</sup> Therefore, age-related loss of SCFAs and bile acids may further lead to the breakdown of the intestinal barrier and bacterial translocation, and subsequently chronic intestinal inflammation.

There is a reciprocal relationship between immunosenescence, the ageing gut microbiome and an impaired intestinal barrier. In the past decade, research has focused on the complex interactions between the microbiome, host immunity and intestinal epithelial barrier, revealing a wide range of mechanisms involved in the crosstalk between these three entities, and how aberrations within this communication network may contribute to the molecular aetiology of a number of multifactorial diseases, including IBD.<sup>205,217–219</sup> During ageing, this immune–microbiota–intestinal barrier cross-talk may be largely mediated by PRRs (i.e. TLRs, nucleotide-binding oligomerization \[NOD\]-like receptors \[NLRs\], and the RIG-like receptors), which sense signals derived from microbial products \[such as PAMPs and MAMPs\] to initiate immune responses and elicit protective barrier responses.<sup>79,205,220</sup> For example, the CD-associated PRR NOD2 triggers a series of immune responses when activated by bacteria-derived muramyl-dipeptide \[MDP\], including the activation of NF-κB, autophagy, and production of AMPs and pro-inflammatory mediators.<sup>218,221,222</sup> Moreover, PRR signals modulate epithelial barrier function by inducing proliferative and growth factors \[COX2, PGE2, and amphiregulin\] as well as strengthening TJs between IECs.<sup>223,224</sup> PRRs also play an important role in limiting bacterial colonization and translocation by stimulating IEC production of AMPs such as defensins and Reg3γ.<sup>219,225</sup> In particular, the PRR members associated with IBD susceptibility play an important role in controlling dysbiosis and colon inflammation, including NOD2,<sup>226</sup> TLR5,<sup>227</sup> NLRP6 <sup>228</sup> and NLRP12.<sup>229</sup> However, chronic activation of PRRs by microbial exposure, which increases with ageing, can drive systemic and local inflammatory responses, resulting in a multitude of pro-inflammatory molecules, thus fuelling inflammageing.<sup>79,205,230</sup> Inflammageing underpins microbial dysbiosis and has been linked to increased gut permeability.<sup>231,232</sup> In addition, recent research has suggested that a ‘gut–muscle axis’ exists in IBD patients, wherein inflammation, gut dysbiosis and malnutrition interact leading to frailty and sarcopenia.<sup>233</sup> Nestled within this concept is a vital role of the ageing gut microbiome, as physical frailty and sarcopenia are marked by a loss of microbial diversity and the depletion of specific microbes such as saccharolytic and butyrate-producing bacteria.<sup>116,234</sup> It has been speculated that gut dysbiosis may alter the immune response and host metabolism, promoting inflammageing which upregulates several molecular pathways that are associated with sarcopenia and frailty,<sup>233</sup> and in turn could contribute to disease pathogenesis in elderly-onset IBD patients.

In summary, advancing age is accompanied by modifications in the microbiome, host immune system and intestinal barrier, which are inextricably linked. Although we are still unable to ascertain the specific causal relationships between these events associated with ageing, a vicious cycle is hypothesized in which immunosenescence, inflammageing and an ageing microbiome form part of a feed-forward feedback mechanism which could contribute to the pathogenesis of elderly-onset IBD \[Figure 2\].

## 5\. Contribution of Other Factors to IBD Pathogenesis in the Elderly

Ageing is an extremely complex, multifactorial process encompassing a wide spectrum of factors that contribute to diminished function and increased risk of morbidity and mortality. In addition to immunosenescence, inflammageing and the ageing microbiome, other variables may also predispose to elderly-onset IBD, including epigenetic modifications, glycosylation and environmental exposures.

Emerging evidence from cohort studies of the elderly suggests that age-associated changes in epigenetic signatures may contribute to inflammageing and immunosenescence, and hence could be associated with ageing-related pathologies. Epigenetic and transcriptomic analyses have linked ageing-related changes in DNA methylation at cytosine-phospho-guanine \[CpG\] sites with circulating levels of inflammageing biomarkers, such as IL-6,<sup>235</sup> C-reactive protein \[CRP\],<sup>236</sup> TNF, IL-8 or IL-10.<sup>237</sup> Epigenetic mechanisms also influence immunosenescence by manipulating the plasticity of immune cells during the ageing process.<sup>238</sup> Likewise, altered epigenetic methylation patterns of IBD-associated genes have been observed in blood and tissue samples from IBD patients,<sup>239–242</sup> in particular genes involved in inflammation and immune response, such as *α-defensin 5* \[*DEFA5*\] and *TNF*,<sup>240</sup> which are implicated in IBD pathogenesis. In addition, there is evidence that IBD patients have dysregulated miRNA levels,<sup>243–245</sup> and miRNAs may influence specific IBD characteristics, such as the loss of barrier integrity and dysregulation of the immune system,<sup>246–249</sup> implying that therapeutics based on miRNAs may be beneficial to patients.<sup>250</sup>

Glycosylation, one of the most common post-translational modifications of proteins, also undergoes profound changes associated with ageing, cancer and inflammatory conditions.<sup>251</sup> *N* -Glycome has been found to be closely associated with the clinical characterization of IBD, including disease localization, activity and response to therapy,<sup>252,253</sup> and may therefore play a role in disease pathophysiology. In CD and UC patients, a wide range of *N* -glycosylation patterns in plasma differ from those found in healthy controls, including fucosylation, bisection, galactosylation and sialylation.<sup>252</sup> Moreover, the intestinal mucus of patients with IBD is characterized by decreased glycosylation levels and impaired mucin synthesis, resulting in barrier dysfunction.<sup>254</sup> Also, changes in the glycosylation of IgG antibodies have been associated with IBD.<sup>253,255</sup> Specifically, a decrease in IgG galactosylation and sialylation in IBD has been linked to a more pro-inflammatory antibody-mediated immune response and advanced disease severity.<sup>253,255</sup> Interestingly this change in IgG glycosylation also occurs with ageing, shifting towards a pro-inflammatory glycotype and potentially contributing to inflammageing.<sup>256–258</sup> Thus, the IgG glycopattern and total glycosylation patterns in the elderly may predispose to the development of inflammatory diseases such as IBD.

Lastly, a variety of other influences may add to the complexity of understanding the pathogenesis of elderly-onset IBD. Smoking and smoking cessation,<sup>259,260</sup> dietary pattern and nutritional intake,<sup>261–263</sup> and vascular endothelial dysfunction,<sup>264</sup> as well as environmental triggers such as air pollution,<sup>265,266</sup> antibiotics,<sup>267</sup> infections/vaccinations,<sup>268</sup> and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs \[NSAIDs\],<sup>269</sup> may contribute to dysbiosis and aberrant immune responses in the elderly. In addition, over 240 genes have been linked with IBD susceptibility,<sup>270,271</sup> including those involved in bacteria-sensing \[e.g. *NOD2*\], the autophagy pathway for microbe clearance \[e.g. *ATG16L1*, *IRGM* and *LRRK2*\], epithelial barrier function \[e.g. *ECM1*\], innate and adaptive immunity regulation \[e.g. *IL23R* and *IL10*\], and integrity of the mucus layer \[*MUC2*\]; however, genetic predisposition is less likely to be a major contributor to elderly-onset IBD as compared to early-onset IBD.<sup>2</sup>

## 6\. Summary and Future Perspectives

This is the first review which has theorized potential mechanisms for the pathogenesis of elderly-onset IBD. There is mechanistic evidence that inflammageing, immune remodelling and alterations in the gut microbiota with ageing may lead to an unyielding cycle that contributes to elderly-onset IBD. Admittedly, our knowledge in this nascent field is limited, and many unknowns remain regarding the complicated crosstalk between ageing and the gut–microbiome–immune axis that results in either healthy ageing or IBD in advanced age. Although clear differences have been demonstrated in both the systemic and mucosal immune systems as well as the gut microbiome between the young and elderly, the impact of these changes on the development of IBD has yet to be explored. Irrespective of the age of onset, aberrant immune responses appear to be crucial to the development of IBD, and while in paediatric-onset IBD this is driven primarily by genetics, in elderly-onset IBD we propose that the immunological changes arise primarily from immunosenescence and inflammageing. It is imperative to bridge the knowledge gap between paediatric-onset, adult-onset and elderly-onset IBD regarding the different risk factors and pathophysiological mechanisms at play. For this to be achieved, future longitudinal studies across various geographies and nationalities should utilize multi-omics platforms for high-throughput profiling on humans,<sup>183,272,273</sup> integrating multiple layers of data on immune modifications, gut microbial composition and function, environmental exposures, genetics, epigenetics, and clinical phenotype. By utilizing various omics signatures <sup>274</sup> together with humanized gnotobiotic rodent models that allow functional validation, we can obtain multidimensional insights into the molecular pathways and risk factors associated with elderly-onset IBD.

Furthermore, upon better understanding the pathogenesis of elderly-onset IBD, pharmacological treatments based on the advances in molecular pathways involved in the reciprocal interactions between immunosenescence and the ageing microbiome, such as those discussed in this review, may help personalize therapeutic options. Non-biologic or small-molecule therapies including microbiota-targeted dietary and probiotic interventions,<sup>187,261,275</sup> FMT,<sup>275</sup> metabolite-based treatments,<sup>276</sup> microbial engineering,<sup>277</sup> epigenetic reprogramming <sup>278–281</sup> or even senolytic drugs <sup>282</sup> are areas that warrant further investigation. There are also promising prospects for developing ageing-driven biomarkers as potential predictors of disease or prognosticators of suboptimal clinical outcomes. Though elderly-onset IBD patients comprise a niche population within IBD, obtaining a better understanding of their disease pathogenesis should prove valuable for improving the care for all elderly IBD patients.
