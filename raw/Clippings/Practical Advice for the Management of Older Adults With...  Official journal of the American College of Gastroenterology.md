There is an exponential rise in the number of older adults with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), both Crohn's disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC), likely due to more effective treatments, decreasing disease-related fatality, and an increasing incidence of disease at older ages (1). Currently, there are nearly 7 million people worldwide who have IBD; approximately one-third of people living with IBD in the Western world are 60 years or older (2,3). Although the rise in prevalence of older adults with IBD is a testament to our advances in care, years of life lived with disability has now doubled with a peak in the 7th decade of life (4). Early consideration of aging-related principles among individuals with IBD can shift this trajectory, improving not only mortality, but also clinical outcomes and thereby quality of life for this unique and rapidly growing cohort of people with IBD.

## EPIDEMIOLOGY & DIAGNOSTIC CONSIDERATIONS

In the U.S., approximately 1% of individuals over the age of 60 years have IBD: the age cohort where IBD is the most prevalent (2). This is comprised of individuals who developed IBD early in life and are now aging, as well as the 15%–20% of individuals who develop IBD at 60 years of age or older (5). For the purposes of this review, when referencing older adults with IBD, this will refer to individuals ≥60 years old, consistent with prior literature.

Diagnosing new-onset IBD in the older adult represents a unique challenge as a myriad of advanced age associated comorbidities can present with similar symptoms and endoscopic findings. These include segmental colitis associated with diverticulitis, colorectal cancer, radiation proctitis, ischemic and infectious colitis, as well as NSAID-induced enterocolitis (6,7). A detailed summary of potential mimickers, along with their clinical characteristics and distinguishing features, is provided in Table 1 of the paper by Taleban et al. (6) When evaluating an older adult with new onset gastrointestinal symptoms such as weight loss, rectal bleeding, abdominal pain, diarrhea and/or constipation, a careful review of medical history, family history (for presence of other immune-mediated conditions), and medications used (both prescribed and over the counter) must first be performed. This, in conjunction with laboratory tests (elevated C-reactive protein, anemia, thrombocytosis, etc.), stool tests (both infectious studies as well as inflammatory [fecal calprotectin and/or lactoferrin]), imaging, endoscopy and pathology can help confirm the potential diagnosis of IBD. Expedited and complete evaluation, recognizing that IBD can present for the first time later in life, can reduce diagnostic delays frequently experienced by older adults. In cases where the diagnosis remains uncertain, close follow-up with repeat labs, imaging, and/or endoscopy is warranted to assess for chronicity and disease extension, both of which may offer important clues supporting a diagnosis of IBD. Early diagnosis is critical, as prolonged inflammation and life-impairing symptoms may contribute to physical and cognitive decline (8,9).

Environmental factors should be considered, as they are posited to play a greater role than genetic factors in the development of older-onset IBD. In 2 recent epidemiologic studies, antibiotics, particularly those with the greatest impact on the intestinal microbiome (e.g., fluoroquinolones, nitroimizadoles), and the presence of atherosclerotic-related diseases, were found to predispose older adults to develop IBD (10,11).

Clinical presentations differ among older adults with incident IBD compared to younger adults who develop IBD. In particular, rectal bleeding is more common among older adults with new-onset CD as compared to younger adults (12). There are also differing phenotypes and areas of disease involvement in older adults with new-onset CD; they are more likely to have isolated ileal or colonic disease but less likely to have ileocolonic disease as compared to younger adults (13). Further, stricturing disease is more common among older adults with CD, with a lower incidence of penetrating disease. In older-onset UC, left-sided colitis is more common, with a lower incidence of isolated proctitis or pancolitis.

## DISEASE COURSE AMONG OLDER ADULTS WITH IBD

A common misconception is that disease-severity is lower among older individuals. In a meta-analysis of population-based cohort studies, older adults who had new onset IBD, had similar clinical trajectories at 1, 5, and 10 years as compared to younger adults. More specifically, need for corticosteroids, IBD-related hospitalization, and IBD-related surgery were similar between the older-onset and younger-onset cohorts, when stratified by both UC and CD (14). Although immunosenescence is classically thought to reflect a reduced immune response, it may instead represent immune dysregulation, characterized by diminished T-cell diversity and loss of tolerance to self-antigens, which may in part explain the similar severity of IBD observed in older adults (15). In a survey-based study of individuals dating back to 1978, nearly one-quarter of adults had active disease even after 20 years of diagnosis (16). Among individuals whose IBD improves with advancing age, there are no clinical or molecular mechanisms to identify these patients.

### Summary: Introduction

- Older adults are the age cohort with the most prevalent IBD.
- Minimize diagnostic (and treatment) delays
- Perform a careful environmental and exposure history
- Some older adults may have less clinically significant IBD with advancing chronologic age, though many older adults still experience robust inflammation.

## TREATMENT CONSIDERATIONS

The therapeutic armamentarium for IBD is rapidly expanding (17). Despite the number of steroid-sparing treatment options, older adults with IBD are significantly less likely to receive steroid-sparing therapy as compared to younger adults (14,18). This may in part explain why older adults with IBD have worse clinical outcomes as compared to younger adults; a finding which cannot be explained by differences in disease course. Older adults are more likely to be hospitalized, require surgical management, and have a disproportionately greater mortality from IBD as compared with younger adults (19–21). Further, advanced chronologic age is frequently used as an exclusion criterion in IBD clinical trials, resulting in a reluctance to use advanced therapies for older adults with IBD. Of the 46 randomized clinical trials (RCTs) reporting age, only 18 (39.1%) included individuals ≥ 65 years old (21). Moreover, of these 18 RCTs, individuals ≥ 65 years old only accounted for 0.6% of the 6,517 trial participants (22,23). Thus, while the management of IBD has advanced overall, older adults have not benefited due to a lack of data.

### Medical treatments

#### Corticosteroids

Over the past century, corticosteroids have been the mainstay of treatment for IBD. However, due to the high-risk of both short- and long-term side-effects, including but not limited to thrombosis, fractures, insulin resistance, adrenal insufficiency and infections, corticosteroid-sparing options have replaced them as the standard of care (24). Additionally, older adults are at higher risk for adverse outcomes due to corticosteroids compared to younger adults (25). Despite this, older adults are often prescribed prolonged courses of corticosteroids for the treatment of IBD (26). We recommend that corticosteroid use should be limited to the induction period and minimized in the maintenance period, with consideration of switching strategies if corticosteroids are needed frequently. If needed, we recommend a trial of budesonide over systemic corticosteroids if appropriate.

#### Mesalamines

Mesalamine agents can be effective for the treatment of mild to moderate UC. However, mesalamine monotherapy is not recommended for maintenance in the setting of moderate to severe UC-related inflammation. Similarly, although data are limited regarding mesalamine use in mild CD, high-quality evidence demonstrates that it is not effective for moderate to severe CD (27,28). Despite this, in a French population-based study of older adults with new-onset IBD, more than 90% of individuals with both UC and CD were prescribed mesalamines, whereas fewer than 20% with UC and 33% with CD were prescribed anti-TNF or immunomodulator therapy within 10 years of diagnosis (29). This pattern is consistent with findings from a Danish national registry study, which reported that individuals with IBD onset at or after age 70 had markedly lower use of immunomodulating therapy and surgery, even compared to those aged 60 and above (30). These low rates likely reflect hesitancy in prescribing immune-modulating therapies due to concerns about adverse events, rather than an accurate reflection of disease severity distribution in older adults. Additionally, since renal function declines with advancing age, kidney function should be monitored at least annually when using mesalamine to ensure prompt recognition of the rare but serious side effect of interstitial nephritis.

#### Immunomodulators

Immunomodulators are older medications with a less established safety profile among older adults. Methotrexate is primarily renally excreted, which should be kept at the forefront when prescribing it for the older adult. Furthermore, methotrexate toxicity is often quite subtle and occurs more commonly in the older adult, with presentation most notable for fatigue and myalgias (31). Thiopurines also carry greater risks with advancing chronologic age (32–34). This is especially the case given the elevated baseline risk of lymphoma that is present among individuals >55 years old, with 1 model suggesting a 9 fold increase in lymphoma incidence between the 4th decade of life and 8th decade of life with thiopurine use (34,35). Additionally, thiopurine use is associated with nearly a twofold increased risk of non-melanoma skin cancer (NMSC), an especially important consideration in older adults who may already have a history of recurrent skin cancers (36). Despite the potential increase in risk of lymphoma, NMSC, and infection, combination therapy may still be appropriate in select older adults; particularly those with ongoing perianal disease or at high risk of developing anti-TNF antibodies. In a post-hoc analysis of the REACT trial, combination therapy was not associated with a higher risk of serious complications or death compared to conventional therapy in older adults (37). However, in those with a history of lymphoma or NMSC, these risks may outweigh potential benefits and should prompt careful consideration of combination therapy. Notably, no dedicated studies of thiopurine withdrawal or replacement among the older adult who has maintained endoscopic remission has been assessed.

#### Biologics

Biologic agents have an established safety profile in older adults, with studies demonstrating the safety of anti-TNF agents, vedolizumab and ustekinumab (38–41). While many studies conclude that biologic agents confer a higher rate of adverse events, including infections and hospitalizations, among older adults as compared to younger adults, it is imperative to understand the drug-attributable risk vs the disease-attributable risk (42,43). In a post-hoc analysis of randomized clinical trial data for older adults with moderate-to-severe UC, effective IBD treatment with an anti-TNF agent resulted in numerically lower rates of serious adverse events, hospitalizations, and severe infections as compared with older adults who were treated with placebo (44). These results support the notion that treatment of disease with anti-TNF therapy does not worsen clinical outcomes, and may in fact, improve clinical outcomes among older adults with IBD. It is therefore paramount to consider both the safety and efficacy of a therapy when choosing a biologic. Further, comorbidities should be considered, as they can both determine the appropriateness of a therapy as well as therapy related risks (45).

When using anti-TNF therapy among older adults with IBD, it is critical to ensure adequate dosing based upon albumin and clinical characteristics. In an analysis of more than 20,000 samples, older adults were more likely to develop antibodies to anti-TNF therapy, and were less likely to receive dose escalation compared to younger adults with IBD (46). This underscores the importance of close therapeutic drug monitoring in this population, particularly in the setting of ongoing symptoms. There is a lack of data demonstrating an association between serum level of anti-TNF and risk of an adverse outcome, thus dosing should be based upon clinical characteristics, and should be determined independent of chronological age.

#### Small molecule inhibitors

In the ORAL surveillance study, individuals with rheumatoid arthritis who were ≥50 years old with at least 1 additional cardiovascular risk factor failed to demonstrate non-inferiority for JAKi as compared to anti-TNF therapy for the outcomes of a major adverse cardiovascular event or malignancy. Venous thromboembolism (VTE) events were also higher for the tofacitinib group as compared to the anti-TNF group, with a dose dependent effect seen (47). However, the safety of small molecule therapy among older adults with IBD has been less well studied. In 1 retrospective study of 123 individuals ≥50 years old on tofacitinib, there was no increased risk of venous thromboembolism or cardiovascular events, as compared to those on anti-TNF therapy (48). Additional studies have suggested the long-term safety of JAK inhibitors, though their applicability is limited by the underrepresentation of older adults and individuals with established cardiovascular or thromboembolic comorbidities (49). Older patients should be aware of the potential safety concerns with this class of medication, but effective and potent therapy is essential when the disease severity merits its use. Further, the risk of malignancy, venous thromboembolism, and major cardiovascular events appear to be greatest in individuals ≥65 years old and/or prior/current tobacco users (50).

In the US, individuals with UC are approved for the use of S1P-receptor modulators (sphingosine-1-phosphate receptor modulators). However, the experience with this category of medications is limited in older adults. S1P-receptors are present on cardiac myocardium and thus associated with a risk of bradycardia, as well as with macular edema. However, induction, maintenance, and long-term extension data reveals the absolute risks to be low among older adults (51).

### Surgical treatment

Individuals with IBD who are refractory to medical therapy are often offered surgical intervention in order to improve quality of life, reduce the time with ongoing inflammation, and improve clinical outcomes. However, this may not be true for older adults. Unfortunately, due to the higher perceived risks of surgical intervention, surgery may be deferred or not offered as a potential treatment option among older adults. This deferral due to chronological age however can paradoxically increase the risk of an adverse outcome when surgery is required; delays in definitive treatment increases time with ongoing inflammation, the use of corticosteroids, malnutrition, and contributes to both physical and cognitive decline (52). In a nationwide study assessing surgical outcomes in IBD, older adults had a 37% risk of a major 30-day adverse outcome, vs 27% among younger adults with IBD (53). This was due to an increased number of older adults requiring emergency surgery, developing preoperative sepsis, having physical decline, and being malnourished; all factors related to potential delays in surgical care.

Recent data have also suggested a benefit for earlier and elective surgical intervention among older adults with IBD. In a real-world study akin to the LIR!C trial, upfront ileocecal resection as compared to initiation of anti-TNF therapy was associated with the greatest long-term benefit among individuals who were diagnosed with CD after the age of 40 years old (54,55). Further, in a prior study of individuals with UC, elective colectomy as compared to continued medical therapy had the greatest 5-year mortality benefit among individuals ≥ 50 years and older (56). Although more data is needed, quality of life as a result of surgical intervention appears to be maintained (57,58).

Comprehensive preoperative assessment is essential to optimize outcomes in older adults with IBD. While the importance of nutrition, sarcopenia, and frailty are addressed in detail in subsequent sections, prehabilitation strategies from the broader geriatric surgical literature, such as nutritional optimization, medication review, physical conditioning, and psychological support, may also be beneficial. For example, in colorectal cancer surgery, multimodal prehabilitation (including nutrition, strength training, and smoking cessation) has been associated with fewer postoperative complications (59). After surgery, elements of Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols, particularly fluid management, opioid minimization, and early mobilization may help improve outcomes, though data specific to IBD remain limited (60). Additionally, given the high prevalence and consequences of postoperative delirium in older adults, preventive strategies should be routinely implemented when feasible. These include minimizing deliriogenic medications, ensuring use of sensory aids, and promoting uninterrupted sleep, especially for those undergoing emergency surgery or with malnutrition or cognitive vulnerability (61). Finally, ostomy planning should incorporate cognitive and functional assessments to ensure patients can manage the appliance independently, particularly in the absence of caregiver support.

### Disease monitoring

Monitoring disease activity in older adults with IBD should be individualized through shared decision-making, as no evidence-based treatment targets specific to this population currently exist. While endoscopic evaluation may carry increased risks in individuals with mobility limitations, multiple comorbidities, or those aged 80 and older, including complications related to bowel preparation, sedation, and the procedure itself, it should not be avoided if it is likely to meaningfully influence diagnosis or management (62). To help mitigate these risks, the use of light sedation, CO2 insufflation, and careful consideration of comorbidities and underlying motility disorders is recommended (see “Colon Section” of this Monograph). When appropriate, lower-risk alternatives such as flexible sigmoidoscopy (e.g., for left-sided colitis) may offer sufficient assessment, and discontinuation of surveillance colonoscopies can be considered in patients over 75 without prior dysplasia, given the limited clinical yield (63). Non-invasive tools such as fecal calprotectin can also aid in monitoring but should be interpreted with caution, as levels may be elevated due to advancing age or concurrent use of PPIs and NSAIDs (64). Intestinal ultrasound (IUS) shows promise as a non-invasive modality, particularly for those with mobility limitations, although data specific to older adults remain limited. Regular follow-up, at least biannually or more frequently as clinically indicated, is essential to ensure treatment targets are met and care remains responsive to each patient's evolving needs.

### Summary: Treatment considerations

- Avoid corticosteroids for maintenance therapy or continuous tapers.
- Immunomodulators have long-term safety concerns.
- Choose the medication most likely to be effective in the older adult with IBD.
- Surgery should not be deferred due to advanced chronological age.
- Earlier surgery among older adults with IBD may lead to improved short- and long-term clinical outcomes.
- Tailor treatment targets to life expectancy, patient's goals and prior IBD-related history.

## GERIATRIC CONSIDERATIONS

Aging and IBD, which are both inflammatory processes, may act synergistically and lead to geriatric syndromes earlier in life as compared to individuals without IBD (65,66). This includes earlier development of infections, malignancies, osteoporosis, cognitive impairment, physical decline (e.g., frailty & sarcopenia), and polypharmacy (66–72).

### Frailty

Frailty is a geriatric syndrome that is defined as an increased vulnerability to stressors that contributes to a higher risk of morbidity and mortality (73,74). It is a marker of biological aging and is more prevalent in older adults, but it is not synonymous with chronological age. Frailty, independent of age, has been associated with an increased risk for infections, hospitalizations, readmissions and mortality among all-aged individuals with IBD (66,75–77). Alterations of immune function, including a heightened inflammatory state, are associated with the increased risk of frailty in older adults with IBD (78,79). Two retrospective studies, demonstrated that frailty can be mitigated and even potentially reversed with treatment of ongoing IBD-related inflammation (80,81). Although the clinical implications of measuring and addressing frailty in patients with IBD are yet to be fully elucidated, it is necessary to consider a patient's overall health and functional status rather than chronologic age alone when making treatment decisions (52,82). It should be noted that, at present, there is no single validated measure of frailty tailored specifically for patients with IBD.

### Sarcopenia

Sarcopenia is defined as the loss of muscle mass, strength, and function, and independent of age, is a physiologic marker that is associated with adverse outcomes among individuals with IBD (83–86). It has been associated with a greater risk for postoperative recurrence of Crohn's disease, as well as adverse postoperative outcomes (87). In a study of 120 older adults with IBD, increasing muscle mass, assessed by total skeletal muscle mass as compared to psoas mass alone, was found to be associated with a lower risk of major adverse 30-day postoperative outcomes (84). Thus, although further work needs to be completed, assessing sarcopenia in older adults with IBD may aid in risk stratification as well as future interventions.

### Malnutrition

Older adults are at increased risk for both malnutrition and micronutrient deficiencies (88,89). While there are evidence-based strategies to address malnutrition in the general older adult population, their applicability to individuals with IBD, who may have ongoing inflammation, altered anatomy, and food intolerances, remains unclear. Although IBD-specific nutritional guidelines for older adults are lacking, regular assessment of weight and routine use of a malnutrition screening tool, such as the Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST), is recommended (90). Micronutrient levels such as iron, B12 (if ileal disease), and Vitamin D should also routinely be checked, and can help identify those with ongoing micronutrient deficiencies (91).

A Mediterranean-style, plant-forward diet low in ultra-processed foods is generally advisable for patients with IBD, as it has been associated with a reduction in overall inflammatory burden (88,92). Tolerable protein sources should be encouraged, given the elevated risk of sarcopenia in the older adult population. Strategies to address decreased appetite and early satiety, such as offering smaller, more frequent meals, enhancing food palatability, managing symptoms like pain or nausea, deprescribing potentially contributing medications, and addressing underlying depression or psychological distress, may also support improved intake (93). However, these approaches have not been well studied in older adults with IBD and should be tailored to individual patient needs.

### Polypharmacy

Older adults are at high risk for polypharmacy, often defined numerically as taking ≥ 5 medications. Polypharmacy is associated with an increased risk of falls, hospitalization, and adverse drug effects (94). Older adults with IBD appear to be at an even higher risk for polypharmacy. Two studies found that >75% of older adults with IBD experienced polypharmacy as compared to 30% among community dwelling older adults (95–97). Further, polypharmacy contributes to an increased risk of all-cause hospitalization at 1 year, and is associated with a lower quality of life (95,96). As polypharmacy is an increasing burden among older adults, deprescribing efforts to reduce the overall pill burden as well as to ensure medication appropriateness are essential (94).

Corticosteroids, mesalamine, immunomodulators and small molecules pose risks for drug-drug interactions (95). Pill burden and increased frequency of dosing may increase the risk for non-adherence and error. Underlying sensory impairments (e.g., vision, cognition) can also impede compliance and increase risk for medication errors. Thus, when prescribing any medication to an older adult, the ease and ability to take the medication, as well as the potential for a drug-drug interaction or side-effect must be considered.

### Cognition

Early deficits in attention and working memory among children with IBD, correspond to the domains most associated with future dementia (98). Two large retrospective administrative-claims based studies demonstrated that IBD is associated with a higher risk for the development of Parkinson's Disease as well as Alzheimer's dementia (99,100). While the relationship between IBD and cognitive decline is relatively unexplored, subtle impairments in cognitive function in the older adult can influence treatment decisions (e.g., infusion-based therapy in which the patient can be monitored every few weeks may be preferred, if transportation/mobility allows). Further, ensuring symptoms of IBD are adequately controlled such that older adults are able to leave their home and engage both cognitively and socially in their usual activities, is critical to maintaining cognitive health (101). Commonly used tools such as the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) can assist in identifying early signs of cognitive impairment in clinical practice (102). Support for caregivers is also essential; the National Institute on Aging provides resources to help manage caregiver burden, and additional support may be available through local or state programs. A multimodal approach involving primary care/geriatrics and social work can further aid in managing cognitive concerns and supporting both patients and their caregivers.

### Falls & Osteoporosis

Older adults are also at increased baseline risk for falls, osteopenia, osteoporosis and osteoporotic fractures (103). While corticosteroid use is often perceived to be the primary risk factor for osteopenia, the inflammatory disease in itself is a risk factor for bone loss likely due to inflammation contributing to micronutrient deficiencies (104–106). Dual energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) scans as well as Vitamin D levels should be routinely checked among older adults with IBD, with a referral made to an endocrine/primary care doctor should osteopenia or osteoporosis be present (107).

Although screening and treatment are important to limit osteoporotic fractures, prevention should be the focus within our aging IBD patient population. Exercise, in addition to having a variety of health benefits, is critical; it can improve bone health, reduce falls, as well as minimize declines in overall physical health (reduce the risk of developing sarcopenia and frailty) (108). It is therefore essential to assess and effectively manage symptoms like urgency and incontinence, which can hinder these activities (52). Additionally, assessing the patient's living environment, including the presence of stairs, handrails, bathroom supports, and availability of family or caregiver assistance, can help reduce fall risk and promote safe, independent living.

### Ageism

Older adults with IBD frequently experience discrimination and prejudice based on advanced chronologic age alone (109). In a QORUS-based study, clinicians were less likely to make changes in the management of older adults with active IBD, stating that changes were less likely to lead to a “meaningful impact.” (110) Similarly, in an international survey of gastroenterologists, the majority reported an advanced chronological age at which they were more likely to withhold certain IBD prescriptions (111). In the United States, ageism may also be structural; older adults are more likely to receive health insurance coverage through Medicare, which has policies in place that are in many ways disparate from private insurers regarding coverage of novel therapeutics and ancillary support services (109). Advocating for your patient's equal right to prescriptions is a critical component of caring for the older adult with IBD. Resources and templates for prior authorization appeals are freely available online.

### Colon cancer screening

When to stop colon cancer surveillance is a clinical conundrum that practitioners often face without much robust data to support a clinical decision. Overall functional status, life expectancy, and surveillance history (e.g., prior dysplasia) should be taken into consideration (63,112). Given the limited data among older adults with IBD, general screening and surveillance practices in the geriatric patient population are discussed in more detail in the “Colon Section” of this Monograph.

### Vaccines

Vaccinations are a crucial aspect of comprehensive care for older adults with IBD, particularly because immunosuppression can heighten their vulnerability to infection-related illnesses. For an in-depth discussion of this, please refer to the “Vaccinations” Section of this Monograph.

### Telehealth

Older adults have also been left behind in the current telehealth revolution. Older adults are less likely to access video-based visits, while they are the ones that are most likely to benefit from video-based visits over in-person and telephone based visits (113). This is particularly true among individuals >80 years old, as well as among those without significant social support (114). Ensuring access to geriatric-friendly resources, as well as dedicated coaching however, may bridge this gap, and improve care (115).

### Summary: Geriatric considerations

- Individuals with IBD may develop aging-related conditions earlier in life: infections, malignancies, decreased bone mineral density, sarcopenia, frailty, etc.
- Frailty and sarcopenia may aid therapeutic risk-stratification, however further validation work is needed.
- When making treatment decisions, consider polypharmacy, cognitive impairment, and fall risk.

## CONCLUSION

While medical decisions in younger adults focus on medication efficacy and safety, geriatric-oriented medical decisions must also consider comorbid disease, quality of life, mobility, and cognition. Further, it is essential to recognize that undertreatment of ongoing symptoms can lead to disproportionately higher decrements in function and quality of life over time among older adults (101,116,117). Overall, care for the older adult with IBD should involve a comprehensive initial assessment to establish short and long term treatment goals and priorities, including risk stratification of clinical course, and engage in a multi-disciplinary conversation to formulate the most optimal treatment strategy (Table 1; Figure 3) (7).

## TAKE HOME POINTS

- Older adults with IBD can experience robust inflammation.
- Advanced therapies should not be withheld due to chronological age.
- Early and effective treatment can improve outcomes for older adults with IBD.
- Ongoing inflammation due to IBD is more detrimental to the older adult than the risk of side effects with currently approved treatments.
