## Summary

The burden of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) in older adults (ie, aged over 60 years old) is increasing due to a combination of an ageing population with compounding prevalence of IBD and increasing incidence of elderly-onset (ie, onset over the age of 60 years) IBD. Despite the increasing prevalence of IBD, there is a paucity of evidence on which to base management of older adults with IBD, leading to substantial variability in care. This population is under-represented in clinical trials and has a high burden of chronic corticosteroid use, low uptake of steroid-sparing immunosuppressive agents, and high rates of unplanned health-care use and disability. Management of IBD in older adults requires carefully weighing an individual patient's risk of IBD-related complications, IBD-directed immunosuppressive therapy, and non-IBD comorbidities. A deeper understanding of biological and functional age, dynamic risk stratification strategies (including frailty-based risk assessment tools), comparative effectiveness and safety of current therapies and treatment strategies, and shared decision making to inform treatment goals and targets is needed to improve outcomes in older adults with IBD. In this Review, we discuss the epidemiology, natural history, pathophysiology, and medical and surgical management of older individuals living with IBD and identify key research gaps and approaches to address them.

### Natural history of IBD in ageing people with adult-onset or paediatric-onset IBD

Few data exist on how ageing modifies the natural history of IBD in people with adult-onset and paediatric-onset IBD ([panel](#box1)). There is a popular belief that IBD becomes milder (colloquially, burns out) with age, implying that the disease enters an extended period of quiescence with low risk of complications; however, this concept might be wishful thinking.

20.

Etienney, I ∙ Bouhnik, Y ∙ Gendre, JP ∙ et al.

**Crohn's disease over 20 years after diagnosis in a referral population**

*Gastroenterol Clin Biol.* 2004; **28**:1233-1239

These studies suggest that a subset of individuals with adult-onset IBD have disease complications, hospitalisation, and surgery for the first time only 10–20 years after their initial diagnosis (possibly at >60 years).

Panel

Key research gaps in the management of older people with inflammatory bowel disease and approaches to address them

**Epidemiology and natural history**

Does inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) burn out with age? What are the effects of ageing on disease course in people with adult-onset or paediatric-onset IBD and the factors associated with low disease activity and severity in later years of disease?

- In population-based cohorts, or inception cohorts, examine the longitudinal risk of disease-related complications (disease progression, surgery, hospital admission, etc) by each decade of life in people with adult-onset or paediatric-onset IBD.
- Identify long-term trajectories with longitudinal evaluation of disease activity in people with IBD, and identify time-evolving patient-related, disease-related, and treatment-related factors associated with quiescent disease in older age.

**Pathophysiology**

What leads to elderly-onset IBD? What are the genetic, immunological, microbiome, and environmental factors that lead to elderly-onset IBD, and how does elderly-onset IBD differ from adult-onset and paediatric-onset IBD in older people?

- In inception cohorts, use multiomic studies to examine factors associated with ageing and development of IBD in older age.

**Treatment approach**

How should older people with IBD be risk-stratified? Can dynamic risk stratification be used to identify older people at high risk of IBD disease-related versus IBD treatment-related versus non-IBD chronic disease-related complications?

- Use dynamic modelling studies based on time-varying statistical analyses and machine learning, incorporating IBD-related and ageing-related biomarkers to classify people's risks of various complications.
- Examine the bidirectional relationship between frailty (and other aspects, such as comprehensive geriatric assessment and measures of biological age) and treatment safety and effectiveness.

What is the comparative effectiveness and safety of different therapies and treatment strategies (eg, early surgery *vs* conventional medical therapy) for the management of IBD in older people, especially those with multimorbidity?

- Conduct robust observational comparative effectiveness studies, using large databases and registries to compare different medications and treatment strategies on the basis of propensity-score methods and novel approaches, such as target trial emulation.

What are people's values and preferences for treatment goals and targets, and how can shared decision making be promoted in older patients, congruent with people's preferences and in a multidisciplinary team-based context?

- Use a qualitative research methodology to understand values and preferences of older patients with IBD, including people with prevalent IBD and incident elderly-onset IBD, recognising that preferences might be dynamic and shift rapidly in the context of non-IBD comorbid conditions.

In a population-based cohort of 306 people with Crohn's disease in Olmsted County, MN, USA, Thia and colleagues observed that the cumulative risk of disease complications (ie, development of stricturing or penetrating complications) was 39% at 10 years, 51% at 20 years, and 54% at 30 years after disease onset, implying that a subset of people with Crohn's disease have disease complications for the first time 10–30 years after disease onset.

### Natural history of elderly-onset IBD

In contrast to the natural history of IBD in ageing people with prevalent IBD, considerably more data exists on the phenotype and natural history of elderly-onset IBD. Although studies in referral centres variably suggest a milder course of elderly-onset IBD than of adult-onset IBD, subsequent population-based studies have refuted that notion, suggesting that elderly-onset IBD is not necessarily milder.

27.

Ananthakrishnan, AN ∙ Shi, HY ∙ Tang, W ∙ et al.

**Systematic review and meta-analysis: phenotype and clinical outcomes of older-onset inflammatory bowel disease**

*J Crohn's Colitis.* 2016; **10**:1224-1236

In a systematic review of nine population-based cohort studies comparing the natural history of elderly-onset IBD with adult-onset IBD, Rozich and colleagues observed that the overall risk of disease progression, surgery, and hospitalisation was similar in people with elderly-onset IBD and adult-onset IBD.

28.

Rozich, JJ ∙ Dulai, PS ∙ Fumery, M ∙ et al.

**Progression of elderly onset inflammatory bowel diseases: a systematic review and meta-analysis of population-based cohort studies**

*Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol.* 2020; **18**:2437-2447

People with elderly-onset Crohn's disease were more likely to have stricturing behaviour at presentation than those with adult-onset Crohn's disease. Cumulative 1-year risk of major abdominal surgery in people with elderly-onset Crohn's disease was 13%, 5-year risk was 22%, and 10-year risk was 28%, and cumulative 1-year risk of colectomy in individuals with elderly-onset ulcerative colitis was 2%, 5-year risk was 8%, and 10-year risk was 9%. These rates of surgery were similar to the risk observed in those with adult-onset IBD. Factors associated with an increased risk of disease complications in individuals with elderly-onset IBD were also similar to those observed in those with adult-onset IBD. Complex disease behaviour with penetrating, stricturing, and perianal Crohn's disease (compared with non-stricturing, non-penetrating disease) and extensive colitis and left-sided colitis (compared with proctitis) were associated with increased risk of hospitalisation in people with elderly-onset IBD. Ileal and ileocolonic location (compared with colonic Crohn's disease) and penetrating and stricturing Crohn's disease (compared with non-stricturing, non-penetrating disease) were associated with increased risk of surgery in individuals with elderly-onset Crohn's disease; extensive colitis and left-sided colitis (compared with proctitis) and early need for corticosteroids and corticosteroid-dependence (compared with no use of or need for corticosteroids) were associated with increased risk of surgery in those with elderly-onset ulcerative colitis. There was little stratification of findings by sex, but where reported, no differences were observed between men and women.

Few studies have directly compared the risk of disease-related and treatment-related complications in older patients with long-standing prevalent IBD versus those with incident elderly-onset IBD. These individuals conceivably have different risks and tolerability of IBD-directed therapy, driven in part by their lived experience with IBD and its treatment clinically, psychologically, and biologically. In a single-centre study of 356 older patients (ie, aged ≥60 years) with IBD comparing those with prevalent IBD with those with incident elderly-onset IBD, Rozich and colleagues did not observe any significant differences in the risk of disease-related complications but observed significantly lower risk of treatment-related complications in ageing patients with prevalent adult-onset IBD than in people with incident elderly-onset IBD. These findings suggest that older disease-experienced and treatment-experienced individuals with prevalent IBD might tolerate immunosuppressive therapies better than people with incident elderly-onset IBD.

## Pathophysiology of IBD in older adults

The pathogenesis of IBD is complex, involving a dysregulated immunological system responding to intestinal microbes in a genetically susceptible host, resulting in chronic intestinal inflammation ([figure 1](#fig1)). Notable immunological changes occur with ageing that might affect the pathogenesis of IBD, both in older people with elderly-onset IBD and ageing individuals with prevalent IBD.

## Treatment approach and risk stratification in older adults with IBD

Management of IBD in older individuals should weigh and balance the risks of disease-related versus treatment-related complications in the context of a person's treatment goals, functional status, and comorbidities ([figure 2](#fig2)). In younger adults at high risk of disease-related complications, at low risk of treatment-related complications, and with a low burden of comorbidities, treatment targets of symptomatic and endoscopic remission are well defined and accepted, and iterative treatment adjustment is recommended to reach these targets; aspirational targets of histological remission are being considered by researchers and experts in the IBD field.

### Risk and risk factors for IBD-specific disease-related complications

Although no specific disease severity index has been developed for older individuals with IBD, conventional disease severity indices that account for the effect of disease on lifestyle, cumulative bowel damage, and inflammatory burden can be used to identify older people with a high disease burden and who are at a high risk of disease-related complications. ## Comparative effectiveness and safety of therapies and treatment strategies in older patients with IBD

Older people are under-represented in clinical trials of immunosuppressive therapies for IBD.

4.

Kochar, B ∙ Kalasapudi, L ∙ Ufere, NN ∙ et al.

**Systematic review of inclusion and analysis of older adults in randomized controlled trials of medications used to treat inflammatory bowel diseases**

*Inflamm Bowel Dis.* 2021; **27**:1541-1543

Hence, there is a limited evidence base on comparative efficacy and safety of specific therapies and treatment strategies in older individuals with IBD, both ageing patients with prevalent IBD and those with elderly-onset IBD. Post-hoc analyses of clinical trials suggest that age might not significantly affect treatment efficacy for biological agents. In a pooled analysis of four clinical trials of infliximab and golimumab in patients with moderate to severe ulcerative colitis, Cheng and colleagues did not observe any significant differences in the likelihood of inducing and maintaining remission with TNF antagonists in older versus younger participants (ie, aged ≥60 years *vs* <60 years) with ulcerative colitis.

53.

In cohort studies, age at treatment initiation does not seem to affect treatment effectiveness of biologics in people with IBD. In a systematic review of predictors of response to biological therapy in individuals with Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, older age at treatment initiation was variably associated with superior or inferior treatment effectiveness of TNF antagonists in some studies, but most studies showed that age did not have any significant effect on effectiveness.

*J Crohn's Colitis.* 2020; **14**:694-709

In the OCTAVE-Sustain trial of maintenance therapy of tofacitinib in patients with moderate to severe ulcerative colitis, older age at time of treatment initiation was independently associated with increased odds of remission at week 52 (per 10 years odds ratio \[OR\] 1·19, 95% CI 1·02–1·39).

*Inflamm Bowel Dis.* 2022; **28**:1338-1347

Similar data for selective JAK1 inhibitors, upadacitinib and filgotinib, are scarce.

Similar to findings for specific therapeutic agents, age might not significantly affect the effectiveness of treatment strategies for people with IBD. In a post-hoc analysis of the REACT cluster randomised trial of 1981 patients with Crohn's disease, the magnitude of benefit with an algorithmic early combined immunosuppression strategy (in response to presence of symptoms) over conventional management in maintaining corticosteroid-free clinical remission and delaying risk of Crohn's disease-related surgery, hospital admission, or serious disease-related complications was similar in older versus younger participants (ie, aged ≥60 years *vs* <60 years).

77.

**Early combined immunosuppression may be effective and safe in older patients with Crohn's disease: post hoc analysis of REACT**

*Aliment Pharmacol Ther.* 2019; **49**:1188-1194

Older patients might be more susceptible than younger people to treatment-related complications with combination therapy of biological agents and immunomodulators, but no significant differences were observed in the risk of serious infections in older participants treated with early combined immunosuppression versus conventional management. In a prospective cohort of 110 individuals with IBD who were treated with infliximab, Paul and Roblin observed that the risk of immunogenicity was higher in older patients (ie, aged >60 years) than in younger patients (ie, aged ≤60 years).

78.

Paul, S ∙ Roblin, X

**Letter: immunogenicity of anti-TNF in elderly IBD patients**

*Aliment Pharmacol Ther.* 2019; **50**:336

Few observational comparative effectiveness and safety studies exist comparing different biologics and advanced small molecules in older people with IBD. [Table 2](#tbl2) highlights key studies comparing the effectiveness and safety of different immunosuppressive therapies in older individuals with IBD; most studies focus on ageing patients with prevalent IBD, with a small proportion of patients with elderly-onset IBD. In a Danish nationwide propensity-score-matched comparative effectiveness study of older individuals (ie, aged ≥50 years) with IBD, Singh and colleagues observed that those who were treated with vedolizumab were significantly more likely to have treatment failure than were those treated with TNF antagonists. In a subgroup analysis by IBD phenotype, vedolizumab was associated with a significant higher risk of treatment failure (defined as a composite of IBD-related major abdominal surgery, IBD-related hospital admission, and need for corticosteroids) than TNF antagonists among patients with Crohn's disease (hazard ratio \[HR\] 1·77, 95% CI 1·21–2·58), whereas no significant differences were observed among patients with ulcerative colitis (1·04, 0·75–1·43).

No significant differences were observed between men and women. By contrast, in a retrospective cohort study of older patients (ie, aged ≥65 years) with IBD in the US Medicare database, investigators observed no significant differences in the incidence of IBD-related hospital admission, IBD-related surgery, and corticosteroid use between patients treated with vedolizumab versus those treated with TNF antagonists.

## Surgical management of IBD in older patients

In some instances (eg, patients with Crohn's disease with short symptomatic fibrostenotic strictures), surgical interventions can offer a nearly curative approach obviating the need for long-term immunosuppressive therapy. In a randomised controlled trial of 143 patients with non-complicated Crohn's ileitis, Ponsioen and colleagues observed that there was no significant difference in IBD-related quality of life at 12 months in patients who were treated with laparoscopic ileocecal resection after failure of conventional therapy compared with infliximab therapy;

## Conclusions

In summary, the incidence and prevalence of IBD in older people is rapidly increasing. The natural history of IBD can vary between disease-experienced and treatment-experienced ageing patients with adult-onset or paediatric-onset IBD, who constitute most of the older population with IBD, and individuals with incident elderly-onset IBD. Management decisions are challenging, requiring careful risk–benefit synthesis with different treatment strategies, in the context of disease-related and treatment-related complications and overall health and functional status. Future studies focusing on the natural history of IBD, improved dynamic risk stratification, and comparative effectiveness and safety of advanced immunosuppressive therapies are needed to inform decision making regarding optimal treatment strategies both for ageing individuals with long-standing IBD and for individuals with elderly-onset IBD.

## Search strategy and selection criteria

For this Review, we searched PubMed with the search terms: “older”, “elderly”, “ageing”, and “geriatric” combined with “inflammatory bowel diseases”, “IBD”, “ulcerative colitis”, or “Crohn's disease” for papers published from Jan 1, 1970, to Aug 31, 2022. Articles were also identified through searches of the authors' own files. Only papers published in English were reviewed. The final reference list was generated on the basis of originality and relevance to the broad scope of this Review.
