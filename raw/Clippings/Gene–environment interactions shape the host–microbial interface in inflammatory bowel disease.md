## Abstract

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a complex, multifactorial inflammatory disorder of the gut characterized by an imbalance in host–microbiota interactions. Here, we review how early events of IBD are shaped by gene–environment interactions, especially those involving microbial perturbations. Those perturbations eventually lead to chronic inflammation and tissue damage of the gastrointestinal tract. IBD is a multi-hit process in which infectious and noninfectious agents initiate a cascade of immune activation in genetically susceptible individuals. Ultimately the process results in irreversible immunological and physical scarring. These interactions are host specific, with genetic variants influencing the threshold for immune activation and the degree of damage, thus leading to variability in disease progression and therapeutic outcomes. Finally, we discuss challenges, including addressing health disparities and potential strategies for more personalized and effective therapies that target host–microbiota interactions during the preclinical phase of IBD.

## Main

IBD, encompassing Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis, represents a substantial clinical challenge owing to its complex etiology, cycles of relapse and remission, and the variability in patient response to conventional treatments. Crohn’s disease is characterized by patches of inflammatory damage that can penetrate deep into the tissue and appear anywhere along the gastrointestinal tract. Ulcerative colitis is associated with continuous inflammation in the colon and rectum. The immune reactions that cause this inflammatory damage display hallmarks of an imbalanced relationship with microorganisms, especially the gut microbiota. The gut microbiota is a complex dynamic consortium of microorganisms that exist in a symbiotic state with the host through coadaptations shaped by evolution. Members of the microbiota, especially bacteria, benefit the host by breaking down food, mediating colonization resistance to opportunistic pathogens, and providing metabolites that serve as cues for differentiation of the immune system. Genetic associations implicate innate immune dysfunction, especially for Crohn’s disease, that catalyze inappropriate and excessive adaptive immune reactions. Interventions include immunosuppression, such as steroids, and biologics and chemical inhibitors that target cytokine signaling (tumor necrosis factor (TNF), interleukin-23R (IL-23R) and JAK–STAT pathway) or lymphocyte trafficking (integrins and sphingosine-1 phosphate receptors). Failure of these drugs to maintain remission and other complications leads to surgical removal of affected regions and lifelong disability for a substantial proportion of the millions of individuals living with IBD.

In this Review, we discuss how the preclinical and early phases of IBD [^1] are shaped by genotype—environmental (GxE) interactions, especially those impacting immune responses to the microbiota and its products. Gut microbiota studies have traditionally focused on bacterial colonizers. More recent findings emphasize a holistic view that includes microorganisms representing multiple kingdoms that can be beneficial or harmful depending on the context, blurring the line between an infection and a colonization event that occurs during the asymptomatic stage of IBD. We discuss how environmental perturbations, such as diet and infections, on a susceptible genetic background, disrupt symbiotic relationships with the microbiota that are necessary for supporting host immune and barrier functions. We also discuss how microbial triggers can be specific yet not exclusive, and introduce lessons learned from examining viruses, fungi and parasites. By integrating insights from host–pathogen interactions with advances in microbiota science, we aim to provide an overall perspective on the role of the microbiota in IBD. Lastly, we highlight challenges and future directions for research and therapeutic development to target these interactions during the preclinical phase of IBD.

## Microbial homeostasis in the intestine

Gut microorganisms are surrounded by a single layer of epithelial cells organized into protrusions and invaginations designed for nutrient absorption and immune surveillance [^2] (Fig. [1a](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41590-025-02197-5#Fig1)). Oxygen levels decrease from the vascularized subepithelial compartment toward the lumen, as well as from upper to lower gastrointestinal tract. This oxygen gradient, together with the immune system, pH and nutrient availability, selects for the presence of commensal microorganisms that are adapted to the anaerobic environment of the distal small intestine (ileum) and colon. The adapted microbiota provides resistance to invasion by pathogenic bacterial species [^3]. Specialized epithelial cell types include enterocytes that facilitate nutrient absorption, goblet cells that produce mucin to form the protective mucous layer and Paneth cells that secrete antimicrobial peptides to further maintain microbial balance. Additionally, enteroendocrine cells contribute to gut hormone production, while tuft cells are involved in sensing the microbiota and responding to pathogens. Microfold (M) cells, located in organized lymphoid structures called Peyer’s patches, mediate transcytosis of antigens from the lumen to underlying immune cells to stimulate adaptive immune responses.

Beneath the epithelium, stromal cell populations establish a vascularized connective tissue (lamina propria) that includes a dense population of myeloid and lymphoid cells to match the diversity of the microorganisms in the lumen. Dendritic cells and macrophages (myeloid cells) that serve as professional antigen-presenting cells (APCs) are generally skewed toward promoting tolerance. They produce the anti-inflammatory cytokines transforming growth factor-β (TGFβ) and IL-10, as well as vitamin A and tryptophan byproducts, which are important for CD4 <sup>+</sup> regulatory T (T <sub>reg</sub>) cell differentiation. APCs that uptake innocuous antigens migrate to the mesenteric lymph nodes to promote differentiation of IL-10-producing effector T <sub>reg</sub> cells. These T <sub>reg</sub> cells are distinguished from lymphoid-associated central T <sub>reg</sub> cells by their localization upon migration back to the lamina propria. There, they interact with CD206 <sup>+</sup> macrophages that amplify IL-10 responses, facilitating coexistence with the microbiota [^4]. Upon innate immune activation during an infection, effector CD4 <sup>+</sup> T cell populations, such as type 1 helper T (T <sub>H</sub> 1) cells, T <sub>H</sub> 2 cells and the T <sub>H</sub> 17 subset of helper T cells and their innate lymphoid cell (ILC) counterparts, are deployed to combat intracellular pathogens, multicellular parasites and extracellular pathogens, respectively. Conventional and innate-like CD8 <sup>+</sup> T cells, such as γδ T cells, represent intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs) that intercalate and survey the intestinal epithelium. They increase their movement between epithelial cells during infection by pathogens [^5] [^6].

During homeostasis, low-affinity IgA and IgG produced by B cells are broadly reactive to most gut commensal microorganisms [^7] [^8]. Antigen-specific IgA and IgG target pro-inflammatory and translocating microorganisms, respectively, that straddle the line between commensal and pathogen, referred to as pathobionts [^9] [^10]. T cells also recognize both shared and unique epitopes from commensals [^11]. In addition to the transcription factor FOXP3, T <sub>reg</sub> cells that differentiate in response to phylogenetically diverse commensals in the colon depend on the transcription factor RORγt [^12]. The differentiation of T <sub>H</sub> 17 cells is also promoted by RORγt, in response to colonization by adherent microorganisms that induce IL-23 from APCs and IL-22 from ILCs in the local environment [^13] [^14]. For antigen-specific responses against pathobionts, IL-10 ensures preferential differentiation of T <sub>reg</sub> cells over inflammatory T <sub>H</sub> 17 subsets [^4] [^15] [^16]. The intricate relationship between these cellular components and the microbiota becomes unraveled during the pathogenesis of IBD through genetic and environmental factors discussed below. These factors disrupt the microbiota composition and function, cytokine levels, immune cell programming, and epithelial and stromal cell homeostasis (Fig. [1b](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41590-025-02197-5#Fig1)).

## Genetic and environmental determinants of an inflammatory response

The degree of immune reactivity is a continuum across the human population, ranging from immune deficiency to hyperreactivity. Individuals who develop immune-mediated inflammatory diseases (IMIDs) such as IBD can arise at either end of this spectrum (Fig. [2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41590-025-02197-5#Fig2)). For example, weakening of antimicrobial responses (from loss of NOD2 function) or hyperactivity (from IL-10 dysregulation) are both implicated in intestinal inflammation and IBD. Indeed, the same disease symptoms could be the result of very different host–microbial interactions that disrupt intestinal homeostasis through disparate mechanisms. Thus, IBD represents a collection of disease subtypes.

Fig. 2: Genetic and environmental determinants of IBD.

The relative contribution of genetic and environmental factors to immune variation remains an area of active investigation and controversy [^17]. Some studies have indicated that cytokine production has a stronger genetic component [^18], whereas cell quantities show a stronger environmental component [^19] [^20] [^21], including cohabitation [^22]. With a large sample size coupled with detailed immunophenotyping, the Milieu Intérieur Consortium proposed that innate immune cell parameters were predominantly controlled by genetic variation, and adaptive immune cell characteristics were driven by nongenetic factors [^23]. For instance, smoking (which strongly affects IBD) can have long-term persistent effects associated with epigenetic memory on T cell responses [^24]. Genetic variants that affect metabolic function can affect the function of innate immune cells. A coding variant that disrupts the purine nucleoside enzymatic activity of FAMIN compromises mitochondrial redox in macrophages, resulting in increased risk of Crohn’s disease [^25]. The contribution of genetic and environmental factors in shaping immune variation will be interlinked in most situations, as the individual genotype will determine the impact of environmental changes on the immune system [^26]. Understanding these interactions is crucial for explaining why some individuals develop IBD, while most do not despite exposure to similar environmental conditions.

## Genetic susceptibility to IBD interacts with gut microorganisms

The high familial prevalence of IBD has long indicated a genetic component for the disease. The rare but devastating very early-onset IBD in infants and children is associated with mutations that directly disrupt immune homeostasis, such as those that impair phagocytes (for example, *CYBB* and *CYBA*) and T <sub>reg</sub> cells (for example, *IL10RA* and *CTLA4*) [^27]. In contrast to these monogenic forms of the disease, the adult-onset form of IBD is frequently diagnosed in a person’s twenties but sometimes much later in life. This is consistent with a disease model in which prolonged or cumulative environmental instigators evoke responses shaped by a more complex genetic background. A careful comparison of syndromic features and molecular mechanisms between monogenic and polygenic IBD indicates that there is a functional basis for shared inflammatory pathways [^28]. Notably, patients with high-penetrance IBD (>5%) mutations were substantially younger than those with moderate-penetrance and polygenic IBD. There is, however, considerable heterogeneity and variable penetrance with a broad age of onset. Even among the high-penetrance genes, almost 40% were associated with a median age of onset above 6 years of age [^28]. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have linked over 300 loci with IBD, many of which are associated with common variants in genes or regulatory elements that affect host–microorganism interactions. However, they only confer a modest increase in disease risk. A recent analysis of 30,000 patients identified additional low-frequency and rare variants of Crohn’s disease, which were not previously observable from earlier GWAS with more limited coverage [^29]. Several of these rare variants affect pathways implicated by the earlier studies, such as the autophagy enzyme *ATG4C* and the STAT3 interacting partner *PDLIM5* (ref. [^29]). These recent findings increase the confidence that common variants are bona fide susceptibility factors, albeit with modest effects on overall disease development. An analogy may be the contribution of sex chromosomes and HLA haplotype to autoimmunity. These genomic risk factors do not directly cause disease but are accepted variables that yield insight into mechanisms of disease.

How risk variants drive disease is poorly understood because most risk variants are found in noncoding genomic regions. Noncoding risk variants are thought to affect gene regulation, but linking these loci to causal genes functioning in specific cell types in disease conditions is exceptionally difficult. Conservation of gene regulatory loci between mice and humans is also weaker than in coding sequences, posing a challenge for approaches that rely on animal models. Recently, a monocyte/macrophage-specific enhancer that regulates the ETS2 transcription factor involved in inflammatory cytokine production was identified in an intergenic region linked to IBD through GWAS [^30]. This study applied functional genomics to identify macrophage-centered disease mechanisms that could potentially be targeted therapeutically via MEK1/MEK2 inhibition.

Identification of coding variants associated with IBD has enabled investigation of GxE interactions in mice (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41590-025-02197-5#Tab1)). NOD2 is a cytosolic signaling molecule triggered by the peptidoglycan byproduct muramyl dipeptide from the bacterial cell wall, although other activation signals have been identified as well [^31] [^32]. The three major loss-of-function variants in *NOD2* affect homeostatic relationships with microbiota members such as *Phocaeicola vulgatus* (formerly *Bacteroides vulgatus*) [^33] [^34] [^35] [^36] [^37]. In particular, myeloid cell interactions with neighboring immune and nonimmune cell types are dependent on NOD2 (refs. [^38] [^39]). Such gene–microbiota interactions could be complicated by additional variables. For example, colonization by the helminth (parasitic worm) *Trichuris* can increase the presence of Clostridia bacteria in humans and mice through a type 2 immune response. This reverses defects in goblet cells caused by *P. vulgatus* in the *Nod2* -deficient mice [^40]. In the human population, the risk of developing Crohn’s disease conferred by these *NOD2* variants varies greatly depending on the specific allele and number of copies (10–40-fold for homozygosity or compound heterozygosity) and displays incomplete disease penetrance [^41]. Helminth eradication and other practices associated with modernization coincide with increases in the incidence of IBD. Then, it is tempting to speculate that changes to the environment that impact the microbiota have created conditions that unmask the adverse consequences of certain gene variants.

Microbiota-mediated NOD2 activation in myeloid cells induces production of IL-1β, which acts on lymphoid cells that secrete IL-2 and IL-22 to promote T <sub>reg</sub> cells and epithelial barrier function, respectively [^42] [^43]. Consistent with this evidence in mice, the microbiota of individuals with IBD displays reduced NOD2 stimulatory activity owing to loss of bacteria that secrete peptidoglycan hydrolases, such as SagA from *Enterococcus* species, which generates muramyl dipeptide [^42] [^44]. Also, other gene variants impair NOD2 function, such as the T300A substitution in *ATG16L1*. NOD2 can act upstream of ATG16L1 (refs. [^45] [^46]), which mediates autophagy and related membrane trafficking pathways that target substrates to the lysosome for destruction, such as internalized bacteria and damaged organelles [^47]. In a NOD2- and ATG16L1-dependent manner, outer membrane vesicles from the commensal bacterium *Bacteroides fragilis* induce an autophagy-like pathway that biases dendritic cells toward a tolerogenic state, favoring differentiation of T <sub>reg</sub> cells that dampen intestinal inflammation [^48]. However, these two IBD genes can have opposing roles. ATG16L1 suppresses immune reactions to gut microorganisms by removing pro-inflammatory mitochondria that increase the production of interferon and IL-1β [^49] [^50] [^51]. IBD variants that increase expression of *MMTR3*, an inhibitor of autophagy, are associated with increased IL-1β production by macrophages upon NOD2 stimulation [^52].

ATG16L1 has epithelial-intrinsic functions consistent with GxE interactions. Primary tissue specimens and epithelial organoids derived from individuals with Crohn’s disease homozygous for *ATG16L1* <sup><i>T300A</i></sup> display Paneth cells with aberrant morphology and reduced viability [^53] [^54] [^55]. Lethal intestinal disease ensues when *Atg16l1* and autophagy is deleted in Paneth cells of mice and combined with infections, chemical injury or another genetic hit such as the IBD and endoplasmic reticulum stress gene *Xbp1* (refs. [^56] [^57] [^58] [^59] [^60]). Paneth cells are long-lived epithelial cells with a high secretory burden. They are vulnerable to genetic disruptions in autophagy and organelle homeostasis and have a propensity to lose their antimicrobial function and undergo inflammatory forms of cell death, such as necroptosis. The latter is triggered by environmental factors such as smoking or murine norovirus (MNV) infection [^54] [^56] [^61]. MNV can promote beneficial immune maturation and protection against bacterial infections [^62] [^63]. When acting as an inflammatory trigger, MNV inhibits γδ T cells in the IEL compartment that protect *Atg16l1* mutant Paneth cells through the production of the cell viability factor apoptosis inhibitor 5 (API5) [^64]. In another murine model of Crohn’s disease in which TNF is overproduced, small intestinal disease is preceded by depletion of anti-inflammatory γδ T cells bearing CD39, an immunoregulatory ectoenzyme that hydrolyzes extracellular ATP [^65]. The abundance and activity of γδ T cells and other IELs are skewed toward pro-inflammatory states in individuals with IBD [^64] [^66] [^67], indicating that homeostatic lymphocyte–epithelial interactions are disrupted similarly in human IBD.

To summarize, reproducible consequences of mutations in *NOD2* and *ATG16L1* include inappropriate cytokine production by myeloid cells and decreased resilience of the epithelium. Other IBD genes such as *CARD9*, *LRRK2*, *IRGM* and *IL18RAP* (or similar counterparts in mice) functionally interact with NOD2 or ATG16L1 to regulate cytokine and antimicrobial responses to gut microorganisms in animal models [^68] [^69] [^70] [^71] [^72] [^73] [^74] [^75] [^76] [^77] (Table [1](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41590-025-02197-5#Tab1)). These cytokine defects underly or reinforce adaptive immune reactions associated with intestinal inflammation. Macrophages from *IL10R* -deficient individuals produce more IL-1β in response to bacterial lipopolysaccharide, and their gut is enriched in T <sub>H</sub> 17 cells owing to increased sensitivity of T cells to IL-1β [^78] [^79], supporting decades of mechanistic research in mice. Also, as exemplified by cell biological studies of INAVA (innate immune activator, also known as C1ORF106), deeper characterizations of other disease variants are revealing overlapping yet distinct functions of IBD genes in tuning cytokine responses by myeloid cells and their effects on the epithelial barrier [^80] [^81]. When considered in the context of the large-scale human immune variation studies mentioned earlier, findings in the IBD field support a role for gene variants in regulating the impact of gut microorganisms through setting thresholds of immune activation and damage.

## Microbial dysbiosis in IBD

IBD risk factors—diet, infections, antibiotics, age, genetics, smoking and others—all impact the microbiota. The density of the microbiota is highest in the ileum and colorectal regions of the lower gastrointestinal tract, where IBD lesions are most common. Surgically diverting the fecal stream relieves Crohn’s disease [^82]. Also, the gut microbiota from individuals with IBD causes an imbalance between T <sub>H</sub> 17 cells and T <sub>reg</sub> cells when transferred into germ-free mice [^83]. Despite considerable interest in fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) as therapy [^84], the findings are still inconsistent, and the American Gastroenterological Association recommends against its use, except in clinical trials [^85]. Understanding the mechanism of microbiota dysbiosis will provide insight into the challenges surrounding these approaches.

Individuals with IBD often exhibit decreased gut microbiota diversity with a reduction in Clostridia such as *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and *Roseburia hominis* due to both host genetics and nongenetic factors [^86]. These bacteria ferment dietary fiber to produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) such as butyrate, providing energy to epithelial cells that consume oxygen and sustain the anaerobic environment [^87]. SCFAs from the microbiota also inhibit histone deacetylases, leading to broad consequences. These include reprogramming dendritic cell gene expression in ways that promote differentiation of extrathymic T <sub>reg</sub> cells dependent on the intronic enhancer CNS1 [^88] [^89] [^90]. Depletion of fermenting bacteria increases availability of oxygen and nitrates used for anaerobic respiration by pathobionts [^91], such as *Escherichia coli*, *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, *Veillonella parvula* and *Fusobacterium nucleatum*, that originate from the oral cavity [^92] [^93]. Strains enriched in the IBD microbiota are genotypically distinct from conspecific bacteria associated with health [^94] and use virulence strategies that trigger innate immune and inflammatory T <sub>H</sub> 1 and T <sub>H</sub> 17 responses. Examples include fimbriae and type 3 secretion system effectors that mediate adhesion and invasion of the epithelium, or toxins and genotoxic metabolites [^92] [^95] [^96]. Inflammation induced by these virulence factors reinforces colonization by these bacteria, especially Enterobacteriaceae members *E. coli* and *K. pneumoniae*, by increasing availability of the electron acceptors nitrate and oxygen [^96] [^97]. However, in mouse models, adhesion and local damage mediated by microorganisms often trigger homeostatic T cell responses that require additional hits (for example, IL-10 deficiency) to induce disease. BATF3-dependent dendritic cells increase CD8 <sup>+</sup> T cells in the presence of *Klebsiella* from human salivary samples [^92]. Upon penetration of the intestinal epithelium by segmented filamentous bacteria, local production of serum amyloid A acts on T <sub>H</sub> 17 cells recognizing an endocytosed bacterial antigen to ensure a gut-specific IL-17 response [^14] [^98]. Homeostatic IL-17, which in some cases is independent of IL-23 associated with inflammatory T <sub>H</sub> 17, maintains balanced relations with the microbiota and promotes differentiation of secretory epithelial cells in mouse and human organoids [^99] [^100] [^101] [^102] [^103]. Biologics targeting IL-17 induce or exacerbate IBD [^104], suggesting that other T <sub>H</sub> 17 effector mechanisms underlie IBD. In another counterintuitive example, *E. coli* prevents CX3CR1 <sup>+</sup> APCs from migrating to the lymph nodes to promote T <sub>H</sub> 1 responses [^105] [^106]. These findings suggest that pro-inflammatory microorganisms evoke a restrained response in genetically resilient individuals.

Certain bacteria enriched in Crohn’s disease display niche adaptations to promote pathological states, such as *Clostridium innocuum* in the mesenteric adipose tissue that gives rise to ‘creeping fat’ in which visceral fat wraps around the intestine [^107]. Macrophage-derived kynurenine could be particularly important in this process [^108]. In another example, the inflammatory bacterium *Ruminococcus gnavus* is adapted to feed on mucus and outcompete other mucolytic bacteria in settings of reduced Paneth cell-derived lysozyme in mice [^109]. How other kingdoms of life relate to IBD is only beginning to become clear. Fungal *Candida*, *Malassezia* and *Debaryomyces* species expand in a pro-inflammatory gut and trigger damage through their unique growth and virulence strategies [^110] [^111] [^112] [^113] [^114]. *C*. *albicans* strains that dominate the colon of individuals with ulcerative colitis are distinguished by increased production of the toxin candidalysin that elicits IL-1β from macrophages. Introducing these cytotoxic strains into mice increases colitis associated with T <sub>H</sub> 17 and neutrophil infiltration [^112]. Less is known about archaea and protists, but animal models are beginning to reveal a range of gut T cell responses to metabolites produced by *Tritrichomonas* protists [^115] [^116] [^117]. MNV and other enteric viruses induce responses that overlap with bacterial members of the microbiota, including IL-22 from lymphoid cells [^62] [^118]. Deleting the innate immune signaling molecules *CARD9* and *TLR3/TLR7* in mice, which are also IBD susceptibility genes, impairs homeostatic functions of gut bacteria, fungi and viruses suggesting multiple kingdoms of microorganisms participate in GxE interactions [^70] [^71] [^119].

Immunological memory, an inherent property of lymphocytes, in reaction to commensals is difficult to undo once established. Individuals with ulcerative colitis display numerous alterations to lymphocytes identified by single-cell sequencing. These include expansion of an IgG1 <sup>+</sup> plasma cell cluster, remodeling of the γδ T cell compartment and clonal expansion of CD8 <sup>+</sup> tissue-resident memory T cell subsets that establish an inflammatory state through the transcription factor EOMES [^120] [^121]. An initial breach in the barrier followed by a gastrointestinal infection can break tolerance and promote differentiation of commensal-reactive effector T cells and IgG antibodies correlated with Crohn’s disease, such as those reactive against flagellin derived from the Lachnospiraceae family of butyrate producers [^9] [^122] [^123]. Similarly, CD4 <sup>+</sup> T cells reactive to a ubiquitous epitope derived from SusC found in the Bacteroidales order are characterized by IL-10 production in healthy individuals. However, these SusC-reactive CD4 <sup>+</sup> T cells have a T <sub>H</sub> 17 signature in individuals with active Crohn’s disease [^124]. Also, repetitive exposure to fungi that colonize the gut or consumed with food selects for cytotoxic T <sub>H</sub> 1 cells in individuals with Crohn’s disease responsive to fungal antigens [^125]. Thus, commensal-reactive T cells acquire a pro-inflammatory fate in Crohn’s disease.

Gastrointestinal infections and persistent cytokine production in mice, especially TNF, cause long-term damage to the lymphatics. Damage caused by interference with the egress of APCs and molecules from the gut disrupt homeostatic lymphocyte differentiation, catalyzing tertiary lymphoid structures, ectopic lymphoid organs in nonlymphoid tissue [^126] [^127]. Tertiary lymphoid structures form when TNF family cytokines induce transdifferentiation of fibroblasts into those that produce CXCL13 and CCL19. These fibroblasts support the formation of germinal center cognate B cell and T cell reactions resembling lymph nodes, contributing to pathological antigen-specific lymphocyte responses over time. These long-lasting consequences on immune cell differentiation and trafficking likely impact the success of interventions such as FMTs.

## Diet and metabolites

Diet shapes the gut microbiota composition, production of metabolites and subsequently immune responses [^128] [^129]. Dietary interventions (for example, exclusive enteral nutrition) are an important component of IBD management, with changing guidelines that reflect our increasing understanding of a healthy microbiota [^130]. Although consumption of fibrous materials can be of concern for intestinal strictures (intestinal wall thickening) or obstructions [^130], greater microbial diversity and SCFAs could be beneficial by establishing an anti-inflammatory and resilient environment through mechanisms discussed earlier. By contrast, diets high in processed foods and saturated fats exacerbate intestinal inflammation in animal models. The lack of dietary fiber increases bacteria that degrade the mucous barrier, causing susceptibility to pathogens and colitis [^131] [^132]. Processed foods contain emulsifiers and additives that erode the protective mucous layer and alter the microbiota, increasing bacterial translocation and inflammation [^132] [^133]. Mice fed a high-glucose diet have fewer T <sub>reg</sub> cells directed toward the commensal *Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron* due to glucose-mediated repression of a bacterial antigen, linking sugar, gut microorganisms and inflammation [^134]. The sugar fucose in the diet, however, is anti-inflammatory because it decorates the surface of commensal bacteria such as *B. fragilis* and promotes germinal center B cell reactions in Peyer’s patches and IgA recognition [^135]. A loss-of-function mutation in the fucosyltransferase gene *FUT2*, which mediates processing of fucose, is associated with IBD susceptibility and microbiota dysbiosis. In mice, *FUT2* mutations are also associated with Paneth cell defects and microbially induced inflammation [^135] [^136] [^137].

A high-fat diet, particularly one rich in saturated fats, disrupts metabolism of bile acids synthesized in the liver and secreted into the intestines to aid in fat digestion, which can promote pathobiont expansion and T <sub>H</sub> 1-associated colitis in IL-10-deficient mice [^138]. The microbiota converts primary bile acids from the liver into secondary bile acids, which have numerous immune consequences. Feeding mice a Western diet increases levels of the secondary bile acid deoxycholic acid, which activates FXR and interferon signaling, leading to Paneth cell dysfunction that is also observed in humans with obesity [^139]. Additionally, the microbiota further converts the secondary bile acid lithocholic acid (LCA) to 3-OxoLCA and isoalloLCA. 3-OxoLCA inhibits T <sub>H</sub> 17 differentiation by binding RORγt, whereas isoalloLCA promotes *Foxp3* expression to increase T <sub>reg</sub> cells [^140] [^141]. A nutrient-rich diet promotes T <sub>reg</sub> cell differentiation and suppresses experimental colitis in a manner dependent on commensal bacteria that convert bile acids [^142]. Thus, individual bacterial taxa and metabolites shape the impact of diet on intestinal inflammation.

Diet-induced inflammation in these animal models requires additional hits, such as IL-10 deficiency or an infection. In humans, antibiotics can exacerbate microbiota dysbiosis (increased Enterobacteriaceae and reduced Clostridia) induced by high-fat diet consumption setting the stage for IBD [^143]. In addition to macronutrients, gut microorganisms respond to and metabolize xenobiotics in a manner specific to an individual’s microbiota composition [^144]. This activity is predicted to have profound effects on the toxicity of our diet and efficacy of drugs delivered through the oral route. Also, ingested chemicals from the environment may have direct effects on immune cells. The herbicide propyzamide inhibits aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AHR) signaling leading to increases in NF-κB and C/EBPβ inflammatory transcriptional activity in dendritic cells and T cells [^145]. AHRs activated by exogenous and endogenous ligands such as indole and tryptophan metabolites promote IL-22 production by lymphoid cells. Therefore, mice in which AHR ligand availability is disrupted through microbiota dysbiosis (for example, CARD9 deficiency) display increased susceptibility to colitis [^71]. With better understanding of bacterial metabolism and dietary chemicals, we come increasingly closer to designing interventions that can cooperate with a healthy diet to promote intestinal homeostasis.

## A multi-hit model for IBD

A genetically predisposed individual may experience a series of synergistic environmental insults—microbiota dysbiosis, infections and dietary insults—that collectively overwhelm regulatory mechanisms that maintain intestinal homeostasis, triggering chronic inflammation (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41590-025-02197-5#Fig3)). These events could occur during the asymptomatic stage before IBD diagnosis. This model would account for the heterogeneity among individuals with IBD, where different combinations of GxE interactions lead to diverse disease phenotypes. Although some may develop IBD following a specific infection, others may be triggered by diet-induced dysbiosis. The diversity of genetic backgrounds ensures that each individual’s immune response to these environmental hits is unique. The protective IL-1β response by myeloid cells, induced by commensals found in some individuals, fails to occur in the presence of a *NOD2* variant and is pathologically exaggerated in an IL-10R-deficient or autophagy-deficient background, driving inflammatory disease [^42] [^153]. Hence, the inflammatory response in IBD is the product of distinct genetic predispositions that interact with a changing environment.

Such a model is supported by evidence of preclinical phases of IBD, consisting of an initiation event and a disease expansion phase occurring at least 2 years before diagnosis [^1]. Serum biomarkers can identify individuals who will develop IBD up to 5 years before diagnosis [^154]. But our understanding of GxE interactions during these phases is restricted mainly to animal models. Preclinical phases of other IMIDs such as rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus and type 1 diabetes are better characterized with the identification of biomarkers, such as spikes in autoantibodies before disease diagnosis.

There is ample evidence for gastrointestinal infectious triggers of IBD [^155]. Yet even very large studies identify diverse microbial associations. A study utilizing the Swedish National Patient Register compared the incidence of prior enteric infections in 44,214 people with IBD and 436,507 individuals in the general population and found infection with several agents increased the odds of developing IBD [^156]. Norovirus infections were among the highest predictors of developing Crohn’s disease but previous episodes of *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, *Yersinia enterocolitica*, *Clostridioides difficile* and *Amoeba* infections also displayed associations. These associations are not easy to decipher given that a pro-inflammatory environment favors infection by many of these same pathogens, as highlighted by the observation that individuals with IBD are at risk for recurrent *C. difficile* infections. Microbiota dysbiosis is also an early event, with one study that prospectively followed at-risk individuals (first-degree relatives) demonstrating that increases in proteases from *P. vulgatus* precedes disease [^157]. The serum biomarkers that predict IBD include antibodies recognizing fungal and bacterial antigens [^154], supporting a role for gut microorganisms in disease initiation or acceleration. Also, a recent study found that an Epstein–Barr virus infection increases the likelihood of developing Crohn’s disease 5–7 years later [^158].

Such heterogeneity could be the result of different host–microbial interactions that cause the same disease symptoms. An analogy could be made to respiratory tract infections, whereby different viral (for example, respiratory syncytial virus, SARS-CoV-2 and influenza) and bacterial (for example, *Streptococcus pneumoniae* and *Haemophilus influenzae*) infections cause similar disease activity. Another complexity is that genetic hits may continue to have a role in shaping interactions with the environment throughout the course of disease, although variants we have discussed are involved in predisposition and have less influence on prognosis [^159]. In a pediatric Crohn’s disease cohort, early anti-TNF intervention prevented complications associated with penetrating inflammation but not stricturing, which instead had a unique association with *Ruminococcus* in the microbiota [^160]. One interpretation is that the variables influencing immune cell infiltration during disease onset are separate from those involved in dissolution of tissue integrity.

## Therapeutic challenges and outlook

We hypothesize that microbial interventions, such as prebiotics and probiotics, will be more effective in the preclinical phases of IBD (Fig. [3](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41590-025-02197-5#Fig3)). Fecal microbiota products such as Clostridia spores, which are effective for treating *C. difficile* infections, have failed to improve ulcerative colitis [^84]. Thus, a more recent trial ([NCT05370885](https://www.clinicaltrialsregister.eu/ctr-search/search?query=NCT05370885)) evaluating a synthetic spore cocktail is enrolling a biologic-naive ulcerative colitis population with milder disease to circumvent issues caused by immunological scarring from symptomatic disease. Once this inflammatory damage reaches a certain point, host-directed therapies targeting dysregulated immunological hubs may be needed to restore homeostatic interactions with the microbiota.

The concept of targeting immunological hubs for IMIDs exemplifies this approach [^161]. Instead of specific bacterial taxa, perturbations of major immunological hubs that are common to multiple bacterial and nonbacterial microorganisms likely lead to the histopathological outcomes that define IBD subtypes. Because the number of host immune pathways is limited, disease manifestations will share commonalities, even if the microbial triggers are distinct. TNF inhibition is a prime example of successful targeting of multiple inflammatory processes in IMIDs. However, substantial subsets of individuals do not display a durable response to treatment. In treatment responders, vedolizumab administration to patients with ulcerative colitis inhibits the interaction between mucosal vascular addressin cell adhesion molecule 1 (MAdCAM-1) on intestinal endothelial cells and α4β7 integrin on lymphocytes to reduce the size of lymphoid aggregates and IgG <sup>+</sup> plasma cells [^162]. JAK inhibitors, which block STAT-dependent signaling of multiple cytokines, provide another example of hub-targeting therapy, although the specific target pathways and cells are not clear. The IBD gene *PTPN2* dephosphorylates STAT proteins to protect against Paneth cell abnormalities and microbiota dysbiosis in mice [^163], raising the possibility that JAK inhibitors target dysfunctional responses to microorganisms. Further refinement of our basic immunological understanding of drug action could lead to superior second-generation therapies, such as dual-acting monoclonals.

Genetic information may improve personalization of such strategies. Targeting of TNF-like cytokine 1A (TL1A) and its receptor death-domain receptor 3 (DR3), members of the TNF and TNFR superfamily, is a leading example for applying precision medicine to IBD. Variants of TL1A (encoded by *TNFSF15*) are associated with IBD risk. TL1A is produced by microorganism-sensing APCs, and when inappropriately upregulated, act on multiple target cells bearing DR3 to amplify innate immune signals and T <sub>H</sub> 1 and T <sub>H</sub> 17 polarization [^164]. For instance, an adherent invasive *E. coli* strain from a patient with Crohn’s disease induces TL1A from CX3CR1 <sup>+</sup> APCs that evokes IL-22 from ILC3s to protect against acute colitis in mice. However, it can also upregulate co-stimulatory molecule OX40L and major histocompatibility complex class II molecules on ILC3s to promote T <sub>H</sub> 1-driven progressing colitis in mice and Crohn’s disease in humans [^165]. Also, overexpressing *Tnfsf15* in mice to mimic human genetic susceptibility leads to fibrotic intestinal inflammation dependent on the microbiota [^166]. Clinical trials examining the efficacy of anti-TL1A biologics that stratify patients based on a genetic-based diagnostic tool and biomarkers are underway and showing promising preliminary results [^164]. Genetic variants in *IL23R* may influence response to biologics targeting this pathway, such as guselkumab, although this has not been demonstrated clinically. In vitro assays that recreate the dysregulated immune hub could be used to predict individual clinical responsiveness before treatment initiation. Efficacy of the JAK inhibitor tofacitinib was predicted by organoids generated from biopsy samples procured prospectively from patients with ulcerative colitis in a clinical trial [^167]. In this study, tofacitinib blocked epithelial cell death induced by T <sub>H</sub> 1 and antiviral cytokines in organoids generated from patients who clinically responded to the drug but not the nonresponders.

A future approach would be to target the preclinical phase of IBD, similar to the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved teplizumab that targets CD3 (T cell antigen receptor signaling) to delay the onset of type 1 diabetes. Unfortunately, the preclinical phase of IBD is poorly characterized because direct assessment of mucosal tissue or barrier function requires invasive techniques that are difficult to apply to large populations for screening purposes. A better understanding of disease initiation through mechanistic studies and artificial intelligence may identify biomarkers specific to a genetic landscape. Using such information, one can imagine applying advances in capsule endoscopy, biosensors and imaging modalities to a narrower population of individuals at risk. Such screening and treatment pipelines must be made available across income levels to avoid exacerbating health disparities.

Preclinical interventions could include treatment with engineered or adapted bacterial strains in probiotic formulations. For example, to facilitate probiotic use, the butyrate-producing anaerobe *F. prausnitzii* was adapted to tolerate oxygen exposure by co-isolation with *Desulfovibrio piger* [^168]. Manipulating T cell populations in the gut to encourage T <sub>reg</sub> cell differentiation could be achieved by the delivery of bacterial strains that preferentially activate specific T cell antigen receptors [^11]. Infection by helminths or their secretory products could promote expansion of immunoregulatory Clostridia and restore the mucous barrier [^40] [^169] [^170]. Individuals with increased abundance of *K. pneumoniae* could benefit from phage therapy and defined probiotic cocktails that reduce colonization by the pathobiont and break the cycle of self-perpetuating inflammation [^171] [^172]. Because dietary interventions work together with the microbiota, they may also be more successful during the preclinical phase.

Mechanisms of GxE interactions can inform personalizing treatments. However, findings thus far have focused on how GxE interactions disrupt homeostatic communication between APCs, lymphocytes and epithelial cells. The enteric nervous system (ENS) acts as a conduit between gut microorganisms and lymphoid cells that dynamically responds to food consumption [^173]. The recently described role for ENS-derived IL-18 in promoting antimicrobial responses by goblet cells may hint at a GxE interaction involving the *IL18RAP* locus [^77]. Also, single-cell sequencing and spatial transcriptomics are showing that specialized subsets of fibroblasts are positioned to coordinate immune functions, with pathogenic subtypes acting with neutrophils through IL-1 signaling [^174]. Indeed, lutikizumab, a dual-variable-domain immunoglobulin targeting IL-1α and IL-1β, is in phase 2 trials for ulcerative colitis ([NCT06257875](https://patientsatheart.com/trials/nct06257875)). It will be important to know how GxE interactions disrupt immune functions of the ENS and fibroblasts. Treatment strategies inspired by underlying GxE interactions may involve pairing microbial or metabolite-targeting therapies with tests indicating the presence of disease-inducing communities or markers associated with disruption of the intestinal immune system. Once the disease progresses to a point in which physical structures lose integrity, as exemplified by fistulas and creeping fat, targeting the luminal microbiota may be fruitless without tissue engineering and surgery to repair damaged architecture. Combination or sequential therapies may be necessary to reestablish healthy physical, immunological and microbial states of the gut.
