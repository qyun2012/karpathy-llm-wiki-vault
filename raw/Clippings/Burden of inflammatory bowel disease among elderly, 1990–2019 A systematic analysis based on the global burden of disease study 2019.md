## 1\. Introduction

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a chronic nonspecific [intestinal disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/enteropathy) that includes [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease) (CD) and [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ulcerative-colitis) (UC). There are two peaks of age onset of IBD, the first in the 2th and 3th decades of life and the second in the 6th and 7th decades \[,\]. Patients with IBD aged ≥60 years are defined as elderly IBD \[\]. Elderly IBD are characterized by complex disease manifestations, comorbidity, and [polypharmacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/polypharmacy), and are at high risk for disease and treatment-related complications, posing significant challenges in diagnosis, treatment, and management \[,\]. However, due to the current lack of guidance on evidence-based medicine, there are significant disparities in the treatment and management of [geriatric](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/geriatrics) IBD across regions \[\]. With population growth and population aging, the number of elderly patients with IBD has increased dramatically. It is projected that by 2030, more than one-third of IBD patients will be over 60 years old, which undoubtedly represents a huge burden on healthcare systems in all regions and countries around the world \[\]. It is important to have a clear understanding of changes in the burden of elderly IBD across regions and countries for healthcare policy planning in each country and will help to reduce the burden on patients and society. However, there is a lack of comprehensive systematic analysis of elderly IBD concerning epidemiological characteristics at the global, regional, and territorial levels over the past 30 years. The Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Study 2019 provides the most recent epidemiologic estimates of 369 diseases and [injuries](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/injury) in 204 countries and territories from 1990 to 2019 \[,, \]. As such, it provides a golden opportunity to systematically analyze the burden of disease in older adults with IBD across regions and countries globally.

In this study, we comprehensively analyzed the global disease burden of IBD patients aged 60 years and older over the past 30 years at the region, country, age group, and gender levels based on data from GBD 2019. This study aims to raise awareness of the latest epidemiological burden of elderly IBD and help governments and relevant healthcare decision-makers to optimize their strategies.

## 2\. Methods

### 2.1. Data source

We obtained cross-sectional epidemiologic data on IBD from GBD 2019 using the GlobalHealth Data Exchange query tool (GHDx, [http://ghdx.healthdata.org/gbd-results-tool](http://ghdx.healthdata.org/gbd-results-tool)). In GBD 2019, IBD is defined according to the [International Classification of Diseases Tenth Revision](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/icd-10) (ICD-10), with specific ICD-10 codes of K50-K51.319, K51.5-K52, and K52.8-K52.9. The variables obtained from the database included global, regional, countries and territories level prevalence, incidence, disability-adjusted life-years (DALYs), and mortality annual values and rates with 95 % uncertainty (UIs). DALY is a measure of the overall burden of disease, defined as the sum of years of life lost due to [premature mortality](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/premature-mortality) (YLLs) and years lived with disability (YLDs). Patients with IBD aged 60–89 years were included in our analysis and divided into six age groups (60–64, 65–69, 70–74, 75–79, 80–84, 85–89). Geographically, the world was divided into 6 regions according to the World Health Organization (WHO) including Africa, Eastern Mediterranean, Europe, the Americas, South-East Asia, and Western Pacific. In addition, the Socio-demographic Index (SDI) is used to classify countries and territories into five categories: high, high-middle, middle, low-middle, and low SDI. The SDI is a composite indicator of the development status of a country or territory, which is assessed by a combination of per capita income, average educational attainment, fertility rate, etc. The higher the value, the better the development status of the country and territories.

### 2.2. Statistical analysis

To mitigate variations in the [age structure](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/age-distribution) across regions, each indicator utilizes the age-standardized rates (ASRs) to assess the burden of IBD across different time periods, genders, and locations. World population age standard was extracted from the GBD database to calculate ASRs for each age group \[\]. The formula is $\frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{N} \alpha_{i} W_{i}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{N} W_{i}}$, where $\alpha_{i}$ signifies the age-specific rate in the $i$ th age group, and $W_{i}$ represents the population count (or weight) in the corresponding age group within the GBD standard population. $N$ denotes the total number of age groups. The 95 % UIs were determined as the 25th and 975th values among the ordered 1000 draws \[\]. The Estimated Annual Percentage Change (EAPC) is employed to evaluate the average trend of an indicator over a defined period. The natural logarithm of ASR is assumed to fit the [linear regression](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/linear-regression-analysis) model y = α + βx + ε, where y equals ln (ASR), x denotes the calendar year, and ε represents the error term. The EAPC was calculated as 100 × (exp (β) −1). The 95 % confidence intervals (CIs) of EAPC were estimated using the linear regression model \[\]. An increasing trend in the corresponding age-standardized indicator was inferred if the 95 % CI of the EAPC was >0; a decreasing trend if the 95 % CI was <0; and a stabilizing trend if the 95 % CI included zero. All statistical analyses and visualizations were conducted utilizing R software (version 4.1.3).

## 3\. Results

### 3.1. Global burden and its trend of IBD in the elderly

The global crude prevalence of IBD in the elderly rose from 864,819 (95 % UI, 735,773 to 101,945) in 1990 to 1,327,208 (95 % UI, 1,156,458 to 1,533,748) in 2019, reflecting a 53.4 % increase. In contrast, the age-standardized prevalence rate (ASPR) of elderly IBD in 2019 stood at 22.04 cases per 100,000 (95 % UI, 19.15 to 25.15), marking a 37.7 % decrease from 1990. Globally, the ASPR of elderly IBD experienced an average annual decline of −0.84 % (95 % CI -1.06 to −0.62) from 1990 to 2019.

In 2019, the global burden of elderly IBD amounted to 594,081 DALYs (95 % UI 511,534 to 676,415), with an age-standardized rate of 7.34 DALYs per 100,000 individuals. Globally, the age-standardized DALYs rate (ASDR) due to elderly IBD declined annually by an average of −0.81 % (95 % CI -0.85 to −0.76) from 1990 to 2019.

In 2019, the global incidence of new IBD cases among individuals aged 60–89 years was estimated at 78,624 (95 % UI 63,795 to 96,829) cases, with 25,833 (95 % UI 22,152 to 28,156) deaths. The estimated age-standardized incidence rate (ASIR) and mortality rate (ASMR) of IBD among the elderly in 2019 were 0.95 (95 % UI 0.78 to 1.15) per 100,000 and 0.33 (95 % UI 0.29 to 0.35) per 100,000, respectively. Over the period from 2010 to 2019, the global ASIR and ASMR of elderly IBD decreased by an average of −0.62 % (95 % CI -0.82 to −0.42) and − 0.69 % (95 % CI −0.84 to −0.55).

### 3.2. Regional burden and its trend of elderly IBD

Among the six regions designated by the WHO, the European region exhibited the highest ASPR of 29.7 (95 % UI 26.18 to 33.21), ASDR of 12.26 (95 % UI 10.47 to 13.64), and ASMR of 0.54 (95 % UI 0.45 to 0.58) for elderly IBD in 2019. Concurrently, the Americas region recorded the highest ASIR of 2.22 (95 % UI 1.87 to 2.61) for elderly IBD (,, S1, S2). In contrast, the African region reported the lowest ASPR of 2.9 (95 % UI 2.3 to 3.61) and ASIR of 0.31 (95 % UI 0.23 to 0.4) for IBD, while the Western Pacific region documented the lowest ASDR of 4.44 (95 % UI 3.75 to 5.18) and ASMR of 0.17 (95 % UI 0.15 to 0.2) for IBD in the elderly (,, S1, S2).

### 3.3. Variation in elderly IBD burden at territorial level

From 1990 to 2019, the number of prevalence cases increased by more than 300 % in 15 countries and territories, Qatar (1091.6 %), United Arab Emirates (937.68 %), Jordan (798.4 %), Bahrain (563.48 %), Taiwan province of China (513.13 %), Djibouti (504.26 %), South Korea (496.86 %), Turkey (459.81 %), China (392.47 %), Iraq (363.7 %), Timor-Leste (353.98 %), Malaysia (352.02 %), Kuwait (351.72 %), Vietnam (328.93 %), and Thailand (303.1 %). Notably, only 7 countries exhibited a decline in prevalence cases, including Poland (−45.51 %), the United States (−16.15 %), Greece (−13.96 %), and Bulgaria (−10.4 %) (A, Table S3).

### 3.4. Burden and its time trend of elderly IBD by SDI

For the SDI country classification, the ASPR, ASIR, ASDR, and ASMR have consistently been highest for elderly IBD in high SDI countries over the past 30 years.

Between 1990 and 2019, the ASPR in high SDI countries and high-middle SDI countries declined on average each year, whereas the ASPR in middle SDI, low-middle SDI, and low SDI countries increased on average each year. The largest average annual decline in ASPR was in high SDI countries at −0.88 % (95 % CI -1.17 to −0.6) per year, while the largest average annual increase in ASPR was in middle SDI countries at 1.37 % (95 % CI 1.2 to 1.54) per year. High SDI countries and low-middle middle SDI countries experienced a sharp decline in ASPR between 1990 and 2000, which leveled off after 2000 (A, ).

Between 1990 and 2019, the ASIR declined on average each year in high SDI countries and high-middle SDI countries, remained stable in middle SDI countries, and increased on average each year in low-middle SDI and low SDI countries. The largest average annual decline in ASPR was in high-middle SDI countries at −0.71 % (95 % CI -0.78 to −0.64) per year, while the largest average annual increase in ASIR was in low SDI countries at 0.69 % (95 % CI 0.65 to 0.73) per year. ASIR in high SDI countries declined sharply between 1990 and 2000, rebounding after 2000 (B, Table S1).

Between 1990 and 2019, the ASDR in high SDI countries remained stable, while ASDR in other SDI level countries declined on average per year. The largest average annual decline in ASDR was in middle SDI countries at −1.53 % (95 % CI -1.64 to −1.41) per year. Further analysis revealed a significant decline in ASDR in high SDI countries between 1990 and 2000, a rebound between 2000 and 2012, followed by another decline after 2012 (C, ).

Between 1990 and 2019, the ASMR in high SDI countries rose on average by 0.74 % (95 % CI 0.57 to 0.91) per year. In contrast, ASMR in other SDI level countries declined on average each year, with the largest decline observed in middle SDI countries at −2.34 % (95 % CI -2.52 to −2.16) per year (D, Table S2).

### 3.5. IBD burden among genders and 5-year age groups

Globally, among elderly IBD patients, females outnumber males in prevalence, incidence, DALYs, and mortality, while they are fewer in ASDs. Upon further age stratification, among IBD patients aged 60–89 years, the 60–64 age group exhibited higher ASPR, ASIR, and ASDR compared to other age groups. ASPR, ASIR, and ASDR decline with increasing age among elderly IBD patients. Conversely, ASMR peaks in the 80–84 and 85–89 age groups, escalating with the age of elderly IBD patients. Across all age groups of 60–89 years, ASDs are slightly higher in males than in females across all measures, albeit insignificantly (,,, S1, S2).

## 4\. Discussion

The GBD 2019 reveals a significant global [IBD burden](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/disease-burden) among the elderly. Since 1990, there has been a substantial increase in the number of prevalent cases, incident cases, DALYs, and deaths of elderly IBD worldwide. However, the burden of elderly IBD exhibits disparities across multiple dimensions. Therefore, it is important to understand the changes in the burden of elderly IBD in different locations for healthcare policy planning in each country.

### 4.1. Regional difference in the burden of elderly IBD

In this study, we observed disparities in the burden across different countries, with high SDl countries shouldering the greatest burden. In 2019, high SDI countries accounted for approximately half of the global prevalent and new onset of elderly IBD. In accordance with conventional wisdom, IBD is considered to be a disease prevalent in developed countries \[\]. Countries with high SDI are predominantly located in Europe and North America. Previous studies have observed an increasing proportion of elderly individuals among IBD patients in Europe and North America. In Denmark, the proportion of all individuals with IBD aged ≥65 years rose from 18 % in 1995 to 22 % in 2016 \[\]. In Canada, 20 % of adults ≥60 years with IBD in 2010; expected to rise to 38 % by 2030. \[\] In terms of DALYs and mortality, the European region exhibited the highest numbers and ASRs, with an increasing trend. This trend may be attributed to the prevalence of comorbidities, poor [drug tolerance](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/drug-tolerability), malnutrition, and cognitive decline among elderly IBD patients \[,,, \]. Age-related conditions such as cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and cancer are common comorbidities, which can contribute to increased mortality among patients \[\]. This will undoubtedly increase the complexity of treatment, affect treatment strategies, and place a huge burden on the healthcare system in the European region. After 2000, the trend of ASPR changes in high SDI countries reached a plateau, which could potentially be linked to the simultaneous increases in ASIR and ASDR. This phenomenon may indicate that the development of IBD in high SDI countries is gradually moving into the Prevalence Equilibrium phase \[\]. In that stage, the aging of the population of individuals with IBD and the increase in mortality due to age-related comorbidities make it possible for the prevalence rate to decrease, stabilize, or reach equilibrium.

The burden in middle SDI countries is rapidly increasing. The larger increases are mostly concentrated in Asia, Africa, and South America. A meta-analysis showed that the incidence has been increasing since 1990 in emerging economies in Africa, Asia, and South America \[\]. Firstly, the rapid increase in prevalence in these countries may be related to improved medical care. As awareness of IBD among the elderly increases and medical resources improve, it will help to identify more potential elderly IBD patients. Secondly, the rapid increase in incidence may be related to urbanization, industrialization, as well as alterations in dietary patterns \[\]. Many Asian countries have witnessed a transition to a Western-style diet, marked by increased intake of red meat, processed foods, and refined sugars \[,\]. This dietary shift may contribute to the rising prevalence of IBD. In terms of DALYs and mortality, the Eastern Mediterranean, Western Pacific, and South-East Asia regions exhibited a decreasing trend in ASDR and ASMR. This positive shift can be attributed to improvements in the standard of care and therapeutic advancements, such as the widespread adoption of biologics, enhanced colon [cancer surveillance](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/cancer-epidemiology), and advancements in surgical techniques \[,\].

Consequently, more resources for the prevention and management of elderly IBD are needed. In regions with high SDI where elderly IBD prevalence has reached a plateau, it is imperative to investigate suitable therapeutic interventions to enhance the [quality of life](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/quality-of-life) for this demographic group. In regions facing both poor medical resources and a rapid increase in prevalence, there is a critical need to bolster healthcare infrastructures and develop comprehensive medical insurance systems to enhance the accessibility and affordability of diagnosis and treatment for elderly individuals affected by IBD. International cooperation and the exchange of insights are highly encouraged.

### 4.2. Age differences in the burden of elderly IBD

Age is an important factor influencing the burden of elderly IBD. In an age-group stratified analysis, it was observed that those aged 60–64 years bore the highest burden of disease, while individuals aged 80–89 years faced the highest risk of mortality. Compared to younger IBD patients, elderly IBD patients tend to present with a higher prevalence of comorbidities, malnutrition, limited physical function, [frailty](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/frailty), and [polypharmacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/polypharmacy) \[,,,,,, \]. These factors contribute to the complexity surrounding the choice of pharmacologic or surgical interventions, which increases with age. Therefore, age-stratified management and personalized treatment approaches are imperative for effectively addressing IBD in older adults. This ensures that older adults with IBD receive care that aligns with their unique needs and preferences, ultimately improving their overall health outcomes and quality of life.

### 4.3. Gender differences in the burden of elderly IBD

In the analysis of gender stratification within this study, no significant gender differences were found, aligning with previous research encompassing all age groups \[\]. However, gender disparities manifest in more specific aspects. Previous research has indicated a greater risk of UC in adult males and CD in adult females \[,,,, \]. Men with IBD [face](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/agricultural-and-biological-sciences/face) elevated risks of complications, including colitis-associated [colorectal cancer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/colorectal-cancer), small [bowel cancer](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/intestine-cancer), and [primary sclerosing cholangitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/primary-sclerosing-cholangitis) \[,,, \]. Gender disparities in IBD may be attributed to [female sex hormones](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/female-sex-hormone) and [genetic factors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/heredity) \[,\]. Nevertheless, the [absence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/epileptic-absence) of fundamental mechanistic studies concerning gender differences in IBD necessitates further investigation into their impact and the roles that sex hormones and genetic factors played in disease etiology.

### 4.4. Limitations

This study acknowledges several limitations: (1) Reliance on GBD data: The study's estimation of IBD burden in older adults depends on the availability and quality of GBD 2019 raw data. This data collection process could be influenced by factors such as healthcare system quality, diagnosis accuracy, and reporting biases, potentially leading to inaccuracies in estimating disease burden. (2) Failure to differentiate between CD and UC: Limits the ability to capture differences in epidemiological approaches between these two forms of IBD. (3) Lack of in-depth analysis of aging countries: The study did not provide a detailed analysis of countries with significant population aging. Given the unique characteristics of aging countries, further research focusing on these countries could provide valuable insights into the burden of elderly IBD.

## 5\. Conclusion

Elderly IBD represents a substantial global health burden. High SDI countries exhibited a large population affected by elderly IBD, albeit with recent stabilization in prevalence rates. In newly industrialized and developing nations, there is a notable upward trend in both the incidence and prevalence of elderly IBD. Simultaneously, variations in disease burden exist among different age groups. Consequently, [public health](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/public-health) officials need to recognize the influence of aging populations and other determinants on the burden of IBD among older individuals, crafting tailored interventions to mitigate this burden effectively.
