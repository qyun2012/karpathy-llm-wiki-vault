## Summary

### Background

Uncertainty exists concerning the risk of infection and cancer associated with biologic therapies in elderly patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).

### Aims

To identify, synthesise and critically appraise the available evidence on the topic.

### Methods

We systematically searched Medline/PubMed, Embase and Scopus, through October 2019, and recent conference proceedings, to identify studies investigating the risk of serious infections, opportunistic infections, any infection and cancer in elderly IBD patients (>60 years) exposed to biologics as compared to those unexposed to biologics. Two reviewers independently extracted study data and assessed each study’s risk of bias. We examined heterogeneity, and calculated summary effect estimates using fixed- and random effects models. Quality of evidence was determined with GRADE.

### Results

We included 15 studies (one post hoc analysis of a randomised trial, nine cohort and five case-control studies). Elderly IBD patients treated with biologics were at increased risk of developing serious infections (random effects summary relative risk: 2.70, 95% CI: 1.56-4.66; seven studies; *I* <sup>2</sup> = 57%) and opportunistic infections (3.16, 1.09-9.20; four studies; *I* <sup>2</sup> = 73%). The occurrence of any infection (1.67, 0.51-5.43; five studies; *I* <sup>2</sup> = 75%) and cancer (0.90, 0.64-1.26; nine studies; *I* <sup>2</sup> = 0%) was not significantly affected. Nevertheless, our confidence in the effect estimates is rather limited; the quality of evidence is low to very low.

### Conclusions

Biologics are likely to increase the risk of serious and opportunistic infections in old IBD patients. Large prospective studies are needed to further assess the biologic treatments’ long-term safety profile in this population.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a chronic, disabling disease with relapsing-remitting symptoms (eg abdominal pain, severe diarrhoea, intestinal ulcerations) mainly comprising Crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC).[1](#apt15692-bib-0001), [2](#apt15692-bib-0002) Genetic and environmental factors can contribute to its development.[3](#apt15692-bib-0003) - [5](#apt15692-bib-0005) Therapeutic options include conventional treatments (ie corticosteroids, aminosalicylates and immunosuppressants) and biologics (anti- tumour necrosis factor \[TNF\] agents, anti-integrin agents and ustekinumab: an anti-interleukin IL-12 and IL-23 agent). Biologics are very effective treatments that have revolutionised IBD care,[6](#apt15692-bib-0006), [7](#apt15692-bib-0007) even though, due to their mechanism of action, they might be associated with an increased risk of infection and cancer.[8](#apt15692-bib-0008), [9](#apt15692-bib-0009)

Population is ageing, and about one of four IBD patients is diagnosed at an age of over 60 years.[10](#apt15692-bib-0010) The prevalence of the disease is the highest among the elderly.[11](#apt15692-bib-0011) Old patients display slightly milder or similar clinical symptoms and have similar chance to receive surgery than young ones,[12](#apt15692-bib-0012) - [14](#apt15692-bib-0014) but comorbidities and delayed presentation can worsen their clinical outcomes.[14](#apt15692-bib-0014) Since old people show an increased baseline risk of infections and cancer,[15](#apt15692-bib-0015), [16](#apt15692-bib-0016) older patients are less likely to receive biologics.[10](#apt15692-bib-0010) Additionally, the risk of infection and cancer is associated with disease activity.[17](#apt15692-bib-0017), [18](#apt15692-bib-0018) For these reasons, collecting and appraising the evidence concerning the potential harms associated with use of biologics in elderly IBD patients is very important.[19](#apt15692-bib-0019) However, randomised controlled studies of biologics in IBD rarely enroll old participants, and do not routinely report safety data for this subpopulation. Observational studies, on the other hand, lack the experimental design involving random allocation to optimally test exposure-outcomes hypotheses, making it difficult to interpret the findings. Some studies have estimated the risk of infections and malignancies in elderly people with IBD exposed to biologic agents by using not clinically meaningful comparison groups (eg younger exposed patients), providing estimates that could be misinterpreted by clinicians.[20](#apt15692-bib-0020), [21](#apt15692-bib-0021) A recent meta-analysis on the topic included only six studies of elderly patients treated with biologics compared with those unexposed.[22](#apt15692-bib-0022) Thus, we conducted an updated systematic review and meta-analysis of published studies to examine the risk of serious infections, opportunistic infections, tuberculosis, any infection and cancer in elderly IBD patients exposed to biologic treatments as compared to those unexposed to biologic treatments.

## 2 METHODS

Our study protocol was registered on the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO— [www.crd.york.ac.uk/prospero/)](http://www.crd.york.ac.uk/prospero/\)).[23](#apt15692-bib-0023) Moreover, this work followed guidance published by the Cochrane collaboration,[24](#apt15692-bib-0024) and adhered to the GRADE and PRISMA guidelines.[25](#apt15692-bib-0025), [26](#apt15692-bib-0026)

### 2.1 Search strategy

We systematically searched Medline/PubMed, Embase and Scopus databases, through October 2019, to identify eligible observational (prospective and retrospective) and experimental studies examining potential harms of biologics in elderly patients (>60 years of age) with IBD. Search terms included: “biologic(s), tumo(u)r necrosis factor, TNF, anti(-)integrin(s), anti(-)adhesion, adalimumab, golimumab, infliximab, certolizumab, natalizumab, vedolizumab or ustekinumab, combined with inflammatory bowel disease, Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis, and with infection(s), neoplasm(s), cancer(s), tumo(u)r(s), malignancy, malignancies or tuberculosis.” We performed the search including titles and abstracts and pertinent MeSH terms. The search algorithms, for each one of the databases, are presented in Figure [S1](#support-information-section). We have also searched the Cochrane Library, and recent conference proceedings (European Crohn’s and Colitis Organisation, Digestive Disease Week, United European Gastroenterology Week; 2017-2019). The bibliographies of the articles identified, and of relevant systematic review articles, were scrutinised to identify additional pertinent studies. We screened the reference lists of identified studies, and asked experts to provide additional evidence. We excluded editorial articles and case series, and limited the search to studies conducted in humans. We imposed no other restrictions.

### 2.2 Selection criteria

Two investigators (DP and SB) scrutinised independently titles and abstracts of the articles identified in the search and excluded the irrelevant ones. We screened the full text of potentially eligible studies for inclusion. We considered biologic therapies that have been authorised for use in IBD by either the European Medicines Agency, or the US Food and Drug Administration: that is, adalimumab, certolizumab pegol, golimumab, infliximab, natalizumab, vedolizumab and ustekinumab.

We considered the following clinical outcomes: (a) serious infections; (b) opportunistic infections; (c) tuberculosis; (d) any infection; and (e) cancer. Serious infections include infections associated with hospital admission, use of intravenous antibiotics or death. Opportunistic infections are caused by pathogens that take advantage of an opportunity such as a weakened immune system of the host. Examples of opportunistic infections include *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, JC virus infection, *Nocardia* infection, Cytomegalovirus or Epstein-Barr virus infection, oral or oesophageal candidiasis, varicella-zoster virus infection, herpes zoster infection, herpes simplex infection, *Pneumocystis jirovecii* infection, *Histoplasma capsulatum* infection and Legionella-induced pneumonia.

We considered eligible for inclusion any observational (case-control or cohort) or experimental study that reported (or provided sufficient data to calculate) a relative risk (RR) estimate for the above outcomes in elderly IBD patients exposed to biologics against placebo, no treatment or nonbiologic therapies. We included all doses of biologics. We excluded studies not investigating patients with IBD, and those not reporting any data for patients over the age of 60 years. We used the primary studies’ definitions of elderly patients, with cut-offs ranging from ≥60 to ≥75 years of age. We applied no restrictions in terms of length of follow-up.

### 2.3 Data extraction and statistical analysis

Two authors independently extracted the following data from each study into a standardised form: citation data; study design; setting; number of subjects; population characteristics (age, concomitant treatments, disease duration); underlying condition; exposure definition; outcomes reported; effect estimates and 95% confidence intervals (CI); and control for confounders by matching or adjustment. We extracted the most adjusted estimate of RR. When multivariable analysis was not performed, we calculated the crude effect estimate and 95% CI, as appropriate. We included studies reporting different measures of effect (ie risk ratio, odds ratio, hazard ratio and incidence rate ratio). In practice, these measures of RR yield similar estimates because the outcomes considered are relatively rare. We used both fixed- and random effects models to calculate summary effect estimates. We used the inverse variance method to pool the adjusted and unadjusted effect estimates. Because high heterogeneity was detected across most of the analyses, the random effects estimates (ie the more conservative approach) [27](#apt15692-bib-0027) are reported in the text, while both types are reported in tables.

We conducted separate analyses for each one of the outcomes. When possible, we performed subgroup analyses by condition, study design, risk of bias (RoB), class of biologic drug and combination therapy with other treatments, to investigate potentially different effects on risk. We assessed whether the subgroups were associated with significantly different risk estimates using a test of interaction.[28](#apt15692-bib-0028)

We examined heterogeneity across studies using the Cochran’s *Q* test,[29](#apt15692-bib-0029) with a 0.10 level of significance, and the *I* <sup>2</sup> statistic, which describes the percentage variation across studies due to heterogeneity rather than chance.[30](#apt15692-bib-0030) We considered the cut-offs of <30%, 30%-59%, 60%-75% and >75% to suggest low, moderate, substantial and considerable heterogeneity respectively. Given that each pairwise comparison included a limited number of studies, we could not formally assess small-study effects. We used Stata 15 (Stata Corp.) for statistical analysis. For all the tests (except for heterogeneity), we considered a two-tailed probability value <0.05 as statistically significant.

### 2.4 Assessment of the quality of evidence

Two authors (DP and SB) evaluated the RoB in included studies using the Newcastle-Ottawa scale.[31](#apt15692-bib-0031) It addresses the following key domains: selection of the study groups; comparability of the groups; and ascertainment of either the exposure or the outcome of interest for case-control or cohort studies respectively. We discussed discrepancies, and agreement was reached by consensus.

We used GRADE to assess the quality of evidence.[25](#apt15692-bib-0025) GRADE assessment begins with study design (evidence from randomised studies starts as high quality, while that from observational studies starts as low quality) and then addresses five reasons to possibly rate down the quality of evidence (ie RoB, imprecision, indirectness, inconsistency and publication bias) and three reasons to possibly rate up the quality (large magnitude of effect, dose-response gradient and effect of plausible residual confounding). GRADE results in an assessment of the quality of a body of evidence in one of four grades: high, moderate, low or very low. We summarised the evidence into a GRADE Summary of Findings Table, an approach easily understandable to decision makers and other stakeholders.[25](#apt15692-bib-0025)

## 3 RESULTS

### 3.1 Search results

A summary of the search and selection process is shown in Figure [1](#apt15692-fig-0001) (flow diagram). Out of 5303 abstracts identified and screened after duplicates removal, we retrieved 151 publications for detailed evaluation of the full texts, and identified 10 eligible studies. The most common reason for exclusion was that no data (or insufficient data) were reported for elderly patients (Figure [1](#apt15692-fig-0001)). After careful screening of the reference lists, we identified three additional pertinent studies. The assessment of conference proceedings yielded two more studies. In total, 15 studies [32](#apt15692-bib-0032) - [46](#apt15692-bib-0046) (13 full-text articles [32](#apt15692-bib-0032) - [39](#apt15692-bib-0039), [41](#apt15692-bib-0041) - [44](#apt15692-bib-0044), [46](#apt15692-bib-0046) and two abstracts [40](#apt15692-bib-0040), [45](#apt15692-bib-0045)) met the eligibility criteria and reported the occurrence of infections or malignancies in IBD patients ≥60 years old. Nine were cohort studies,[32](#apt15692-bib-0032), [34](#apt15692-bib-0034), [37](#apt15692-bib-0037) - [40](#apt15692-bib-0040), [42](#apt15692-bib-0042), [44](#apt15692-bib-0044), [45](#apt15692-bib-0045) five were case-control studies [33](#apt15692-bib-0033), [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [36](#apt15692-bib-0036), [41](#apt15692-bib-0041), [43](#apt15692-bib-0043) and one was the post hoc analysis of a randomised controlled trial (RCT).[46](#apt15692-bib-0046) All studies reported at least one relevant outcome for IBD patients, and two of them [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [40](#apt15692-bib-0040) also reported data regarding CD and UC patients, separately. We excluded one study because it reported zero events in both the exposed (n = 22) and unexposed (n = 17) groups.[47](#apt15692-bib-0047) Most studies were carried out in Europe (n = 8), but some were conducted in North America (n = 5), one in Asia, and one was a multicentre RCT. Publication dates ranged between 2011 and 2019. A summary of the study characteristics is given in Table [S1](#support-information-section).

### 3.3 Results of meta-analyses

#### 3.3.1 Serious infections

Data from seven studies, reporting at least one case of serious infection, were synthesised.[33](#apt15692-bib-0033), [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [39](#apt15692-bib-0039) - [41](#apt15692-bib-0041), [43](#apt15692-bib-0043), [45](#apt15692-bib-0045) The frequency of serious infections was 7.0% among those exposed to biologics and 2.7% among the unexposed. Use of biologics in elderly IBD patients was associated with an increased risk of serious infections (RR: 2.70; 95% CI: 1.56-4.66; Table [^1]). The RR estimates with 95% CIs for the primary studies and the meta-analysis results are shown in Figure [2](#apt15692-fig-0002). The Cochran’s *Q* test had a *P* value of 0.03, and the corresponding *I* <sup>2</sup> was 57%, both showing moderate but significant variability between studies. The study by Lee et al was an outlier, hence we conducted a sensitivity analysis by removing it.[41](#apt15692-bib-0041) The pooled effect estimate changed slightly (RR: 2.51; 95% CI: 1.51-4.16) but remained statistically significant. The Cochran’s *Q* test had a *P* value of 0.047, and the corresponding *I* <sup>2</sup> was 56%, both confirming moderate but significant variability between studies.

The subgroup difference reached statistical significance (test of interaction: *P* = 0.03). The sensitivity analysis incorporating the three studies [33](#apt15692-bib-0033), [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [39](#apt15692-bib-0039) rated as of low RoB (ie high quality) yielded a slightly nonsignificant estimate with large CI (RR: 3.56; 95% CI: 0.98-12.9; Table [^1]) and substantial heterogeneity (*I* <sup>2</sup> = 70%; *P* = 0.04) within the subgroup. The risk of serious infections in elderly patients exposed to anti-TNF agents plus immunosuppressants, vs immunosuppressants alone, was significantly increased (HR: 2.30; 95% CI: 1.14-4.65) in one study.[39](#apt15692-bib-0039)

#### 3.3.2 Opportunistic infections and tuberculosis

We synthesised the results of four studies reporting the occurrence of opportunistic infections.[35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [38](#apt15692-bib-0038), [39](#apt15692-bib-0039), [41](#apt15692-bib-0041) The frequency of the event was 0.85% in the groups exposed to biologics and 0.27% in the unexposed. Use of biologics in elderly IBD patients was associated with an increased risk of developing opportunistic infections (RR: 3.16; 95% CI: 1.09-9.20; Table [^1]); however, the CI was very wide. The RR estimates and the 95% CIs for the primary studies, and the summary results, are presented in Figure [3](#apt15692-fig-0003). The Cochran’s *Q* test had a *P* = 0.01, and the corresponding *I* <sup>2</sup> statistic was 73%, both indicating substantial heterogeneity between studies. As also observed with serious infections, we noted much more conservative estimates when we summarised the cohort studies [38](#apt15692-bib-0038), [39](#apt15692-bib-0039) (RR: 1.61; 95% CI: 1.29-2.01) as compared to case-control studies [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [41](#apt15692-bib-0041) (RR: 29.2; 95% CI: 3.46-246; Table [^1]). This subgroup difference reached statistical significance (test of interaction: *P* < 0.01). The sensitivity analysis including the two studies [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [39](#apt15692-bib-0039) rated as of low RoB (ie high quality) yielded a nonsignificant effect estimate (RR: 3.33; 95% CI: 0.39-28.7; Table [^1]) with wide CIs and substantial heterogeneity within the subgroup (*I* <sup>2</sup> = 73%; *P* = 0.06).

Two studies investigated the risk of opportunistic infections in elderlies exposed to anti-TNF agents plus immunosuppressants, vs immunosuppressants alone.[38](#apt15692-bib-0038), [39](#apt15692-bib-0039) The risk was not significantly increased (RR: 1.35; 95% CI: 0.82-2.22), and there was no significant heterogeneity between the two studies (*I* <sup>2</sup> = 0%; *P* = 0.43), however, data were scarce.

Only two small studies [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [41](#apt15692-bib-0041) reported data for tuberculosis. Use of biologics in elderly IBD patients was associated with an increased risk of tuberculosis (RR: 10.8; 95% CI: 1.26-91.4; Table [^1]); however, the summary estimate showed a very wide CI. The only study [35](#apt15692-bib-0035) rated as of low RoB displayed an effect estimate including the null effect (RR: 4.04; 95% CI: 0.21-77.3).

#### 3.3.3 Any infection

We synthesised data from five studies reporting the risk of any infection.[35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [36](#apt15692-bib-0036), [43](#apt15692-bib-0043), [44](#apt15692-bib-0044), [46](#apt15692-bib-0046) The frequency of infections was 33.4% in the exposed groups and 23.1% in the unexposed. Use of biologics in elderly patients with IBD was not associated with a significantly increased risk of developing any infection (RR: 1.67; 95% CI: 0.51-5.43; Table [^1]). The RR estimates with 95% CIs for the primary studies and the summary results are shown in Figure [S2](#support-information-section). The Cochran’s *Q* test had a *P* < 0.01, and the *I* <sup>2</sup> was 75%, both showing substantial heterogeneity between studies. This accounted, possibly, for the high discrepancy seen between prospective [44](#apt15692-bib-0044), [46](#apt15692-bib-0046) and retrospective studies [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [36](#apt15692-bib-0036), [43](#apt15692-bib-0043) (test for interaction: *P* = 0.03). A sensitivity analysis including the two studies [35](#apt15692-bib-0035), [46](#apt15692-bib-0046) rated as of low RoB (ie high quality) yielded a RR estimate with a very wide CI including the null effect (RR: 2.01; 95% CI: 0.03-154; Table [^1]).

The risk of any infection in elderly patients exposed to vedolizumab was not increased (RR: 0.21; 95% CI: 0.03-1.43) in one study [46](#apt15692-bib-0046); however, the number of events was limited.

#### 3.3.4 Cancer

Use of biologics in elderly patients with IBD did not significantly affect the risk of developing cancer (RR: 0.90; 95% CI: 0.64-1.26; Table [^1]). In this meta-analysis, the evidence quality was judged as ‘very low’ for serious and opportunistic infections and for any infection, whereas it was judged as ‘low’ for cancer, for the following reasons: most of the evidence derived from observational studies (evidence from observational studies is initially classified as low quality); the meta-analytic effect estimates were not precise (except for serious infections) and the results were not consistent (except for cancer);

On the other hand, given the likelihood that clinicians would have been more alert to cancer and any infection in patients exposed to biologics and could have therefore over-reported their occurrence, and that no association was found, we have upgraded the evidence for any infection and cancer by one level.

## 4 DISCUSSION

In this systematic review and meta-analysis of published studies, we have synthesised and critically appraised the existing evidence regarding risk of serious infections, opportunistic infections, any infection and cancer among elderly IBD patients exposed to biologics as compared to the unexposed. We observed an almost threefold higher risk of serious infections; however, this finding should be interpreted with caution as our analysis suffers from significant heterogeneity. Cohort studies showed an attenuated association, while case-control studies displayed severely inflated RR estimates. The estimate obtained by summarising only low RoB studies included the null effect. Similarly, for opportunistic infections, we identified three times higher risk among elderly IBD patients exposed to biologics than among those unexposed to biologics. However, the uncertainty was high, there was substantial heterogeneity, and the prospective cohort studies showed a very attenuated association. When restricting the analysis to studies at low RoB the RR estimate included the null effect. For these reasons, we believe that the real risk of serious and opportunistic infections could be closer to the null effect than it is apparent from these risk estimates.

We did not find any significant increase in the risk of any infection in elderly IBD patients exposed to biologics. This is compatible with the small increase in risk previously observed in adult patients.[8](#apt15692-bib-0008) This result is in contrast with a recent meta-analysis by Borren et al [22](#apt15692-bib-0022) that has reported a more than 10-fold higher risk. However, we have included five studies for this outcome, while Borren et al [22](#apt15692-bib-0022) included only three studies (two of them were case-control studies showing inflated effect estimates), thus we are confident that we have synthesised the current evidence.

We did not observe any association between exposure to biologics and cancer in elderly IBD patients. The lack of any significant heterogeneity between studies is quite reassuring. We found no association despite the likelihood that clinicians would have been more alert to cancer in subjects exposed to biologics and could have therefore over-reported its occurrence. However, even if the follow-up time of most included studies was longer than 3 years, we still cannot exclude a small increase in risk, given the sometimes short period of exposure, and the long latency for most cancers from the subclinical to the clinical phase.

The first publication regarding the risk of harmful effect of biologics in elderly IBD patients was the case-control study by Cottone et al [35](#apt15692-bib-0035) In this highly cited article, the estimates of effect for serious and opportunistic infections were very high (over 10.0), while no effect was found for cancer. Early replication studies tend to be biased against initial overestimated findings, an observation previously termed as Proteus phenomenon.[48](#apt15692-bib-0048), [49](#apt15692-bib-0049) These initial findings are not supported by current evidence, and it is well known that the case-control studies may be more susceptible to biases than prospective cohort studies.

Several observational studies, and a meta-analysis, have compared the risk in elderly IBD patients exposed to biologics with the risk in younger IBD patients exposed to such drugs, publishing highly significant results.[20](#apt15692-bib-0020) - [22](#apt15692-bib-0022) However, these studies do not answer the most meaningful question (ie what are the risks of biologic therapies in elderly IBD patients as compared to administering other nonbiologic treatments). Moreover, they provide misleading effect estimates that may be interpreted as evidence against the use of biologics in older patients, whereas they simply reflect the higher baseline risk of infections and cancer among elderly people.

Our work has limitations. Most evidence relied on observational studies that lack random allocation of participants, which is necessary to optimally test exposure-outcome hypotheses. Sometimes, the analysis of observational data cannot fully address confounding by unknown or unmeasured factors associated with both exposure and outcome. Since disease activity is associated with both infections and cancer,[15](#apt15692-bib-0015), [16](#apt15692-bib-0016) those studies not adjusting for disease extent and severity may report confounded effect estimates associated with biologic drug exposure. Studies reporting the risks of biologic therapies in elderly IBD patients are scarce. To synthesise all the evidence, we have merged different comparators (eg amminosalycilates, thiopurines, corticosteroids, or their combination) and intervention groups (eg anti-TNF, vedolizumab, anti-TNF plus thiopurines, etc). This may have contributed to the heterogeneity observed across studies concerning infection outcomes. Finally, only few primary studies reported how many patients were on corticosteroids or have controlled for that variable, while it is well recognised that corticosteroids are a major risk factor for infections.

Despite these limitations, we believe that our work has significant merits. We have conducted a rigorous and extensive literature search to collect all the eligible publications, and we were able to identify and analyse more than double the studies of a very recent meta-analysis on a similar topic.[22](#apt15692-bib-0022) Two independent reviewers have carefully undertaken the data extraction, and discrepancies were checked and resolved through consensus. We have, for the first time, critically appraised the evidence on this topic by using GRADE, the most well-recognised system to evaluate the quality of evidence and determine the confidence in the risk estimates.[25](#apt15692-bib-0025) Most studies adjusted for important confounders including gender, age, duration of IBD or type/extension of disease. Additionally, we performed sensitivity analyses by study design (cohort/case-control) and quality.

In summary, IBD patients over 60 years old exposed to biologics are more susceptible to serious and opportunistic infections, than same-age unexposed patients. In contrast, there is no evidence of an increased risk of any infection and malignancies. However, given the limitations we have highlighted and the low confidence that we have in the effect estimates, the real risk of serious and opportunistic infections is probably smaller and compatible with the risk already observed in adult patients.[8](#apt15692-bib-0008) Similarly, the effect estimate for any infection cannot exclude a small increase in risk. We did not observe any association between biologics and cancer. Even though most studies about the risk of malignancy had a follow-up time longer than 3 years, we cannot totally exclude a small effect of biologics on the risk of malignancy.

The results of the current study are relatively reassuring to clinicians willing to administer biologic drugs to their patients as compared to previous studies on the topic that had found a very elevated risk of infections in elderly IBD patients exposed to biologics.[22](#apt15692-bib-0022) However, clinicians should take into account that sparse evidence showed that the risk of serious and opportunistic infections in older patients may increase further with combination therapy (ie biologics and thiopurines, or biologics thiopurines and corticosteroids).[39](#apt15692-bib-0039), [41](#apt15692-bib-0041), [50](#apt15692-bib-0050) Moreover, almost the totality of evidence about safety of biologics in elderlies concerns anti-TNF drugs. There is still very limited and inconclusive data for anti-integrins,[46](#apt15692-bib-0046) and no evidence for ustekinumab. Also, no data exist concerning the risks of biologics independent of steroid therapy for elderly patients. These topics still represent important gaps in the literature.

We need large, well-conducted cohort studies to further assess the biologic treatments’ comparative and long-term safety profiles in the elderly, and increase our confidence in the estimates of effect, which is still low. Concerning cancer, we need more prospective studies with long follow-up times (over 5 years) and an adequate period of exposure to exclude a small increase in risk in elderly IBD patients exposed to such drugs. Finally, as the IBD population is ageing, new RCTs should include a larger proportion of elderly patients and report analyses by age to permit adequate access to the relevant efficacy and safety data.
