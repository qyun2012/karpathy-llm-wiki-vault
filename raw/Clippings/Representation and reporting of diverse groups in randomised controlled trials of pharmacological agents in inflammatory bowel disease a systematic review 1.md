## ## Summary

[Inflammatory bowel disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/inflammatory-bowel-disease) (IBD) is now recognised as a global disease, with incidence rapidly increasing in newly industrialised countries in South America, Asia, and Africa. Trials in IBD, therefore, should adequately represent diverse groups with respect to gender, age, place of residence, race, and ethnicity to ensure the global applicability and generalisability of their findings. In this [systematic review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/systematic-review), we searched PubMed and Embase for [randomised controlled trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/randomized-controlled-trial) (RCTs) published in English from Jan 1, 1995, to Jan 13, 2023, evaluating the efficacy of any pharmacological intervention in patients with IBD. Of 7543 records yielded in the search, we included 617 records reporting data from 627 RCTs and 108 986 participants. The results show a paucity of adequate representation of diverse groups in these RCTs. This finding was true for various groups, including racially and ethnically diverse populations, **older (aged >65 years)** and younger (aged <18 years) populations, those who identify outside of the gender binary, and people from South America and Africa. Also, some regions had an apparent scarcity of funding sources for trials. Pharmaceutical companies and [clinical trial](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/clinical-trial) organisations should aim to ensure adequate representation of such under-represented groups in future IBD trials.
## Introduction

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), comprising [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease) and [ulcerative colitis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/ulcerative-colitis), is estimated to affect around 10 million people worldwide, and the numbers are increasing rapidly. The past two decades have also seen rapid advances in the understanding of the [pathophysiology](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/pathophysiology) of IBD, enabling an array of diverse therapeutic options for clinical practice. Biologics and small molecules, in particular, have revolutionised the management of patients with IBD, with some treatments altering the natural history of IBD, enabling prolonged remission and changes in [disease course](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/disease-course) in a proportion of patients with IBD.

Although IBD has historically been regarded as a disease confined to White individuals from countries of western Europe as well as those countries shaped by western European culture, IBD is now recognised as a global disease, with the rapid increase in incidence extending into newly industrialised countries in South America, Asia, and Africa., There is increasing focus on the effect of race and ethnicity on the potential factors driving the global increase in IBD incidence, including dietary and [environmental factors](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/environmental-factor). Phase 2 and phase 3 trials of IBD therapeutic agents are usually conducted in Western populations, possibly due to commercial and logistical reasons. This focus on specific populations in the Western world could potentially hamper our understanding of the efficacy, [adverse events](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/adverse-event), and challenges of therapies in diverse global populations. Such trials must be representative of various regions, ethnicities, races, ages, and gender groups to enable full understanding of the effect of these diversities on therapeutic responses, safety, and acceptability. In addition, health-care providers and policy makers need to consider the effect of the sociocultural and financial implications of newer therapies on individuals, families, and society in diverse settings worldwide, especially in the wake of widening global health inequities.,,

To understand the effect of diverse demographic factors on therapy choices and variation in response to therapeutic agents for IBD, as well as to provide equitable and evidence-based [health care](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/health-care), it is crucial to ensure adequate representation of demographic profiles in clinical randomised controlled trials (RCTs). In this [systematic review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/systematic-review), we aimed to analyse any disparities in the inclusion of diverse demographic characteristics, including gender identity, age, racial and ethnic group, socioeconomic status, and participants' country of residence, in the conduct of RCTs of different therapeutic options in IBD and identify time trends in their representation.

## Methods

### Search strategy and selection criteria

This systematic review was done per PRISMA guidance. We searched the major databases PubMed and Embase from Jan 1, 1995, to Jan 13, 2023. The keywords used were “ulcerative colitis” OR “Crohn's disease” OR “Inflammatory bowel disease”, and the search included them as both Medical Subject Headings and free text. The limits for “randomised controlled trials” were applied in the search. The literature search was restricted to studies published in English. The bibliographies of included studies were searched for additional studies. The detailed search strategy is shown in the. Duplicate studies were removed before the title and abstract screening. Titles and abstracts were independently reviewed by two authors (MMP and AKVR), followed by full-text screening of potentially eligible records (MMP, AKVR, and AJ). Any discrepancies were resolved after discussion with a third reviewer (VS or SS).

All fully published RCTs evaluating the efficacy of any pharmacological intervention in patients with IBD were included. We excluded observational studies, case reports, case series, reviews, commentaries, letters, and editorials. We also excluded studies that did not report results or original data, as well as studies that reported the efficacy of non-pharmacological interventions (eg, non-drug therapies or invasive interventions). We excluded trials of interventions using natural remedies as well as dietary interventions, as diets were presumed to be region-specific and would limit generalisability on a global scale. We excluded RCTs that were not focused on IBD, such as the management of [irritable bowel syndrome](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/irritable-colon) in IBD. We excluded abstracts without full texts as their reporting might have been constrained by available word limits. We excluded papers of post-hoc analyses of published RCTs. Because we wanted to specifically examine diverse group reporting in full published reports, we did not search [clinical trial](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/clinical-trial) registries to find papers for inclusion; however, we did search [clinical trial](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/clinical-trial) registries for deposited appendices and additional information and data for the RCTs identified by our search.

### Data analysis

Data from included studies were extracted to a predefined Excel form by two authors independently (MMP and AKVR). Extracted data included the primary location of the study (single country or multiple countries \[determined by the global lead investigator site location\]); the year of publication; the number of patients with IBD who were enrolled; and the number of patients with each subtype of IBD (ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease, or undifferentiated). We also extracted data on the median age, sex or gender (including genders outside the gender binary), race, ethnicity, sexual orientation, religion, education, health-care insurance status, socioeconomic status (using the Kuppuswamy scale), income, employment status, and country of residence of the participants. We specifically identified trials that recruited patients younger than 18 years, as well as those that recruited older patients with IBD (aged >65 years). We also extracted information about whether the trial was funded by academic funders or by pharmaceutical companies. We extracted data on the drugs used in the RCTs and independently categorised them as steroids, aminosalicylates, [immunomodulators](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunomodulating-agent), [immunosuppressives](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/immunosuppressive-agent), antibiotics, biologics (eg, anti-TNF, anti-integrins, anti-interleukins), small molecules, and other. We used both the standard-of-care and [experimental drugs](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/experimental-drug) to categorise the trials regarding the type of intervention (eg, if a trial was steroid versus anti-TNF it would count twice—once in the steroid group and once in the anti-TNF group).

We grouped countries into continents per the UN Geoscheme. We divided countries by income per World Bank classification. We grouped countries into geographical regions by use of UN 2023 World Economic Situation and Prospects data.

Across demographic characteristics, we present categorical data as number (percentage) of the trials or as number (percentage) of the patients in each analysis using the mean of means. Some trials had more than one type of intervention, or took place in more than one geographical region; thus, the percentages for these measures are not mutually exclusive. We also analysed temporal changes in the reporting or recruitment of diverse populations in IBD trials by year of publication (1995–2000 *vs* 2001–10 *vs* 2011–20 *vs* 2021–23). SPSS version 23·0 (Armonk, NY, USA) was used for the analysis.

### Race and ethnicity

The race of participants was only reported in 179 (28·5%) of the 627 included RCTs. Of the 51 149 participants from these 179 trials, 43 650 (85·3%) were White, 3992 (7·8%) were Asian, 1035 (2·0%) were Black, and 95 (0·2%) were Pacific Islander. Multiracial and participants of unknown race were listed under the category of other in the published trials, which comprised 2377 (4·6%) participants. Reporting of race increased from seven (8%) of 91 RCTs in 1995–2000 to 43 (19%) of 230 RCTs in 2001–10, to 88 (37%) of 238 RCTs in 2011–2020, and 41 (60%) of 68 RCTs in 2021–23 (appendix pp 5–7; ).

244 (39%) of the 627 included trials were solely conducted in Europe. Of the 448 RCTs that did not report on race, 220 (49%) were solely conducted in Europe, 95 (21%) were solely conducted in Asia, 46 (10%) were solely conducted in North America, two (<1%) were solely conducted in South America, five (1%) were solely conducted in Oceania, two (<1%) were solely conducted in Africa, and 78 (17%) were conducted across continents.

Ethnicity was reported in only 67 (11%) of 627 RCTs, with data for 18 077 participants. Of these 18 077 participants, 12 640 (69·9%) reported being non-Hispanic or non-Latinx. 2815 (15·6%) participants identified as being Hispanic or Latino and 2622 (14·5%) participants were reported as having an unknown ethnicity. ### Age, gender, and socioeconomic status

The mean age of participants in the investigational agent or test group was 37·9 years (SD 11·7), and in the placebo or comparison group was 38·0 years (SD 18·1). All RCTs reported the age range of participants, but not all reported data specific to each age group. Most of the trials (519 \[83%\] of 627) reported the lower limit of the age group for inclusion of participants, but only 177 (28%) trials reported the upper limit of the age group for inclusion of participants. 40 (6%) trials recruited 2086 participants who were younger than 18 years, and 34 (5%) trials recruited 855 [paediatric](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/pediatrics) participants exclusively (; ). 33 (5%) trials recruited 855 participants who were older than 65 years (; ). A list of RCTs in IBD that have reported the involvement of people younger than 18 years or older than 65 years is included in the.

Among 627 included RCTs, 615 (98%) reported on gender or sex. 55 832 (51·9%) of 107 621 trial participants with gender data were male and 51 789 (48·1%) were female. None of the trials reported beyond binary gender categorisation, or on sexual oritentation. The included studies were checked for reporting of socioeconomic status, religion, employment status, income, and educational background of participants. None of the studies reported the socioeconomic status or religion of participants. Only three trials reported on employment status,,, or educational background.,, Of the three trials reporting employment background, 157 (58%) of 273 participants were employed full-time and 116 (43%) were employed part-time or unemployed. Health-care insurance status of participants was not reported in any RCT; however, two trials mentioned [absence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/epileptic-absence) of insurance and restrictions based on the type of insurance held by the participants as one of the reasons for attrition.,

### Geography

189 (30%) of 627 RCTs (63 687 participants) were cross-continental, mainly spread across upper-middle-income and high-income countries. Europe had the greatest representation among the investigator sites in all RCTs of IBD (431 \[69%\] of 627 trials), with at least one of the RCT investigator sites being situated in the continent. This was followed by North America (239 \[38%\]) and Asia (207 \[33%\]; ). Oceania had low trial site representation (73 \[12%\] trials). Africa (56 \[9%\]) and South America (34 \[5%\]) were under-represented among RCTs of IBD. Africa, South America, and Oceania have had the lowest representation in trials of IBD since 2001 (). Regarding participants in trials undertaken solely in the given continents, 24 685 (22·7%) resided in Europe, 10 161 (9·3%) resided in Asia, 9638 (8·8%) resided in North America, 584 (0·5%) resided in Oceania, 123 (0·1%) resided in Africa, and 108 (0·1%) resided in South America. 63 687 (58·4%) participants were recruited into trials that took place over multiple continents.

### Therapeutic interventions

Regarding intervention type, 179 (29%) of 627 trials explored the efficacy of biologics. Anti-TNF drugs were the most investigated biological intervention (93 \[52%\] of 179). Among other therapeutic interventions, steroids were used in 79 (13%) of 627 trials, aminosalicylates in 137 (22%), [immunomodulators](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/immunomodulating-agent) (single or combination) in 49 (8%), [immunosuppressives](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/immunosuppressive-agent) in 25 (4%), [antibiotics](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/antibiotics) in 31 (5%), and small molecules in 30 (5%). The 193 (31%) remaining trials were not classified. Of the 179 trials investigating biologics, only 15 (8%) had at least one trial site in Asia. Of these, 13 (7%) had at least one trial site in Japan. Of the 30 trials studying the efficacy of small molecules, all 30 (100%) had at least one site in Europe and four (13%) were conductd solely in Europe. 23 trials (77%) had at least one site in Asia or North America, 16 (53%) had at least one site in Africa or Oceania, and seven (23.3%) had at least one site in South America. Since 1995, there has been a decline in RCTs of traditional therapies, with an increase in trials investigating biologics and small molecules ().

### Funding of trials

421 (67%) of the 627 included RCTs were funded solely by pharmaceutical companies. 153 (24%) trials had funding from sources other than pharmaceutical companies. The funding source was not mentioned in 53 (8%) trials. Of the 104 trials conducted solely in Asia, only 38 (37%) were funded by pharmaceutical companies, compared with 56 (23%) of 239 of all trials conducted solely in North America and 138 (57%) of 244 of those conducted solely in Europe. Only one trial was conducted solely in Africa, two trials solely in South America, and three trials solely in Oceania, and they were all sponsored by pharmaceutical companies (). Out of 421 trials funded by pharamceutical companies, 160 (38%) reported race and 62 (15%) reported the ethnicity of participants. Among 153 trials with funding sources other than pharmaceutical companies, there was even lower reporting of race (16 \[10%\] of 153 trials) and ethnicity (three \[2%\] of 153 trials).

## Discussion

Race, socioeconomic status, age, and gender are fundamental [determinants of health](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/determinants-of-health). Our systematic review identifies a distinct lack of diversity in representation in most of the assessed domains among the participants of RCTs of IBD (). This scarcity of representation, particulrly with regards to race and ethnicity, appears to be a concern irrespective of whether the trials were sponsored by pharmaceutical companies or were academic trials. Furthermore, the scarcity of representation was a concern across various groups, including ethnically and racially diverse populations, older and younger patients, and participants from South America and Africa. In addition, we identified an [absence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/epileptic-absence) of reporting regarding the inclusion of patients with other protected characteristics, such as sexual orientation and gender identity.

There has been low reporting of race and ethnicity since 1995, with only a modest improvement in more recent years. We found that 71% of IBD trials did not report the race of participants and, in extension, could not analyse or report differences in outcomes in racially disparate groups.

If one were to assume that in these trials the study population largely reflected the population of the host countries, 464 (74%) of 627 included trials were either conducted solely or had at least one trial site in Europe; therefore it can be assumed that participants were largely White Europeans. This is concerning as the results of these RCTs of selectively represented groups of populations are then extrapolated globally to influence choice of treatment in diverse and under-represented populations, which may vary in both safety and efficacy profiles.

An analysis of RCTs in the USA using data from [ClinicalTrials.gov](http://clinicaltrials.gov/) revealed that ethnicity reporting and representation were suboptimal. Further analysis showed only modest improvement since the April, 2017, requirement by [ClinicalTrials.gov](http://clinicaltrials.gov/) for authors to report race and ethnicity information (if collected) during the submission of results for trials. Multiple previous studies have shown suboptimal and inconsistent reporting of race and ethnicity in [clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/clinical-trial) across disease areas., To our knowledge, this Review is one of the first exhaustive systematic analyses of the demographics of RCT populations for IBD., A 2011 paper reporting on clinical trial enrolment, including those for [oncology](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/oncology), cardiovascular disease, [internal medicine](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/internal-medicine), and infectious disease, found that only around 21% of the included studies had not reported racial and ethnic data. In our systematic review, 71% of IBD [clinical trials](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/clinical-trial) did not report on race and 89% did not report on ethnicity. One systematic review of North American clinical trials in non-alcoholic fatty liver disease reported that only 346 (11·6%) of 2983 trial participants were Hispanic. Another study outlined the under-representation and under-reporting of minority populations in [drug development](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/drug-development) for [Crohn's disease](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/crohns-disease) in all phases of clinical trials, with only 20 (22·0%) of 91 trials reporting the race of the participants (3078 \[90·5%\] of 3339 were White) and only one (1·1%) reporting ethnicity. A further study from Cohen and colleagues indicated that approximately 90% of participants in phase 2, phase 3, and long-term extension studies in IBD were White. Our study also found that 15 trials that reported on ethnicity had classified ethnicity into Caucasian and non-Caucasian.

Current estimates show an increasing incidence, as well as compounded prevalence, of IBD in patients older than 65 years, as well as an increasing incidence in children and adolescents younger than 18 years., In our analysis, only 6% of trials included patients younger than 18 years. IBD in children has scarce therapeutic options, and the under-representation of children in drug trials does not help in managing this real-world burden. Similarly, only a few trials (33 \[5%\]) recruited participants older than 65 years. This result indicates an unjustified exclusion of older participants from RCTs, despite older adults being some of the highest consumers of medical interventions and therapies across all specialties. Age and comorbidities are important considerations in the choice of therapies for IBD, both for the health-care professional and the patient; therefore, the scarce inclusion of these populations in RCTs, possibly resulting in age-related biases, is concerning.

There are differences in the way people of different gender identities experience IBD, including its effect on psychosexual health, and aspects relating to menstruation, intimacy, contraception, and pregnancy. There are scarce data regarding the prevalence of IBD among populations of various gender identities, especially as several identities defined in previous [International Classification of Diseases](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/international-classification-of-diseases) codes were pathologising and have only recently been replaced. In addition, gender-specific differences in treatment adherence, decision making, and [adverse events](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/adverse-event) in relation to medical and surgical therapies have been reported. Patients within the LGBTQ+ community face substantial challenges and disparities in care. However, gender reporting, as evidenced in our study, continues to be strictly binary, which in itself implies that the recruiting process is not inclusive of people of diverse gender identities. Furthermore, there continues to be a scarcity of studies exploring various gender-affirming procedures (including the use of hormones and puberty blockers) and their potential concurrent effects on IBD medications. There is a growing need among health-care professionals to understand the effect of therapy choices for IBD on gender-affirming treatments.

Although RCTs are considered the gold standard for research evidence, and particularly for therapeutic decisions, it is important to be aware of the reported disparities in these trials and the generalisability of their results across diverse populations globally. Despite overwhelming evidence that IBD is a disease that does not discriminate on the basis of gender, ethnicity, age, or other sociocultural characteristics, the same cannot be said about current IBD clinical trial inclusion and participation. These existing biases could mean that the patients included in IBD clinical trials might not be representative of the global population of people with IBD. More inclusive enrolment in IBD trials could help us to understand the factors that could affect therapy choices, in addition to differences in treatment response among various populations. These potential differences (or lack thereof) might have implications in choosing the most effective IBD treatment and in preventing delays in treatment by avoiding trial and error, potentially preventing unnecessary disability and morbidity due to IBD. Furthermore, individualised medication can help to maintain [quality of life](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/quality-of-life) among patients.

The unequal distribution of different markers of diversity among study populations for RCTs highlights the inequity in access to [health care](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/health-care) globally, which has the potential to strain physician–patient relationships when clinicians are prescribing medications to their patients that have not been studied in people of similar backgrounds. It is thus very important to ensure adequate representation of diverse populations in RCTs to provide equitable and evidence-based health care to all.

A more committed approach in recruiting participants with diverse demographic profiles is required from researchers. We recommend greater transparency in reporting demographic characteristics of participants in RCTs, with a minimum dataset required by regulatory bodies and journal editors. We also recommend extending this transparency when marketing and prescribing [pharmaceutical therapies](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/pharmacotherapy). Artificial intelligence could be beneficial in aiding purposeful recruitment to RCTs to ensure representation of diverse populations by professional organisations and research bodies, as well as in increasing representation from historically under-represented groups. Also, recruitment to trials should attempt to simulate the representation of various diverse populations with IBD.

There are many barriers to achieving representation within clinical trials. These barriers include: access to clinical trials (particularly in low-income and middle-income countries, as well as in rural areas), logistical factors in conducting studies in diverse locations (including health-care infrastructural challenges in different locations), differences in language and communication, scarcity of information and knowledge among potential participants about [clinical research](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/clinical-research) and what it entails, cultural factors, beliefs and attitudes towards research participation, distrust of the drug being investigated, distrust of the medical or pharmaceutical industries as a whole, discomfort with the research process, time and resource constraints (including any financial impact), stringent eligibility criteria excluding patients of some demographics, and an overall reluctance of sponsors to work with new investigators and institutions (). Additionally, bias, lack of [cultural competence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/cultural-competence), and disease-specific knowledge among investigators and study staff might affect inclusive research.

Increasing diversity in clinical trials requires a long-term commitment and investment from the research community, sponsors, and regulatory bodies. Information, education, and awareness about research are fundamental to achieving diversity in research. Much can be learned from the COVID-19 vaccine trials, which recruited participants of all ages who were ethnically and racially diverse. We need to capitalise on this increased awareness of the need for research. Researchers and study teams should improve their understanding of social and cultural influences that are important to patients and participants through cultural competency training. This training goes beyond a general awareness of factors for researchers to consider, such as genetic variations and linguistic barriers, among different racial and ethnic groups and includes socioeconomic status and the role of caregivers as they affect the individual's decision to participate in research. Identifying and including minority thought leaders in the promotion of research participation and increasing the pool of principal investigators from diverse populations might also improve participation among minority groups. Industry leaders also need to make trials more inclusive and accessible to diverse populations by decentralising studies, locating clinical trial sites in areas with diverse populations, and making research more accessible to participants from [underserved populations](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/underserved-population) by providing logistical, transportation, and financial support to the participants. There is a need to develop comprehensive outreach by building online communities, collaborating with [patient advocacy](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/patient-advocacy) organisations, such as the South Asian IBD Alliance, networking with health-care professionals, and engaging with the wider community. There is also a need to tighten regulator frameworks for clinical trials. The US Food and Drug Administration has issued diversity-focused guidance that mandates for results of a clinical trial to apply to the general population, the demographic mix of participants must mirror that of the entire US population.

Public and patient involvement from the earliest stages of research development to dissemination is recommended, which would mean that research is conducted with and by patients, rather than done to, about, or for them. This involvement can range from discussions relating to the relevance and acceptability of research, to direct contributions in research coproduction, design, and conduct. Our recommendation is that patient and public involvement should be considered a requirement for research funding allocation by all funding bodies to avoid tokenistic involvement of patients. Patient advocates and patient-led organisations can provide insights not only into important areas of research but also on how to promote research participation in all communities, including those who are under-represented.

Our systematic review has some limitations. We did not compare the demographics of participants to the characteristics of the population of the country the trial was held. In addition, we did not correlate participation to the IBD prevalence in individual countries. We could not analyse RCTs that were not in English. Our study also did not include non-randomised trials. Our systematic review also has strengths, such as the large number of RCTs included. For better homogeneity of studies, we excluded trials involving non-drug interventions. We took a holistic approach to diversity, including multiple metrics rather than a single factor, such as race or gender. We also involved an IBD patient advocate for improvement of relevance and meaningfulness of results.

In conclusion, our systematic review highlights the substantial disparities in the representation of various groups in RCTs designed to assess the efficacy of pharmacological interventions for IBD. This unequal representation severely limits the generalisability of results and perpetuates existing [health disparities](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/pharmacology-toxicology-and-pharmaceutical-science/health-disparity). Thus, it is crucial for researchers to actively make efforts towards inclusive recruiting practices for trials, to ensure that all individuals regardless of their age, gender identity, socioeconomic status, race, ethnicity, and geographical location have an equal opportunity to benefit from innovative interventions in this rapidly evolving field.
