## Abstract

Background

Despite the growing proportion of older adults with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), their lived experience is not well understood. IBD literature is generally focused on younger adults, and few studies are qualitative. Older adults may report well-being differently than younger adults, so it is important that we learn about their goals and priorities with a chronic disease.

Objective

The study sought to understand the lived experience of older adults with IBD and explore their perceptions and priorities.

Methods

We conducted in-depth interviews with patients ≥60 years of age with IBD to evaluate the impact and perception of IBD in the context their overall health and life. We used a hybrid inductive-deductive thematic analysis of our transcripts to identify underlying patterns.

Results

We achieved thematic saturation after 22 interviews. We produced 4 major themes: (1) having IBD at an older age, (2) financial ramifications of IBD at an older age, (3) expectations for a meaningful life, and (4) unmet needs. Prominent subthemes included (1) ageism, loss of autonomy, and barriers to healthcare; (2) retirement and insurance issues; (3) redefining quality of life and gratitude; and (4) social isolation and navigating daily life with IBD.

Conclusions

Having IBD later in life presents unique challenges. Physicians treating older patients should consider age-sensitive communication, susceptibility to social isolation, and practices for healthy aging in the context of IBD. Patient priorities for further investigation include more representation in the media and educational material tailored for older adults with IBD.

## Lay Summary

In this qualitative study, we employ in-depth interviews to report the lived experience of older adults with inflammatory bowel disease and explore their perceptions and priorities of living with this chronic disease.

Key Messages

What is already known?

- Older adults are the most prevalent age cohort of patients with inflammatory bowel diseases in the United States. Older adults have different health priorities, especially as it pertains to this chronic disease.

What is new here?

- Having inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) at an older age presents unique challenges. We identified financial and insurance based challenges faced by older adults with IBD. We report the unmet needs in older adults.

How can this study help patient care?

- We present strategies for physicians to consider age-sensitive communication. We discuss best practices to discuss healthy aging in the context of IBD.

## Introduction

Older adults are the most prevalent age cohort of people with inflammatory bowel diseases (IBDs). Nearly one-third of patients living with IBD are ≥60 years of age.<sup>1</sup> However, older adults with IBD are understudied and not well represented in clinical trials and the literature.<sup>2,3</sup> Older adults may experience and report well-being differently than younger adults, and may require differential management considerations taking into account comorbidity, polypharmacy, functional status, and shifting risk-benefit profiles.<sup>4-8</sup> Existing studies of IBD in older adults are largely retrospective studies focusing on clinical outcomes and pharmacoepidemiology.<sup>8-10</sup>

A number of studies report quantitatively on the toll of IBD on quality of life for patients living with this chronic disease.<sup>11-13</sup> A systematic review of qualitative studies of people with IBD globally including 825 patients, mostly from the United Kingdom and Europe, concluded that the major theme was living in isolation and exclusion. They noted that living with exhaustion, a flawed body, in secrecy and fear, and with restrictions were major subthemes.<sup>14</sup> However, the majority of these studies focused on young people. One thesis included a single 65-year-old, and a separate study on the significance of food included 8 older adults.<sup>15,16</sup> Only one study included older adults more liberally—a mixed methods study on fecal incontinence with IBD that included patients as old as 78 years of age.<sup>17</sup> In the oncologic literature, data suggest that older and younger adults experience symptoms differently.<sup>18</sup> A recently published scoping review of the experiences of older adults living with IBD concluded that there is an urgent need for more investigation to understand the experiences of older adults living with IBD.<sup>19</sup>

There is a dearth of qualitative studies of older adults with IBD in the medical literature.<sup>19</sup> Understanding a person’s lived experience with the disease is important for physicians as well as for investigators to guide future studies and interventions. Despite the growing proportion of older adults with IBD, their lived experience is not broadly characterized or well understood. In this study, we aim to understand how older adults experience inflammatory bowel disease and explore how their perceptions and priorities shape this experience. We conducted in-depth interviews with a broad range of older adults with IBD, employed qualitative description methodology to develop themes, and used reflexive thematic analysis as the primary means of describing the data.<sup>20,21</sup>

## Methods

### Setting

The study was conducted between February 2022 and September 2022 in the outpatient clinical practice of the Crohn’s and Colitis Center at the Massachusetts General Hospital, a large, urban tertiary care hospital in Boston staffed during the study period with 5 gastroenterologists and 1 full-time nurse practitioner. At this center, there are approximately 4500 unique IBD patient visits annually. The demographics of older adults seen at this site parallel those of older adults who receive care at other tertiary IBD centers across the United States.<sup>22</sup>

### Sampling

We used stratified purposeful sampling to guide recruitment of patients ≥60 years of age who were treated for IBD and comfortable in spoken English.<sup>23</sup> While there is no one definition of who is an older adult with IBD, being older than 60 years of age is a generally accepted age for differential practice considerations in the management of IBD.<sup>24</sup> We aimed to recruit 20 patients to reach adequate thematic saturation; we continued interviewing until we felt saturation (defined as no novel patterns of meaning across cases) was achieved at 22 patients.<sup>25,26</sup>

### Study Instrument

With assistance from University of North Carolina’s Connected Health for Applications and Interventions Core, 3 investigators (B.K., gastroenterologist; C.R., geriatrician; and A.R., study coordinator) developed a semi-structured individual interview tool informed by the Patient Priorities Care conceptual model.<sup>27</sup> This is a framework that was developed in geriatrics to highlight the importance of making medical decisions with each patient’s health priorities in mind in order to best address uncertainty and treatment burden that often accompanies treatment of chronic conditions. The interview guide was designed to evaluate the impact of IBD, the rationale behind personal treatment decisions, and patients’ perception of their overall health. The questions were formulated specifically for conversation to characterize an older adult’s lived experience with IBD. An example question is, “How does IBD affect your life?”; a follow-up question is, “What kind of day-to-day activities have been altered or changed due to your IBD?”

### Data Collection

A.R. approached all patients about the study to minimize the risk of physician coercion to participate and obtained informed consent through the REDCap (Research Electronic Data Capture) eConsent template. A.R. conducted all of the interviews to promote a safe space for commenting on medical care to a person who was not their medical provider. Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, we conducted the interviews over the phone. Interviews were recorded with a handheld digital audio recorder and transcribed using TranscribeMe, an online Health Information Portability and Accountability Act–compliant transcription service. A.R prepared all transcripts for analysis by manually redacting identifiable information including patient and doctor names. Demographic data and relevant medical history (age, diagnosis, age at diagnosis, current IBD medications, history of surgery for IBD) were collected from the medical record and confirmed with the patient at the initiation of the interview. This information was not included in the interview transcripts. Interviews lasted 30 to 60 minutes. Participants were offered a $40 check as a token of appreciation.

### Ethics

This study was approved by the Mass General Brigham Institutional Review Board (Protocol #2021P000217).

### Data Analysis

We used qualitative description as the research approach. This approach is relevant to gain firsthand knowledge of patients’ experiences.<sup>19</sup> Each “theme” is a summary of responses to a question. Reflexive theoretical analysis was used for data interpretation, which is suitable to contextualize lived experiences.<sup>26</sup> We employed coding reliability and codebook analysis to keep structure. We conducted a hybrid inductive-deductive thematic analysis of our transcripts to identify underlying themes specific to the lived experience of older adults with IBD.<sup>28</sup> A.R. deductively developed a preliminary codebook organized by sections in the interview guide, while B.K. created a framework “in vivo” as she received each redacted transcript. After several interviews had been coded, A.R. and B.K. collaborated on a new codebook prioritizing findings that we felt were both rich and important. We created the codebook using NVivo data analysis software version 12 (QSR International), which is available upon request. Consistent with our interview guide, we began with 4 superordinate domains: (1) impact of IBD, (2) treatment decisions and outcomes, (3) IBD in the context of overall health, and (4) closing comments. We specified parent codes such as “autonomy,” “socializing,” and “what matters most” and child codes such as “high quality of life/travel,” “food related/staying home/stigma/support group,” and “enjoyment/family/health.” During coding, investigators A.R. and B.K. inductively added parent and child codes as novel information emerged from the data.

### Data Reporting

We followed the Standards for Reporting Qualitative Research and used the COREQ (COnsolidated criteria for REporting Qualitative research) to provide validity.<sup>29,30</sup> We also adhered to quality assurance strategies for reflexive thematic analysis like open-ended and organic coding and theme review and refinement to ensure the prolonged and deep engagement with the data necessary for meaningful qualitative analysis.<sup>26</sup>

## Results

We conducted in-depth qualitative interviews of 22 adults ≥60 years of age with IBD. We selected patients to ensure balanced distribution of sex (12 women, 10 men), type of IBD (8 with Crohn’s disease), disease duration (7 had a new diagnosis of IBD at ≥60 years of age), IBD treatment (14 on advanced therapies), surgical history (7 with a history of surgery for IBD), and remission status (67% met criteria for remission by a validated disease activity index). The median age of patients interviewed was 69 years; with the exception of 2 patients, all were White, which parallels reported disease demographics of patients seen at tertiary care centers.<sup>22</sup> Participant characteristics are summarized in **Table 1**. We refined themes and subthemes to produce 4 major themes: (1) having IBD at an older age, (2) financial ramification of IBD at an older age, (3) expectations for a meaningful life, and (4) unmet needs (**Table 2**). **Figure 1** depicts the branching nature of the themes, subthemes, and supporting quotes.

### Theme 1: Having IBD at an Older Age

The first major theme, having IBD at an older age, describes how interviewees perceived their more advanced age influenced their daily experience with IBD. Subthemes included ageism (discrimination based on age); visual representation of IBD (how visible IBD was in the media); loss of autonomy (losing the ability to do what they wanted, when they wanted because of IBD, age, or both); age-associated barriers to healthcare; disentangling disease, aging, and medications; and IBD relative to other comorbidities (how IBD compared with other diagnosed conditions).

Older adults who were diagnosed with IBD at an older age commented on the discordance of having what is perceived as a “younger person’s disease” at an older age. In this study, 12 patients were diagnosed with IBD after 50 years of age; 7 were diagnosed after 60 years of age. One patient succinctly stated what many others experienced: “This was a total shock to me that you could get it at my age.” Another patient reported, “I didn’t think you could get Crohn’s at 71.”

#### Ageism

While ageism was not a prevalent theme, it was important when noted. Unprompted, 1 patient broached the issue of discrimination based on age, ageism, with a poignant reflection of an interaction with a resident describing her as “fragile.” A representative quotation is included here, with other relevant quotations in **Table 2**:

> *I’m 74. I’m considered, quote-unquote, “a geriatric patient.” I’m so far from geriatric that—my age is no indication of who I am as a human being, as an individual, as a woman. You’ve got to look and talk to me a little bit.*

#### Visual representation of IBD

Also unprompted, 2 patients discussed media portrayals of IBD. One patient pointed out that the target audience of most TV commercials related to IBD medications seems to be markedly younger people with IBD. On the other hand, another patient was grateful to the media for depicting social challenges with the disease, which helps her combat the stigma associated with this disease.

#### Loss of autonomy; age-associated barriers to healthcare; and disentangling disease, aging, and medications

Many patients voiced frustrations with the impact on their autonomy and changes to their retirement plans. While 1 patient did not experience it herself, she noted that there may be many age-associated barriers to healthcare due to mobility or sensory issues. Another patient pointed out the struggle to separate out the effects of the disease, medications, and aging.

#### IBD relative to other comorbidities

Because older adults are more likely to have other comorbidities, we prompted patients to discuss their IBD in the context of their overall health and other comorbidities. Patients in this cohort had comorbidities ranging from asthma to heart disease to breast and prostate cancer. However, most patients reported that IBD was their most bothersome medical problem, including in comparison with cancer. Patients used words like “impactful,” “life-limiting,” “prevalent,” “bothersome,” “troublesome,” and “the worst” when describing their relative experience with IBD compared with their other comorbidities. Another patient reflected on the inability to modify their IBD with widely recommended lifestyle modifications:

> *I was a little bit overweight, and I decided to change my diet and to eat more of a heart-healthy diet, \[…\] in the five years since I retired, I’ve dropped almost 29 pounds, so that helps with my cholesterol, helps with my heart, helps with my blood pressure, and it didn’t change the ulcerative colitis.*

Not all older adults have multiple chronic conditions. A few patients noted that IBD was their only major comorbidity and, therefore, it felt very consequential. One patient presented an analogy for the multisystem nature of the disease:

> *Having IBD is like it’s a tree. That’s your initial problem. And then you grow branches, like arthritis branches. I have other medical issues, kidney issues, that are probably related to Crohn’s. \[…\] But everything has to go back to, “What treatment are you going to give me? Wait a minute. I have Crohn’s.” That’s the most important medical issue I have.*

### Theme 2: Financial Ramifications of IBD at an Older Age

Our second theme, financial ramifications of IBD at an older age, encompasses the discussions patients had about monetary considerations. Subthemes included retirement (examining the impact of IBD on next-stage-of-life decisions), navigating insurance (finding the means to pay for a chronic disease later in life), and impact on decision making (choosing treatment paths based on affordability).

Both patients who aged with the disease and who received a new diagnosis at older ages mentioned the monetary costs of IBD, including those who were not on advanced therapies. Medications may cost thousands of dollars per month and are not universally covered by all insurances. Patients associated the frequently astronomical prices with terms like “fear” and “concern” (see **Table 2**: Financial ramifications of IBD at an older age).

#### Retirement

The financial ramifications of the disease especially at an older age, on a fixed income with government health insurance, were mentioned frequently.

> *Just the fact that I retired, I went from an employee in insurance to Medicare. So that’s a big change right there, having a lot more rules and regulations to follow. Certain drugs that may not ever get approved by Medicare, \[…\] but that makes it difficult. So I guess if I was 60 and I was thinking of retirement, and then I got diagnosed with ulcerative colitis, it would be really, really difficult.*

Multiple patients voiced frustrations about not being able to lead the kind of retired life that they worked hard for. Some had not planned for the type of large, monthly out-of-pocket costs that accompany a chronic disease. Others noted the impact that IBD has had on their plans at older ages:

> *They are not things that I could pack in a suitcase and take to Southeast Asia and backpack around in the heat…\[sic\] we’re still talking about an expensive refrigerated medication that I can’t take to say Thailand or Laos.*

#### Navigating insurance

A patient who was an insurance professional reflected passionately on the logistic burden and challenges of navigating insurance coverage transitions simply due to age (**Table 2**). Several of the patients in this cohort had recently switched to Medicare, and others that were close to retiring mentioned changing insurance as a factor in deciding when to retire. A tone of aggravation accompanied many comments about insurance:

> *I’m not happy with health insurance, I’ll tell you that. They make things very difficult a lot of times… I’ve been on this medication that the urologist has prescribed to me, and all of a sudden they don’t want to pay for it.*

#### Impact on decision making

We asked all interviewees how they make medical decisions, like choosing to begin a biologic or proceeding with surgery. All patients cited doctor’s recommendation as their primary decision motivator, but as we explored other topics, finances emerged as a leading factor in medical decision making:

> *I’ve been through \[…\] five different medications, and nothing’s worked to this point. And there was the option of trying one other one, but I made the decision not to. One, financially, I couldn’t afford it. And then there’s no guarantee that that would work either, so I decided to go with the surgery.*

Discussing subcutaneous self-administered medication options, another person noted:

> *I do have means and I can pay it, but when you’re retired, it’s a lot of money to be putting out of pocket when you think you’re doing a favor to the system by giving it to yourself. Right?*

### Theme 3: Expectations for a Meaningful Life

The third theme that we produced, expectations for a meaningful life, represents how older adults with IBD reconciled their lived experience of IBD with what they saw as a high quality of life. Subthemes included defining quality of life, highlighting what matters most, and gratitude (how older patients come to terms with their chronic disease).

#### Defining quality of life

Older adults often defined a high quality of life by the ability to do the activities that they want. Most identified spending time with family and traveling as activities they cherished. Some older adults defined a high quality of life by symptoms and only reported symptoms pertaining to their IBD.

#### Highlighting what matters most

Drawing upon the Geriatric 5Ms (mind, mobility, medications, multicomplexity, and matters most to me) framework, we also asked older adults with IBD what “matters most” to them.<sup>31</sup> Most responded that family matters most, many reported the ability to enjoy every day, and others noted autonomy and being able to live the life that they envisioned for this final stage of life.

> *To be able to do whatever I want. To have my normal routine not interrupted \[…\] I’m not talking about doing anything spectacular, just normal routine is a high quality of life.*

#### Gratitude

However, many older adults reflected with gratitude as well, noting that their age helps them appreciate life, even with a chronic disease, and provides perspective, sometimes because they have achieved what they had wanted to achieve in life.

> *\[I find\] appreciation for not so much what I don’t have but having gratitude for the things that I still do have.*

### Theme 4: Unmet Needs

The final theme we report, unmet needs, elucidates areas in which patients felt that they lacked support for their IBD. Many of the patients we interviewed highlighted areas of unmet needs as an older patient with IBD; this theme informs opportunities for further intervention. Subthemes included social isolation (being separated from family and friends because of IBD symptoms or external factors like COVID-19), busy medical providers (patients felt that they did not get enough time with their doctors), and advice on navigating daily life (desire for a packet of information with resources pertaining to older adults).

#### Social isolation

Social isolation emerged as a prominent theme across all patients. One patient mused that “being isolated from friends or family may be more of an \[adverse\] effect” than her ulcerative colitis. While many patients noted the social isolation that can accompany symptoms of IBD, some highlighted that it was not discussed as a medical issue with their treatment teams. Addressing the social ramifications of this chronic disease with their doctors was important to older patients.

> *It can really have an effect on your life \[…\] Depending on your attitude and how you handle it mentally, I mean, it could be almost debilitating where you just become antisocial, and it probably affects your work and your home life.*

#### Busy medical providers

Many patients mentioned the lack of time that medical providers have despite their best efforts. This was highlighted by one particular reflection appreciating a medical student’s efforts:

> *I had a medical student who came to my bedside and talked to me about it. He brought his medical book. He talked to me about what it was and what the future was. He sat with me for hours. He was a wonderful human being. He sat with me for hours, and he talked to me about the changes and the life-altering situation I was in.*

#### Advice on navigating daily life

Other patients would have appreciated more support from their doctor’s offices for living their daily life with this chronic condition. One patient specifically mentioned a “checklist of items” with which to travel for an emergency as one example of something that could be very helpful from their doctor’s office when they were newly diagnosed at an older age. A couple of patients hoped for more tailored activity guidance from their IBD doctors. One patient reported the need to have an “older adult IBD starter kit” with resources pertaining to IBD tailored to older adults instead of younger adults.

## Discussion

Understanding the lived experiences and reflections of older adults is very important as we care for a rising number of older adults with IBD. In this in-depth, qualitative study of older adults with IBD, we identified 4 major themes: (1) having IBD at an older age, (2) financial ramifications of IBD at an older age, (3) expectations for a meaningful life, and (4) unmet needs. As one patient astutely reflected: “having IBD is like a tree”—everything is tied to the disease somehow. The findings of this study provide a framework for moving toward patient goal-directed care for older adults with IBD.

The majority of qualitative literature in IBD to date has focused on children, younger adults, and caregivers of younger patients.<sup>14</sup> There have been informative studies on resilience and mental health in younger patients as well as in caregivers’ experiences with transitions from pediatric to adult care.<sup>32-34</sup> As demographics of IBD in the Western world are rapidly evolving, such that older adults are the most prevalent age group of people with IBD, there is an important need to increase qualitative investigation focused on older adults with IBD.<sup>35,36</sup> In-depth investigation can be a powerful tool to understand the root causes of inequities that older adults are likely to experience.<sup>37,38</sup>

Many of our interviewees experienced a malignancy or compared IBD with a malignancy during the course of their interview. An in-depth study of cancer survivors 60 years of age and older interestingly revealed many similar themes: acceptance of the condition as a chronic disease, having differential informational needs, seeking emotional support, and trying to remain positive.<sup>39</sup> The poignant reflections of older IBD patients’ interactions with clinicians and the healthcare system are informative. They highlight the need to emphasize geriatric communication skills for clinicians caring for older adults in gastroenterology training programs and as part of continuing medical education. In a study of gastroenterology trainees undergoing an objective structured clinical examination, gastroenterology trainees were most deficient in geriatric-specific communication.<sup>40</sup> Interestingly, in a mixed methods study of older Canadians with IBD, collaboration between patients and their providers was the major identified area for improvement, highlighting the acuity of this across healthcare systems.<sup>21</sup> As the proportion of older patients with IBD is rapidly rising, ensuring knowledge about age-sensitive communication will be critical and should be incorporated into training.

The social isolation conferred by IBD featured prominently in many of the interviews. Social isolation is associated with and postulated to be on a causal pathway to significant comorbidities such as cognitive decline, depression, and even death.<sup>41-43</sup> Older adults reflected that IBD compounded the social isolation already inherent in being older and even the COVID-19 pandemic—our patients specifically mentioned losing joy-giving contact and volunteer opportunities during this time. The “tied to the bathroom” mentality rendered many older adults homebound and evoked feelings of anxiety, depression, and stress. Additionally, the lack of adequate visual representation of older adults with IBD augmented the sense of isolation. Addressing social isolation in older patients with IBD specifically may be critical for the patient’s mental health and overall well-being.

Aging is often a time of transition and increased risk for functional decline. Furthermore, there is often an expectation mismatch in what patients expect to experience at older ages and what the reality is; this was noted in a large nationally representative survey and in our cohort.<sup>44</sup> Some patients voiced concern for their ability to care for themselves and lose independence due to issues with their IBD, such as caring for an ostomy. Having an incurable disease requiring expensive treatments at a time of transitions such as retirement or downsizing and living on a fixed income featured prominently on patients’ minds. Furthermore, many patients, even those in remission, noted that IBD prevented them from engaging in physical activities like playing golf or walking their dog to the same extent as they normally would without IBD. Staying active is a critical component of aging healthily.<sup>45</sup> Therefore, focusing on older-age-friendly strategies to stay active and mobile that consider IBD-specific anatomical and medical history will facilitate healthier aging.

This study highlights unique concerns of older adults with IBD that are important to providing patient-centered care. The need for an interdisciplinary approach to caring for the older patient with IBD emerged often. An “IBD Tool Kit” for older adults could include tips on navigating Medicare-type insurances, financial planning on a fixed income, travel checklists, and fun ideas for staying physically active and socially connected. As with anything, every experience cannot be anticipated comprehensively; however, this study makes it clear that older adults with IBD expect more from their IBD care than simply the decision making about medications and surgery.

This study has numerous strengths, including the robust sample size with interviews conducted until thematic saturation was reached. Another strength is that the interviews were conducted by a member not involved in their healthcare to elicit the most candid responses. As with all studies, there are limitations as well. We only recruited from patients seeking care at 1 urban tertiary care center in the New England region, which may impact the generalizability of the findings to other areas of the United States and globally. The demographics of the cohort reflect the population of older patients with IBD getting care at tertiary centers, but qualitative work should be expanded to include the increasingly diverse population of older patients with IBD. Furthermore, interviews were conducted by phone or over a zoom meeting, which may have resulted in lost opportunities to probe further based on body language.

## Conclusions

Using qualitative methodology, we identified themes and subthemes that characterize the lived experience of older adults with IBD. The findings from this study have clinical, policy, and investigative implications, which we summarize in **Table 3**. The qualitative reflections themselves may help frame clinical conversations for practitioners caring for older adults with IBD. This study, along with others, should serve as an impetus to develop dedicated geriatric communication tools for gastroenterologists. The importance of spending time with patients to deliver care that addresses patients’ priorities is acutely communicated in these data. These data can serve as evidence for advocacy agendas and identify future avenues for investigation.
