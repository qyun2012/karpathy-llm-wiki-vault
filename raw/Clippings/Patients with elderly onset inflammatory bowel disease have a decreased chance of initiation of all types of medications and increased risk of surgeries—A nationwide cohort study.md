## Summary

### Objective

In patients with elderly (≥60 years) onset inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), we studied initiation of medications, drug persistency and surgeries.

### Design

A nationwide cohort study based on Danish registries, comprising incident IBD patients ≥18 years from 1995 to 2020 (*N*  = 69,039). Patients were divided into elderly (*N*  = 19,187) and adult onset (*N*  = 49,852). Outcomes were initiation of thiopurines, 5-ASA, biologics and corticosteroids within 1 and 5 years after diagnosis, and for those who initiated medications, we estimated drug persistency. Surgeries were examined within 1 and 5 years. We used regression models controlling for covariates.

### Results

In elderly patients, the adjusted hazard ratios (aHR) for initiating thiopurines, 5-ASA and biologics within 1 year were 0.44 (95% CI 0.42–0.47), 0.77 (95% CI 0.75–0.79) and 0.29 (95% CI 0.26–0.31) respectively. The results were similar within 5 years. In elderly patients, drug persistency for thiopurines, 5-ASA and biologics was not impaired within 5 years. The aHR of stopping steroids within 1 and 5 years were 0.80 (95% CI 0.76–0.84) and 0.77 (95% CI 0.74–0.80) respectively. The risk of surgeries was increased in the elderly patients (in ulcerative colitis, within 5 years, aHR 1.39 \[95% CI 1.27–1.52\], and in Crohn's disease 1.13 \[95% CI 1.04–1.23\]).

### Conclusion

We found significantly low chance of initiation of IBD medications in elderly patients, the reason may not be due to mild disease course. In elderly patients, drug persistency was comparable to adults. Clinicians should carefully consider whether they underuse IBD-specific medications in elderly patients, and special attention should be applied to timely discontinuation of corticosteroids.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

The evidence on treatment in elderly onset IBD is scarce regarding important questions related to the appropriate medical therapeutic approach, drug persistency, surgical treatments, complications, implications of comorbidities and clinical outcomes.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0001" aria-label="Reference 1 - J Crohn's Colitis">1</a> - <a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a></sup> Typically, there is a lack of evidence of drug efficacy as elderly patients are often excluded from clinical trials.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0004" aria-label="Reference 4 - J Crohns Colitis">4</a></sup> Furthermore, most large observational studies that have been published on drug efficacy, and important clinical outcomes, have not reported separate analyses of the effects in elderly patients.

The challenges related to the treatment of elderly patients are becoming increasingly relevant as the incidence of elderly onset IBD is rapidly increasing, along with adult onset.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a> - <a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a></sup> In this context, the widely accepted definition of elderly onset IBD is disease onset at an age of 60 years or older.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0004" aria-label="Reference 4 - J Crohns Colitis">4</a></sup> Some studies have shown that elderly patients have an increased risk of discontinuation of anti-tumour necrosis factor alpha (anti-TNF alpha) agents, and underuse of immunosuppressives.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0005" aria-label="Reference 5 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">5</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0007" aria-label="Reference 7 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">7</a> - <a href="#apt17520-bib-0010" aria-label="Reference 10 - J Crohns Colitis">10</a></sup> In general, however, a significant variation in medication prescription rates has been found across countries.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0004" aria-label="Reference 4 - J Crohns Colitis">4</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0007" aria-label="Reference 7 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">7</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0011" aria-label="Reference 11 - J Crohns Colitis">11</a></sup> These variations, and discrepancies between patients with elderly and adult onset, may be a consequence of factors such as country-specific clinical guidelines, adherence to clinical guidelines, interpretation of efficacy studies, patient and physician preference, risk of side effects, drug interactions and different policies for medication coverage. In general, potential medical therapies in elderly patients are the same as in other age groups and include 5-aminosalicylic acid (5-ASA), corticosteroids, thiopurines and biologics. During the last decade, medical therapies for moderate-to-severe IBD have expanded to include not only anti-TNF alpha agents but also biological therapies such as alpha4beta7 integrin (vedolizumab), IL12/IL-23 inhibitors (ustekinumab) and recently, small molecules, that is, JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib).

We aimed to add to the evidence on treatment strategies in patients with elderly onset IBD. We examined (i) use of IBD medications (start and discontinuation of treatments), (ii) surgeries and (iii) IBD-related hospitalisations. For this purpose, we identified all incident patients with IBD from Danish nationwide health registries in the period of 1 January 1995 through 2020.

## 2 METHODS

### 2.1 Setting, design and data sources

In Denmark, the population constitutes approximately 5.6 million inhabitants (> 90% Caucasians), and all citizens have equal and free access to the tax-supported health care system. The uniform organisation of the health care system, and access to data from nationwide health registries with mandatory registration of activities, allowed us to perform a nationwide cohort study. We retrieved data from several health registries. All Danish inhabitants are assigned a unique civil registration number from the Civil Registration System, which provides the basis for linking data across all registries.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0012" aria-label="Reference 12 - Scand J Public Health">12</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0013" aria-label="Reference 13 - Eur J Epidemiol">13</a></sup> The Danish National Patient Registry (NPR) includes information on all hospital contacts of Danish inhabitants since 1977, including key data on the date of admission and discharge, information on performed procedures and diagnoses given at the hospital. From 1994 and onwards, diagnoses are coded according to the Danish version of ICD-10, and surgeries are coded according to the Danish Classification System for surgical procedures.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0014" aria-label="Reference 14 - Clin Epidemiol">14</a></sup> The Danish National Prescription Registry includes information on all redeemed prescriptions from all Danish pharmacies since 1994. The information comprises time and place of redemption, and type of medication classified according to the ATC (Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical Classification) system.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0015" aria-label="Reference 15 - Scand J Public Health">15</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0016" aria-label="Reference 16 - Int J Epidemiol">16</a></sup>

### 2.2 Study population

The study population included all patients with a first-time discharge diagnosis of ulcerative colitis (ICD-10 code DK51) or Crohn's disease (ICD-10 code DK50) registered in the NPR in the period 1 January 1995 to 31 December 2020. The study population included all patients with at least one diagnosis of IBD, and we studied the use of 5-ASA, thiopurines and corticosteroids. Biologic therapies were also studied but from 1 January 2001, where these therapies were regularly used for IBD in Denmark.

### 2.3 Exposed and unexposed cohort

The exposed cohort constituted those with elderly onset IBD (first diagnosis of IBD at age ≥60 years), and the unexposed cohort constituted those with adult-onset IBD (first diagnosis of IBD at age 18–59 years).

### 2.4 Outcomes

The examined outcomes were (i) start of first specific IBD medication, (ii) stop of the specific medical therapy, (ii) IBD surgeries and (iv) IBD hospitalisations.

Medical therapies were examined according to (1) 5-ASA, (2) thiopurines, (3) systemic corticosteroids and (4) biological therapies. Start and stop of medical treatments were examined from a 1- and 5-year perspective. 5-ASAs were identified according to ATC A07EC. Thiopurines were identified according to ATC L04AX01 and L01BB02, and procedure code BWHB83, systemic corticosteroids ATC H02AB0, H02AB2, H02AB4, H02AB6, H02AB7 and H02AB9, and biologics ATC L04AB, L04AC05 and L04AA33, and procedure codes BOHJ18A1, BOHJ18A3 BOHJ18A4, BOHJ18B3 and BOHJ19H4. In biological therapies, we included infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab, ustekinumab and vedolizumab.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0017" aria-label="Reference 17 - BMC Gastroenterol">17</a></sup>

*Start of first treatment* of 5-ASAs, thiopurines, corticosteroids or biologics was defined as the time from the first IBD diagnosis until the first occurrence of registered relevant drug treatment.

As evidence of drug persistence, in those who started a specific medical therapy, we examined the *time until stop of the specific treatment*. The time until stop of treatment was defined as the difference in days between the first and the last medical therapy in a sequence. In these analyses, to avoid the time until stop being zero, we included only patients with two or more treatments of the specific drug of interest. Stop of treatment with 5-ASAs and thiopurines was defined as a gap of more than 6 months between redeemed prescriptions/treatments. Stop of treatment with corticosteroids was defined as a gap of more than 3 months between redeemed prescriptions, and stop of biologics was defined as a gap of more than 5 months between treatments. If a stop in treatment was related to death or immigration within the given time span, we considered the stop as censoring.

*IBD surgeries* were examined within 1 and 5 years after the first diagnosis of IBD.

*IBD-related hospitalisations* were defined as hospitalisations with a primary diagnostic code for IBD, and a hospitalisation of at least 3 days duration. The frequency of hospitalisations was assessed within 1 and 5 years after the first diagnosis of IBD. The frequency of hospitalisations was divided into three groups: zero, one or two, and ≥3 hospitalisations.

### 2.5 Covariates

Information on age and sex on all individuals was retrieved from the Civil Registration System. Charlson Comorbidity Index was calculated based on the history of all individuals' information in the NPR up to 10 years before their first IBD diagnosis, and was categorised into no comorbidity (CCI 0), intermediate comorbidity (CCI 1–2) and high comorbidity (CCI 3+).<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0018" aria-label="Reference 18 - J Chronic Dis">18</a></sup> When examining the chance of starting a specific drug treatment and the risk of surgery, we took into account preceding use of other IBD medications; for instance, when estimating the chance of starting biologics, we took into account preceding treatment with thiopurines, steroids and 5-ASA.

### 2.6 Statistical analyses

We constructed contingency tables for the main study variables according to patients with elderly and adult-onset IBD. Kaplan–Meier curves of time from first registered IBD diagnosis until first treatment of interest were constructed stratified according to elderly onset and adult-onset IBD. We estimated the hazard of getting the first specific drug in patients with elderly onset IBD, relative to adult-onset IBD. Here, we used the Fine and Gray competing risk regression model,<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0019" aria-label="Reference 19 - J Am Stat Assoc">19</a></sup> estimating the sub-hazard, with 95% confidence intervals (CI), of first drug treatment under the competing risk of getting an IBD-relevant surgery prior to the first drug treatment. Adjusted analyses were performed including sex, calendar year of IBD diagnosis, CCI and preceding use of other IBD drugs, that is, in the analysis of the chance of starting biologics, we adjusted for preceding use of thiopurines (yes/no), steroids (yes/no) and 5-ASA (yes/no). Among the patients undergoing treatment of specific drugs, Kaplan–Meier curves were constructed according to drug persistence, that is, time until stop of treatment, stratified according to elderly and adult-onset IBD. We estimated the hazard ratio (HR), with 95% CI, of stopping the first treatment of interest in elderly onset IBD, compared to adult-onset IBD, using Cox regression. Adjustment was made for the same variables as mentioned above. All analyses were conducted with a follow-up of 1 and 5 years from initiation of the specific medical therapy. We examined the risk of surgeries by constructing Kaplan–Meier curves of time to first surgery within 1 and 5 years, and we estimated the HR in elderly onset IBD, compared to adult-onset IBD, using Cox regression (ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease were analysed separately). Adjustment was made for sex, calendar year of IBD diagnosis, CCI and preceding drug treatments as mentioned above. The IBD-related hospitalisations were described (numbers and proportions) within 1 and 5 years after the IBD diagnosis in the elderly and adults. To compare the frequency of IBD hospitalisations in the exposed versus the unexposed groups, a chi-squared test was made comparing zero, one or two hospitalisations with ≥3 hospitalisations.

In sub-analyses, we examined the use of newer drugs, vedolizumab, ustekinumab and tofacitinib.

In a sensitivity analysis, we required that all eligible patients had at least two diagnoses of IBD to be included in the study population.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0020" aria-label="Reference 20 - Clin Epidemiol">20</a></sup>

In sub-analyses, we stratified the analyses according to ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease. We estimated the crude and adjusted sub-hazard ratios according to start of medical treatments in elderly patients within the first year after the diagnosis of ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease. Similarly, we estimated the crude and adjusted hazard ratios according to stop of medical treatments in elderly patients with ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease within the first year after initiation of the medical therapy.

All data preparation and analyses were made using STATA 17.0 (StataCorp LP).

### 2.7 Permissions

The study was approved by the Danish Data Protection Agency (j.nr. 21/21823). This study follows all currently applicable Danish laws regarding scientific research. According to Danish law, no ethical approvals of register-based studies are necessary. There was no contact between researchers and the study population. The study was non-interventional and did not require direct patient contact.

## 3 RESULTS

The study population included 69,039 incident patients, 19,187 had elderly onset and 49,852 had adult-onset IBD. The basic characteristics of patients with elderly and adult-onset IBD are presented in Table [^1]. Among the elderly patients, 13,758 (71.7%) had ulcerative colitis and 5429 (28.3%) had Crohn's Disease. The corresponding figures among those with adult onset were 65.0% and 35.0%, respectively. As expected, more patients with elderly onset IBD had comorbidities compared to adult onset (CCI ≥3 13.3% vs. 1.6%). Within 1 year after the IBD diagnosis, those with elderly onset were treated less often with 5-ASA, thiopurines and biologics, compared to those with adult onset (5-ASA 41.9% vs. 54.9%, thiopurines 6.8% vs. 16.6% and biologics 3.6% vs. 12.6%) (Table [^1]). This was the same 5 years after the IBD diagnosis.

### 3.1 Start of medical therapies within 1 and 5 years after the IBD diagnosis in elderly patients

The start of medical therapies, within 1 and 5 years after an IBD diagnosis, is visualised in Figure [1](#apt17520-fig-0001). In general, the use of all types of IBD medications was used less often in those with elderly onset compared to adult onset. Steroids were used equally in the two cohorts.

### 3.4 IBD-related hospitalisations in patients with elderly onset IBD

The patients with elderly onset IBD had fewer IBD-related hospitalisations compared to those with adult onset (within 1 year 7.8% vs. 10.1% for ≥3 hospitalisations \[*p*  < 0.001\], and within 5 years 16.7% vs. 24.3% \[*p*  < 0.001\]).

#### 3.4.1 Sub-analyses

We could not perform analyses on ustekinumab and tofacitinib as fewer than 10 elderly patients had received these drugs, and we did not have sufficient follow-up time on these to give any meaningful results on drug persistency. The data on vedolizumab were limited. In those with elderly onset IBD who started biological treatment within the first year after diagnosis, a total of 61 (8%) started vedolizumab within 1 year after diagnosis, and 98 (19%) started vedolizumab within 5 years after IBD diagnosis. The corresponding numbers for those initiating vedolizumab in the adult population were 258 (4%) and 354 (7%) respectively. Kaplan–Meier plots for time to first treatment are presented in Figure [S1](#support-information-section).

In a sensitivity analysis, we required that all eligible patients had at least two diagnoses of IBD to be included in the study. These analyses did not change any of the main results or conclusions.

In sub-analyses, we stratified the main analyses according to ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease. Our results were robust across the stratified analyses and the overall conclusions were the same. The crude and adjusted sub-hazard ratios according to start of medical treatments in elderly patients within the first year after the diagnosis of ulcerative colitis or Crohn's disease are presented in Tables [S1](#support-information-section) and S2. ## 4 DISCUSSION

This nationwide cohort study included 19,187 incident patients with elderly onset IBD and 49,852 with adult onset. We found that elderly patients had a significantly decreased chance of initiating IBD medications (thiopurines, 5-ASA and biologics) within 1 and 5 years after the diagnosis, compared to adult onset. Initiation of thiopurines within the first year after IBD diagnosis was 56% lower in patients with elderly onset IBD than in adult onset. The corresponding figure for 5-ASA was 23% lower, and for biologics 71% lower. We also examined drug persistency, and once having started IBD medications, drug persistency in a 5-year perspective was at least as good in elderly patients as in adults. In elderly patients who started thiopurines, we found no difference in drug persistency in a 5-year perspective. Regarding drug persistency and 5-ASA, there was better persistency in elderly patients than in adults in a 5-year perspective, and regarding biologics and drug persistency, we found no differences across patients with elderly and adult onset. Regarding systemic corticosteroids, elderly patients did not have more steroid prescriptions within 1 year, and after 5 years, the use of corticosteroids was comparable between those with elderly and adult onset. Once started on steroids, however, elderly patients had a significantly decreased chance of discontinuing the treatment at 1 and 5 years. Finally, elderly patients did not have more IBD-related hospitalisations, but they had a significantly increased risk of surgeries within 1 and 5 years after the diagnosis.

To our knowledge, this study presents real-world data on the largest number of patients with elderly onset IBD. In contrast to other studies on pharmacologic therapies on elderly patients, we have not only estimated the chance of starting a specific medical therapy, but additionally, we examined drug persistency. There are no other solid data on drug persistency, and therefore our result on drug persistency cannot be compared to others. We found a surprisingly low proportion of use of 5-ASA in those with elderly onset IBD, and even lower than reported by Everhov et al (72% had 5-ASA at 1 year and 57% at 5 years).<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a></sup> There is no obvious reason for a general restricted use of 5-ASA in elderly patients. Previous studies have reported that thiopurines are less frequently used among elderly patients with IBD, compared to those with adult onset.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0005" aria-label="Reference 5 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">5</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0021" aria-label="Reference 21 - Digest Liver Dis">21</a></sup> Everhov et al (*N*  = 6443 elderly patients) reported that the use of immunomodulators after 5 years in elderly Crohn's Disease patients was 33% versus 54% in adults, and for ulcerative colitis patients, 17% for elderly and 28% for adults.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a></sup> Mak et al. reported that the cumulative probability of immunomodulatory use in elderly patients at 5 years was 20.9% versus 30% in adults.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a></sup> In our study, only 10.4% of elderly patients at 5 years had thiopurines versus 24.9% in adults. Even though previous studies have reported low use of biologics in elderly patients with IBD, we found surprisingly low use of biologics. At 5 years after diagnosis, only 5.7% of those with elderly onset had biologics versus 18.7% in adults. Studies from the Netherlands, Sweden, Italy and Hong Kong have also reported lower use of biologics among elderly patients.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0005" aria-label="Reference 5 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">5</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0021" aria-label="Reference 21 - Digest Liver Dis">21</a></sup> Everhov et al. found that after 5 years, 2% of elderly UC patients used biologics, compared with 7% of adults, and the corresponding proportions in Crohn's Disease were 6% and 20% respectively.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a></sup> Mak et al. reported that the use of biologics in elderly patients at 5 years was 1.2% versus 4.4% in adults.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a></sup> In Denmark, the low use of biologics is not related to the costs as the costs are reimbursed, and furthermore, the restraint related to malignancies is not a cause of concern as for thiopurines. We found that when biologics were first initiated, the chance of staying on biologics was the same across those with elderly and adult-onset IBD. Increased discontinuation rates of biologics have been suggested in other studies,<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0001" aria-label="Reference 1 - J Crohn's Colitis">1</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0007" aria-label="Reference 7 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">7</a></sup> but based on small cohorts and not specifically on incident patients with IBD. The use of vedolizumab was markedly higher in elderly patients compared to those with adult onset. This may not be surprising as some patients with elderly onset IBD will initiate vedolizumab because of fear of side effects from other types of biologics.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0022" aria-label="Reference 22 - Am J Gastroenterol">22</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0023" aria-label="Reference 23 - Scand J Gastroenterol">23</a></sup> Former data on corticosteroids in elderly patients have been conflicting.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0021" aria-label="Reference 21 - Digest Liver Dis">21</a></sup> We did not find an increased risk of the use of corticosteroids in elderly patients within 1 year, and at 5 years, the initiation of corticosteroids was comparable to patients with adult onset. Once initiated, however, elderly patients had a significantly lower chance of discontinuing corticosteroids. This finding is disturbing as side effects of corticosteroids are well described,<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0024" aria-label="Reference 24 - Am J Gastroenterol">24</a> - <a href="#apt17520-bib-0028" aria-label="Reference 28 - Gastroenterology">28</a></sup> and all available data indicate an even higher risk for serious adverse events with prolonged use of corticosteroids in elderly patients with IBD, compared to younger patients.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0004" aria-label="Reference 4 - J Crohns Colitis">4</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0024" aria-label="Reference 24 - Am J Gastroenterol">24</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0029" aria-label="Reference 29 - Am J Gastroenterol">29</a> - <a href="#apt17520-bib-0031" aria-label="Reference 31 - J Crohns Colitis">31</a></sup>

Obvious questions are (i) whether patients with elderly onset have more mild disease compared to patients with adult onset, and therefore are treated less intensively; (ii) whether elderly patients do have a high disease activity but are offered surgeries rather than medical therapies; (iii) whether a low use of medications such as thiopurines and biologics reflect concerns in prescribing these therapies for the elderly patients due to fear of complications and drug interactions; or (iv) whether elderly patients for other reasons are not offered appropriate IBD-specific medications. Our data cannot give answers to these questions, but several aspects can be discussed. Regarding disease activity, we have no opportunity to review all medical records on the entire Danish population of IBD patients, and it is impossible, based on nationwide data, to have precise data on the individuals' levels of disease activities. We, therefore, have to interpret our data with caution and with respect to these limitations. Regarding the severity of IBD in elderly patients, diverging results have been presented. Some have suggested a milder disease course in elderly onset,<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0008" aria-label="Reference 8 - J Crohns Colitis">8</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0009" aria-label="Reference 9 - Gut">9</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0032" aria-label="Reference 32 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">32</a></sup> while some have suggested the opposite.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0021" aria-label="Reference 21 - Digest Liver Dis">21</a></sup> Based on our data, it could be argued that elderly patients have only minor disease activity as elderly patients had fewer IBD-related hospitalisations than those with adult onset. This is, however, not necessarily true as extensive treatment can be provided in outpatient settings. We also examined medications used just after the IBD diagnosis is settled, and not just at a random point in time during the course of life with IBD. It is difficult to imagine that the low use of IBD-specific medications, applied within the first year after an IBD diagnosis in elderly patients, reflects a low degree of disease activity. Most patients experience disease activity at the debut of IBD, and most patients need medications or surgeries, or both. If low drug use in elderly patients does not reflect low disease activity, an explanation could be that clinicians may be more reticent about prescribing IBD medications to elderly patients. The reasons could be concerns related to drug interactions and fear of side effects. Regarding the use of immunomodulators in elderly patients, caution has been advised due to an increased risk of adverse effects. Concerns have been applied to lymphoma, non-melanoma skin cancer and infections.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0004" aria-label="Reference 4 - J Crohns Colitis">4</a></sup> Use of thiopurines of at least 1 year in elderly patients has thus been associated with lymphoma, with a relative risk of 4.78 in patients older than 50 years.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0033" aria-label="Reference 33 - Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol">33</a></sup> Also, advanced age and use of thiopurines, corticosteroids, and biologics have been associated with an increased risk of opportunistic infections.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0024" aria-label="Reference 24 - Am J Gastroenterol">24</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0028" aria-label="Reference 28 - Gastroenterology">28</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0034" aria-label="Reference 34 - Gastroenterology">34</a> - <a href="#apt17520-bib-0036" aria-label="Reference 36 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">36</a></sup>

Our results could also be interpreted in favour of elderly patients having a higher level of disease activity than adults. In elderly patients, we found a significantly increased risk of surgeries within 1 and 5 years after IBD diagnoses, which points towards a rather severe disease. Previous studies have reported that surgery rates are not different from those with elderly onset IBD,<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0005" aria-label="Reference 5 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">5</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0006" aria-label="Reference 6 - Jf Crohns Colitis">6</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0021" aria-label="Reference 21 - Digest Liver Dis">21</a></sup> but others have reported the opposite.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0037" aria-label="Reference 37 - Inflamm Bowel Dis">37</a></sup> Our data on surgery proportions concur with the Swedish data from Everhov et al.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0003" aria-label="Reference 3 - Gastroenterology">3</a></sup> Regarding the high risk of surgeries, an explanation could be that because of severe disease in the elderly patients, the clinicians prefer surgeries and thereby may avoid problems related to medications. It is striking that the increased risk of surgeries persists at 5 years from the diagnosis, which indeed speaks for a not insignificant level of disease activity in elderly patients. Another result could also be interpreted in favour of elderly having rather severe disease. We found that elderly patients, who started medication, actually stayed on their IBD medications in a 5-year perspective, and this speaks for the need for continuous medications to control disease activity. Finally, within 5 years from diagnosis, the need for initiating corticosteroids was the same in elderly and adults, indicating that the elderly are just as ill as the patients with adult onset. Elderly patients who initiated corticosteroids had a significantly reduced chance of discontinuing the treatment. This could indicate continuing corticosteroids demanding disease activity among the elderly, disease activity that is not well treated with prophylactic compounds or reflect that corticosteroids are not withdrawn properly among elderly patients.

The study has several strengths. It is a major strength that we had access to routinely collected nationwide data on all incident patients with IBD, which increases generalisability. We had access to valid data in the Danish registries, which are important tools for clinical epidemiological studies.<sup><a href="#apt17520-bib-0013" aria-label="Reference 13 - Eur J Epidemiol">13</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0014" aria-label="Reference 14 - Clin Epidemiol">14</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0016" aria-label="Reference 16 - Int J Epidemiol">16</a>, <a href="#apt17520-bib-0038" aria-label="Reference 38 - Clin Epidemiol">38</a></sup> We had complete prescription data for the entire Danish population of IBD patients for a period of 25 years, and it is a strength that drug information is based on registry data and not on recall, as self-reported drug use may lead to recall bias. We have complete follow-up on all individuals, and as no patients are lost during follow-up, selection bias was prevented. The large sample size enhances precision and allowed us to report exact estimates. The limitations are linked to the use of data from nationwide registries, and even though it is important to report real-world data, the study is limited by its non-randomised design. The most important limitation is lack of precise data on disease severity and activity, but in a population-based setting like this, it is not possible to collect such data. Regarding generalisability, our results may not apply to other countries with social systems and general health care systems different from the Danish.

Our study cannot determine the underlying causes of the large differences in drug therapies in elderly patients with IBD. Overall, we found a low chance of initiating IBD-specific medications after an IBD diagnosis, and several of our results suggest that this is not necessarily due to a milder course of disease. In elderly patients, who actually initiated medical therapies, drug persistency was comparable to those with adult onset in a 5-year perspective. It was disturbing to find that those with elderly onset IBD had a decreased chance of discontinuation of corticosteroids and the reason for that should be studied. Our results on medical therapies in elderly onset IBD warrant further studies.
