## Abstract

### INTRODUCTION:

Patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) are predisposed to the reactivation of viral infections such as cytomegalovirus (CMV). Clinical discrimination of disease flares and colonic CMV reactivation is difficult in patients with established diagnosis of IBD, and there are no reliable noninvasive diagnostic tools yet. Furthermore, the influence of novel therapeutics including biologicals and Janus kinase inhibitors on the risk of CMV colitis is unclear. The goal of this study was to identify risk factors and clinical determinants of CMV colitis that could serve as minimally invasive markers both for active CMV colitis and relapse.

### METHODS:

To this end, a retrospective analysis of 376 patients with suspected or confirmed CMV colitis 2016–2023 was performed.

### RESULTS:

Previous administration of systemic steroids increased the odds of CMV colitis to OR 4.6. Biologicals did not change the incidence of CMV colitis but decreased the OR of a relapse to 0.13. Clinical parameters such as severely bloody diarrhea, intense microscopic ulcerative damage, and decreased serum tryptophan correlated with detection of CMV. Importantly, persistent decrease of tryptophan was observed in patients with CMV relapse. Furthermore, tryptophan degradation through the kynurenine pathway was increased in CMV-positive patients.

### DISCUSSION:

Taken together, we identify decreased serum tryptophan as a novel potential minimally invasive marker to aid identification of IBD patients with active CMV colitis and at high risk for relapse.

**KEYWORDS:** IBD, CMV, UC, CD, tryptophan

## INTRODUCTION

Active chronic inflammatory disease such as inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) has severe effects on the daily quality of life for affected individuals and goes along with major organ complications. For patients with IBD suffering from one of the 2 main subentities, Crohn's disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC), complications range from chronic loss of nutrients, fluid and blood to development of fistula, strictures, abscessation, and peritonitis or toxic megacolon with need of surgery. Furthermore, the risk of malignancy in the inflamed tissue is increased.

Determination of IBD disease activity in patients with established diagnosis of IBD relies on a multilayered diagnostic workup, including (i) clinical, (ii) biochemical, (iii) endoscopic, and (iv) histologic assessment of disease severity. Furthermore, unspecific systemic markers of inflammations such as C-reactive protein (CRP) and blood cell count are assessed. Nonetheless, specific biomarkers of IBD disease activity allowing distinction from other intestinal pathology, such as coinciding cytomegalovirus (CMV) colitis, are lacking. The metabolic biomarker serum tryptophan is a candidate to fill this gap: Increased tryptophan degradation paralleled by decreased serum tryptophan has been observed to correlate with disease activity in IBD ([^1]). To better understand tryptophan metabolism in IBD and complications such as virus reactivation, tryptophan was assessed in all patients in this study.

CMV is a highly frequent virus in the general population with a seroprevalence of approximately 66% in western countries and latently persists in immune cells after primary infection. Immunocompromised patients are at increased risk of CMV reactivation. Local immune dysregulation and mucosal damage in the intestinal tract during IBD flares predisposes CMV replication in intestinal tissue and eventually aggravation of mucosal damage. In case of worsened symptoms such as pain, diarrhea, and bloody stools in patients with IBD, CMV reactivation causing CMV colitis has to be considered as an important differential diagnosis. CMV colitis is diagnosed by testing colonoscopic biopsies for CMV DNA and/or performing CMV immunohistochemistry on intestinal tissue samples. For specificity reasons, CMV polymerase chain reaction (PCR) of tissue specimen is preferred over blood CMV PCR, if available. Although CMV-based detection is regarded to be more sensitive than immunohistochemistry-based detection, the clinical significance of low copy numbers is unclear. In contrast to increased disease activity, which is frequently treated with intensification of immunosuppressive therapy, CMV colitis must be handled with virustatic therapy and potentially lowered immunosuppressants to support the immune system in clearing off the virus. Overall, CMV therapy is demanding, and relapses occur frequently.

Acute IBD disease flares are often resolved with corticosteroid therapy. Of all things, however, steroid treatment, especially on prolonged administration, has been associated with CMV reactivation in different clinical settings including IBD and other diseases. The effect of IBD-specific medication on the risk of gastrointestinal infections including Clostridioides difficile or reactivation of latent viral infections such as the herpesviridae CMV and varicella zoster virus is still matter of debates. Theoretically, gut-specific therapeutic targeting e.g., with the α-1-β-7-integrin antagonist vedolizumab, might influence intestinal CMV reactivation ([^2],[^3]). Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitors such as tofacitinib and filgotinib are suspected to increase the risk of herpesvirus reactivation.

So far, diagnostics of CMV colitis require invasive sampling of intestinal tissue during colonoscopy. The main goal of our study was to identify clinical determinants and prognostic factors for CMV colitis that could serve as novel minimally invasive biomarkers. The first part of this study consists of a cross sectional comparison of patients positive vs negative for CMV DNA in intestinal biopsies. In the second part, longitudinal analyses of the clinical course and therapy response of patients with positive CMV DNA were performed.

## METHODS

### Study design and patient cohort

In this study, 376 patient cases compromising CMV PCR measurement from colon biopsy specimens between 2016 and 2023 were analyzed. The study was monocentric and conducted retrospectively at University Hospital Schleswig Holstein in Kiel, which is a tertiary reference center with a large outpatient clinic for patients with IBD. The cohort combined patients who were identified for study inclusion in 2 different ways (Figure [1](#F1)). A search in the clinical information system identified 249 patients meeting all of the following parameters: (i) consented to data analyses (Broad consent Universitätsklinikum Schleswig-Holstein), (ii) diagnosed with IBD as determined by *ICD-10* diagnostic code according to International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems K 50.x/K51.x/K52.x, (iii) received ileocolonoscopy as determined by OPS-2021 (Operations and Procedures Code): 1–65, and (iv) CMV PCR was performed on intestinal biopsy. Patients lacking one of the criteria were excluded, e.g., in case of missing CMV PCR result or alternative diagnosis leading to endoscopy. Furthermore, 129 patients were identified as part of a scientific IBD-biopsy cohort. The ethics committee has approved both parts of the study (P2N-transfer request 2021-011; Scientific biopsy cohort EA B231/98).

![Figure 1.](https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/7651/11346877/ae5c9c0d7c14/ct9-15-e00731-g002.jpg)

Study cohort with suspected CMV colitis. CMV, cytomegalovirus; PCR, polyerase chain reaction.

Clinical parameters and laboratory results were exported from the clinical information system. Disease activity indices were automatically calculated. Stool frequency is given as total daily count. Stool was termed bloody if streaks of blood were reported <50% or severely bloody if blood was present in >50% of bowel movements.

### Amino acid measurement

In the main study, serum tryptophan was part of clinical routine laboratory features and determined using a high-performance liquid chromatography ([^1]). Reference range was given 48–109 μmol/L.

In the subgroup with quantification of tryptophan, kynurenine, serotonin, 3-Indoleacetic acid, Indolepropionic acid, and indoxyl sulfate, amino acids were examined using the Biocrates MxP Quant 500 XL Kit (Biocatres Life Sciences AG, Innsbruck, Austria) with a workflow as recommended by the manufacturer.

### Quantitative CMV PCR

Total DNA was isolated from biopsy specimen using the Alto Star Automation System AM 16 (altona Diagnostics GmbH, Hamburg, Germany) according to the manufacturer's instructions. PCR was performed using the Alto Star CMV PCR Kit 1.5 (altona Diagnostics GmbH), and PCR products were analyzed using Biorad CFX96 DX and CFX 3.1 software (both Bio-Rad Laboratories GmbH, Feldkirchen, Germany). This method enables detection of 250–100,000,000 IU/mL.

### Statistical analyses

Statistical analyses are primarily descriptive. For group comparisons, the χ <sup>2</sup> test was used for nominal/ordinal variables and the Student *t* -test for metric variables. Analyses were performed using the software GraphPad Prism (version 9.5.1) and Apple Number (version 11.7).

## RESULTS

### Demographics of IBD patients with suspected and confirmed CMV colitis reveal age, UC and previous CMV colitis as predominant risk factors of active CMV colitis

To characterize clinical determinants of CMV colitis, we established a database of 324 different cases of patients with IBD who underwent ileocolonoscopy with determination of CMV. The mean age of patients was 44.5 years, and the mean body mass index was 25.1 kg/m <sup>2</sup> (Table [1](#T1)). 55.9% of included patients were male. IBD entities were almost evenly distributed, with 57.7% patients with UC and 42.3% patients with CD, respectively. The mean disease duration since diagnosis was 12.0 years. 6.6% of the patients had a history of previous CMV colitis (Table [1](#T1)).

It has previously been shown that serum tryptophan levels act as a surrogate marker of severity of inflammation in IBD ([^1],[^4]). We therefore assessed serum tryptophan in patients with UC and CD and remarkably observed serum tryptophan levels to be lower in CMV-positive patients compared with CMV-negative patients with IBD. Of note, fecal calprotectin as an established biomarker of IBD disease severity was found to exhibit no detectable changes with CMV status, although it needs to be considered that fecal calprotectin was only measured in 15.7% of patients included in this study (Figure [3](#F3)).

Medical therapy included mesalazine in almost half the patients. 32.4% received biologics or JAK inhibitor therapy. There was no significant association of IBD therapy in general or specific agents such as infliximab, adalimumab, vedolizumab, ustekinumab, tofacitinib, or filgotinib with positive CMV PCR (Table [3](#T3)). However, there was a tendency toward increased risk of CMV colitis in patients from our cohort currently treated with tofacitinib. Furthermore, the relative risk of patients currently receiving systemic steroids was 3 times higher (odds ratio 5.2; Table [3](#T3)).

In conclusion, severe bloody diarrhea, especially despite administration of systemic steroids, and serum tryptophan reduction may identify patients at risk of positive CMV.

### Histopathological presentation of CMV colitis associates with augmented epithelial and ulcerative damage and depleted serum tryptophan

To further substantiate the hypothesis that CMV infection results from uncontrolled chronic mucosal inflammation, we assessed the endoscopic and histopathological scoring of mucosal biopsies from CMV-positive patients. Higher endoscopic activity scoring correlated with detection of CMV (Figure [4](#F4) a). As the majority of CMV-positive patients were patients with UC (47 UC vs 5 CD patients), we only examined the Nancy histological index for patients with UC, which is a validated score to assess disease activity: 0 denotes no or mild chronic inflammation, 1 denotes mild chronic inflammation, whereas a score of 2–4 corresponds to increasing grades of acute inflammation. Overall, we observed that the median Nancy index for CMV-positive cases was significantly higher compared with CMV-negative patients, as 75.3% of CMV-negative patients with UC exhibited acute inflammation (Nancy 2–4), compared with 90.2% in the CMV-positive subgroup (Table [4](#T4)). Most importantly, 43.9% of CMV patients exhibited severe ulcerative tissue damage in histology (Nancy 4; Table [4](#T4)). The proportion of patients with severe ulcerations was significantly lower in the CMV-negative group (Table [4](#T4) and Figure [4](#F4) b). Of note, in patients with Nancy 3 or 4, we found serum tryptophan to be significantly depleted in CMV-positive patients compared with CMV-negative patients, again implying that the amino acid can function as an independent surrogate marker of CMV colitis on high disease severity (Figure [4](#F4) c).

### Clinical course and relapse of CMV colitis correlates with serum tryptophan

Patients with UC diagnosed with CMV colitis received an acute treatment with intravenous ganciclovir for 2 weeks, according to national and international guidelines ([^5]). 9.5% of patients were diagnosed with known or suspected resistance to ganciclovir treatment and were subsequently switched to foscarnet. To understand whether clinical and laboratory features that associated with diagnosis of CMV colitis could also be utilized to assess or even predict the success of CMV colitis therapy, we retrospectively screened all patients undergoing antiviral therapy. 98.1% of patients received antiviral therapy in a hospitalized setting due to severe disease course. In this context, therapy surveillance was conducted by follow-up sigmoidoscopy and PCR quantification of mucosal CMV load around day 10 after therapy initiation. In the follow-up endoscopy, which was performed 11 days after diagnosis, 45.8% of the mucosa samples were CMV negative and CMV load significantly decreased in 50.0% of cases, oftentimes <25% of the initial quantitation result (Figure [5](#F5) a).

Stool frequencies were not significantly lowered in therapy responders than before the therapy; however, fecal blood loss decreased, although this did not directly translate into restored levels of hemoglobin (Figure [5](#F5) b). In parallel, serum tryptophan levels, which we identified to be decreased at the time point of CMV diagnosis, gradually restored (Figure [5](#F5) c). Most importantly, we observed that restoration of serum tryptophan levels at day 10 after the initiation of antiviral therapy was strongly associated with long-term viral clearance: Patients not experiencing a relapse of CMV colitis displayed significantly higher serum tryptophan levels compared with patients suffering a relapse of CMV colitis (Figure [5](#F5) c), which exhibited a persistent decrease of tryptophan at follow-up. Of note, serum tryptophan reduction did not correlate with quantitative CMV PCR.

The proportion of patients receiving biologic treatment was more than twice as high in the group without relapse, with biological therapy decreasing the odd's ratio (OR) to 0.13, and treatment with systemic steroids in comparison with the initial manifestation that did not significantly alter the risk of relapse, suggesting a supportive effect of intensified pharmacologic IBD therapy on CMV clearance (Figure [5](#F5) a).

### Tryptophan degradation through the kynurenine pathway is increased during CMV colitis

Breakdown of tryptophan can occur through different mechanisms. A majority is degraded through the kynurenine pathway. Furthermore, tryptophan can be degraded into serotonin and melatonin or, into indole derivatives, e.g., during bacterial catabolism in the gut. To better understand the mechanism of decreased tryptophan during CMV colitis and raise mechanistic hypotheses, metabolites were examined in a subgroup of patients. Kynurenine was increased in CMV-positive patients, paralleled by an increased kynurenine to tryptophan ratio (Figure [6](#F6)). Serotonin, 3-Indoleacetic acid, Indolepropionic acid, and indoxyl sulfate were not altered. Thus, tryptophan seems to be increasingly degraded through the kynurenine pathway during CMV colitis.

## DISCUSSION

In this study, we present the largest available compilation of IBD patients with suspected or confirmed colonic CMV reactivation. The study population consists of IBD patients with at least moderate clinical disease activity—this bias is unsurprising, since quantitative CMV PCR from biopsy samples is not mandatory and more likely to be tested in macroscopically inflamed mucosa. However, in contrast to stool frequency, CRP, and leukocytes, severely bloody stool paralleled by histological observation of ulcerations was found to be characteristic for CMV colitis. Interestingly, patients with UC appear at higher risk of CMV colitis than patients with colonic CD. The continuous, not just segmental, epithelial inflammation might prime a more severe epithelial barrier dysfunction and cause a locally immunocompromised milieu. In addition, the cytokine pattern driving UC pathophysiology might augment susceptibility toward viral infections ([^6],[^7]). T helper cell 2 lymphocytes, interleukin-13, and tumor-necrosis factor α, which play important roles in UC pathophysiology, in comparison with CD-related immune response are inferior in fighting viral pathogens, which might exacerbate local dysregulation of the immune system and open the window for CMV.

In parallel to other studies, we also show that steroid therapy is associated with CMV reactivation. Interestingly, the risk of relapse was not altered. Systemic steroids constitute the primary acute therapy of IBD disease flares. However, it is not possible to discriminate whether high disease activity led to steroid treatment and hence, CMV reactivation, or whether CMV reactivation necessitated steroid therapy due to worsened symptoms. Along the same line, patients treated with biologicals and high disease activity can suffer either from a nonresponse, a loss of function of their therapeutic, or from CMV colitis. In our study, targeted biological therapy did not appear to alter the risk of CMV colitis. However, JAK inhibitors here are observed to go along with an increased tendency of CMV reactivation ([^3]). This tendency might be confounded by low number of patients on JAK inhibitors or the fact that in clinical practice, this medication is often administered at a later stage of disease.

It has been previously shown that serum tryptophan was degraded in patients with IBD and its reduction correlated with disease activity ([^1],[^8]). In these studies, tryptophan was more pronouncedly reduced in patients with CD than in patients with UC ([^1]). Interestingly, CD patients less frequently suffer from CMV colitis. Serum tryptophan appears to be associated with CMV reactivation independent of disease severity, since among patients with severe ulcerative epithelial damage, serum tryptophan levels of CMV-positive patients were lower than in CMV-negative patients. The decrease in tryptophan caused by autoimmune and virus-induced inflammation apparently added to each other. Thus, lower tryptophan levels in UC, together with other parameters, may identify patients at risk of both CMV colitis and relapse. In parallel, an association of tryptophan and other amino acid catabolites and CMV reactivation has been described in transplant recipients ([^9]). The breakdown of tryptophan through the kynurenine pathway was higher in CMV-positive patients. This effect might be explained by upregulation of the key enzyme of this pathway, indoleamine-2,3-dioxygenase (IDO). Viral replication induces an interferon-γ-dominated immune response and interferon-γ-induced IDO, in turn, contributes to limiting CMV replication in epithelial cells ([^10]). The interaction of viral infection, autoimmunity, and amino acid metabolism is complex, for example, IDO induction *in vivo* inhibits T-cell activation and function ([^11]). In concordance with other observations with herpesviridae infection, restored serum tryptophan level during virostatic therapy was found to be associated with virus clearance, while persistently decreased tryptophan went along with augmented relapse frequency irrespective of confirmed therapy response by means of CMV PCR ([^12]). These kinetics of serum tryptophan might reflect both virus clearance and healing of the mucosal barrier with sustainedly low tryptophan indicating continued, unresolved damage and persistent upregulation of tryptophan-degrading enzymes ([^4],[^13]).

Notably, pharmacological targeting and restoration of tryptophan metabolism is recent matter of research in different fields ([^8],[^14]). Deciphering the pathophysiology of serum tryptophan in CMV reactivation may enable utilization of these advances in IBD.

The strength of this study is that it presents the largest available dataset to date on colonic CMV reactivation in patients with IBD. The cohort has been thoroughly phenotyped, and clinical parameters have been assessed at multiple time points. The study combines standard parameters used in state-of-the art clinical care and tryptophan as a noninvasive marker with growing evidence and potential availability in clinical routine.

A major limitation comes with the retrospective and single center design of the study. The study population was majorly European or Asian, and the results might not be generalizable to other ethnicities. It has been previously shown that tryptophan is decreased in patients with IBD compared with healthy controls, however in this study involved only IBD patients with clinical signs of inflammation. Future analyses, also including other study centers, are necessary to prove the effect. The quantitation of amino acid degradation products and calprotectin was available for a very low number of patients and should be replicated too.

## CONCLUSIONS

Noninvasive confirmation or ruling out of CMV colitis is not established yet. However, diagnosis of UC, steroid nonresponse, severely bloody stool, and low serum tryptophan may identify patients at particular risk. The study identified tryptophan as a novel marker in CMV colitis.
