A substantial increase in the incidence of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD; Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis) has been observed globally in the 21st century.

1.

Ng, SC ∙ Shi, HY ∙ Hamidi, N ∙ et al.

**Worldwide incidence and prevalence of inflammatory bowel disease in the 21st century: a systematic review of population-based studies**

*Lancet.* 2017; **390**:2769-2778

There is growing evidence for the influence of genetic and environmental factors in the pathogenesis of IBD. Diet is a promising and potentially modifiable risk factor with mounting evidence supporting its therapeutic benefit. Specifically, exclusion diets aimed at eliminating pro-inflammatory foods and restoring microbial diversity have been proposed for the induction and maintenance of remission in IBD.

At present, the only guideline-recommended nutritional therapy for induction of remission is exclusive enteral nutrition in paediatric patients with Crohn's disease.

This method of treatment, however, is not a form of maintenance therapy and effects are not sustained after the reintroduction of whole foods. Identification of sustainable dietary interventions for the prevention and treatment of IBD is increasingly a focus of research.

in *The Lancet Gastroenterology & Hepatology* assessed the use of a whole-food Crohn's disease exclusion diet (the Crohn's disease exclusion diet \[CDED\]) on induction and maintenance of remission in adults with mild-to-moderate biologic naive ileal Crohn's disease. The results of this study build on previous research supporting CDED with partial enteral nutrition for the induction of remission in paediatric patients with Crohn's disease when compared with exclusive enteral nutrition alone.

Considering the variable efficacy of partial enteral nutrition for the treatment of Crohn's disease, the efficacy of CDED monotherapy is less clear. In the trial by Yanai and colleagues, patients were randomly assigned (1:1) to either CDED with partial enteral nutrition or CDED alone, and were followed up for 24 weeks. This comparison examined the contribution of partial enteral nutrition to the success of the paediatric trial

by separating dietary exclusion from partial enteral nutrition. At week 6, 63% of all patients were in clinical remission, and 50% had sustained remission at 24 weeks. The proportion of patients who had achieved clinical remission was not significantly different between treatment groups at 6 or 12 weeks. At 24 weeks, the number of patients in the CDED with partial enteral nutrition group who had achieved remission was numerically higher than that of the CDED alone group; however, the difference between groups was not significant. In both groups, median C-reactive protein and faecal calprotectin concentrations significantly decreased between baseline and 12 weeks, and around a third of patients had achieved endoscopic remission at week 24. The findings indicate that the use of CDED with or without partial enteral nutrition was effective for induction and maintenance of remission in this cohort of patients. Assessment of composite diets such as the CDED is important since they can be incorporated into daily life. The relative efficacy of each of the included and excluded foods, however, is not clear. Since the CDED is a composite of increased and decreased amounts of various foods, whether strict adherence is essential remains uncertain. Although this study by Yanai and colleagues included the expected limitations of dietary intervention trials such as limited sample size, exclusion of patients with more advanced and complicated disease, and inability to mask participants to their assigned intervention, the incorporation of periodic rigorous assessments with biochemical and endoscopic monitoring allowed for meaningful interpretation of targeted mucosal healing.

Diet as a form of therapy has a variety of potential benefits. First, if diets are nutritionally adequate, they are safe overall. Diets do not carry the risks of potential adverse effects from immunosuppressive therapy, including infectious complications or risk of malignancy. Second, diets are more cost-effective than drug therapy. Although more nutrient dense foods can be more costly than less healthy alternatives, the currently available medical treatments are also expensive and pose financial challenges. Additionally, in contrast to medical therapies, diet therapy is not subject to disruption by burdensome authorisation policies and pharmacy-associated delays.

The consideration to incorporate diet for Crohn's disease care should be centred around patients interests, goals, and disease states. Diet for primary therapy might be considered in individuals with uncomplicated mild-to-moderate disease activity with low risk of disease progression who are highly motivated, have appropriate resources, and close clinical follow-up. Adjunctive dietary measures might be considered in all interested patients as a method of improving gastrointestinal-related symptoms and quality of life, with the potential to achieve a higher and more sustained level of remission.

The CDED is promising and paves the way for inclusion of dietary monotherapy in the treatment of Crohn's disease. Future studies incorporating larger, randomised trials with long-term follow-up are essential to guide treatment decisions and further identify which patients might derive benefit from this dietary approach.
