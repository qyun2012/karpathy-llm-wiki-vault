## Executive Summary

The demographic transformation of the global population represents the most significant structural and economic challenge to modern healthcare delivery. By 2050, the global population of individuals over the age of 65 is projected to reach 2.1 billion, with a disproportionate increase in the burden of acute and chronic gastrointestinal, hepatic, and pancreatobiliary diseases.1 The intersection of this unprecedented demographic shift with rising incidences of metabolic, inflammatory, and neoplastic digestive disorders necessitates a fundamental restructuring of gastroenterological research and clinical paradigms. Spanning a rigorous synthesis of literature, guidelines, and emerging multi-omic data from 2010 to 2026, this report executes a comprehensive scoping and horizon scanning exercise to map the trajectory of Geriatric Gastroenterology.

The traditional reactive, disease-centric model is proving systematically inadequate for the older adult population, a cohort defined by multimorbidity, polypharmacy, and high phenotypic heterogeneity.2 Current clinical guidelines persistently rely on low-quality evidence extrapolated from younger cohorts, failing to account for the unique physiological decline, altered pharmacokinetics, and distinct disease presentations intrinsic to biological aging.4 The future of digestive disease management relies on transitioning from chronological age-based protocols to functional, biologic longevity assessments facilitated by advanced molecular markers, single-cell multi-omics, and precision gerontology.6

This comprehensive report is structured across five primary analytical pillars: Domain-Specific Landscape Mapping, Evidence Gap Analysis, Critical Unmet Needs, Geriatric Gastroenterology Cross-Cutting Analysis, and a Prioritized Research Agenda. It evaluates transformative innovations—including the advent of highly selective hepatic pharmacotherapeutics, artificial intelligence in advanced endoscopy, real-time three-dimensional robotic reconstruction, and the critical integration of comprehensive geriatric assessments—while exposing critical epistemic deficits in trial design and care delivery. By synthesizing these elements, the ensuing analysis isolates high-impact, clinically meaningful, and fundable research opportunities designed to mitigate the impending burden of digestive diseases in the aging population.

## Domain-Specific Landscape Mapping

### Hepatology: The Metabolic and Viral Epidemiological Shift

The landscape of chronic liver disease in the older adult population has undergone a profound epidemiological shift over the past two decades. Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD) has emerged as the fastest-growing etiology of chronic liver disease, hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), and liver-related mortality, affecting approximately 30% to 38% of the global adult population.9 For decades, the absence of targeted pharmacotherapy rendered MASLD management entirely dependent on lifestyle interventions, which suffer from notoriously poor long-term adherence in older adults encumbered by mobility limitations, osteoarthritis, or sarcopenia.12

Furthermore, the Asian theater presents a unique diagnostic and therapeutic challenge regarding the "thin-fat" phenotype, colloquially known as lean MASLD. While Western diagnostic paradigms inextricably link MASLD and MASH to overt obesity, a substantial proportion of older Asian patients develop severe steatohepatitis despite possessing a normal body mass index (BMI).10 Driven by specific genetic polymorphisms, particularly variants in the PNPLA3 and TM6SF2 genes, alongside excess visceral adiposity and insulin resistance, lean MASLD carries equivalent or greater risks of hepatocellular carcinoma and cardiovascular mortality compared to obesity-driven MASLD.10 Consequently, traditional BMI-centric screening algorithms and primary care referral patterns systematically fail to identify high-risk non-obese elderly patients, necessitating the immediate validation of novel metabolomic and lipidomic biomarkers to detect early-stage fibrosis.20

Simultaneously, the burden of viral hepatitis-induced HCC in Asia remains exceptionally high, despite the advent of curative direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) for Hepatitis C and universal neonatal vaccination programs for Hepatitis B.22 The aging population of patients previously infected with HBV or HCV continues to harbor significant HCC risk due to irreversible epigenetic alterations and fibrotic architectural changes incurred prior to viral clearance or suppression.9 This historical viral burden is increasingly compounded by rising alcohol intake across the Asia-Pacific region and concurrent metabolic dysfunction, creating a synergistic hepatocarcinogenic environment that demands aggressive, longitudinal surveillance in the geriatric cohort.9

### Luminal Gastroenterology: The Evolution of Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD), historically conceptualized primarily as a disease of youth and early adulthood, is experiencing an unprecedented epidemiological transition toward older demographics. This shift is driven by a bimodal phenomenon: the natural aging of patients with long-standing disease and a stark, global rise in incident elderly-onset IBD (EO-IBD).24 EO-IBD represents a distinct clinical and pathophysiological entity. Compared to their adult-onset counterparts, EO-IBD patients face substantially altered disease trajectories. Data from the nationwide Epi-IIRN cohort in Israel, which matched 2,826 EO-IBD cases to non-IBD controls and adult-onset IBD patients, revealed that elderly-onset Crohn's disease patients exhibit significantly elevated risks for major abdominal surgery (adjusted hazard ratio 1.23) and increased IBD-specific mortality, despite presenting with lower risks for perianal surgery (HR 0.27).25

The pharmacological management of IBD in older adults is systematically hindered by therapeutic conservatism rooted in the legitimate fear of iatrogenic complications. Older patients exhibit a natural decline in immune function, termed immunosenescence, which is frequently exacerbated by subclinical malnutrition.29 Consequently, treatment with systemic immunosuppressants, particularly anti-TNF agents, is associated with a significantly higher risk of systemic opportunistic infections and malignancy.29 Due to these valid safety concerns, older adults are often managed with prolonged, low-dose corticosteroid courses, leading to deleterious secondary outcomes including uncontrolled disease progression, increasing polypharmacy, and accelerated bone density loss.24

Recent therapeutic advancements emphasize the prioritization of gut-selective biologics and advanced targeted therapies to mitigate these risks. ### Advanced Endoscopy, Artificial Intelligence, and Robotics

The integration of computer vision, artificial intelligence (AI), and robotic platforms has rapidly evolved from theoretical exploration to regulated clinical reality, fundamentally augmenting diagnostic accuracy and therapeutic capability in older adults. Endoscopic AI modules, specifically computer-aided detection (CADe) and computer-aided diagnosis (CADx) systems, have demonstrated profound improvements in mucosal pathology recognition. In large-sample studies encompassing over 3,000 asymptomatic patients, the implementation of AI-assisted colonoscopy increased the adenoma detection rate (ADR) to 39.9%, compared to a baseline of 32.4% achieved without AI intervention.33 This improvement was consistent across both expert and non-expert endoscopists, mitigating the risk of post-colonoscopy colorectal cancer (PCCRC).33 Advanced deep learning algorithms have also been deployed on decentralized edge computing devices, allowing real-time histological classification of all phases of gastric carcinogenesis. These models differentiate between normal, atrophic, dysplastic, and neoplastic tissues within 2 to 3 milliseconds on GPU architecture, achieving inference accuracy exceeding 93% without disrupting procedural workflow or inducing latency.34

In the therapeutic surgical domain, robotic endoscopy and minimally invasive robotic surgery are redefining the boundaries of resectability for gastrointestinal malignancies in frail patients. The application of Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) algorithms allows for real-time three-dimensional (3D) reconstruction of the intra-abdominal cavity.35 Historically, determining the spatial transformation between a robotic camera and the robotic flange required complex hand-eye calibration protocols after every instrument exchange.35 Modern SLAM algorithms utilizing feature patch extraction bypass this requirement, accurately mapping featureless mucosal surfaces and aiding in spatial navigation with root-mean-square errors (RMSE) as low as 1.3 mm to 5.2 mm, depending on image resolution.35 In highly complex procedures such as robotic pancreaticoduodenectomy and gastrectomy, this enhanced 3D visualization, combined with robotic tremor filtration and augmented dexterity, enables precise lymphadenectomy around the hepatoduodenal ligament and delicate vascular control during the dissection of superior mesenteric vessels.37 This precision significantly reduces operative trauma, limits intraoperative blood loss, and facilitates faster postoperative recovery in physiologically vulnerable older adults.37

In the assessment of advanced chronic liver disease, Endoscopic Ultrasound-guided portal pressure gradient (EUS-PPG) measurement represents a monumental clinical breakthrough. Historically, portal pressure assessment required invasive transjugular catheterization utilizing ionizing radiation.40 EUS-PPG utilizes a 25-gauge fine-needle aspiration (FNA) needle and a compact manometer to directly measure portal and hepatic venous pressures across the gastric or duodenal wall.40 Pilot and systematic cohort studies involving older adults with cirrhosis demonstrate technical success rates exceeding 94.6%, with clinical success (concordance with fibrosis staging) reaching 85.4%.40 Crucially, the safety profile is exceptionally favorable; the pooled rate of adverse events is approximately 10.9%, predominantly limited to mild abdominal pain, with zero reported severe adverse events such as perforation or fatal hemorrhage.41 EUS-PPG effectively identifies clinically significant portal hypertension (CSPH) and provides highly accurate diagnostic cutoffs, such as 11.5 mm Hg for the presence of gastroesophageal varices (sensitivity 80.3%, specificity 89.5%) and 15.75 mm Hg for the development of ascites.43 This provides an unprecedentedly safe, accurate, and direct modality to stage portal hypertension and predict decompensation in frail populations who may not tolerate traditional interventional radiology procedures.42

### Gastrointestinal Oncology: The Dissonance of Screening and Palliation

The approach to gastrointestinal oncology in older adults demands a highly nuanced calibration between aggressive preventive screening and palliative symptom management. Colorectal cancer (CRC) screening cessation remains one of the most contentious and clinically dissonant topics in geriatric gastroenterology. Universal guidelines from the United States Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF), the American College of Gastroenterology (ACG), and the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) mandate the absolute cessation of routine CRC screening at age 85, emphasizing that the procedural harms—perforation, hemorrhage, and sedation-induced cardiopulmonary complications—overwhelmingly negate any long-term survival benefit.7 For the 76-85 age cohort, guidelines dictate that screening should be strictly individualized, offered primarily to never-screened individuals with an estimated life expectancy of greater than 10 years, as the benefits of prophylactic polypectomy require approximately a decade to materialize into a tangible mortality reduction.7

Despite the clarity of these guidelines, real-world clinical practice demonstrates severe diagnostic dissonance. Current epidemiological data reveal that up to 28% of all past-year CRC screenings in older adults were performed on individuals with a calculated life expectancy of less than 10 years.7 This phenomenon peaks at an alarming 65.6% within the 80-84 age bracket.7 Patient psychology plays a significant role in this over-screening; recent surveys indicate that nearly 40% of older adults find the recommendation to halt routine screenings at age 75 entirely unacceptable, regardless of their personal health status or comorbidities.45 Conversely, massive retrospective analyses of over 123,000 propensity-matched patients indicate that first-time screening colonoscopy in robust, obese individuals over the age of 75 significantly reduces CRC incidence (OR 0.157) and all-cause mortality (OR 0.510) compared to unscreened controls, with a number needed to treat (NNT) of 227 to prevent one CRC case.46 This profound clinical paradox underscores the absolute necessity of transitioning from rigid chronological age limits to validated biological mortality indices (e.g., the Schonberg mortality index) to guide procedural decision-making, ensuring that robust octogenarians are not denied life-saving screening while frail septuagenarians are protected from iatrogenic harm.7

In the palliative oncology sphere, the management of end-stage gastrointestinal malignancies—specifically malignant bowel obstruction (MBO) and refractory malignant ascites—requires advanced multidisciplinary expertise. MBO, frequently arising from peritoneal surface malignancies, ovarian, and colorectal metastases, is initially managed through a conservative, non-operative pathway defined by Delphi consensus guidelines.48 This palliative regimen prioritizes anti-secretory agents (octreotide, scopolamine), antiemetics (metoclopramide, provided there is no severe colicky pain), and corticosteroids (dexamethasone) to control severe nausea, vomiting, and distension.48 Surgical interventions and stenting demonstrate limited benefits and high morbidity in patients with multifocal obstructions, poor performance status, or high-burden peritoneal disease, making medical management or upper GI decompression the favored approaches.49

## Evidence Gap Analysis

The contemporary literature and clinical guidelines governing geriatric gastroenterology are fundamentally compromised by structural flaws in clinical trial design and pervasive epistemological deficits, resulting in a systemic failure to adequately inform evidence-based clinical practice.

### The Extrapolation Fallacy and GRADE Discrepancies

The most critical and pervasive evidence gap is the systematic exclusion of patients over the age of 65—and unequivocally those over 75 with established multimorbidity—from pivotal randomized controlled trials (RCTs). Consequently, major societal guidelines from the British Society of Gastroenterology (BSG), the American Gastroenterological Association (AGA), the European Society of Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ESGE), and the European Association for the Study of the Liver (EASL) routinely issue recommendations for older adults based heavily on "low" or "very low" quality evidence under the GRADE (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation) methodology.4

For example, BSG and ESGE guidelines suggesting the discontinuation of surveillance for Barrett's esophagus in patients over 75 with a life expectancy under five years, or recommendations regarding the safety of prophylactic endoscopic clipping for antithrombotic management during colorectal resections, rely almost exclusively on weak recommendations derived from low-quality observational data.59 Similarly, guidance surrounding the use of CT colonography as an alternative to colonoscopy for iron deficiency anemia (IDA) in frail patients, or the stratification of acute lower gastrointestinal bleeding (LGIB) using the Oakland score, are supported only by moderate-to-low quality evidence.61 This structural void forces clinicians to extrapolate pharmacokinetic, pharmacodynamic, and surgical outcome data from younger, healthier cohorts. Such extrapolation is mathematically and biologically unsound, directly contributing to the iatrogenic cascades, adverse drug reactions, and preventable hospital readmissions that disproportionately afflict the geriatric population.4

### Pharmacokinetic and Pharmacodynamic Dimorphism

The aging digestive system exhibits pronounced physiological alterations—including reduced gastric motility, decreased intestinal enzymatic activity, diminished splanchnic blood flow, and reduced glomerular filtration rates—that dramatically alter drug absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.2 This physiological shift is heavily compounded by an often-ignored factor: sex-specific dimorphism. Older women exhibit a significantly smaller volume of distribution for lipophilic compounds, larger free fractions of circulating drugs due to altered protein binding, and slower clearance rates via hepatic glucuronidation and side-chain oxidation compared to older men.64

Consequently, older women are 50% to 75% more likely to experience severe adverse drug reactions than men.63 For instance, the first-pass metabolism of ethanol and the absorption dynamics of iron differ vastly between the sexes, and the hepatic expression of specific gastric enzymes, such as alcohol dehydrogenase, is inherently lower in females.64 Furthermore, differences in bile acid composition—males exhibit higher levels of cholic acid, while females exhibit greater concentrations of chenodeoxycholic acid—fundamentally alter the micellar solubilization and absorption of complex lipophilic drugs like cyclosporine.67

Despite these profound differences, current pharmacological guidelines in gastroenterology and hepatology rarely mandate sex-specific or age-adjusted dosing algorithms. For older women receiving narrow-therapeutic-index drugs (e.g., digoxin, specific statins, or prokinetics), the reduced clearance and altered volume of distribution frequently lead to toxic serum concentrations and heightened risks of arrhythmogenesis (e.g., torsades de pointes).63 The absence of sex-disaggregated pharmacokinetic modeling in modern clinical trials represents a profound safety gap that must be immediately rectified.66

### Expansion of Multimodal Prehabilitation Initiatives

For older adults requiring major hepatopancreatobiliary (HPB) or colorectal resections for malignancy, the incidence of postoperative complications, readmission, and delayed gastric emptying is unacceptably high. The critical unmet need lies in transitioning the surgical paradigm from reactive post-surgical rehabilitation to proactive "prehabilitation".13 Trimodal prehabilitation—encompassing targeted physical exercise to combat baseline sarcopenia, rigorous nutritional optimization, and psychological support implemented for a minimum of five to fourteen days prior to surgery—has demonstrated remarkable efficacy.13

Clinical cohorts demonstrate that outpatient prehabilitation programs significantly increase preoperative serum albumin levels, improve the prognostic nutritional index, and significantly reduce the overall post-operative length of hospital stay by an average of 1.78 days across diverse digestive cancers.13 While Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols—which include short preoperative fasting times and early mobilization—are now commonplace, standard ERAS lacks the specific, intensive physical conditioning components necessary to preserve functional independence in frail geriatric patients undergoing neoadjuvant chemoradiation prior to surgery.13 Expanding access to supervised, multidisciplinary prehabilitation is therefore a critical priority.

### Co-Management Care Delivery Models for Complex Multimorbidity

The fragmentation of subspecialty care remains a lethal structural flaw in modern health systems. Older patients presenting with Disorders of Gut-Brain Interaction (DGBI), advanced IBD, or decompensated MASH frequently possess overlapping cardiovascular, renal, and neurological comorbidities. Managing the gastrointestinal pathology in isolation inevitably leads to therapeutic cascades and dangerous polypharmacy. To combat this, the implementation of specialized, co-managed "Onco-Geriatric" or "Hepato-Geriatric" clinics is urgently required.13 These synergistic models combine the procedural and disease-specific expertise of the gastroenterologist with the holistic, polypharmacy-mitigating expertise of the geriatrician.13 Evidence indicates that dedicated geriatric co-management extending into the post-operative phase yields a notable reduction in 3-month mortality rates (odds ratio 0.58) for digestive cancer patients, systematically addressing interacting variables such as malnutrition, delirium prevention, and complex medication reconciliation.13

## Geriatric Gastroenterology Cross-Cutting Analysis

### The Microbiome-Gut-Brain Axis and Neurodegeneration

The biological aging process precipitates profound, systemic alterations in the gut microbiota, characterized by a loss of overall microbial diversity, an expansion of pathogenic enterobacteria, and a reduction in commensal strains responsible for producing critical short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs). This localized dysbiosis fundamentally disrupts the Microbiome-Gut-Brain Axis (MGBA), an intricate, bidirectional communication network linking the enteric nervous system to the central nervous system via complex neural, endocrine, metabolic, and immune pathways.73

In older adults, the disruption of the MGBA manifests clinically in the rising prevalence of somatoform gastrointestinal disorders, newly categorized under Rome IV criteria as Disorders of Gut-Brain Interaction (DGBI), including Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) and Functional Dyspepsia (FD).73 Recent transcriptomic analyses of colonic mucosal biopsies have illuminated the specific pathophysiology of these disorders. Researchers have identified pronounced Tuft cell hyperplasia and elevated secretion of the Tuft cell marker Interleukin-25 explicitly in subsets of patients with non-post-infectious diarrhea-predominant IBS (IBS-D).73 Furthermore, sex-specific differences in Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) mRNA expression in the colonic mucosa—with females exhibiting significantly higher expression than males—implicate distinct hormonal and neural signaling shifts in the female predominance of DGBI.73

More broadly, the chronic mucosal inflammation driven by age-related dysbiosis, commonly referred to as "inflammaging," has severe neurological implications. The constant release of microbial metabolites and inflammatory cytokines into systemic circulation increases blood-brain barrier permeability, fosters systemic neuroinflammation, and accelerates protein misfolding, serving as a critical exacerbating factor in the pathogenesis of neurodegenerative conditions such as Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis.74 Recognizing this profound connection, large-scale European research initiatives, such as the SmartAge network, are currently deploying longitudinal multi-omics trials to identify how interventions targeting the MGBA—through precision nutrition, physical activity, and targeted postbiotics—can not only resolve gastrointestinal symptoms but actively preserve cognitive plasticity and delay neurodegeneration in the elderly.76

### Cognitive Impairment and Procedural Efficacy

The cross-cutting impact of cognitive decline directly and measurably influences the efficacy, safety, and diagnostic yield of routine gastroenterological procedures, particularly colonoscopy. Inadequate bowel preparation (IBP) occurs in up to 25% of screening and surveillance colonoscopies, resulting in missed neoplasia, prolonged procedure times, lower cecal intubation rates, and the necessity for costly repeat interventions.78 While chronological age is frequently cited as an independent risk factor for IBP, nuanced clinical investigations utilizing validated cognitive proxies—such as the Mendes-Santos clock drawing test—indicate that underlying cognitive impairment is a primary driver of preparation failure.79

In a robust cross-sectional cohort study encompassing 2,016 participants across multiple academic centers, 21% of patients exhibited an abnormal clock drawing test.81 After controlling for age, the odds of experiencing inadequate bowel preparation were significantly higher (odds ratio 1.44) for individuals with abnormal cognitive scores compared to those with normal scores.80 This cognitive barrier is heavily compounded by socioeconomic variables, where low household income (<$40,000) correlated with a 2.48 to 2.73 times higher odds of IBP compared to higher-income brackets.79 Consequently, standardized pre-procedural protocols must evolve beyond simple written instructions. Clinics must implement routine cognitive screening prior to scheduling, mandating the utilization of modified, simplified split-dose prep regimens, intensive educational patient navigation, or direct caregiver involvement for older patients exhibiting early-stage cognitive or socioeconomic vulnerabilities.78

### Single-Cell Multi-Omics and Organoid Modeling in Precision Gerontology

The pursuit of true precision gerontology relies heavily on the integration of single-cell multi-omics and advanced three-dimensional (3D) organoid modeling. Traditional two-dimensional cell cultures and murine animal models fundamentally fail to recapitulate the complex spatial architecture, microenvironmental cues, and immense cellular heterogeneity of the aging human gastrointestinal tract.82

Organoid technology—utilizing self-organizing 3D structures derived from adult intestinal crypts, embryonic stem cells, or induced pluripotent stem cells cultured in Matrigel or synthetic hydrogels—provides an unprecedented ex vivo platform to model intestinal aging and disease pathology.82 These mini-tissues retain the precise signaling dynamics and lineage hierarchy of native organs, allowing researchers to interrogate the Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype (SASP), mitochondrial dysfunction, and epigenetic drift in a highly controlled environment.82

When coupled with single-cell transcriptomics, epigenetics, and spatial metabolomics, researchers can track the non-linear biological transitions of the aging gut with extraordinary resolution. Recent longitudinal multi-omic profiling has successfully mapped the expansion of specific exhausted immune subsets—such as CD16+ monocytes and PD-1+ expressing CD8+ T cells—that directly drive vascular inflammation and mucosal barrier breakdown in aged individuals.6 This technological nexus is critical for the high-throughput screening of novel senolytic drugs and personalized regenerative therapies, pushing therapeutic interventions past broad, systemic organism-level treatments down to precise, cell-type-specific modifications capable of restoring tissue homeostasis.82

## Prioritized Research Agenda

To effectively address the escalating burden of digestive diseases in the aging population by 2026 and beyond, international research funding bodies and clinical trial sponsors must rigidly align with the following strategic imperatives:

1. Mandatory Inclusion and Age-Stratified Trial Design: Regulatory bodies must enforce the inclusion of adults over the age of 75—explicitly including those with defined multimorbidity and polypharmacy—in Phase 3 RCTs for all novel gastrointestinal and hepatic pharmacotherapeutics (e.g., next-generation biologics, GLP-1/GIP receptor agonists, and senolytics). Trials must be statistically powered to report sex-disaggregated pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic data to inform precise, age-and-sex-adjusted dosing algorithms.
    
2. Biomarker Validation for Chronological vs. Biological Age: Significant longitudinal funding must be allocated to validate non-invasive multi-omic biomarkers capable of accurately assessing biological age and tissue senescence. These biomarkers must ultimately replace arbitrary chronological age cutoffs in determining the appropriateness of CRC screening cessation, advanced endoscopic mucosal resections, and major surgical candidacy.
    
3. Phenotypic Profiling and Diagnostics for Lean MASLD: Establish large-scale, multi-center longitudinal registries focusing specifically on the non-obese, "thin-fat" MASLD phenotype in Asian and globally dispersed populations. Research must focus on identifying actionable metabolic pathways and genetic modifiers (e.g., PNPLA3, TM6SF2) to develop reliable, non-invasive screening protocols and fibrosis estimators independent of Body Mass Index.
    
4. Digital and Robotic Autonomy in Frail Environments: Accelerate the clinical development and deployment of edge-computing AI and SLAM-based robotic endoscopy. Research should focus on utilizing these technologies to reduce procedural time, minimize the need for deep sedation, and eliminate the requirement for ionizing radiation (as demonstrated by EUS-PPG) in highly vulnerable patients with cardiopulmonary and hepatic compromise.
    
5. Prehabilitation and Onco-Geriatric Implementation Science: Fund rigorous implementation science protocols that evaluate the exact cost-effectiveness, operational feasibility, and long-term outcome benefits of universal Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) and trimodal prehabilitation pathways integrated directly within standard gastrointestinal care delivery networks.

## Critical Appraisal Commentary

The rigorous synthesis of literature spanning the past 15 years reveals a highly active but structurally fragmented research landscape within Geriatric Gastroenterology. The literature demonstrates robust, occasionally unprecedented technological innovation, particularly in the spheres of machine learning algorithms, robotic instrumentation, and single-cell multi-omic sequencing. However, the translational bridge connecting these molecular and technological advancements to pragmatic, day-to-day geriatric care remains perilously fragile.

A central epistemic weakness consistently identified across the assessed literature is the overwhelming reliance on retrospective, observational cohort studies to define care protocols for patients over the age of 80. While observational data successfully highlight essential macro-trends—such as the inherent dangers of compounding polypharmacy, the clinical paradox of CRC over-screening in patients with limited life expectancies, and the undeniable safety and efficacy of novel techniques like EUS-PPG—they are universally subject to severe selection bias. In standard clinical practice, only the most robust, physiologically intact elderly patients are selected for complex interventions or novel drug therapies. The systematic exclusion of frail, multimorbid older adults from randomized controlled trials fundamentally undermines the validity of consensus guidelines, forcing practitioners to navigate a clinical vacuum guided only by "low-quality" or "very low-quality" evidence.

Furthermore, while the theoretical understanding of the Microbiome-Gut-Brain Axis and the cellular mechanisms of senescence is advancing exponentially, the clinical literature often treats "the elderly" as a monolithic biological cohort. The reality, underscored by emerging multi-omic data and organoid models, is that biological aging is a highly individualized, non-linear trajectory driven by intersecting genetic, environmental, socio-demographic, and sex-specific variables. Future clinical research must transcend broad, arbitrary chronological age categorizations and adopt precision phenotyping. Ultimately, the successful management of the impending global digestive disease burden requires a fundamental paradigm shift: the immediate integration of foundational, evidence-based gerontology into the core curriculum, clinical pathways, and trial design of modern gastroenterology.
