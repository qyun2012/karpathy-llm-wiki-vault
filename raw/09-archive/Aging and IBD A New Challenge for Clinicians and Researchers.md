## Abstract

Evidence from recent epidemiological data suggests that the patient population with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is chronologically aging. As these individuals become older, cellular senescence leads to a state of chronic inflammation. This process, known as inflammaging, is thought to be closely linked with biological aging and may be upregulated within IBD. As a consequence, we see an increased risk of aging-related disorders within IBD. In addition, we see that frailty, which results from physiologic decline, is increasing in prevalence and is associated with adverse clinical outcomes in IBD. As such, in this review we explore the potential overlapping biology of IBD and aging, discuss the risk of aging-related disorders in IBD, and describe frailty and its relation to clinical outcomes within IBD. Finally, we discuss current considerations for clinical care and potential research avenues for further investigation.

## INTRODUCTION

Over the past decade, physicians have made significant strides in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) care. Our treatment armamentarium has increased, as has our understanding of pathophysiology.<sup>1</sup> In the next decade, however, clinicians and researchers will face a new challenge; caring for an aging patient population with IBD.

Inflammatory bowel disease, comprising ulcerative colitis (UC) and Crohn disease (CD), was previously thought to be predominantly a disease of the young. As such, in 2010, it was noted that patients aged 65 years and older represented <20% of the total patient population with IBD.<sup>2</sup> However, with no known cure, limited mortality, and a relatively stable incidence of disease, older patients are now the largest increasing patient population with IBD.<sup>3, 4</sup> Furthermore, according to a recent article by Kaplan and Windsor,<sup>5</sup> older individuals are forecast to represent more than one-third of the patient population with IBD in the coming decade.<sup>6</sup> As a result of an increasingly older patient population with IBD, IBD care will continue to evolve, requiring careful consideration of comorbidities, polypharmacy, and the safety and efficacy of therapies within this subpopulation.<sup>7-10</sup>

Moreover, to provide the highest level of care for our patients, physicians will need to further explore the relationship between ongoing inflammation and aging and the clinical consequences of biological aging within the context of IBD.

## INFLAMMAGING AND CELLULAR SENESCENCE

Although the exact pathophysiology of IBD remains elusive, a complex interplay of environmental factors, genetics, and microbial factors lead to a dysregulation of the immune system.<sup>11</sup> As intestinal epithelial cells, dendritic cells, and macrophages are exposed to inciting factors, the result is an increase in the production of inflammatory cytokines; most notably tumor necrosis factor (TNF) α, interleukin (IL)-1, IL-6, IL-18, IL-12, and IL-23.<sup>12, 13</sup> Through further activation of our innate system, tissue destruction ensues, leading to the clinical features of IBD.<sup>14</sup>

From ongoing aging research, we know that chronic stimulation of the innate immune system leads to accelerated biological aging, a process known as “inflammaging.”  <sup>15</sup> This development is thought in part to be driven by a process known as cellular senescence.<sup>16, 17</sup> As patients age, a low-grade sterile inflammation develops, leading to senescence, which is defined as a state of cell cycle arrest. The development of senescence is a means to limit the propagation of damaged cells, but its presence also exerts continued inflammatory effects.<sup>18</sup> Although these cells are not actively replicating, they are highly active metabolically, secreting a number of proinflammatory cytokines such as TNFα and IL-6.<sup>19</sup> The outcome of this process is the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP), and it can have both beneficial and deleterious effects. In the acute phase, this upregulation of the innate immune system can serve to clear damaged cells, but chronically it can also accelerate biological aging by inducing changes (and senescence) in neighboring cells, thereby propagating the senescence phenotype.<sup>17, 18, 20, 21</sup>

Cellular senescence is seen not only in advancing chronological age but also among patients with IBD. In particular, a recent study by Wang et al <sup>22</sup> showed that patients with CD had increased expression of the cellular senescence markers p16 and p21 in gastrointestinal stem and columnar cells. Additional studies have shown analogous findings, specifically that markers of biological aging such as telomere shortening and expression of the DNA damage response pathways are upregulated in certain cell lines among patients with IBD.<sup>23, 24</sup> In a 2008 study by Risques et al,<sup>23</sup> colonocytes had a shorter telomere length as compared to controls, although telomere length within colonic stromal cells did not vary. In a study of human endothelial cells, prolonged exposure to TNFα was also shown to induce cellular senescence.<sup>25</sup> Among patients with rheumatoid arthritis, similar findings have been reported, showing that CD28 T cells produce proinflammatory cytokines similar to the SASP.<sup>26</sup>

Because the percentage of older patients with IBD is increasing, both their underlying IBD and their advancing chronological age may increase exposure to the SASP.<sup>5</sup> Although senescence has beneficial effects within IBD, specifically by limiting cellular progression to carcinogenesis, its long-term presence may lead to continued inflammation and biological aging.<sup>27</sup> Further support for this hypothesis comes from a recent study by Schafer et al,<sup>28</sup> which showed that the presence of circulating SASP proteins was predictive of biological age and clinical outcomes. Within the study, a panel of 7 biomarkers assessing SASP factors was better able to predict frailty and postoperative outcomes among patients undergoing aortic valve and ovarian surgery as compared to chronological age.

## AGING-RELATED DISEASES AND IBD

Biological aging within IBD, as a consequence of both epidemiology and immunology, can have important clinical implications. For example, patients with IBD have a shorter life expectancy and an increased risk of developing aging-related diseases such as cardiovascular disease, dementia, and type 2 diabetes.<sup>29</sup>

### Mortality and Life Expectancy

In a recently published article by Kuenzig et al,<sup>30</sup> from 1996 to 2011, patients with IBD consistently had a lower life expectancy as compared with patients without IBD. Although the overall life expectancy among patients with IBD increased throughout this time, the life expectancy gap in comparison with patients without IBD actually widened. More specifically, in 2011, female patients with IBD had a 7.7-year lower life expectancy (95% confidence interval \[CI\], 7.0-8.4) than did female patients without IBD, whereas male patients with IBD had a 6.1-year lower life expectancy (95% CI, 5.3-6.9) than did male patients without IBD (Table 1).<sup>30</sup>

Although mortality studies have shown conflicting data, some studies have reported increased mortality for both patients with UC and patients with CD.<sup>31-33</sup> In a Canadian population-based study by Bitton et al,<sup>32</sup> patients with UC had a standardized mortality ratio (SMR) of 1.21 (95% CI, 1.12-1.32) and patients with CD had an SMR of 1.45 (95% CI, 1.34-1.58). In a more recent nationwide study of Taiwanese patients with IBD, similar results were seen, with patients with CD having an SMR of 3.72 (95% CI, 3.02-4.55) and patients with UC having an SMR of 1.44 (95% CI, 1.26-1.65).<sup>34</sup> Although these studies, in addition to a 2013 meta-analysis by Bewtra et al,<sup>31</sup> showed increased mortality from IBD, there have also been a number of studies that have not found an increased risk (Table 1).<sup>35-37</sup> Most recently, Aniwan et al <sup>36</sup> analyzed 895 residents of Olmstead County, Minnesota, from 1970 to 2016 and concluded that overall mortality was not significantly higher among patients with CD (hazard ratio \[HR\], 1.26; 95% CI, 0.97-1.63) or patients with UC (HR, 0.89; 95% CI, 0.70-1.14). Given these differences, further assessment through an updated meta-analysis is needed to understand the overall mortality risk that IBD confers.

### Cardiovascular Disease

As with many immune mediated diseases such as psoriasis, rheumatoid arthritis, and systemic lupus erythematosus, IBD predisposes patients to having cardiovascular disease. In a 2013 population-based study in Denmark, the risk of ischemic heart disease was increased among patients with IBD, with the highest risk seen within the first year of diagnosis.<sup>38</sup> More recently, in a cross-sectional study of more than 29 million U.S. patients, patients with IBD had higher odds of having a myocardial infarction as compared to patients without IBD (odds ratio \[OR\], 1.25; 95% CI, 1.24-1.27).<sup>39</sup> However, cross-sectional data assessing hospitalization rates as a result of myocardial infarctions have not shown similar results. In a study by Barnes et al, patients with IBD were actually less likely to be hospitalized for an acute myocardial infarction as compared to patients without IBD (OR, 0.51; 95% CI, 0.50-0.52).<sup>40</sup> One limitation of many of these studies was the inability to control for disease severity. In a Danish population-based study controlling for such factors, the risk of myocardial infarction was noted to be elevated during disease flares (relative risk \[RR\], 1.49; 95% CI, 1.16-1.93) but similar to risk in control patients when in remission (RR, 1.01; 95% CI, 0.89-1.15).<sup>41</sup>

Although not all cross-sectional studies have affirmed this elevated risk, recent meta-analyses exploring this topic have done so. In one recent meta-analysis, both patients with CD (RR, 1.24; 95% CI, 1.04-1.48) and patients with UC (RR, 1.21; 95% CI, 1.17-1.24) had an elevated relative risk of ischemic heart disease.<sup>42</sup> Another meta-analysis assessing the risk of both coronary disease (RR, 1.17; 95% CI, 1.07-1.27) and myocardial infarction (RR, 1.12; 95% CI, 1.05-1.21) found both to be elevated among patients with IBD (Table 1).<sup>43</sup> In addition, meta-analyses assessing arterial stiffness and atherosclerosis, which are both markers of cardiovascular risk, have similarly shown an increased risk among patients with IBD.<sup>44, 45</sup>

### Dementia

Although data assessing the risk of dementia in patients with IBD are limited, a recent nationwide study in Taiwan showed a significant association between IBD and the development of dementia.<sup>46</sup> In this matched cohort study controlling for dementia-related comorbidities, patients with IBD were more than twice as likely to develop dementia (HR, 2.45; 95% CI, 1.91-3.37). In addition, the onset of dementia in patients with IBD tended to occur 7 years earlier (age 76.2 years vs age 83.5 years) as compared with control patients (Table 1). On subgroup analysis, Alzheimer’s dementia, which has been most closely associated with aging, carried the highest risk among patients with IBD. As such, the overall incidence of Alzheimer’s dementia was 1.9% in patients with IBD as compared with 0.2% in patients without IBD, corresponding to an HR of 6.19 (95% CI, 3.31-11.57).<sup>46</sup>

### Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes, which is now thought to be regulated through cellular aging pathways, has also recently been shown to be associated with IBD.<sup>47-49</sup> In a United Kingdom matched cohort study by Dregan et al,<sup>49</sup> patients with UC had a higher risk of developing type 2 diabetes (HR, 1.29; 95% CI, 1.11-1.49), although patients with CD did not (HR, 0.96; 95% CI, 0.76-1.21). In a more recent nationwide study assessing this risk within the Denmark population, Jess et al <sup>48</sup> found that both patients with CD (standardized incidence ratio \[SIR\], 1.54; 95% CI, 1.47-1.67) and patients with UC (SIR, 1.57; 95% CI, 1.47-1.67) had an increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes (Table 1). On sensitivity analysis, this increased risk persisted when comparing users of corticosteroids in both the IBD and the general patient population (SIR, 3.88; 95% CI, 3.52-4.28) and additionally when comparing patients who did not use corticosteroids (SIR, 1.35; 95% CI, 1.30-1.40.<sup>48</sup>

## FRAILTY

Another consequence of biological aging is the development of frailty.<sup>50, 51</sup> As patients undergo age-related cellular changes, physiologic deficits accumulate, resulting in a state of frailty.<sup>52-54</sup> Defined as a state of decreased reserve, frailty ultimately increases susceptibility to adverse events.<sup>55</sup> In one of the first observational studies assessing this condition, Fried, Tangen, et al <sup>55</sup> found that among a population of patients aged 65 years and older, frailty was independently predictive of worsening mobility, hospitalization, and death. Since then, frailty has not only been shown to be associated with adverse clinical outcomes among older community dwellers but has also been shown to be associated with adverse outcomes in patients with chronic disease such as cirrhosis, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and AIDS.<sup>56-58</sup>

### Frailty in IBD

More recently, frailty has been shown to be associated with adverse outcomes among patients with IBD. In a study by Kochar et al in *Gastroenterology,*<sup>59</sup> the presence of frailty within 2 years of initiating immunosuppressive therapy was associated with increased odds of an infection after treatment. More specifically, of 11,001 patients with IBD assessed, frailty doubled the odds of a resulting infection after anti-TNF therapy (OR, 2.05; 95% CI, 1.07-3.93) and immunomodulator therapy (OR, 1.81; 95% CI, 1.22-2.70), even after adjusting for age and comorbidities (see Table 2).<sup>59</sup> In a separate study by Singh et al,<sup>60</sup> however, frailty was not shown to be associated with an increased risk of serious infection after initiation of anti-TNF therapy (HR, 1.03; 95% CI, 0.83-1.27), although an increased risk was seen among patients initiating vedolizumab (HR, 1.69; 95% CI, 1.03-2.79). Although unexpected, the authors conjectured that frailty may have impacted biologic choice, leading to a higher risk of infections or infection-related hospitalizations with the use of vedolizumab.

When considering mortality and hospitalization, as in other patient cohorts, frailty is independently associated with these outcomes among patients with IBD. In a follow-up study by Kochar et al in *Alimentary Pharmacology & Therapeutics,*<sup>61</sup> among the 11,001 patients with IBD, frailty was independently associated with mortality (OR, 2.90; 95% CI, 2.29–3.68) after adjusting for age, comorbidities, medication use, and other clinically relevant factors (Table 2). Similarly, in 2 nationwide studies analyzing hospitalized patients with IBD, frailty was associated with an increased likelihood of readmission and readmission mortality after hospital discharge.<sup>62, 63</sup> In Qian et al,<sup>62</sup> frail patients with IBD had a 57% higher risk of mortality (HR, 1.57; 95% CI, 1.34-1.83) and a 21% higher risk of readmission after discharge (HR, 1.21; 95% CI, 1.17-1.25). In a study by Faye et al,<sup>63</sup> analogous results were seen, with frailty increasing both the risk of readmission (RR, 1.16; 1.14-1.17) and readmission mortality (RR, 1.12; 95% CI, 1.02-1.23). On subgroup analysis, the authors also found that frailty was independently associated with hospital readmission among patients aged 60 years and older and among those who had both medical- and surgery-related hospitalizations.<sup>63</sup>

In a study specifically looking at postoperative outcomes after colectomy in UC, frailty was also associated with an increased odds of sepsis, cardiopulmonary complications, and overall morbidity (Table 2).<sup>64</sup> In a prior study, however, having at least 1 trait related to frailty was not found to be associated with major postoperative complications or length of stay after ileal pouch-anal anastomosis.<sup>65</sup> The differences may have resulted from the definitions of frailty used in each study. Notably, in the latter study, there were no subgroup analyses stratifying patients by the number of traits related to frailty.

When assessing overall length of stay, Qian et al <sup>62</sup> found that frail patients with IBD spent 4 more days in the hospital annually as compared to those who were not frail, and they incurred more hospitalization-associated costs. Similarly, in the study by Faye et al,<sup>63</sup> frail patients had a 3-day longer length of stay on readmission (9.6 vs 6.5 days) and higher overall hospitalization costs ($20,916 vs $13,539).

The impact of frailty on morbidity, mortality, and health care–related costs is of particular importance because the prevalence of frailty within the patient population with IBD seems to be increasing. In a nationwide study of hospitalized patients with IBD, the prevalence of frailty steadily increased from 10.2% to 11.5% during 2010 to 2014.<sup>63</sup> This change is likely a result of an aging patient population with IBD, which increases the risk of developing frailty.<sup>55</sup> In the follow-up study by Kochar et al,<sup>61</sup> the overall prevalence of frailty in patients with IBD increased as age increased (4% in patients aged 20–29 years vs 25% in patients aged ≥90 years). In addition, in the study by Singh et al, the prevalence of frailty approached 40% among patients with IBD starting biologics. Note that although the studies by Kochar et al <sup>61</sup> and Singh et al <sup>60</sup> used the same frailty score, the overall prevalence of frailty was markedly different between the 2 studies (5% vs 39%). This finding may be explained in part by the differing number of frailty-defining *International Classification of Diseases* codes used in each study but needs further evaluation and standardization across the field.

## CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS

As the patient population with IBD grows older, understanding the impact of biological aging within the context of IBD will be critically important.

### Clinical Care

Because physicians cannot modify the evolving epidemiologic landscape of IBD, what can we do now? Although validated prospective assessments are needed to identify frailty states within the context of a clinical visit, understanding the components of frailty can help target interventions. Whereas there are a number of different measures of frailty, one of the mostly widely used is the Fried frailty phenotype, which focuses on physical strength.<sup>55</sup> Based upon this phenotype, interventions aimed at improving core strength have been associated with both prevention and improvement of frailty in older adults.<sup>66, 67</sup> As such, recommending exercise and/or physical therapy for our older patients with IBD may be one method by which we can impact clinical outcomes. In addition, although data on dietary interventions and their impact on frailty have been mixed, adequate nutrition should be assessed during clinical visits, with referral to a nutrition specialist as needed.<sup>68, 69</sup>

Additional ways to moderate frailty include limiting age-related risks that may cause further decline in physiologic reserve. Thus, minimizing polypharmacy, assessing safety within the home to limit falls, and ensuring continuity in care are essential.<sup>69-71</sup> In close collaboration with a primary care team or geriatrician, these steps should serve as the basis of care for aging patients with IBD (Table 3).

### Future Research

Where do we go from here? To date, retrospective studies have suggested that frailty may be an important and independent predictor of clinical outcomes in IBD, but further prospective evaluation is needed. In retrospective studies, frailty is often defined once a disability or a clinical event is present or occurs.<sup>53</sup> However, to best address frail and pre-frail states, prospective measures such as strength and mobility should be assessed before clinical deficits are noted. In doing so, physicians can better characterize the at-risk patient population with IBD, assess frailty’s predictive role as a risk-stratification tool, and implement study interventions aimed at limiting its progression.

In addition, assessing how pharmacologic treatments can modulate biological aging and/or the SASP may prove to be clinically important within IBD. Although studies looking at this topic have been mostly in the context of cancer therapeutics, preliminary studies have shown that therapies used in IBD can have an impact on cellular senescence.<sup>28</sup> In a study by Prattichizzo et al,<sup>72</sup> patients with psoriasis treated using adalimumab (anti-TNFα therapy) had evidence of an attenuated senescence phenotype among circulating angiogenic cells. Similarly, JAK inhibitors have been shown to inhibit the SASP in certain cell lines.<sup>73</sup> In another study by Khare et al,<sup>74</sup> both mesalamine and azathioprine promoted senescence and the restoration of the integrity of the intestinal epithelium. Although senescence in that study was acutely induced, long-term maintenance of the epithelial barrier is integral in minimizing ongoing inflammation and may ultimately limit the presence of a chronic SASP. As pharmacotherapy continues to be studied within the context of aging and frailty, further studies specifically assessing these impacts within the context of IBD are needed (Table 3).

In summary, as the patient population with IBD continues to age, both clinicians and researchers will be faced with new challenges. Clinicians will need to become comfortable managing IBD amidst a number of aging-related disorders and must be able to both identify and manage states of frailty. Researchers, on the other hand, should focus on validating prospective measures of frailty, explore the relationship between IBD and biological aging, and assess the impact of pharmacotherapy on senescence-associated pathways.
