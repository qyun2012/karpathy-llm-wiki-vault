## ABSTRACT

### Background and Aims

Elderly-onset inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) patients (age ≥ 60 at diagnosis) have unique characteristics that require special consideration. Using a real-life registry-based cohort, we compared disease phenotypes and treatment exposures between adult-onset (18 ≤ age < 60 years) and elderly-onset IBD patients.

### Methods

Demographics, disease characteristics, and treatment were compared between adult- and elderly-onset IBD patients diagnosed during 2000–2022 with ≥ 12 months follow-up.

### Results

Of 3307 adult IBD patients, 290 (9%) were elderly-onset. This group exhibited a higher prevalence of colon-only involvement, with higher rates of ulcerative colitis (UC, 38.3% vs. 31.4%, *p* = 0.02) and more colonic L2 Crohn's Disease (CD, 21% vs. 12%, *p* < 0.001) then adult-onset group. Elderly-onset CD also showed less ileocolonic L3 disease (14% vs. 29%, *p* < 0.001), less penetrating B3 phenotype (7.4% vs. 19%, *p* < 0.001), and less perianal involvement (10% vs. 20%, *p* < 0.001). Elderly-onset CD and UC patients received more 5-ASA (36% vs. 17%, *p* < 0.001 in CD and 75% vs. 63%, *p* = 0.02 in UC). In contrast, these patients were exposed to considerably less biologics and/or JAK inhibitors (37% vs. 56% for CD and 20% vs. 35% for UC, *p* < 0.001), with higher 15-year biologic-free survival among elderly-onset IBD. First-line biological choices also substantially differed, with adult-onset receiving more anti-TNFs and elderly-onset receiving more Vedolizumab. We did not observe higher rates of IBD-related surgeries and steroid use between the groups.

### Conclusions

Elderly-onset IBD shows higher prevalences of colon-only IBD (UC and L2 CD). Treatment strategies in elderly-onset IBD favor 5-ASA and show reduced biological use, with preferences for Vedolizumab over anti-TNFs.

## 1 Introduction

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), encompassing Crohn's disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC), is a chronic condition that can affect individuals across all age groups. IBD exhibits a bimodal age distribution with two distinct peaks of onset. The first peak occurs in the second and third decades of life, and a second smaller peak occurs in the sixth and seventh decades \[[1](#ueg270092-bib-0001), [2](#ueg270092-bib-0002)\]. This elderly-onset IBD (eIBD), defined as IBD diagnosed in patients aged 60 years or older, presents unique challenges in terms of disease characteristics, management, and treatment considerations \[[3](#ueg270092-bib-0003)\]. Recent studies indicate that approximately 10%–15% of all new IBD diagnoses occur in individuals older than 60 years, and this proportion is increasing \[[4](#ueg270092-bib-0004), [5](#ueg270092-bib-0005)\].

The clinical presentation and disease course of eIBD often differ from those observed in younger patients. In elderly-onset CD (eCD), isolated colonic involvement (L2) is more common than ileocolonic (L3) disease, and the disease phenotype is predominantly inflammatory (B1), followed by stricturing (B2) and penetrating (B3) \[[4](#ueg270092-bib-0004), [6](#ueg270092-bib-0006) - [8](#ueg270092-bib-0008)\]. Elderly-onset UC (eUC) patients are more likely to present with left-sided colitis \[[6](#ueg270092-bib-0006), [7](#ueg270092-bib-0007), [9](#ueg270092-bib-0009)\]. Generally, eIBD tends to have a more indolent course, with less aggressive disease behavior and a lower likelihood of disease progression \[[1](#ueg270092-bib-0001), [4](#ueg270092-bib-0004), [10](#ueg270092-bib-0010), [11](#ueg270092-bib-0011)\]. Previous studies have shown that eIBD patients are less likely to receive immunomodulators or biological therapies compared with younger patients, often due to concerns about adverse effects \[[12](#ueg270092-bib-0012), [13](#ueg270092-bib-0013)\]. Understanding the unique features of eIBD in real-life experience is crucial for optimizing patient care and tailoring treatment strategies. We used data from an IBD registry to compare disease characteristics and treatment exposures between adult-onset (aIBD) and eIBD patients diagnosed during the years 2000–2022 to inform clinical decision-making for elderly patients with IBD.

## 2 Methods

### 2.1 Cohort

The Local Research Ethics Committee at Sheba Medical Center approved the study, and all data curation was conducted under relevant guidelines and regulations. Since we utilized registry data from electronic medical records (EMR) being used as part of routine clinical work and the lack of identifiable patient information, the local Ethical Review Board exempted the requirement for patient consent. We utilized the computerized Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) registry at Sheba Medical Center \[[14](#ueg270092-bib-0014), [15](#ueg270092-bib-0015)\], which is continuously updated in an automated manner from the hospital EMR. This registry includes all patients diagnosed with IBD who have been evaluated at Sheba Medical Center, the largest tertiary hospital in the country. The cohort consists of both referral- and population-based patients who are being monitored in the gastrointestinal unit by a multidisciplinary team adhering to established guidelines. Data from gastroenterology clinic visits are automatically captured into the registry and include patient demographics, disease type and phenotype, smoking habits, and details regarding treatment prescriptions and their duration. The accuracy and completeness of this data are reviewed weekly by dedicated physicians, while any missing information related to disease characteristics and treatments is periodically examined and corrected by a dedicated research coordinator. We retrieved the latest information on patient phenotype and smoking habits (including both active and past smoking) as indicated in the maximum follow-up. However, clinical measures of disease activity and biomarkers were not available for most patients and were consequently excluded from this analysis. The registry provides extensive information about the disease phenotype characteristics as well as longitudinal data regarding medication recommendations and prescriptions.

Patients diagnosed with IBD from the year 2000 onwards were included in this study, coinciding with the approval of biologics for IBD in Israel and the wider implementation of EMR in the hospital. A total of 3307 individuals with either CD or UC diagnosed between 2000 and 2022 were analyzed (Figure [1](#ueg270092-fig-0001)). The follow-up period for each patient was determined by the time between their last recorded visit and diagnosis, with follow-up durations summarized in Tables [1–4](#ueg270092-tbl-0001 "Link to table"). The data collected included demographic information, the Montreal classification (maximal disease extent over the follow-up period), disease phenotype, medications administered, and the duration of each medication during routine clinical follow-up. The primary comparisons were between adult-onset (18 ≤ age < 60 years) UC (aUC) and elderly-onset (age ≥ 60 at diagnosis) UC (eUC) and between adult-onset CD (aCD) and elderly-onset CD (eCD). Sankey diagrams were generated using the HoloViews package (v1.20.0) in Python to visualize the per-patient use of first- and second-line biological drugs and the transitions from the first to the second biological drug.

### 2.2 Statistical Analyses

*Descriptive statistics:* Continuous variables were assessed for normality. Variables that followed a normal distribution were described using means and standard deviations (SDs), while those with non-normal distributions were characterized using medians and interquartile ranges (IQRs). For univariate analyses, t-tests were applied to normally distributed variables, and the Mann-Whitney *U* test was used for variables that did not meet the criteria for normality. Categorical variables were reported as frequencies and percentages, with associations analyzed using the Chi-square test.

*Survival without biologics:* Survival time without biologics was calculated from the date of diagnosis to the initiation of treatment, with up to 15 years (180 months) of follow-up from diagnosis. We employed Kaplan–Meier survival analysis and Cox proportional hazards models to analyze the 15-year survival rate without biologics. Covariates such as Sex assigned at birth and follow-up time were adjusted for in the Cox proportional hazards models. Follow-up duration was calculated between the date of diagnosis and the end of follow-up defined as the earliest of either the initiation of biological treatment or the end of the follow-up period for the patient. The Cox proportional hazards model is specifically designed for survival analysis and allows for adjustments based on follow-up time and Sex assigned at birth. Hazard ratios (HRs) along with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were computed to quantify the associations between covariates and outcomes.

*Statistical Significance:* Statistical significance was determined using a two-sided alpha level of 0.05, with *p* -values below this threshold indicating statistically significant differences. Statistical analyses were performed using R (version 4.2.0) and RStudio (RStudio Team, 2020).

## 3 Results

### 3.1 Database

The study population consisted of 3307 patients with IBD, who were followed for over 12 months at the gastroenterology clinic at Sheba Medical Center, with diagnoses made between 2000 and 2022. 2247 (68%) had CD and 1060 (32%) had UC. Among these patients, 290 (9%) were diagnosed with elderly-onset IBD. The median follow-up duration was 5.8 years (interquartile range \[IQR\], 2.6–11.4) for those with CD and 6.8 years (IQR, 2.9–12.4) for those with UC. In the elderly-onset group, there was a higher fraction of UC, 38.3% (111/290 IBD), compared with only 31.4% (949/3017) UC cases in the adult-onset group (*p* = 0.02).

### 3.2 eCD Compared to aCD Presented With More Colonic L2 Disease and Less Exposure to Biological Therapies

Of the 2247 patients with CD, 179 had eCD and 2068 had aCD (Table [^1]). In total, there were 1062 (47.0%) females. The median ages at diagnosis for the eCD and aCD groups were 66 years (IQR, 62–70) and 27 years (IQR, 22–39), respectively. The median interval from diagnosis to the last follow-up was 5.5 years (IQR, 2.6–10.1) in eCD and 5.8 years (IQR, 2.6–11.5) in aCD. Smoking (current or past exposure) rates were similar: 28% in eCD and 27% in aCD. Patients with eCD compared with those with aCD presented with more colonic L2 disease (21% vs. 12%, *p* < 0.001) and less ileocolonic L3 disease (14% vs. 29%, *p* < 0.001). Additionally, patients with eCD compared with aCD presented less frequently with penetrating B3 phenotype (7.4% vs. 19%, *p* < 0.001) and with less perianal involvement (10% vs. 20%, *p* < 0.001), respectively (Table [^1]).

### 3.3 eUC Received More 5ASA and Fewer Biologics and/or JAK Inhibitors.

Of the 1060 patients with UC, 111 had eUC, and 949 had aUC (Table [^3]). The median age at diagnosis was 67 years (IQR, 64–73) for eUC and 30 years (IQR, 23–43) for aUC, and there were 50% females. The median interval from diagnosis to the last follow-up was 6.7 years in eUC (IQR, 3.4–10) and 6.9 years in the aUC (IQR, 2.9–12.8) groups. The smoking (current or past exposure) rate was higher in the eUC group than in the aUC group, 29% versus. 18% (*p* = 0.006). Although disease extent (E1/E2/E3) was similar in both age groups (Table [^3]), treatment exposures during follow-up differed significantly (Table [^4], Figure [4](#ueg270092-fig-0004)) with eUC receiving significantly more 5ASA (75% vs. 63%, *p* = 0.02) and considerably fewer biologics and/or JAK inhibitors (20% vs. 35%, *p* < 0.001). Steroid use was similar in both groups: 43% in eCD and 42% in aCD. These differences in treatment exposures in eUC versus aUC were further supported when performing Cox Regression Analysis and controlling for Sex assigned at birth; HR for 5ASA treatment was 1.4 (95% CI 1.1–1.8, *p* = 0.003), for biologics and/or JAK inhibitors treatment 0.6 (95% CI 0.4–0.9, *p* = 0.02). Kaplan–Meier curve and Cox proportional hazard analyses showed significantly higher 15-year (180 months) biologic-free survival from diagnoses among elderly-onset compared to adult-onset UC (*p* < 0.01, Figure [4](#ueg270092-fig-0004)). Similar to **e** CD versus aCD there were also differences between the types of first-line biologics received between the two groups, with aUC receiving significantly more anti-TNF class medications (63% vs. 23%, *p* < 0.001) and eCD receiving more Vedolizumab as first-line biological (68% vs. 38%, *p* < 0.001, Table [^4]). Those differences are illustrated using Sankey diagrams showing the per-patient first- and second-line biological therapies for those who started biological therapies (Figure [3C,D](#ueg270092-fig-0003)). IBD-related surgery rates were similar between the groups: 5.4% in eUC and 6.2% in aUC. E3 (extensive colitis including pancolitis) phenotype was present in 378 patients with aUC and 37 with eUC. Within this subgroup, exposures to biologics/JAK inhibitors were still higher in the aUC group than in the eUC group: 49.2% versus 29.7% (*p* = 0.02).

## 4 Discussion

This real-world registry reveals differences in disease characteristics and treatment exposures between elderly-onset and adult-onset IBD patients, providing insights into the unique features of elderly-onset IBD (eIBD). eIBD was characterized by a higher prevalence of colon-only involvement, including UC, and predominance of L2 CD, compared with adult-onset IBD, aligning with previous studies \[[4](#ueg270092-bib-0004), [6](#ueg270092-bib-0006) - [8](#ueg270092-bib-0008), [16](#ueg270092-bib-0016)\]<sup>,</sup>. Additionally, eCD patients showed lower penetrating (B3) phenotype rates and perianal involvement. While earlier studies have suggested that patients with elderly-onset ulcerative colitis (eUC) are more likely to present with left-sided colitis \[[6](#ueg270092-bib-0006), [7](#ueg270092-bib-0007), [9](#ueg270092-bib-0009)\], our data demonstrated similar patterns of UC localization (E1/E2/E3) in both age groups. Interestingly, treatment exposure between L2 eCD and eUC did not differ significantly, unlike the expected differences seen with higher biologics exposure in L2 aCD and higher 5ASA in aUC. Furthermore, steroids and IBD-related surgeries did not differ between aIBD and eIBD. Overall, these findings support the idea that despite less treatment exposure, eIBD often follows a more indolent course with less aggressive disease behavior, as previously suggested \[[1](#ueg270092-bib-0001), [4](#ueg270092-bib-0004), [10](#ueg270092-bib-0010), [11](#ueg270092-bib-0011)\].

Older patients are often underrepresented in clinical trials for IBD treatments, creating a significant knowledge gap in evidence-based medicine for this population \[[17](#ueg270092-bib-0017)\]. A systematic review of 46 randomized controlled trials for currently approved IBD medications found that only 0.9% of patients included were 65 years or older \[[18](#ueg270092-bib-0018)\]. This exclusion stems from various factors, including concerns about comorbidities and polypharmacy, the potential for increased adverse effects, and challenges in the recruitment of elderly participants. As a result, treatment decisions for eIBD patients frequently rely on observational studies and clinical experience or extrapolation from younger populations. The lack of robust clinical trial data may lead practitioners to exercise greater caution with this more vulnerable population, underscoring the need for more inclusive clinical trials and tailored treatment strategies for eIBD patients. Current guidelines suggest that the management of eIBD should be tailored to the individual patient, considering factors such as comorbidities, polypharmacy, and age-related changes in physiology \[[19](#ueg270092-bib-0019)\]. Additionally, the selection of appropriate therapies must carefully balance efficacy with potential risks, particularly concerning immunosuppressive treatments \[[20](#ueg270092-bib-0020), [21](#ueg270092-bib-0021)\]. Despite the noted shift in recent years toward biological treatments in IBD, elderly patients still receive fewer biologics compared with younger patients \[[4](#ueg270092-bib-0004), [7](#ueg270092-bib-0007), [12](#ueg270092-bib-0012), [13](#ueg270092-bib-0013), [22](#ueg270092-bib-0022)\]. A recent study by Angyal et al. found lower biological treatment exposure in eCD compared to aCD, but similar exposure was seen among UC patients \[[22](#ueg270092-bib-0022)\]. Our dataset revealed that for both CD and UC, elderly-onset patients received significantly more 5-aminosalicylates (5-ASA 36% in eCD vs. 17% in aCD and 75% in eUC vs. 63% in aUC) and fewer biologics and/or JAK inhibitors (37% in eCD vs. Fifty-six percent in aCD and 20% in eUC vs. 35% in aUC). This trend was also observed when limiting the comparison to subgroups with the more extensive disease phenotype L3 in CD and E3 in UC to examine if the treatment intensification was attributed to disease severity, and in the significantly higher 15-year biologic-free survival from diagnosis among elderly-onset compared to adult-onset IBD patients (*p* < 0.001). These treatment patterns may indicate a more conservative approach to managing eIBD, possibly due to concerns about the adverse effects of immunosuppressive therapies in older patients. Additionally, this could suggest variations in the underlying biological mechanisms and immunological responses associated with eIBD. While we cannot differentiate between these explanations, it is noteworthy that there were no significant differences in steroid exposures and IBD-related surgery rates between elderly-onset and adult-onset IBD for CD and UC, which may suggest that insufficient therapeutic exposure may not be a significant concern.

Interestingly, when biologics were initiated, there were notable differences in the selection of first-line agents between the two groups (Figure [3](#ueg270092-fig-0003)). aIBD patients received significantly more anti-TNF class medications as first-line biologics (CD: 90% vs. 20%, *p* < 0.001; UC: 63% vs. 23%, *p* < 0.001), while eIBD patients received more Vedolizumab (CD: 46% vs. 10%, *p* < 0.001; UC: 68% vs. 38%, *p* < 0.001). This preference for Vedolizumab in eIBD aligns with recent guidelines suggesting the use of biologics with lower risks of infections and malignancies in older patients, such as vedolizumab and ustekinumab, which are increasingly considered first-line options for moderate-to-severe IBD in the elderly \[[19](#ueg270092-bib-0019)\]. A review on the safety of newer IBD treatments in older patients concluded that from a safety profile perspective, vedolizumab, ustekinumab, and Risankizumab should be considered as first-line treatment options for moderate-to-severe eIBD \[[18](#ueg270092-bib-0018)\]. Furthermore, a recent study from our center evaluated ustekinumab and vedolizumab efficacy and safety in eCD, and no significant differences were observed in clinical response and remission rates between eCD and aCD. Elderly patients treated with Ustekinumab exhibited higher rates of hospitalization, but this difference did not persist over time \[[23](#ueg270092-bib-0023)\]. Similarly, a recent systematic review and meta-analysis concluded that Vedolizumab is equally safe and effective for clinical and endoscopic remission in elderly and younger populations \[[24](#ueg270092-bib-0024)\].

To better understand physicians' preferences and approaches when treating elderly patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), we conducted a brief survey among 10 IBD specialists at the Gastroenterology Department of Sheba Medical Center (questions and responses can be found in Supporting Information [S1](#support-information-section)). The survey aimed to explore the differences in treatment goals for elderly versus younger patients, the priorities in managing IBD, preferred treatment methods, and the thresholds for referring elderly patients for IBD-related surgeries. The results revealed that treatment goals for elderly patients often differ from those for younger patients, with a particular emphasis on achieving endoscopic remission in the younger group and on achieving symptom control and minimizing immunosuppression in the elderly group. Vedolizumab was identified as the most preferred treatment for elderly patients due to its favorable safety profile, while thiopurines were largely avoided. Most respondents indicated that they maintained a similar threshold for IBD-related surgery in elderly patients as they did for younger patients.

Our study's strengths lie in its relatively large cohort database, the prolonged follow-up period, and the focus on disease characteristics and treatment exposure in the recent biological era, providing a contemporary understanding of IBD management in elderly patients. However, certain limitations should be acknowledged. The single-center design may limit the generalizability of findings to other populations. Additionally, the registry lacks clinical activity measures, biomarkers, and data regarding performed imaging and endoscopic examination. For most patients, which limits our ability to assess disease severity and its impact on treatment decisions. Finally, the retrospective nature of the analysis might introduce biases, and the reliance on EMR may have impaired data completeness and accuracy.

## 5 Conclusions

This real-world registry analysis reveals that eIBD is characterized by a higher prevalence of UC and colonic-only L2 CD, with less CD B3 penetrating and perianal disease. Treatment strategies in elderly-onset IBD favor 5-ASA and show reduced biological use, with preferences for Vedolizumab versus. anti-TNFs in eIBD. As the global population ages and the incidence of eIBD rises, further research and development of specific guidelines for this patient population are crucial to optimize care and improve outcomes.
