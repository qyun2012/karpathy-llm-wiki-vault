## Summary

As the worldwide population ages, oncologists are often required to make difficult and complex decisions regarding the treatment of older people (aged 65 years and older) with cancer. Chronological age alone is often a poor indicator of the physiological and functional status of older adults, and thus should not be the main factor guiding treatment decisions in oncology. By contrast, a geriatric assessment can provide a much more comprehensive understanding of the functional and physiological age of an older person with cancer. The geriatric assessment is a multidimensional tool that evaluates several domains, including physical function, cognition, nutrition, comorbidities, psychological status, and social support. In this Series paper, we discuss the use of a geriatric assessment-based approach to cancer care, and provide clinicians with tools to better assess the risks and benefits of treatment to engage in shared decision making and provide better personalised care for older people with cancer.

This is the second in a **Series** of three papers about geriatric oncology

## Age-related physiological changes

Age-related changes have been identified in all the body's cells, tissues, organs, and systems. One of the most notable characteristics of ageing is the failure to maintain homoeostasis under conditions of physiological stress. Although many age-related physiological changes might not be relevant under normal conditions, they can become apparent when the individual is faced with physiological stressors, such as cancer and its treatment. ## Geriatric assessment in older people with cancer

In the absence of an easily measurable or precise marker of ageing, clinical tools are still the gold standard for comprehensively evaluating an older patient with cancer, and to accurately assess their individual functional and physiological status. A geriatric assessment is a complex multidimensional and multidisciplinary evaluative tool that can provide a comprehensive overview of an older individual's functional abilities, physical performance, nutritional status, comorbidities, cognition, psychological state, and social support.

### Functional status

Oncologists often measure an individual's functional status through subjective scales, such as the Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) or Karnofsky performance status scales. However, in older people, assessments that evaluate activities of daily living (ADL) and instrumental activities of daily living (IADL) are equally important because they provide additional information beyond performance status alone.

41.

Jolly, TA ∙ Deal, AM ∙ Nyrop, KA ∙ et al.

**Geriatric assessment-identified deficits in older cancer patients with normal performance status**

*Oncologist.* 2015; **20**:379-385

ADL are composed of basic self-care skills required to live independently at home, and IADL often include tasks completed when living independently in the community. In addition to ADL and IADL, objective measures of physical function, such as gait speed, grip strength, balance, and leg strength, are associated with overall mortality and clinical outcomes in older people.

A history of falls is also a notable indicator of overall functional status in an older individual. More than a third of older people aged 65 years and older fall each year, and approximately half of those falls are recurrent.

44.

Wildes, TM ∙ Dua, P ∙ Fowler, SA ∙ et al.

**Systematic review of falls in older adults with cancer**

*J Geriatr Oncol.* 2015; **6**:70-83

### Comorbidity

The incidence of comorbid medical conditions increases with age. Comorbidities have been associated with worse survival in older people with cancer, particularly among those with localised and potentially curable disease.

For instance, older people with diabetes undergoing adjuvant therapy for breast cancer have an increased rate of hospitalisation and all-cause mortality, which might be related to breast cancer undertreatment and complications of diabetes.

### Polypharmacy

The prevalence of polypharmacy among older people with newly diagnosed cancer, which can lead to a significantly increased risk of drug interactions, morbidity and adverse events, has been reported to be 80%.

57.

Prithviraj, GK ∙ Koroukian, S ∙ Margevicius, S ∙ et al.

**Patient characteristics associated with polypharmacy and inappropriate prescribing of medications among older adults with cancer**

*J Geriatr Oncol.* 2012; **3**:228-237

Polypharmacy can be defined as the use of an increased number of medications, inappropriate medication use, or medication duplication. In one study of older people with cancer beginning treatment with a new chemotherapy regimen, 48% of patients used five or more medications at the time of starting treatment. Therefore, health-care providers should assess polypharmacy in older people with cancer before starting treatment and stop the use of potentially inappropriate medications.

## Use of functional and physiological age to guide treatment

One of the main challenges of geriatric oncology is deciding whether the expected benefits and outcomes of treatment are superior to the risks.

36.

National Comprehensive Cancer Network

**Older Adult Oncology (Version I.2016)**

[https://www.nccn.org/professionals/physician\_gls/pdf/senior.pdf](https://www.nccn.org/professionals/physician_gls/pdf/senior.pdf)

Date accessed: October 1, 2016

[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=National+Comprehensive+Cancer+NetworkOlder+Adult+Oncology+%28Version+I.2016%29https%3A%2F%2Fwww.nccn.org%2Fprofessionals%2Fphysician_gls%2Fpdf%2Fsenior.pdf)

When first evaluating an older adult with cancer, clinicians should ask the following questions: what is this patient's life expectancy? What are the goals of treatment? What are this patient's preferences and values regarding cancer treatment? What is this patient's risk of developing severe treatment-related toxicities, and how will the proposed treatment affect function, independence, and quality of life? Although chronological age provides little guidance in answering these questions, the physiological and functional variables obtained from a geriatric assessment can provide more information and can help guide treatment decisions.

### Estimation of life expectancy

Many older people with cancer have concurrent medical illnesses that can affect both cancer treatment and the patient's prognosis, and failure to consider the overall life expectancy in the context of clinical decision making can lead to both undertreatment and overtreatment.

68.

Yourman, LC ∙ Lee, SJ ∙ Schonberg, MA ∙ et al.

**Prognostic indices for older adults: a systematic review**

*JAMA.* 2012; **307**:182-192

To choose treatments wisely, an estimate of life expectancy should be considered against the risk of cancer relapse or cancer-related death during this projected time period. Tools are available to predict mortality risk among older people using epidemiological data, comorbidities, functional parameters, and data obtained from the geriatric assessment ([figure 4](#fig4)). A simple, albeit imprecise, method of assessing life expectancy is life tables, such as those published by WHO ([panel](#box1)). Although life tables can provide an estimate of survival, they do not address each patient's individual characteristics and functional status. Because of this, several non-disease-specific prognostic indices, many of which include functional measures easily obtained from a geriatric assessment, have been designed and validated to more accurately predict the absolute risk of all-cause mortality in older people.

68.

**Prognostic indices for older adults: a systematic review**

### Estimation of treatment benefit

When treating older people with cancer, the goals of treatment should be clearly defined to guide shared decision making. For patients with early-stage disease, the proposed treatment will be potentially curative. By contrast, in individuals with advanced disease, the objective of treatment is to control symptoms, improve or maintain quality of life, and to prolong survival. When considering patients for curative treatment, it is important to assess the risk of cancer-related mortality against other causes of death to better understand the potential benefits of therapy. Currently, several clinical and biomolecular prediction tools are available to aid the decision-making process to offer potentially curative treatment to patients with various tumours, which should be weighed against the patient's estimated life expectancy and treatment preferences. ## Conclusions

In an ageing world, oncologists cannot rely on chronological age alone when making decisions related to the treatment of older people with cancer. Ageing is a heterogeneous process that involves all organs and systems, and individuals of the same chronological age can have striking differences in their functional and physiological status. Several biomarkers of ageing, which could provide valuable information on the physiological age of older people with cancer, are currently being investigated. Various clinical tools, which together form a geriatric assessment, can gather information on functional ability, physical health, cognition, mental health, and social circumstances that typically would not be obtained in an oncology visit, and provide a comprehensive view of the patient's functional and physiological age. A simple geriatric assessment can be performed in any clinical setting, regardless of the available resources, and provide invaluable data to guide shared treatment decision making. The information gathered through a geriatric assessment can help the clinician and the patient to assess the benefits and risks of treatment, and guide interdisciplinary interventions aimed at providing personalised care for a growing population of older patients with cancer.

Search terms used were “elderly”, “older adults”, “aging”, “geriatric assessment”, “age”, “aged”, “geriatrics”, “chronological”, “biological”, “physiological”, “biomarker”, “life expectancy”, “cancer”, “surgery”, “chemotherapy”, “radiotherapy” and their combinations. Articles and summaries were also identified by searching the authors' files and the reference lists of selected articles. We reviewed papers published in all languages. We chose final references on the basis of their relevance to the current practice of geriatric oncology.
