Although prior research has linked high UPF intake to an increased risk of IBD, the specific risk reduction associated with lower intake levels remains unclear. We therefore performed a propensity score–matched analysis within the Prospective Urban Rural Epidemiology (PURE) cohort, aiming to quantify the 20-year IBD risk associated with lower UPF consumption, with a focus on populations with high baseline intake.

The PURE study is a large, multinational prospective cohort involving 21 countries, with design and sampling methods described previously.

*Lancet.* 2017; **390**:2050-2062

[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=M.DehghanLancet390201720502062)

Participants’ baseline habitual dietary intake was documented through country-specific, validated food frequency questionnaires (FFQs). To reduce confounding, we calculated propensity scores based on age, sex, urban vs rural setting, education, body mass index, smoking status, daily energy intake, and overall diet quality via the Alternative Healthy Eating Index. Participants were matched one-to-one across categories of UPF intake, with "low UPF" defined as <1 serving/d. We used the parametric g-formula, a method for causal inference from observational data, with Monte Carlo simulation to estimate 20-year risk differences and risk ratios for developing IBD, comparing intervention groups to a “usual diet” reference group. IBD diagnoses were self-reported at follow-up visits, with new diagnoses requiring validation questionnaire completion. Subsequently, a randomly selected 20% subset was validated by medical record review. Full methodologic details are provided in [Supplementary Appendix 2](#supplementary-material).

Between January 2003 and January 2024, 124,598 participants were included in the study after excluding 23,028 who had incomplete FFQ data. Over a median follow-up period of 12.9 years, 605 incident cases of IBD were identified, comprising 497 cases of ulcerative colitis (UC) and 108 cases of Crohn's disease (CD). The primary analysis focused on 13,970 propensity score–matched participants from North America and Europe, among whom IBD developed in 218 (163 UC and 55 CD). The mean intake of UPFs in this Western cohort was 7.5 servings/d, and only 6.3% of participants consumed <1 UPF serving/d. This study was approved by the research ethics committees at all participating centers and by the Hamilton Integrated Research Ethics Board through protocol 03-206.

As summarized in [Table 1](#tbl1), consuming <1 UPF serving/d was associated with a 20-year IBD risk of 0.64% (95% confidence interval \[CI\], 0.63%–0.65%) compared with 1.21% (95% CI, 1.18%–1.25%) for the usual diet group. This corresponds to a risk ratio of 0.53 (95% CI, 0.51–0.55) and an adjusted hazard ratio (aHR) of 0.56 (95% CI, 0.54–0.58). Significant risk reductions were also observed for specific UPF subtypes. For example, consuming <1 serving/week of ultraprocessed grains was associated with a 20-year IBD risk of 0.21% (aHR 0.20), fried foods with a risk of 0.26% (aHR, 0.24), processed meat with a risk of 0.50% (aHR, 0.45), soft drinks with a risk of 0.25% (aHR, 0.23), and sweets with a risk of 0.21% (aHR, 0.19).

A clear exposure-response relationship was observed, with progressively lower IBD risk at lower levels of UPF consumption. The lowest risk (0.53%) was seen in those consuming <0.5 servings/d, which was substantially lower than the risk for those consuming 1.5 to <3 servings/d (0.95%) and ≥3 servings/d (1.24%). The findings were directionally consistent when CD and UC were analyzed separately. For CD, consuming <1 UPF serving/d was associated with an aHR of 0.46 (95% CI, 0.39–0.54); for UC, the aHR was 0.52 (95% CI, 0.49–0.55).

Time-stratified analyses demonstrated that the protective association grew stronger over longer follow-up durations (aHR of 0.62 in the first 5 years to 0.30 at 15 to 20 years in the Western cohort). A similar pattern was observed in non-Western countries. Exposure-response analyses are provided in [Supplementary Figure 1](#supplementary-material). The risk of developing CD and UC by UPF intake is detailed in [Table 1](#tbl1). Detailed time-stratified results are summarized in [Supplementary Table 1](#supplementary-material).

In this propensity-matched analysis, a diet with <1 UPF serving/d was associated with a nearly 50% lower 20-year risk of IBD in a Western cohort. This finding strengthens and extends previous research.

Although its universal feasibility is challenging, the exposure-response relationship observed suggests that any progressive reduction in UPF consumption is likely to yield benefit.

These findings have significant public health implications. A desirable target of <1 UPF serving/d may be beneficial for IBD prevention, but its feasibility may be challenging, as demonstrated by only 6.3% of participants consuming <1 UPF/d. Socioeconomic factors are a critical barrier, because UPFs are often cheaper, more convenient, and more accessible than whole and minimally processed foods. Therefore, effective prevention requires population-level strategies, such as promoting dietary guidelines that explicitly encourage UPF reduction, alongside policies that address these inequities.

Key strengths of this study include its foundation in the large, multinational PURE cohort with a long follow-up period, the use of propensity score matching, and the validation of IBD diagnoses. However, several limitations must be considered. Despite matching, this is an observational study with the potential for residual or unmeasured confounding (eg, by ethnicity), which may attenuate the observed association. Dietary intake was assessed at baseline using FFQs, which are subject to recall bias, and this analysis did not account for dietary changes over the long follow-up period. Finally, the NOVA classification of UPFs, although widely used, involves some subjectivity.

In summary, this analysis demonstrates that a lower intake of UPFs is significantly associated with a reduced 20-year risk of developing IBD in Western populations. Therefore, effective prevention requires population-level strategies, such as promoting dietary guidelines that explicitly encourage UPF reduction, alongside policies that address these inequities, which could include subsidizing minimally processed foods or taxing UPFs. Despite the challenges of implementation, these findings highlight that reducing UPF consumption is a potentially impactful public health intervention to mitigate the rising global burden of IBD.
