From Postoperative Rescue to Precision Prevention: Reframing Crohn’s Disease After Surgery Through the Immune–Ecological Remodelling Window

Abstract

Postoperative recurrence remains one of the central unsolved problems in Crohn’s disease (CD). Despite biologic advances, up to 70–80% of patients develop endoscopic recurrence within the first postoperative year, and a substantial proportion ultimately require repeat surgery. In 2019, the UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) identified major evidence gaps regarding the role of enteral nutrition (EN) in maintaining postoperative remission, particularly its benefits, risks and cost-effectiveness. Seven years later, the field has generated its first randomised postoperative EN trials, expanded microbiome and multi-omic profiling capabilities, and introduced biologic prophylaxis strategies such as vedolizumab supported by high-quality evidence. Yet these developments have not resolved the central conceptual problem: **postoperative CD continues to be managed within a fragmented therapeutic architecture in which surgery, biologics, nutrition and surveillance operate as independent interventions rather than coordinated components of a unified biological system**.

Here, we argue that postoperative CD should instead be conceptualised as a **finite, biologically decisive period** of immune–ecological instability during which microbial recolonisation, epithelial barrier repair and mucosal immune reprogramming collectively determine long-term disease trajectory. We define this period—approximately **0–6 months after resection**—as the postoperative immune–ecological remodelling window (PIERW). **[CONCEPTUAL CLARIFICATION: PIERW differs from general postoperative recovery in that during this interval, the intestine exhibits simultaneous epithelial fragility, microbial dysbiosis and immune plasticity, creating a period in which ecological and immunological interventions may durably reshape disease biology before inflammatory architecture becomes fixed.]** Within this framework, nutritional therapy functions not merely as caloric support or adjunctive therapy, but as ecosystem engineering capable of reshaping microbial and metabolic recovery after resection. We further propose a stratify–adapt–dynamic (SAD) model integrating **multimodal baseline phenotyping, continuous biomarker-driven surveillance, mechanism-matched biologic deployment, and ecological modulation**—replacing static prophylaxis with dynamic trajectory management.

This Perspective critically reassesses the NICE 2019 research agenda in light of 2019–2026 evidence. We examine why enteral nutrition produces stronger endoscopic than symptomatic benefit, **why this apparent paradox reflects differences in the biological and behavioural time scales of mucosal healing versus symptomatic recovery,** why adherence rather than toxicity has emerged as the principal limiting factor, and why conventional health technology assessment (HTA) frameworks inadequately capture the longitudinal value of integrated postoperative prevention. Finally, we outline a next-generation research agenda centred on dynamic risk modelling, mechanism-based adaptive trials, precision nutrition and patient-centred outcome integration. We argue that the future of postoperative CD management lies not in increasingly aggressive rescue strategies, but in precision prevention during the immune–ecological remodelling window.

  

Introduction: The unresolved paradox of postoperative Crohn’s disease

Intestinal resection remains unavoidable for a large proportion of patients with Crohn’s disease despite the biologic era. Contemporary cohorts continue to demonstrate that approximately half of patients require surgery within 10 years of diagnosis, while postoperative endoscopic recurrence develops in the majority of patients within the first year after ileocolonic resection. Although surgery removes macroscopic disease, it rarely restores immunological normality. The neoterminal ileum rapidly reacquires inflammatory features, often before symptoms emerge, highlighting the disconnect between clinical remission and biological recurrence. **[LOGICAL FOUNDATION: This establishes the core paradox—surgery achieves local disease elimination but permits rapid biological recurrence, suggesting that the postoperative problem is not anatomical but ecological and immunological.]**

The 2019 NICE guideline on Crohn’s disease management identified this disconnect indirectly through Recommendation NG129/1, which asked whether enteral nutrition could safely and cost-effectively maintain remission after surgery. At the time, the committee found no randomised controlled trial (RCT) evidence addressing postoperative enteral nutrition. Existing evidence was extrapolated largely from induction-remission studies or observational maintenance cohorts. Biologic evidence was also limited, heterogeneous and economically uncertain. Consequently, postoperative management became dominated by pragmatic but conceptually fragmented approaches: **thiopurine or azathioprine prophylaxis** in some centres, anti-TNF escalation in high-risk patients, surveillance endoscopy at 6–12 months, and nutritional interventions used inconsistently and often empirically. **[EVIDENCE NOTE: These fragmented approaches lack integration because they were developed independently, without a unifying biological framework.]**

Seven years later, the evidence landscape has evolved substantially. The REPREVIO trial has established vedolizumab as an effective postoperative prophylactic strategy. Randomised postoperative enteral nutrition trials have finally emerged. Multi-omic studies have characterised microbiome recolonisation patterns associated with recurrence. AI-assisted imaging and predictive modelling are increasingly feasible. Yet the central problem identified by NICE remains unresolved because **the original question was framed as a single-intervention problem ("does EN work?") rather than as a systems-level question about how to orchestrate multiple biological processes.**

**[KEY INSIGHT: The critical issue is not simply whether individual interventions work in isolation. Rather, the field must confront a deeper biological question: what determines whether the postoperative intestine re-establishes tolerance or re-enters chronic inflammation?]** The answer requires understanding the **temporal coordination** of microbial recolonisation, epithelial repair and immune reprogramming—not evaluating therapeutics independently.

We therefore propose that postoperative Crohn’s disease should be reframed as a transient but biologically decisive immune–ecological state. This state is characterised by epithelial vulnerability, microbial instability and immune plasticity following surgical resection. **Within this period—which we define as approximately 0–6 months post-resection—interventions that modify luminal ecology, mucosal immunity and barrier integrity may fundamentally and durably alter disease trajectory**, potentially before inflammatory architecture becomes progressively fixed.

We define this finite interval as the **postoperative immune–ecological remodelling window (PIERW).**

  

Surgery is not a reset: the biology of postoperative recurrence and the temporal basis of PIERW

Traditional postoperative paradigms implicitly assume that surgery returns the intestine to a near-normal baseline from which recurrence subsequently develops. However, accumulating evidence suggests that surgery instead creates a highly unstable ecological and immunological niche—the biological substrate of PIERW.

**Microbial dysbiosis and barrier dysfunction.** Immediately after ileocolonic resection, the neoterminal ileum undergoes rapid microbial recolonisation. This recolonisation is not random. Studies have demonstrated that early recurrence correlates with specific dysbiotic microbial signatures, including depletion of butyrate-producing organisms such as Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and enrichment of pro-inflammatory taxa.⁶,⁷ **[MECHANISM: These butyrate producers generate short-chain fatty acids critical for epithelial barrier integrity and immune homeostasis, and their depletion creates vulnerability to barrier dysfunction.]** Concurrently, epithelial barrier integrity remains incomplete. Tight-junction proteins are dysregulated, epithelial permeability increases, and local innate immune activation persists despite removal of visible lesions.

**Persistent immune activation despite macroscopic disease resolution.** The postoperative intestine exists in a state of incomplete immunological resolution. Histological studies demonstrate persistent plexitis, mast-cell infiltration and altered cytokine signatures at the anastomotic margin even in patients who appear clinically well.⁸ Transcriptomic analyses of resection specimens further suggest that recurrence-prone patients already possess distinct molecular programmes at the time of surgery, **[EARLY STRATIFICATION POTENTIAL: suggesting that baseline risk stratification before or immediately after surgery may be feasible].**

**Temporal dynamics: the biological window closes.** This postoperative biological state is fundamentally dynamic rather than static. During the first 3–6 months after surgery, microbial composition, epithelial repair and immune activation co-evolve in bidirectional fashion. **[CRITICAL TIMING: By 3–6 months, the system either stabilises toward homeostasis (favoured by ecological and immunological interventions) or progresses toward inflammatory entrenchment.] By 12 months, however, inflammatory architecture becomes increasingly stabilised and fibrotic remodelling may become progressive.** Once endoscopic recurrence is established and fibrotic changes develop, mesenteric immune activation and structural remodelling may become progressively more difficult to reverse. **[LOGICAL CONSEQUENCE: This temporal dynamics defines PIERW as ending roughly at 6 months—by which point either remodelling has succeeded or has become locked into recurrence.]**

**Mismatch between current practice and biological reality.** Current postoperative management strategies fail to align with this temporal biology. In routine practice, biologics are often initiated reactively after endoscopic recurrence has already been documented at 6–12 months. Surveillance relies heavily on ileocolonoscopy at 6–12 months, by which point **the critical window for preventive ecological modulation has likely already narrowed or closed.** **[IMPLEMENTATION GAP: The current standard of "prophylaxis" is actually surveillance-triggered therapy—reacting to detected recurrence rather than preventing it during PIERW.]**

The concept of PIERW reframes postoperative recurrence as a failure of remodelling—a failure to maintain ecological stability, barrier integrity and immune tolerance—rather than simply a failure of inflammatory suppression. This distinction is crucial because it implies that **successful postoperative management requires simultaneous ecological engineering, barrier support and immune modulation during the 0–6 month window, not sequential or reactive intervention.**

  

Enteral nutrition as ecosystem engineering during PIERW

The original NICE research recommendation implicitly treated enteral nutrition as a pharmacological substitute—an alternative maintenance therapy to be compared against immunomodulators or biologics in head-to-head trials. This framing fundamentally misunderstood the unique biological role of nutritional therapy, which operates through ecological rather than immunosuppressive mechanisms.

**Mechanism: ecological restructuring, not immunosuppression.** Exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN) does not act primarily through immunosuppression. Instead, it reshapes luminal ecology by functioning as a form of ecosystem engineering. Formula-based feeding reduces exposure to dietary emulsifiers, food additives and poorly characterised antigenic compounds that may destabilise epithelial integrity and microbial composition. Concurrently, EEN modifies bile acid metabolism, short-chain fatty acid production and microbial substrate availability, creating conditions favorable to butyrate-producing commensals. **[CRITICAL DISTINCTION: This ecological mechanism explains why EEN may be particularly effective during PIERW—when the microbiota is unstable and recolonizing, external restructuring via formula may have outsized impact compared to induction therapy in established disease.]**

The emerging literature suggests that the therapeutic signal of enteral nutrition derives less from calorie provision than from ecological restructuring.

**Why endoscopic benefit exceeds symptomatic benefit—a resolved paradox.** This mechanism helps explain several otherwise puzzling observations that have confused the field. First, EEN often achieves stronger endoscopic than symptomatic benefit. **[EXPLANATION: Endoscopic recurrence reflects mucosal biology, microbial interaction and epithelial injury directly, integrating the ecological processes that EN targets. Symptoms, by contrast, emerge only when advanced inflammation develops or stricturing occurs, and remain influenced by psychological and behavioural factors independent of mucosal healing.]** This divergence is not evidence against biological efficacy—it indicates that EN is working on mucosal/ecological outcomes while behavioural factors limit symptomatic improvement.

Second, partial enteral nutrition (PEN) appears to exhibit a dose–response relationship: the greater the proportion of calories replaced by formula, the larger the apparent reduction in recurrence risk. This finding is far more consistent with ecological modulation than simple nutritional supplementation, because a "dose" of ecology-altering compounds would be expected to scale with exposure.

**Mechanistic evidence from recent trials.** Recent postoperative trials reinforce this interpretation. The randomised trial combining azathioprine with three months of EEN demonstrated substantial reductions in endoscopic recurrence compared with azathioprine alone. However, symptomatic outcomes were less impressive, and quality of life deteriorated during the EEN phase. **This divergence is not evidence against biological efficacy; rather, it exposes a fundamental tension: mechanistic benefit (mucosal healing, ecological restoration) operates on a longer time scale than symptomatic recovery, and the behavioural burden of EEN temporarily worsens quality of life even as mucosal healing progresses.**

**The principal limitation: adherence, not toxicity.** Indeed, the principal limitation of postoperative EEN has emerged not as toxicity but as adherence. Unlike thiopurines or systemic corticosteroids, enteral nutrition has minimal intrinsic physiological toxicity. Serious adverse events are uncommon and generally reflect underlying disease activity rather than nutritional intervention itself. However, prolonged liquid-only diets impose substantial psychosocial burden, particularly in adults. Social isolation, altered eating behaviour and reduced treatment acceptability contribute to discontinuation. **[CRITICAL IMPLICATION: The NICE 2019 “risk” question asked whether EN was safe—but the dominant risk proved to be implementation failure, not pharmacological harm.]**

**Evolutionary strategies: balancing potency and sustainability.** Consequently, the field has shifted toward less restrictive ecological strategies. Cyclic EEN, PEN-based maintenance approaches and Crohn’s Disease Exclusion Diet (CDED)-based protocols attempt to preserve microbial modulation while improving tolerability. Importantly, these strategies align more closely with the concept of ecosystem engineering than with conventional drug substitution because they recognize that **effective ecological therapies must be sustainable over the PIERW duration (0–6 months).**

**Evaluation framework for precision nutrition.** Future nutritional therapy should therefore be evaluated according to three dimensions simultaneously:

1. **Ecological potency**: capacity to reshape dysbiotic microbiota and restore barrier-supporting commensals.
2. **Behavioural sustainability**: feasibility of long-term adherence during the PIERW period.
3. **Integration with immune-directed therapy**: potential synergy with concurrent biologic and barrier-supportive strategies.

**[STRUCTURAL NOTE: These three dimensions define what makes a nutritional strategy "precision nutrition"—not merely individualizing nutrition to baseline phenotype, but matching intervention potency to the achievable adherence window while optimizing synergy with concurrent immune therapy.]**

  

Beyond “top-down” versus “step-up”: toward mechanism-matched prophylaxis

The biologic era has transformed postoperative management but has also exposed new conceptual weaknesses.

The REPREVIO trial established vedolizumab as an effective strategy for preventing postoperative endoscopic recurrence. Anti-TNF therapy remains highly effective, particularly when initiated early in high-risk patients. Ustekinumab and other biologics have shown promise in observational studies.

Yet the key unanswered question is no longer whether biologics work. **[LOGICAL PROGRESSION: Universal efficacy is established; now the problem is optimization—how to initiate therapy early enough to prevent damage while avoiding unnecessary treatment in potentially self-healing phenotypes.]** 

The critical questions are: **when should biologics be initiated (early universal prophylaxis vs risk-stratified escalation), in whom (phenotype-specific targeting), and according to which biological logic (what mechanism is driving recurrence in each patient)?**

**The prophylaxis dilemma.** This creates a fundamental tension: Universal postoperative biologic prophylaxis risks overtreatment, unnecessary immunosuppression and escalating healthcare expenditure. **[COST-EFFECTIVENESS: If 70% of patients develop recurrence anyway, universal prophylaxis treats all to prevent recurrence in ~50%, implying substantial unnecessary exposure.]** Conversely, delayed biologic initiation (reacting only after endoscopic recurrence is documented) may permit irreversible transmural injury and fibrotic remodelling during the period of clinically silent recurrence—essentially allowing PIERW to progress unchecked during a months-long surveillance period.

**Why conventional risk stratification fails.** Current risk stratification tools remain inadequate for resolving this dilemma. Traditional models rely heavily on smoking status, penetrating phenotype, previous resections and Rutgeerts scoring. Although clinically useful, these systems were developed before contemporary biologic strategies and **fail to capture molecular and biological heterogeneity—the notion that different patients require different mechanisms of intervention.** A patient with barrier-dominant dysbiosis may respond better to vedolizumab + nutritional modulation, while one with Th1/Th17 activation may need anti-TNF. Static phenotyping misses this specificity.

**Toward multimodal biological phenotyping.** Recent years have seen rapid expansion of multimodal predictive models incorporating:

- plasma microRNA signatures;
- transcriptomic profiling (barrier genes, immune activation modules, fibrotic pathways);
- mucosa-associated microbiota composition;
- inflammatory indices;
- histopathological markers such as plexitis, mast-cell density, and fibrotic features;
- AI-derived imaging features.

**[EVIDENCE INTEGRATION: Studies⁸⁻¹⁰ collectively demonstrate that these multimodal features stratify recurrence risk better than clinical variables alone, and—critically—identify mechanistically distinct subtypes within the postoperative population.]** These studies collectively suggest that postoperative recurrence is biologically heterogeneous—not a monolithic outcome but a consequence of distinct biological failure modes.

**The three mechanistic phenotypes of PIERW.** Some patients appear primarily **barrier-dominant**, characterised by epithelial permeability defects, tight-junction dysregulation and depletion of protective commensals—recurrence driven by ecological failure rather than immune hyperactivation. Others exhibit **immune-dominant** phenotypes with systemic Th1/Th17-driven immune activation, elevated inflammatory markers and mesenteric inflammation despite intact mucosal architecture. Still others demonstrate **fibrosis-dominant** phenotypes characterised by upregulation of profibrotic gene modules, mesenchymal cell activation and early evidence of submucosal remodelling.

This heterogeneity implies that biologic selection should become mechanism-matched rather than empirically sequential. **[THERAPEUTIC LOGIC: A barrier-dominant patient benefits from gut-selective therapy + ecological support; an immune-dominant patient from systemic immune suppression; a fibrosis-dominant patient from structural surveillance and future anti-fibrotic therapy.]** One-size-fits-all prophylaxis inevitably under-treats some phenotypes and over-treats others.

**The stratify–adapt–dynamic (SAD) framework: operationalizing precision prevention.** We propose a three-pillar approach that differs fundamentally from both "universal prophylaxis" and "reactive surveillance" paradigms.

**Pillar 1: Baseline phenotypic stratification.** At the time of surgery (or immediately post-resection), patients undergo integrated multimodal assessment combining:
- clinical phenotype (disease behavior, smoking, prior resections);
- surgical characteristics (anastomotic type, specimen quality);
- histopathology (plexitis, mast-cell density, fibrotic features, epithelial architecture);
- transcriptomics (barrier-function genes, Th1/Th17 modules, TGF-β/fibrotic pathways);
- microbiota composition (diversity, butyrate-producer abundance, dysbiotic signatures);
- biomarker signatures (CRP, fecal calprotectin, plasma markers).

**[IMPLEMENTATION NOTE: This baseline assessment should occur before discharge or within 2 weeks post-operatively, positioning patients within PIERW while ecological and immunological plasticity remains highest.]**

**Pillar 2: Dynamic adaptation during PIERW.** Rather than fixed treatment assignment, recurrence probability is continuously updated using longitudinal data streams:
- fecal calprotectin trajectories (are biomarkers declining, stable or rising?);
- intestinal ultrasound at 3 and 6 months (transmural thickening, mesenteric changes);
- therapeutic drug monitoring (vedolizumab, anti-TNF levels and target engagement);
- AI-assisted endoscopic assessment (automated Rutgeerts scoring, tissue remodelling detection);
- microbiome evolution (are butyrate-producers recovering or remaining depleted?).

**[ADAPTIVE LOGIC: Pre-specified thresholds trigger escalation (dose intensification, class-switching) or de-escalation (spacing/withdrawal), creating a closed-loop control system that mirrors clinical reasoning but operationalizes it systematically.]**

**Pillar 3: Mechanism-directed intervention.** Treatment selection and intensification are matched to dominant remodelling phenotype:
- **Barrier-dominant**: vedolizumab (gut-selective trafficking blockade) + cyclic/partial enteral nutrition or CDED (ecological restructuring) + barrier-supportive measures.
- **Immune-dominant**: anti-TNF or ustekinumab (broad systemic suppression) ± thiopurine augmentation, with close monitoring of inflammatory trajectory.
- **Fibrosis-dominant**: standard biologic prophylaxis + intensive structural surveillance (3-monthly ultrasound, early therapeutic endoscopy) + consideration of investigational anti-fibrotic agents once available.

**[CONCEPTUAL SHIFT: This reframes postoperative therapy from "static prophylaxis" (give everyone the same drug) toward "adaptive trajectory management" (adjust intervention continuously based on evolving biology).]**

  

Nutrition–biologic synergy: the emerging frontier

One of the most underexplored concepts in postoperative CD is the interaction between nutrition and biologics.

Historically, nutritional therapy and biologics have been studied in parallel rather than together. Yet biologic response is profoundly influenced by baseline inflammatory state, barrier integrity and microbial composition.

This raises a central possibility: nutritional modulation may improve biologic efficiency by reshaping the ecological environment into which biologics are introduced.

Several lines of evidence support this hypothesis.

First, enteral nutrition reduces inflammatory burden and normalises biomarkers such as CRP and fecal calprotectin. Second, microbiome restructuring may reduce antigenic stimulation and mucosal permeability. Third, nutritional prehabilitation before surgery reduces postoperative complications, suggesting broader immunometabolic benefits.

Retrospective studies combining biologics with EEN suggest improved transmural healing and reduced repeat surgery compared with biologic monotherapy. Although these studies remain limited and potentially confounded, they support the idea that nutrition may function as a biologic sensitiser.

This concept becomes particularly important during PIERW.

If the postoperative intestine represents a transient state of ecological vulnerability, then combined immune modulation and ecological stabilisation may be more effective than either strategy alone.

We therefore propose that future trials should move beyond drug-versus-drug comparisons and instead evaluate integrated pathway strategies such as:

·      vedolizumab plus cyclic PEN;

·      anti-TNF plus microbiome-directed dietary modulation;

·      preoperative EEN followed by postoperative biologic maintenance;

·      AI-guided escalation triggered by ecological instability.

The central question is no longer “which therapy suppresses recurrence?” but “which combination most effectively stabilises postoperative remodelling?”

  

Why endoscopic success and patient experience diverge: a critical paradox requiring outcome system redesign

One of the most important unresolved tensions in postoperative nutrition research is the profound disconnect between biological efficacy (endoscopic healing, biomarker remission) and lived patient experience (symptomatic remission, quality of life, functional recovery). **This paradox has been interpreted as evidence of therapeutic failure, but it actually reveals a fundamental limitation in how we measure postoperative success.**

Postoperative EEN studies consistently demonstrate stronger effects on endoscopic recurrence than on symptomatic remission or quality of life. This discrepancy should not be dismissed as methodological weakness. Rather, it exposes a deeper limitation in current treatment paradigms.

Contemporary treat-to-target frameworks prioritise objective inflammation control because symptoms correlate poorly with mucosal disease activity—a principle justified by evidence that silent endoscopic recurrence progresses to fibrotic and symptomatic disease. From a biological and disease-prevention perspective, preventing endoscopic recurrence is thus highly meaningful. **[TEMPORAL BIOLOGY: Mucosal healing precedes symptomatic improvement by weeks to months, because epithelial remodelling and immune normalization occur before symptom resolution.]**

However, a prevention strategy that substantially worsens social functioning, eating behaviour or psychological wellbeing may prove unsustainable in real-world implementation.

This creates a paradox that current trial design cannot resolve:

- The interventions most capable of ecological restructuring (exclusive EEN) may be the least behaviourally tolerable—they achieve strong mucosal healing but at the cost of substantial quality-of-life impairment during the intervention period;
- The interventions most tolerable (standard biologic therapy, no diet restriction) may produce insufficient ecological modification to prevent recurrence—better quality of life but less durable remission.

**[DESIGN PROBLEM: Most postoperative studies are powered to detect endoscopic endpoints but under-powered for quality-of-life outcomes, so the trade-off remains largely invisible in trial results.]**

The field therefore requires a new concept of therapeutic efficiency integrating:

·      biological control;

·      behavioural sustainability;

·      patient-valued outcomes.

Current RCTs remain poorly equipped to capture this complexity. Most postoperative studies prioritise endoscopic recurrence while under-measuring:

·      food-related quality of life;

·      treatment fatigue;

·      social burden;

·      long-term adherence;

·      patient preference;

·      functional recovery.

This omission is particularly problematic for health technology assessment.

NICE’s original research recommendation explicitly asked about cost-effectiveness, yet most postoperative nutritional studies still lack preference-based utility measures such as EQ-5D-5L. Consequently, the field can demonstrate biological activity without fully demonstrating societal value.

Future trials should therefore incorporate dual-target outcome frameworks:

1. Biological outcomes:

o   endoscopic recurrence;

o   transmural healing;

o   biomarker remission.

1. Human-centred outcomes:

o   treatment acceptability;

o   food-related quality of life;

o   functional restoration;

o   work productivity;

o   long-term adherence.

Without this integration, even highly effective ecological therapies may fail implementation at population level.

  

Rethinking surveillance: from episodic endoscopy to continuous monitoring

Postoperative surveillance remains anchored around ileocolonoscopy at 6–12 months. Although endoscopy remains indispensable, this model reflects a fundamentally episodic understanding of recurrence.

The emergence of AI-enabled multimodal monitoring allows a shift toward dynamic surveillance.

Intestinal ultrasound (IUS) increasingly demonstrates strong correlation with postoperative recurrence while offering lower burden and repeatability. Home fecal calprotectin platforms permit longitudinal biomarker tracking. AI-assisted endoscopic scoring may improve standardisation and reduce interobserver variability.

These technologies become particularly powerful when integrated.

We envision a tiered surveillance architecture:

Tier 1: continuous low-burden monitoring

·      home fecal calprotectin;

·      wearable-derived functional metrics;

·      symptom-independent longitudinal data capture.

Tier 2: interval structural assessment

·      point-of-care intestinal ultrasound;

·      AI-assisted inflammatory scoring;

·      early transmural change detection.

Tier 3: confirmatory precision endoscopy

·      AI-enhanced ileocolonoscopy;

·      automated Rutgeerts classification;

·      molecular sampling of anastomotic tissue.

Importantly, this approach supports dynamic Bayesian prediction rather than static categorisation.

Risk is not fixed at surgery.

Instead, recurrence probability evolves continuously according to ecological, inflammatory and behavioural trajectories.

Such systems could ultimately allow:

·      early escalation in biologically unstable patients;

·      de-escalation in immune-quiescent phenotypes;

·      reduction of unnecessary prophylaxis.

The integration of AI into postoperative CD therefore matters less as a technological novelty than as a means of operationalising precision prevention.

  

Health technology assessment after NICE 2019: why current economic frameworks are insufficient

The NICE 2019 committee concluded that biologics lacked sufficient certainty and cost-effectiveness for routine postoperative use, while enteral nutrition lacked direct evidence altogether.

In retrospect, these conclusions were reasonable within the evidence framework available at the time.

However, the subsequent evolution of the field exposes important limitations in conventional HTA methodology.

Traditional HTA models typically evaluate therapies as isolated interventions with relatively short-term endpoints. Yet postoperative Crohn’s disease is a longitudinal systems problem.

The value of nutritional therapy may emerge indirectly through:

·      reduction in repeat surgery;

·      delayed biologic switching;

·      preservation of biologic response;

·      reduction in steroid exposure;

·      improved nutritional resilience;

·      prevention of sarcopenia;

·      reduction in healthcare utilisation.

Similarly, the value of AI-guided monitoring may derive less from improved diagnosis than from preventing delayed escalation.

Conventional cost-per-QALY frameworks struggle to capture these interacting pathway effects.

This is especially important because postoperative prevention strategies increasingly function as integrated ecosystems rather than single interventions.

A patient receiving:

·      preoperative nutritional optimisation;

·      postoperative vedolizumab;

·      cyclic PEN;

·      AI-enabled biomarker monitoring;

·      ultrasound-guided escalation,

cannot easily be analysed within a single-intervention economic model.

HTA agencies therefore need to evolve from intervention-centric assessment toward pathway-centric assessment.

Three changes are particularly necessary.

First, postoperative CD should be treated as a secondary-prevention domain

The relevant comparator is not merely placebo or delayed recurrence, but prevention of irreversible bowel damage.

Second, implementation burden must become part of value assessment

Adherence, dietary burden and psychosocial cost materially affect effectiveness.

Third, integrated strategies should be evaluated as systems

The economic value of combined nutritional and biologic approaches may exceed the additive value of individual components.

This shift mirrors broader transitions in precision medicine, where outcome depends increasingly on orchestration rather than singular therapeutic potency.

  

A research agenda for the next decade

The central challenge after 2026 is no longer demonstrating that postoperative recurrence occurs or that nutritional therapy has biological effects.

The challenge is learning how to operationalise precision prevention.

We propose five priorities.

1. Mechanism-matched adaptive trials

Future studies should stratify patients according to biological phenotype rather than randomising heterogeneous populations into uniform interventions.

Barrier-dominant, immune-dominant and fibrosis-dominant phenotypes may require fundamentally different strategies.

2. Precision nutrition platforms

Microbiome and metabolomic profiling should be used to predict responsiveness to:

·      EEN;

·      PEN;

·      CDED;

·      biologic–nutrition combinations.

The future of nutritional therapy lies in ecological targeting rather than universal prescription.

3. Integrated behavioural science

Adherence should not be treated as a secondary implementation issue.

Behavioural sustainability is itself a biological determinant because ecological therapies fail if not maintained.

Digital support platforms, food-behaviour interventions and palatability optimisation deserve formal investigation.

4. AI-enabled longitudinal prediction

Dynamic Bayesian models integrating biomarkers, imaging and ecological data should replace static postoperative scoring systems.

The goal is continuous trajectory prediction rather than episodic classification.

5. Redefinition of endpoints

Future postoperative trials should include:

·      endoscopic healing;

·      transmural healing;

·      ecological restoration;

·      patient preference;

·      functional recovery;

·      health economic durability.

The field requires composite endpoints capable of capturing biological and human sustainability simultaneously.

  

Conclusion: the future is prevention, not rescue

The NICE 2019 research recommendation on postoperative enteral nutrition correctly identified an evidence gap, but the field has since revealed that the true problem was conceptual rather than merely evidentiary.

Postoperative Crohn’s disease is not a stable maintenance phase.

It is a transient immune–ecological remodelling state during which disease trajectory may still be redirected.

The concept of the postoperative immune–ecological remodelling window (PIERW) provides a unifying framework linking:

·      microbiome recolonisation;

·      epithelial barrier repair;

·      immune reprogramming;

·      nutritional modulation;

·      biologic deployment;

·      AI-enabled surveillance.

Within this framework, enteral nutrition becomes more than adjunctive support. It becomes ecosystem engineering.

Similarly, biologics become more than rescue agents. They become tools for stabilising remodelling trajectories.

The future of postoperative CD management will not be defined by increasingly aggressive escalation after recurrence has already emerged.

It will be defined by the ability to identify which postoperative ecosystem is becoming unstable, intervene before irreversible damage develops, and sustain remission in ways that remain biologically effective, behaviourally tolerable and economically viable.

The next decade should therefore move beyond the binary question of whether enteral nutrition works.

The more important question is whether we are finally prepared to treat postoperative Crohn’s disease as a precision-prevention problem.

Ileocolonic resection removes macroscopic inflammatory lesions but initiates a finite period of biological instability during which microbial recolonisation, epithelial repair and mucosal immune reprogramming collectively shape long-term disease trajectory. Within the postoperative immune–ecological remodelling window (PIERW; approximately 0–6 months after surgery), bidirectional interactions among microbiome dynamics, barrier remodelling and immune activation determine whether the neoterminal ileum progresses towards immune homeostasis or recurrent inflammation. Nutritional interventions (exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN), partial enteral nutrition (PEN) and Crohn’s disease exclusion diet (CDED)), biologics and AI-enabled surveillance may redirect this remodelling process before irreversible structural recurrence develops. Failed remodelling results in persistent dysbiosis, barrier dysfunction, transmural inflammation and repeat surgery.

Figure 1 | The three biological phenotypes of the postoperative immune–ecological remodelling window (PIERW): mechanistic diversity requiring phenotype-specific intervention

After ileocolonic resection, the neoterminal ileum may undergo divergent remodelling trajectories determined by distinct mechanistic phenotypes that influence recurrence risk and therapeutic responsiveness. **Barrier-dominant PIERW (teal)** is characterised by epithelial permeability defects, tight-junction disruption and depletion of butyrate-producing commensals (particularly Faecalibacterium prausnitzii), with minimal inflammatory cell infiltration and preserved mucosal architecture. This phenotype has low systemic inflammatory markers but high fecal calprotectin (reflecting epithelial barrier loss), and responds preferentially to combined ecological engineering (enteral nutrition) and gut-selective immune trafficking blockade (vedolizumab). **Immune-dominant PIERW (amber)** is associated with amplified Th1/Th17 signalling, mesenteric inflammation, dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltration, and systemic immune activation marked by elevated CRP and fecal calprotectin. This phenotype requires broad-spectrum systemic immune suppression (anti-TNF or IL-12/23 antagonists) rather than nutritional modulation. **Fibrosis-dominant PIERW (purple)** is characterised by upregulation of profibrotic gene modules (e.g., PLOD2, ZFPM2), mesenchymal cell activation, extracellular matrix remodelling, mast-cell accumulation, and progressive structural change—yet often presents with low inflammatory biomarkers, representing "biochemically silent" structural progression. This phenotype requires intensive structural surveillance and future anti-fibrotic strategies, not standard anti-inflammatory therapy alone. 

The taxonomy proposes that postoperative prevention should be aligned to mechanistically distinct biological phenotypes, with treatment selection matched to dominant remodelling biology—ecological destabilization, immune hyperactivation, or fibrotic progression—rather than applied uniformly across all patients as current practice dictates.

Figure 2 | The Stratify–Adapt–Dynamic (SAD) platform architecture during the postoperative immune–ecological remodelling window: operationalizing phenotype-specific precision prevention

Patients undergoing ileocolonic resection are stratified at baseline (Pillar 1, pre-discharge or within 2 weeks post-surgery) into barrier-dominant, immune-dominant or fibrosis-dominant phenotypes using integrated multimodal assessment: clinical phenotype, surgical pathology, mucosal transcriptomics (barrier, Th1/Th17, and fibrotic gene modules), microbiome composition (diversity, protective commensals, dysbiotic signatures), inflammatory biomarkers (CRP, fecal calprotectin), and AI-assisted histopathology (plexitis, mast-cell density, fibrotic markers). Within each phenotypic stratum, participants undergo mechanism-matched randomisation to phenotype-aligned interventions (Pillar 3). Longitudinal Bayesian monitoring (Pillar 2) continuously updates recurrence probability using: fecal calprotectin trajectories, intestinal ultrasound with shear-wave elastography (3 and 6 months), therapeutic drug monitoring (vedolizumab/anti-TNF levels), AI-assisted endoscopic scoring (automated Rutgeerts assessment), and microbiome evolution. Pre-specified biomarker thresholds trigger adaptive treatment decisions: escalation (dose intensification, class-switching) or de-escalation (biologic spacing, discontinuation in deep remission). Trial endpoints span three domains: biological (Rutgeerts score, transmural healing, biomarker remission, microbiome restoration), patient-centred (EQ-5D-5L, food-related quality of life, treatment burden, adherence durability, work productivity), and systems-level (repeat surgery rate, biologic switching, cumulative steroid exposure, cost per remission-year). This integrated architecture enables simultaneous evaluation of mechanistic efficacy, behavioural sustainability, and health-system impact—generating precision-prevention evidence rather than isolated efficacy data.

Figure 2 | Stratify–adapt–dynamic (SAD) framework for precision postoperative management

A dynamic precision-prevention framework integrating multimodal baseline stratification, adaptive surveillance and mechanism-matched intervention after ileocolonic resection. Clinical phenotype, surgical pathology, transcriptomics, microbiome composition, inflammatory biomarkers and AI-enhanced imaging feed into a continuously updated prediction engine. Patients transition dynamically between immune-quiescent, barrier/ecology-dominant and immune-aggressive trajectories rather than remaining within fixed risk categories. Continuous monitoring through home biomarkers, intestinal ultrasound and AI-assisted endoscopy enables real-time recalibration of recurrence risk and treatment adaptation. The framework reframes postoperative management from static prophylaxis towards longitudinal trajectory control.

Figure 3 | Reframing enteral nutrition from supportive care to ecosystem engineering: mechanism-matched nutritional strategies across PIERW phenotypes

Western dietary exposure after ileocolonic resection promotes dysbiosis (loss of Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and other butyrate-producing commensals), epithelial permeability through dietary emulsifier and additive effects, and inflammatory amplification through antigenic burden and dysbiosis-mediated immune activation. By contrast, enteral nutrition-based strategies reshape the postoperative ecosystem through: microbiome restoration (restoring protective commensals and SCFA production), metabolic normalisation (restoration of short-chain fatty acid metabolism and epithelial barrier function), and barrier repair (reduced antigenic stimulation and epithelial tight-junction stabilization). Exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN), partial enteral nutrition (PEN), Crohn's disease exclusion diet (CDED)-based approaches and biologic–nutrition combination strategies differ critically in their ecological potency (capacity to reshape dysbiosis), behavioural sustainability (feasibility of adherence during PIERW), and mechanistic alignment with patient phenotype. For barrier-dominant phenotypes, formula-based nutritional strategies provide maximal ecological restructuring; for immune-dominant phenotypes, standard biologic therapy alone without nutritional restrictions optimizes patient experience; for fibrosis-dominant phenotypes, standard nutrition (no restriction) is appropriate as their pathology is structural remodeling, not ecological destabilization. The therapeutic value of nutritional therapy therefore derives from deliberate ecological engineering aligned to dominant remodelling phenotype—selecting nutrition strategies that match the mechanistic basis of recurrence risk while remaining behaviorally sustainable during the finite PIERW intervention window.

**Box 1 | Clinical vignettes illustrating postoperative immune–ecological remodelling window (PIERW) phenotypes**

**Patient A | Barrier-dominant PIERW**

A 28-year-old woman with inflammatory ileocolonic Crohn’s disease (B1 phenotype) undergoes first ileocolic resection with stapled side-to-side anastomosis. Before surgery, she achieved clinical remission with exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN). Histopathology demonstrates relatively preserved mucosal architecture with reduced occludin expression and minimal submucosal inflammatory infiltration. Microbiome profiling reveals low α-diversity with depletion of _Faecalibacterium prausnitzii_. At postoperative week 2, faecal calprotectin is modestly elevated (180 μg g⁻¹) despite normal CRP concentrations and absence of systemic symptoms.

She is classified as having a barrier-dominant remodelling phenotype at baseline using the Pillar 1 stratification algorithm (low inflammatory markers, reduced occludin expression, dysbiotic microbiota, minimal mast-cell infiltration).

**Mechanism-matched intervention (Pillar 3):** Vedolizumab (gut-selective α4β7 integrin antagonist) combined with cyclical partial enteral nutrition (50% formula, 50% normal diet). Mechanistic rationale: EN restores butyrate-producing commensals and reduces antigenic burden; vedolizumab prevents pathogenic gut-tropic lymphocyte trafficking.

**Longitudinal adaptation (Pillar 2):** At 3 months, faecal calprotectin declines to 95 μg g⁻¹ and ultrasound shows minimal wall thickening—trajectory supports continuation. At 6 months, ileocolonoscopy demonstrates Rutgeerts i1 (minimal ulceration). Treatment maintained.

**Integrated outcomes at 12 months:** 
- Biological: Deep biomarker remission (FCal <50 μg g⁻¹), restored microbial diversity, Rutgeerts i0-i1
- Patient-centered: Normal eating behavior, restored social participation, returned to work

**Biological interpretation**

Recurrence risk is driven primarily by epithelial dysfunction and ecological instability rather than systemic immune activation. This phenotype responds optimally to combined ecological engineering + gut-selective immunity.

**Mechanism-matched therapeutic priorities**

- Ecological modulation (dietary restructuring via formula);
- Barrier restoration (gut-selective immune blockade without systemic suppression);
- Behavioral sustainability (cyclical EN for adherence during PIERW period).

**Patient B

**Patient B | Immune-dominant PIERW**

A 34-year-old man with penetrating ileocolonic Crohn’s disease (B3 phenotype) undergoes repeat resection following secondary loss of response to adalimumab and prior intra-abdominal abscess formation. Histopathology demonstrates marked mesenteric inflammation (“creeping fat”) with dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltration and prominent Th1/Th17 transcriptomic activation. Serological testing is positive for ASCA-IgG and anti-OmpC antibodies.

At postoperative week 4, CRP concentration is 28 mg L⁻¹ and faecal calprotectin exceeds 1,000 μg g⁻¹. He is classified as having an immune-dominant remodelling phenotype at baseline using Pillar 1 stratification (elevated inflammatory markers, mesenteric inflammation, Th1/Th17 transcriptomic activation, positive serology, dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltration).

**Mechanism-matched intervention (Pillar 3):** Ustekinumab (dual IL-12/IL-23 antagonist targeting Th1/Th17 axis) combined with azathioprine. Mechanistic rationale: His dominant pathology is amplified adaptive immunity and mesenteric inflammation, not ecological dysbiosis—requires broad systemic immune suppression rather than ecological engineering. No restrictive diet needed; standard biologic-immunomodulator combinations are optimal.

**Longitudinal adaptation (Pillar 2):** At 3 months, CRP normalizes to 3 mg L⁻¹ and faecal calprotectin drops to 180 μg g⁻¹. Intestinal ultrasound shows no transmural inflammatory activity—biomarker trajectory excellent. At 6 months, ileocolonoscopy demonstrates Rutgeerts i0 (no recurrence).

**Integrated outcomes at 12 months:**
- Biological: Complete inflammatory remission (FCal <50 μg g⁻¹, CRP normal), Rutgeerts i0, no mesenteric changes on ultrasound
- Patient-centered: Unrestricted diet tolerated, no treatment burden, maintained employment

**Biological interpretation**

Recurrence risk is driven predominantly by persistent adaptive immune activation and mesenteric inflammatory signaling—fundamentally different biology from Patient A. This phenotype requires early, aggressive systemic immune suppression rather than ecological modulation.

**Mechanism-matched therapeutic priorities**

- Early biologic prophylaxis (within 2 weeks of surgery, targeting the inflammatory window);
- Systemic immune suppression (dual targeting IL-12/IL-23 axis in Th1/Th17-dominant disease);
- Tight inflammatory monitoring (monthly biomarkers to detect breakthrough inflammation early).

**Patient C

**Patient C | Fibrosis-dominant PIERW**

A 42-year-old man with recurrent stricturing jejunoileitis (B2 phenotype) presents with high-grade small-bowel obstruction requiring resection after multiple prior balloon dilations. Histology demonstrates extensive submucosal collagen deposition, elevated profibrotic transcriptomic markers (including _PLOD2_ and _ZFPM2_) and dense mast-cell accumulation around the myenteric plexus.

At postoperative month 3, intestinal ultrasound reveals anastomotic wall thickening with increased stiffness on shear-wave elastography despite minimal inflammatory biomarker elevation. He is classified as having a fibrosis-dominant remodelling phenotype at baseline using Pillar 1 stratification (elevated profibrotic transcriptomics, extensive submucosal collagen, mast-cell accumulation, minimal inflammatory markers despite stricturing disease).

**Mechanism-matched intervention (Pillar 3):** Ustekinumab to control any residual immune activation, combined with intensive structural surveillance protocol (ultrasound at 1, 3, 6 months with shear-wave elastography). 

**Dietary management strategy—timing-dependent:**
- **During PIERW prevention phase (0–6 months)**: Standard/normal diet maintained. The fibrotic mechanism (TGF-β signaling, mesenchymal activation, extracellular matrix deposition) is NOT driven by luminal ecology or microbiota factors—dietary modification cannot prevent the structural remodeling process itself. Fiber restriction has no evidence base for preventing fibrosis progression and risks secondary dysbiosis without benefit.
- **Post-detection strategy (after stenosis identified)**: Once asymptomatic anastomotic stenosis is detected on surveillance (as occurred in this patient at 6 months), transition to low-residue/fiber-restricted diet becomes appropriate for symptom management and mechanical stress reduction across the stricture site, in combination with therapeutic endoscopic intervention (prophylactic dilation).

**Mechanistic rationale:** Fibrosis-dominant patients need **early detection** (structural surveillance) and **mechanical intervention** (endoscopic dilation), not prophylactic dietary manipulation. The pathology is mesenchymal activation and extracellular matrix deposition, which requires anti-fibrotic drug targeting (future TGF-β inhibitors) and structural intervention, not ecological engineering.

**Longitudinal adaptation (Pillar 2):** At 3 months, faecal calprotectin remains low (40 μg g⁻¹) but intestinal ultrasound reveals anastomotic wall thickening with increased stiffness on shear-wave elastography (3.5 kPa, elevated). This signals fibrotic progression despite biochemical quiescence—a critical finding unique to fibrosis-dominant phenotypes. Decision to escalate surveillance frequency.

**Integrated outcomes at 24 months:**
- Biological: Controlled inflammation (FCal <50 μg g⁻¹, CRP normal), but early asymptomatic anastomotic stenosis identified at 6 months on surveillance endoscopy
- Clinical action: Prophylactic balloon dilation performed (avoiding repeat surgery)
- Patient-centered: Maintained normal diet and work capacity; stenosis managed endoscopically rather than surgically

**Biological interpretation**

Disease progression is driven primarily by mesenchymal remodeling and structural progression rather than overt inflammatory recurrence. This patient demonstrates why fibrosis-dominant phenotypes REQUIRE intensive structural surveillance—they are "biochemically silent" but structurally progressive. Standard endoscopic surveillance at 6-12 months misses their disease. Early detection via intensive ultrasound prevented repeat surgery.

**Mechanism-matched therapeutic priorities**

- Structural surveillance (3-monthly ultrasound with elastography to detect early remodeling);
- Adaptive dietary management (standard diet during PIERW; fiber restriction post-detection for symptom control);
- Anti-fibrotic strategies (standard biologic + future investigational agents targeting TGF-β pathways);
- Early mechanical intervention (prophylactic endoscopic dilation of detected stenosis before symptomatic obstruction develops).

**Clinical implication**

These vignettes illustrate how similar surgical procedures may initiate biologically divergent postoperative trajectories. Under conventional postoperative algorithms, all three patients would typically receive similar surveillance and prophylactic strategies. Within the PIERW framework, however, postoperative management is aligned to the dominant remodelling mechanism — epithelial-barrier instability, adaptive immune activation or fibrotic progression — enabling mechanism-matched prevention rather than uniform prophylaxis.

This shift represents a conceptual transition from static postoperative risk categorisation towards dynamic biological trajectory management.

**Box 2 | Adaptive precision-prevention trial architecture during PIERW**

**Trial concept.** A biomarker-stratified, response-adaptive platform trial enrolling patients immediately after ileocolonic resection, designed to evaluate integrated prevention _ecosystems_ rather than isolated therapies.

**Step 1 | Baseline biological stratification.** Participants are classified into barrier-dominant, immune-dominant or fibrosis-dominant phenotypes using a pre-specified multi-omics algorithm integrating: (i) anastomotic histopathology (plexitis, mast-cell density, adipocyte morphology); (ii) mucosal transcriptomics (barrier-function, Th1/Th17 and fibrotic gene modules); (iii) faecal microbiota (alpha-diversity, _F. prausnitzii_ relative abundance); (iv) faecal calprotectin and plasma CRP; and (v) AI-derived imaging signatures from resection specimens.

**Step 2 | Mechanism-matched randomisation.** Within each stratum, participants are randomised to mechanism-matched interventions:

- _Barrier-dominant_: vedolizumab alone _versus_ vedolizumab plus cyclical partial enteral nutrition (or CDED).
- _Immune-dominant_: anti-TNF _versus_ ustekinumab, with optional immunomodulator augmentation based on prior exposure.
- _Fibrosis-dominant_: standard biologic prophylaxis _versus_ biologic plus investigational anti-fibrotic agent (e.g., TGF-β pathway inhibitor).

**Step 3 | Dynamic adaptation.** Bayesian adaptive modelling continuously updates individual recurrence probability using longitudinal biomarker trajectories, intestinal ultrasound at 3 and 6 months, and AI-assisted ileocolonoscopy at 6–12 months. Pre-specified biomarker thresholds trigger treatment escalation (within-class dose intensification or cross-class switch) or de-escalation (biologic spacing or withdrawal in deep remission), mimicking a closed-loop control system.

**Step 4 | Integrated endpoints.** Co-primary endpoints span three domains: (i) _Biological_—Rutgeerts score, transmural healing on intestinal ultrasound, biomarker remission (faecal calprotectin <100 μg g⁻¹), and microbiome restoration; (ii) _Human-centred_—EQ-5D-5L utility values, food-related quality of life, treatment burden, adherence durability, and work productivity; and (iii) _Systems-level_—repeat surgery, biologic switching rate, cumulative corticosteroid exposure, and cost per remission-year.

**Conceptual implication.** By embedding nutritional, pharmacological and monitoring interventions within a single adaptive architecture, this design moves beyond the binary drug-versus-placebo paradigm and generates the preference-based, cost-effectiveness evidence required by health technology assessment agencies.
